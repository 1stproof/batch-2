## Lemmas, Theorems, Propositions, Corollaries
- **Proposition 1.2.1.** The number of vertices of odd degree in a graph is always even.
  *Proof:* Uses double counting of edges.
- **Proposition 1.2.2.** Every graph G with at least one edge has a subgraph H with δ(H) > ε(H) ⩾ ε(G).
  *Proof:* Constructs the subgraph iteratively by deleting vertices of degree at most ε without lowering the overall edge-to-vertex ratio.
- **Proposition 1.3.1.** Every graph G contains a path of length δ(G) and a cycle of length at least δ(G) + 1 (provided that δ(G) ⩾ 2).
  *Proof:* Takes a longest path and closes a cycle using the neighbors of its endpoint.
- **Proposition 1.3.2.** Every graph G containing a cycle satisfies g(G) ⩽ 2 diam(G) + 1.
  *Proof:* Argues by contradiction that a shorter cycle would bypass paths of excessive distance.
- **Proposition 1.3.3.** A graph G of radius at most k and maximum degree at most d has no more than 1 + kdk vertices.
  *Proof:* Bounds the size of distance layers progressively outward from a central vertex.
- **Proposition 1.4.1.** The vertices of a connected graph G can always be enumerated, say as v1 , . . . , vn , so that Gi := G [ v1 , . . . , vi ] is connected for every i.
  *Proof:* Iteratively selects neighbors of the already constructed connected components.
- **Theorem 1.4.2.** (Mader 1972) Every graph of average degree at least 4k has a k-connected subgraph.
  *Proof:* Uses induction on the graph's order, leaning on a strengthened condition to ensure a k-connected subgraph or decompose non-connected candidates.
- **Theorem 1.5.1.** The following assertions are equivalent for a graph T : (i) T is a tree; (ii) any two vertices of T are linked by a unique path in T ; (iii) T is minimally connected, i.e. T is connected but T − e is disconnected for every edge e ∈ T ; (iv) T is maximally acyclic, i.e. T contains no cycle but T + xy does, for any two non-adjacent vertices x, y ∈ T .
  *Proof:* (no proof in this paper)
- **Corollary 1.5.2.** The vertices of a tree can always be enumerated, say as v1 , . . . , vn , so that every vi with i ⩾ 2 has a unique neighbour in { v1 , . . . , vi−1 }.
  *Proof:* (immediate from Proposition 1.4.1)
- **Corollary 1.5.3.** A connected graph with n vertices is a tree if and only if it has n − 1 edges.
  *Proof:* Proves the equivalence by inductively counting edges in the sequential vertex enumeration.
- **Corollary 1.5.4.** If T is a tree and G is any graph with δ(G) ⩾ |T | − 1, then T ⊆ G, i.e. G has a subgraph isomorphic to T .
  *Proof:* Embeds the tree inductively using its vertex enumeration.
- **Proposition 1.5.5.** Every connected graph contains a normal spanning tree, with any specified vertex as its root.
  *Proof:* Assumes a maximal normal tree and shows by contradiction that it spans the entire graph.
- **Proposition 1.6.1.** A graph is bipartite if and only if it contains no odd cycle.
  *Proof:* Partitions the vertices based on the parity of their distances from a root in a spanning tree.
- **Proposition 1.7.1.** G is an M X if and only if X can be obtained from G by a series of edge contractions, i.e. if and only if there are graphs G0 , . . . , Gn and edges ei ∈ Gi such that G0 = G, Gn ≃ X, and Gi+1 = Gi /ei for all i < n.
  *Proof:* Applies induction on the order difference between the graph and its minor.

- **Theorem 1.8.1.** (Euler 1736) A connected graph is Eulerian if and only if every vertex has even degree.
  *Proof:* Finds a maximal closed walk and argues by connectedness that it must cover all edges.
- **Proposition 1.9.1.** The induced cycles in G generate its entire cycle space.
  *Proof:* Uses induction on cycle length to decompose cycles with chords.
- **Proposition 1.9.2.** An edge set F ⊆ E lies in C(G) if and only if every vertex of (V, F ) has even degree.
  *Proof:* Uses induction on the number of cycles to establish the parity property.
- **Proposition 1.9.3.** Together with ∅, the cuts in G form a subspace C ∗ of E(G). This space is generated by cuts of the form E(v).
  *Proof:* Verifies that cuts are closed under symmetric difference and are generated by single-vertex cuts.
- **Lemma 1.9.4.** The minimal cuts in a connected graph generate its entire cut space.
  *Proof:* Expresses arbitrary cuts as unions of minimal cuts corresponding to connected components.
- **Theorem 1.9.5.** The cycle space C and the cut space C ∗ of any graph satisfy C = C ∗⊥ and C ∗ = C ⊥ .
  *Proof:* Evaluates orthogonality based on the intersection parity between cycles and cuts.
- **Theorem 1.9.6.** Every connected graph G with n vertices and m edges satisfies dim C(G) = m − n + 1 and dim C ∗ (G) = n − 1 .
  *Proof:* Identifies linearly independent cycles and cuts by utilizing a spanning tree and its remaining edges.

- **Theorem 2.1.1.** (König 1931) The maximum cardinality of a matching in G is equal to the minimum cardinality of a vertex cover.
  *Proof:* Constructs a minimal cover via alternating paths originating from unmatched vertices.
- **Theorem 2.1.2.** (Hall 1935) G contains a matching of A if and only if |N (S)| ⩾ |S| for all S ⊆ A.
  *Proof:* Offered in three variants: via König's theorem, via augmenting paths, and via induction on the size of the partition set.
- **Corollary 2.1.3.** If |N (S)| ⩾ |S| − d for every set S ⊆ A and some fixed d ∈ N, then G contains a matching of cardinality |A| − d.
  *Proof:* Appends dummy vertices to the bipartite graph and applies the marriage theorem.
- **Corollary 2.1.4.** If G is k-regular with k ⩾ 1, then G has a 1-factor.
  *Proof:* Verifies the marriage condition by analyzing edge incidences out of any subset.
- **Corollary 2.1.5.** (Petersen 1891) Every regular graph of positive even degree has a 2-factor.
  *Proof:* Splits vertices on an Euler tour to construct a 1-factor in the resulting bipartite graph.
- **Theorem 2.2.1.** (Tutte 1947) A graph G has a 1-factor if and only if q(G − S) ⩽ |S| for all S ⊆ V (G).
  *Proof:* Assumes edge-maximality without a 1-factor to pinpoint a bad set, ultimately deriving a contradiction via alternating paths to locate a missed perfect matching.
- **Corollary 2.2.2.** (Petersen 1891) Every bridgeless cubic graph has a 1-factor.
  *Proof:* Validates Tutte's condition by counting boundary edges of odd components.
- **Theorem 2.2.3.** Every graph G = (V, E) contains a vertex set S with the following two properties: (i) S is matchable to G − S; (ii) every component of G − S is factor-critical. Given any such set S, the graph G contains a 1-factor if and only if |S| = |CG−S |.
  *Proof:* Inducts on graph size, selecting a maximal set tightly bounding odd components, and applies the marriage theorem backwards to find matchings into S.
- **Theorem 2.3.1.** (Gallai & Milgram 1960) Every directed graph G has a path cover by at most α(G) paths.
  *Proof:* Uses induction to modify a minimal path cover until its endpoints form an independent set.
- **Corollary 2.3.2.** (Dilworth 1950) In every finite partially ordered set (P, ⩽), the minimum number of chains covering P is equal to the maximum cardinality of an antichain in P .
  *Proof:* Applies the path cover theorem to a directed graph mapping the poset relations.

  *Proof:* Proceeds by contradiction, picking an edge and minimizing a separating set to violate component properties.
- **Theorem 3.2.2.** (Tutte 1961) A graph G is 3-connected if and only if there exists a sequence G0 , . . . , Gn of graphs with the following properties: (i) G0 = K 4 and Gn = G; (ii) Gi+1 has an edge xy with d(x), d(y) ⩾ 3 and Gi = Gi+1 /xy, for every i < n.
  *Proof:* Proves the reverse direction by analyzing separating sets of contracted neighbors.
- **Theorem 3.2.3.** (Tutte 1963) The cycle space of a 3-connected graph is generated by its non-separating induced cycles.
  *Proof:* Deploys induction through edge contraction, tracking basis cycles in the minor and lifting them alongside fundamental triangles into the parent space.
- **Theorem 3.3.1.** (Menger 1927) Let G = (V, E) be a graph and A, B ⊆ V . Then the minimum number of vertices separating A from B in G is equal to the maximum number of disjoint A–B paths in G.
  *Proof:* Proved in three distinct ways: via induction on the separating set, via edge induction, and via constructing an alternating walk.
- **Lemma 3.3.2.** If such a walk W exists, then G contains |P| + 1 disjoint A–B paths.
  *Proof:* Follows components in the symmetric difference of path edges and alternating walks.
- **Corollary 3.3.3.** For B ⊆ V and a ∈ V r B, the minimum number of vertices 6= a separating a from B in G is equal to the maximum number of paths forming an a–B fan in G.
  *Proof:* (immediate from Theorem 3.3.1)
- **Corollary 3.3.4.** Let a and b be two distinct vertices of G. (i) If ab ∈/ E, then the minimum number of vertices 6= a, b separating a from b in G is equal to the maximum number of independent a–b paths in G. (ii) The minimum number of edges separating a from b in G is equal to the maximum number of edge-disjoint a–b paths in G.
  *Proof:* Applies Menger's theorem to direct neighbors, and subsequently to the line graph.
- **Theorem 3.3.5.** (Global Version of Menger’s Theorem) (i) A graph is k-connected if and only if it contains k independent paths between any two vertices. (ii) A graph is k-edge-connected if and only if it contains k edge-disjoint paths between any two vertices.
  *Proof:* Establishes general equivalence by using edge removal and vertex separators coupled with Menger's bounds.

- **Theorem 3.5.1.** (Tutte 1961; Nash-Williams 1961) A multigraph contains k edge-disjoint spanning trees if and only if for every partition P of its vertex set it has at least k (|P | − 1) cross-edges.
