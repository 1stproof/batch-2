**Question:** Is there an order-reversing involution $\Phi$ on $\Dr(M)$ that extends the involution $F\mapsto F^\perp$?
**Answer:** No. We provide a counterexample using the Fano matroid.

**Step 1: The Fano Matroid and its Involution**
Let $M = F_7$ be the Fano plane matroid on the ground set $\{1, \dots, 7\}$. The lattice of flats $\cL(M)$ consists of rank 0 ($\emptyset$), rank 1 (7 points), rank 2 (7 lines), and rank 3 (the whole set). A standard polarity of the Fano plane provides an incidence-reversing bijection between points and lines. This polarity induces an order-reversing involution $F \mapsto F^\perp$ on $\cL(M)$ that maps the empty set to the whole set, points to lines, and vice versa. Thus, $F_7$ satisfies the hypothesis of the problem.

**Step 2: Structure of the Ambient Space $\Trop(M)$**
The Bergman fan $\Trop(F_7) \subset \R^7/\R\mathbf{1}$ is a 2-dimensional polyhedral fan consisting of the origin, 14 rays (one for each point $p_i$ and each line $L_j$), and 21 two-dimensional sectors spanning flags $(p_i, L_j)$ for $p_i \in L_j$. The primitive vectors for the rays are $e_i$ for points, and $e_a+e_b+e_c$ for a line $\{a,b,c\}$. 

The relative Dressian $\Dr(F_7)$ is the poset of tropical linear subspaces of $\Trop(F_7)$, partially ordered by inclusion. It is stratified into ranks 0, 1, 2, and 3:
- $\Dr_3$: The unique maximal element $\Trop(F_7)$.
- $\Dr_2$: Rank 2 tropical linear subspaces, which are exactly the balanced tropical lines (metric trees with 7 leaves in directions $e_1, \dots, e_7$) contained within $\Trop(F_7)$.
- $\Dr_1$: Rank 1 tropical subspaces, which are exactly the tropical points, corresponding to all elements $x \in \Trop(F_7)$.
- $\Dr_0$: The empty set.

**Step 3: Characterization of Rank 2 Elements**
Any $T \in \Dr_2$ is a balanced 1-dimensional fan supported in $\Trop(F_7)$. Branching nodes of $T$ must satisfy the zero-sum balancing condition modulo $\mathbf{1}$. 
We consider where $T$ can branch:
1.  **At a point ray $p_1$:** The faces meeting at $p_1$ are the sectors for the 3 lines $L_1, L_2, L_3$ containing $p_1$. The outgoing directions in these sectors are $L_1, L_2, L_3$. Their sum is $L_1+L_2+L_3 = 3e_1 + \sum_{k=2}^7 e_k = 2e_1 + \mathbf{1} \equiv 2e_1 \neq 0$. Balancing here is impossible.
2.  **Inside a 2D sector $(p_i, L_j)$:** Because the sector is flat and only has two independent primitive directions, balancing with strictly positive coefficients is impossible without reversing direction (which creates a straight line, not a branch).
3.  **At a line ray $L_1 = \{1,2,3\}$:** The sectors meeting at $L_1$ go towards $e_1, e_2, e_3$. Their sum is $e_1+e_2+e_3 = L_1$. An incoming trunk from the origin has direction $-L_1$, making the sum exactly 0. Branching here is balanced and valid.

Because $T$ must have exactly 7 leaves with one in each direction $e_i$, and any two lines in $F_7$ intersect, a tree cannot possess branching nodes on two different line rays $L_j, L_k$ without requiring the shared leaf to disconnect from the tree structure (destroying the genus 0 property). Consequently, the space of trees in $\Dr_2$ exactly consists of the central origin tree (standard $U_{2,7}$) and 7 one-parameter families $T_{L_j, t}$ where a trunk extends from the origin to $t L_j$ before branching. 

**Step 4: The Cardinality Contradiction**
Assume for the sake of contradiction that there exists an order-reversing involution $\Phi$ on $\Dr(F_7)$. It must restrict to a bijection $\Phi: \Dr_2 \to \Dr_1$. 
For any tropical line $V \in \Dr_2$, define its principal ideal of points $I(V) = \{p \in \Dr_1 \mid p \subseteq V\}$. Because $V$ is a 1-dimensional metric graph, $|I(V)| = \mathfrak{c}$ (the cardinality of the continuum).
For any point $q \in \Dr_1$, define its principal filter of containing lines $F(q) = \{W \in \Dr_2 \mid q \subseteq W\}$.
Because $\Phi$ is an inclusion-reversing bijection, it maps $I(V)$ bijectively to $F(\Phi(V))$. Therefore, for every $V \in \Dr_2$, the point $q = \Phi(V)$ must satisfy $|F(q)| = \mathfrak{c}$.

However, consider any "generic" point $q$ strictly in the interior of a 2D sector, say $(L_1, p_1)$. By our classification of $\Dr_2$, the only tropical line whose branches pass through this interior is the specific family $T_{L_1, t}$. The segment in this sector starts at $t L_1$ and goes in direction $p_1$. A straightforward coordinate check shows that $q = x p_1 + y L_1$ lies on exactly one such tree: $T_{L_1, y}$. Thus, for generic $q$, $|F(q)| = 1$.

Since $|F(q)| = 1 \neq \mathfrak{c}$, no generic point can lie in the image of $\Phi$. Because the set of non-generic points (the 14 rays) is only a 1-dimensional measure-zero subset of $\Dr_1$, $\Phi$ fails to be a surjection onto $\Dr_1$. This contradicts that $\Phi$ is a bijection, proving that no such order-reversing involution can exist.
