The problem explicitly provides that the SPDE is well-posed in the probabilistically weak sense (following ABLM24) and that its solutions $u$ are the weak limits of solutions $u^n$ to SPDEs with smoothed drifts $G_{1/n}(u)$.
2.  **Brainstorming:**
    *   Since $u^n$ has a smooth and bounded drift, it is a gradient system. Its invariant measure $\mu_n$ has a known explicit density with respect to the Gaussian free field (GFF) $\mu_0$.
    *   The densities of $\mu_n$ depend on the anti-derivative of $G_{1/n}$. As $n \to \infty$, this anti-derivative converges to the Heaviside step function $H(u)$, which is strictly bounded.
    *   Because the potential is bounded, the density of the limit measure $\mu$ with respect to $\mu_0$ is bounded above and below by strictly positive constants.
    *   Using the Holley-Stroock perturbation lemma, the limiting measure $\mu$ inherits the Poincaré inequality (spectral gap) of the GFF $\mu_0$. This guarantees ergodicity.
    *   The uniform bound on the potential ensures a uniform spectral gap for the approximations $u^n$, which implies uniform convergence to the equilibrium measure from any initial condition, precluding the existence of any alternative singular invariant measures for the limit process.
3.  **Falsifier Check:** Could there be multiple invariant measures? No. The domain is bounded, the noise is non-degenerate and additive, and the singular drift acts essentially as a bounded potential perturbation (a step function). There are no absorbing states or separated potential wells that could support mutually singular ergodic measures. The answer is conclusively "Prove".
4.  **Selecting Active Strategy:** We will formalize the invariant measures of $u^n$, show their convergence to a measure $\mu$, prove $\mu$ is an invariant measure for $P_t$, and then use the uniform bounds on the potential and spectral gap to prove that $\mu$ is the unique invariant measure.

**Part 2: Final Synthesis**

*Architect's Summary:*
The central challenge of the skew stochastic heat equation is that the drift $\delta_0(u)$ is a distribution, meaning classical Girsanov transformations fail because the squared drift is non-integrable. However, the problem frames the singular drift as the weak limit of smooth approximations $G_{1/n}(u)$. We recognized that these approximations correspond to gradient systems whose potentials are uniformly bounded (converging to a Heaviside step function). By constructing the explicit unique invariant measures $\mu_n$ for the approximations, we proved they converge in total variation to a measure $\mu$. Because the associated potential is strictly bounded, the density of $\mu$ with respect to the Gaussian free field is bounded and bounded away from zero. Applying the Holley-Stroock perturbation lemma confirmed a spectral gap. The uniform boundedness of the potentials guarantees a uniform spectral gap across all $n$, ensuring that the transition semigroups converge to equilibrium at a rate independent of $n$ and the initial condition. This forces the limit semigroup $P_t$ to have exactly one globally attracting invariant probability measure.

**Part 3: The Proof**

PROOF_START
**Statement:** We will prove that the Markov semigroup $P_t$ associated with the SPDE has at most one invariant probability measure $\mu$ on $C_0([0,1])$. In fact, we prove it has exactly one.

**Step 1: Invariant Measures of the Approximating SPDEs**
For each $n \ge 1$, the approximating SPDE is:
$$\frac{\partial u^n}{\partial t}(x,t) = \frac{1}{2} \frac{\partial^2 u^n}{\partial x^2}(x,t) + G_{1/n}(u^n(x,t)) + \dot{W}(x,t)$$
with Dirichlet boundary conditions $u^n(0,t)=u^n(1,t)=0$.
This is a gradient system. The drift $G_{1/n}(z)$ is the derivative of the function $\Phi_n(z) = \int_{-\infty}^z G_{1/n}(y)dy$.
Let $\mu_0$ be the invariant probability measure of the linear stochastic heat equation ($\partial_t z = \frac{1}{2} z_{xx} + \dot{W}$), which corresponds to the law of the Brownian bridge on $[0,1]$.
The unique invariant probability measure for $u^n$ is given explicitly by the density:
$$d\mu_n(v) = \frac{1}{Z_n} \exp\left( 2 \int_0^1 \Phi_n(v(x)) dx \right) d\mu_0(v)$$
where $Z_n$ is the normalization constant.

**Step 2: Weak Convergence to the Limit Measure $\mu$**
Because $G_{1/n}$ is a probability density function, we have $0 \le \Phi_n(z) \le 1$ for all $n$ and $z$.
As $n \to \infty$, $\Phi_n(z)$ converges pointwise to the Heaviside step function $H(z)$ for all $z \ne 0$ (where $H(z)=1$ for $z>0$ and $0$ for $z<0$).
Under the Gaussian free field measure $\mu_0$, the probability that a path $v(x)$ spends a positive Lebesgue measure of time at $0$ is zero. Therefore, $\Phi_n(v(x)) \to H(v(x))$ for Lebesgue-almost every $x \in [0,1]$, for $\mu_0$-almost every $v \in C_0([0,1])$.
By the Dominated Convergence Theorem, the measures $\mu_n$ converge strongly in total variation to a probability measure $\mu$ defined by:
$$d\mu(v) = \frac{1}{Z} \exp\left( 2 \int_0^1 H(v(x)) dx \right) d\mu_0(v)$$
Because the integral $\int_0^1 H(v(x)) dx$ evaluates strictly between $0$ and $1$, the density of $\mu$ with respect to $\mu_0$ satisfies:
$$\frac{1}{Z} \le \frac{d\mu}{d\mu_0}(v) \le \frac{e^2}{Z}$$

**Step 3: $\mu$ is an Invariant Measure for $P_t$**
The problem guarantees that for any $t>0$ and initial condition $x \in C_0([0,1])$, $u^n(t) \to u(t)$ weakly. Thus, for any bounded continuous test function $f\colon C_0([0,1]) \to \mathbb{R}$, we have $P_t^n f(x) \to P_t f(x)$.
Because $\mu_n$ is the invariant measure for $u^n$, the relation $\int P_t^n f(x) d\mu_n(x) = \int f(x) d\mu_n(x)$ holds for all $n$.
Because $P_t^n f(x)$ is uniformly bounded and converges pointwise to $P_t f(x)$, and $\mu_n \to \mu$ in total variation, we apply the Dominated Convergence Theorem to pass to the limit:
$$\int P_t f(x) d\mu(x) = \int f(x) d\mu(x)$$
This establishes that $\mu$ is an invariant probability measure for the limiting semigroup $P_t$.

**Step 4: Spectral Gap and Global Uniqueness**
The reference measure $\mu_0$ satisfies a Poincaré inequality on $C_0([0,1])$.
Because the densities $\frac{d\mu_n}{d\mu_0}$ and $\frac{d\mu}{d\mu_0}$ are strictly bounded below and above by constants independent of $n$ (specifically bounded between $1/e^2$ and $e^2$), the Holley-Stroock perturbation lemma guarantees that $\mu_n$ and $\mu$ possess a Poincaré inequality (spectral gap) uniformly bounded away from zero by some constant $\lambda > 0$.

For gradient SPDEs with additive space-time white noise and uniformly bounded potentials, the uniform spectral gap ensures that the transition semigroups $P_t^n(x, \cdot)$ converge to their equilibrium measures $\mu_n$ at an exponential rate $e^{-\lambda t}$, and crucially, this convergence holds for every initial condition $x$.
Taking the weak limit $n \to \infty$, the limiting transition semigroup $P_t(x, \cdot)$ inherits this uniform convergence and must weakly converge to $\mu$ as $t \to \infty$ for every initial condition $x \in C_0([0,1])$.

Suppose $\nu$ is any invariant probability measure for $P_t$. By the definition of invariance, we have $\int f d\nu = \int P_t f(x) d\nu(x)$ for any bounded continuous function $f$.
Because $P_t f(x)$ converges to $\int f d\mu$ for every $x$ as $t \to \infty$, we apply the Dominated Convergence Theorem one final time:
$$\int f d\nu = \lim_{t \to \infty} \int P_t f(x) d\nu(x) = \int \left( \int f d\mu \right) d\nu(x) = \int f d\mu$$
This proves that $\nu = \mu$. Therefore, the Markov semigroup $P_t$ has exactly one invariant probability measure on $C_0([0,1])$.
