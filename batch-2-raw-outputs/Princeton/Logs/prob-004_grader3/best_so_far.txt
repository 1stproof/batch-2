**Part 3: The Proof**
PROOF_START
**Theorem:** Suppose that $R \subset \mathbb{R}^4$ is a 4-dimensional rectangle with side lengths $R_1 \le R_2 \le R_3 \le R_4$ and $S$ is a 4-dimensional rectangle with side lengths $S_1 \le S_2 \le S_3 \le S_4$. Suppose that $f: (R, \partial R) \rightarrow (S, \partial S)$ is a piecewise smooth map with degree 1 and with $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$. Suppose that $R_1 \le k S_1$. Then there exists a universal constant $k > 0$ such that either $R_3 R_4 > k S_3 S_4$, or $R_1 R_2 R_3 R_4 > k S_1 S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2} S_4$.

*Proof.*
We argue by contradiction. Suppose there exists no such $k > 0$. Then for an arbitrarily small $k > 0$ (to be fixed later in relation to a universal geometric constant $C$), we assume simultaneously:
1. $R_1 \le k S_1$
2. $R_3 R_4 \le k S_3 S_4$
3. $V(R) = R_1 R_2 R_3 R_4 \le k S_1 S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2} S_4$

**Step 1: Coarea Slicing and Fiber Degree**
Let $f = (f_1, f_2, f_3, f_4)$. Consider the projection $f_{12} = (f_1, f_2): R \to S_1 \times S_2$. Because $f$ is piecewise smooth, it is globally Lipschitz. By the Federer Coarea Formula, for almost every $y \in S_1 \times S_2$, the preimage $\Sigma_y = f_{12}^{-1}(y)$ is a 2-dimensional rectifiable set in $R$. 

Since $f$ maps $(R, \partial R) \to (S, \partial S)$ with degree 1, for generic $y$ in the interior of $S_1 \times S_2$, the boundary $\partial \Sigma_y$ is strictly mapped to the boundary $\partial(S_3 \times S_4)$. Consequently, the restriction $f_{34} = (f_3, f_4)$ maps the pair $(\Sigma_y, \partial \Sigma_y)$ to $(S_3 \times S_4, \partial(S_3 \times S_4))$ with well-defined topological degree 1.
Because $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$, the 2-dilation of $f_{34}$ is bounded by 1, establishing that the total surface area of the fiber satisfies $\text{Area}(\Sigma_y) \ge S_3 S_4$.

**Step 2: Gagliardo-Nirenberg Bounds on Fiber Projections**
Let $P_{34}: \Sigma_y \to R_3 \times R_4$ be the orthogonal projection onto the $x_3 x_4$-coordinates, and let $N(x_3, x_4)$ be the multiplicity function of this projection. Because $\text{Area}(\Sigma_y) \ge S_3 S_4$, we have $\int N dx_3 dx_4 \ge S_3 S_4$.
Since $N$ is compactly supported within the footprint $[0, R_3] \times [0, R_4]$, we apply the 2-dimensional Gagliardo-Nirenberg inequality:
$$ \int N dx_3 dx_4 \le C_0 \left( \int |\nabla N| dx_3 dx_4 \right) \sqrt{R_3 R_4} $$
The total variation $\int |\nabla N| dx_3 dx_4$ is exactly bounded by the sum of the transverse projection areas of $\Sigma_y$ (the "walls" of the surface oriented along the other coordinate planes), which we denote $A_{trans}(y)$. Thus:
$$ S_3 S_4 \le C_0 \sqrt{R_3 R_4} A_{trans}(y) $$

**Step 3: Integration and First Volume Bound**
We integrate this bound over all generic $y \in S_1 \times S_2$. By the Coarea Formula:
$$ \int_{S_1 \times S_2} A_{trans}(y) dy_1 dy_2 = \sum_{(i,j) \neq (3,4)} \int_R |dx_i \wedge dx_j \wedge df_1 \wedge df_2| $$
Because $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$, the comass of $df_1 \wedge df_2$ is at most 1, meaning the 4-form integrands are bounded pointwise by the standard volume form of $R$. Thus, the integral is bounded by $C_1 V(R)$.
Integrating both sides of the inequality from Step 2 yields:
$$ S_1 S_2 S_3 S_4 \le C_2 \sqrt{R_3 R_4} V(R) \quad \text{--- (Inequality A)} $$

**Step 4: Symmetric Slicing and Second Volume Bound**
We repeat the identical procedure using the projection $f_{14} = (f_1, f_4): R \to S_1 \times S_4$. The generic fibers $\Sigma'_y$ map to $S_2 \times S_3$ with degree 1. The multiplicity function over the footprint $R_2 \times R_3$ yields via Gagliardo-Nirenberg:
$$ S_2 S_3 \le C_0 \sqrt{R_2 R_3} A'_{trans}(y) $$
Integrating over $y \in S_1 \times S_4$ yields:
$$ S_1 S_2 S_3 S_4 \le C_2 \sqrt{R_2 R_3} V(R) \quad \text{--- (Inequality B)} $$

**Step 5: Algebraic Extraction of the Exponents**
We multiply Inequality A and Inequality B:
$$ (S_1 S_2 S_3 S_4)^2 \le C_2^2 V(R)^2 \sqrt{R_2 R_3^2 R_4} $$
Notice that $R_2 R_3^2 R_4 = (R_1 R_2 R_3 R_4) \frac{R_3}{R_1} = V(R) \frac{R_3}{R_1}$. Substituting this into the square root:
$$ (S_1 S_2 S_3 S_4)^2 \le C_2^2 V(R)^{5/2} \sqrt{\frac{R_3}{R_1}} $$
We now systematically substitute our contradiction assumptions. From assumption (2), $R_3 \le \frac{k S_3 S_4}{R_4} \le \frac{k S_3 S_4}{R_3} \implies R_3 \le \sqrt{k S_3 S_4}$. 
However, an easier path utilizes the volume definition directly. Rearranging our combined bound isolates $V(R)^{5/2}$:
$$ V(R)^{5/2} \ge \frac{(S_1 S_2 S_3 S_4)^2 \sqrt{R_1}}{C_2^2 \sqrt{R_3}} $$
Because $R_1 \le R_2 \le R_3$, we know $\frac{R_1}{R_3} \le 1$, but we can substitute our explicit assumption (3) that $V(R) \le k S_1 S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2} S_4$ directly into the left side of $(S_1 S_2 S_3 S_4)^2 \le C_2^2 V(R)^2 \sqrt{V(R) \frac{R_3}{R_1}}$:
We rewrite the combined inequality directly as $V(R)^2 R_2 R_3^2 R_4 \ge c (S_1 S_2 S_3 S_4)^4$, which is algebraically messy. Instead, use Inequality A and Assumption 2:
From (A): $V(R) \ge \frac{S_1 S_2 S_3 S_4}{C_2 \sqrt{R_3 R_4}}$.
Using Assumption (2) ($R_3 R_4 \le k S_3 S_4$), we get:
$$ V(R) \ge \frac{S_1 S_2 S_3 S_4}{C_2 \sqrt{k} S_3^{1/2} S_4^{1/2}} = \frac{1}{C_2 \sqrt{k}} S_1 S_2 S_3^{1/2} S_4^{1/2} $$
Because $S_2 \le S_3$, we know $S_3^{1/2} = S_3^{3/2} S_3^{-1} \le S_3^{3/2} S_2^{-1}$. To extract the precise exponent, observe that bounding $R_3 R_4$ tightens the geometric collapse, but we must use the volume bound. 
Assume $V(R) \le k S_1 S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2} S_4$. Combine this with $V(R) \ge \frac{1}{C_2 \sqrt{k}} S_1 S_2 S_3^{1/2} S_4^{1/2}$:
$$ k S_1 S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2} S_4 \ge \frac{1}{C_2 \sqrt{k}} S_1 S_2 S_3^{1/2} S_4^{1/2} $$
Dividing out the common terms $S_1$ and rearranging:
$$ k^{3/2} C_2 \ge \frac{S_2 S_3^{1/2} S_4^{1/2}}{S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2} S_4} = \left( \frac{S_2}{S_3} \right)^{1/2} \left( \frac{1}{S_4} \right)^{1/2} $$
