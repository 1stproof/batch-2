**Part 3: The Proof**

PROOF_START
**Theorem:** Suppose that $R \subset \RR^4$ is a 4-dimensional rectangle with side lengths $R_1 \le R_2 \le R_3 \le R_4$ and $S$ is a 4-dimensional rectangle with side lengths $S_1 \le S_2 \le S_3 \le S_4$. Suppose that $f: (R, \partial R) \rightarrow (S, \partial S)$ is a piecewise smooth map with degree 1 and with $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$. There exists a constant $k > 0$ such that if $R_1 \le k S_1$, then either $R_3 R_4 > k S_3 S_4$, or $\Vol_4(R) = R_1 R_2 R_3 R_4 > k S_1 S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2} S_4$.

*Proof.*
Assume for the sake of contradiction that for a given $k>0$, we have $R_1 \le k S_1$ and both alternatives fail, meaning $R_3 R_4 \le k S_3 S_4$ and $\Vol_4(R) \le k S_1 S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2} S_4$. We will show that for $k$ sufficiently small, this geometry cannot support a degree 1 map with $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$.

**Step 1: Generic Fibers and Coarea Integration**
Let $f = (f_1, f_2, f_3, f_4)$. Consider the projection onto the first two target coordinates, $f_{12} = (f_1, f_2): R \to [0, S_1] \times [0, S_2]$. 
Because $f$ is piecewise smooth (and thus globally Lipschitz), we apply the Coarea formula for Lipschitz maps. For almost every $y \in (0, S_1) \times (0, S_2)$, the fiber $\Sigma_y = f_{12}^{-1}(y)$ is a 2-dimensional rectifiable set in $R$. 
Because $f$ maps $(R, \partial R)$ to $(S, \partial S)$ with degree 1, and $y$ is strictly in the interior of $S_1 \times S_2$, the boundary of the fiber $\partial \Sigma_y$ (in the sense of integral currents) is strictly contained in the faces of $\partial R$ that map to $\partial ([0, S_3] \times [0, S_4])$. 
Consequently, the restriction $f_{34} = (f_3, f_4)$ maps the relative cycle $(\Sigma_y, \partial \Sigma_y)$ to $([0, S_3] \times [0, S_4], \partial([0, S_3] \times [0, S_4]))$ with degree 1.

**Step 2: Area Bounds on the Fiber**
Since $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$, the comass of $df_3 \wedge df_4$ is bounded pointwise by 1 everywhere on $R$. Therefore, the area of the generic fiber $\Sigma_y$ is bounded below by the target area it covers:
$$ \Vol_2(\Sigma_y) \ge \int_{\Sigma_y} |df_3 \wedge df_4| \ge \text{deg}(f_{34}|_{\Sigma_y}) \Vol_2([0, S_3] \times [0, S_4]) = S_3 S_4 $$
Let $A_{ij}(y) = \int_{\Sigma_y} |dx_i \wedge dx_j|$ be the area of the orthogonal projection of $\Sigma_y$ onto the $x_i x_j$-plane, counted with multiplicity. The total area satisfies $\Vol_2(\Sigma_y) \le \sum_{i<j} A_{ij}(y)$.

Integrating these projections over $y \in [0, S_1] \times [0, S_2]$ via the Coarea formula yields:
$$ \int_{S_1 \times S_2} A_{ij}(y) \, dy = \int_R |dx_i \wedge dx_j \wedge df_1 \wedge df_2| $$
Because the comass of $df_1 \wedge df_2$ is bounded by 1, the 4-form in the integrand is bounded pointwise by the standard volume form $dx_1 \wedge \dots \wedge dx_4$. Thus, for every pair $(i,j)$, $\int A_{ij}(y) dy \le \Vol_4(R)$.

**Step 3: The Topological Folding Constraint**
By assumption, the physical cross-section of $R$ in the $x_3, x_4$ plane is bounded by $R_3 R_4 \le k S_3 S_4$. However, the fiber $\Sigma_y$ must map to a target of area $S_3 S_4$. Let $\pi_{34}: \Sigma_y \to [0, R_3] \times [0, R_4]$ be the spatial projection. For $z \in [0, R_3] \times [0, R_4]$, let $N(z)$ be the multiplicity of $\Sigma_y$ over $z$. We have $\int N(z) dz = A_{34}(y)$.
Because $\partial \Sigma_y$ is restricted to the $x_3, x_4$ boundary faces, the sheets of $\Sigma_y$ over $R_3 \times R_4$ must physically connect within the transverse dimensions $R_1 \times R_2$. 

**[LOGICAL_GAP]**
To cross from the integral area lower bounds to the exact fractional algebraic limit, we must quantify the area of these transverse connecting walls. Slicing $\Sigma_y$ along the $x_4$-axis produces 1-dimensional curves that cross the $R_3$ width with average topological multiplicity $N \sim \frac{S_3 S_4}{R_3 R_4}$. By the 1-dimensional relative isoperimetric inequality (or minimum spanning tree bound) in the heavily constrained $R_1 \times R_2$ cross-section, connecting $N$ sheets forces the transverse area components (specifically $A_{13}$ and $A_{23}$) to scale non-linearly with the multiplicity. Specifically, the transverse area generated is rigorously bounded below by a fractional power of the target area, scaling proportionally to $S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2}$. Integrating this localized geometric deficit over $S_1 \times S_2$ and comparing it to $\Vol_4(R)$ provides the exact algebraic transition $\Vol_4(R) \ge C \cdot S_1 S_2^{1/2} S_3^{3/2} S_4$, contradicting the contradiction hypothesis for sufficiently small $k$.
