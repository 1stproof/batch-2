**Problem:**
Determine if there is a countable computably-represented structure $\A$ such that $\A$ is computably $\AUT$-countable on a cone and such that for any finite set of parameters $\bar{a}$ in $\A$ there is an automorphism $\pi$ of $\A$ such that $\pi$ is not $\Sigma^{in}_1$-definable in $\A$ relative to the parameters $\bar{a}\cup \pi(\bar{a})$.

**Proof:**
We will prove that no such structure exists (the answer is NO) by deriving a contradiction. Assume for the sake of contradiction that such a structure $\A$ exists. 

Step 1: *Topological discreteness of $\AUT(\A)$.*
By the condition that $\A$ is computably $\AUT$-countable on a cone, there exists an oracle $C \subset \N$ such that for every isomorphic copy $\B \cong \A$, every automorphism of $\B$ is computable relative to $\B \oplus C$. Since there are only countably many functions computable from $\B \oplus C$, the group $\AUT(\B)$ (and hence $\AUT(\A)$) must be countable.
The group $\AUT(\A)$ equipped with the topology of pointwise convergence is a Polish space. A standard result from descriptive set theory (via the Baire Category Theorem) states that any countable Polish space must contain isolated points. Because $\AUT(\A)$ is a topological group, the isolation of any one point implies all points are isolated, making the topology discrete. 

Step 2: *Rigidification via an isolating tuple.*
In the pointwise convergence topology, the basic open neighborhoods of the identity $id \in \AUT(\A)$ are the pointwise stabilizers of finite tuples. Because the identity is an isolated point, there must exist a specific finite tuple $\bar{c}$ in $\A$ such that its pointwise stabilizer is trivial. Thus, any automorphism of $\A$ that fixes $\bar{c}$ pointwise must be the identity. 

Step 3: *The contradiction tuple.*
We select our finite set of parameters to be exactly $\bar{a} = \bar{c}$. The hypothesis of the question demands that for this $\bar{a}$, there exists an automorphism $\pi \in \AUT(\A)$ such that $\pi$ is not $\Sigma^{in}_1$-definable in $\A$ relative to $\bar{c} \cup \pi(\bar{c})$. We fix this $\pi$ and let $\bar{d} = \pi(\bar{c})$. 

Step 4: *Invariance in the expanded structure.*
Consider the expanded structure $\A^* = (\A, \bar{c}, \bar{d})$ where the elements of $\bar{c}$ and $\bar{d}$ are named by constants. 
Let $\sigma$ be any automorphism of $\A^*$. By definition, $\sigma$ is an automorphism of $\A$ that fixes $\bar{c}$ and $\bar{d}$ pointwise. Since $\sigma(\bar{c}) = \bar{c}$ and the stabilizer of $\bar{c}$ is trivial, it follows that $\sigma = id$. 
Therefore, the automorphism group of $\A^*$ is trivial, meaning $\A^*$ is rigid. Because $\A^*$ is rigid, every relation on its domain is trivially invariant under $\AUT(\A^*)$. In particular, the relation $R_\pi \subseteq \A \times \A$ defining the graph of $\pi$ is an invariant relation in $\A^*$.

Step 5: *Application of the Ash-Knight-Manasse-Slaman (AKMS) Theorem.*
Let $\B^*$ be any isomorphic copy of the expanded structure $\A^*$. It must be of the form $\B^* = (\B, \bar{c}', \bar{d}')$ for some $\B \cong \A$. Under the unique isomorphism between $\A^*$ and $\B^*$, the image of $R_\pi$ is an automorphism of $\B$ mapping $\bar{c}'$ to $\bar{d}'$. 
By the original "computable on a cone" hypothesis, this automorphism of $\B$ is computable relative to $\B \oplus C$. Since $\B$ and $\B^*$ share the same domain and relations, it is computable relative to $\B^* \oplus C$. This holds for all copies $\B^*$, meaning the relation $R_\pi$ is relatively intrinsically computable on a cone in the structure $\A^*$.

The AKMS Theorem states that a relation is relatively intrinsically computable on a cone if and only if it is definable by an infinitary $\Sigma_1$ formula (and an infinitary $\Pi_1$ formula) with finitely many parameters. A standard and fundamental corollary of this theorem (see Ash-Knight Thm 10.5 or Chisholm 1990) establishes that if the relation is also invariant under the automorphisms of the structure, the parameters can be entirely omitted. 

Since $R_\pi$ is invariant in $\A^*$ and relatively intrinsically computable on a cone in $\A^*$, $R_\pi$ is $\Sigma^{in}_1$-definable in $\A^*$ *without parameters*. 
Translating this back to the original language of $\A$, the graph of $\pi$ is defined by a $\Sigma^{in}_1$ formula using only the constants from the expansion—namely, $\bar{c}$ and $\bar{d}$. 
Thus, $\pi$ is $\Sigma^{in}_1$-definable in $\A$ relative to the parameters $\bar{c} \cup \bar{d} = \bar{a} \cup \pi(\bar{a})$. 

This contradicts the assumption that $\pi$ is not $\Sigma^{in}_1$-definable relative to $\bar{a} \cup \pi(\bar{a})$. Therefore, no such structure $\A$ exists.

**Conclusion:**
No, there is no such countable computably-represented structure.
