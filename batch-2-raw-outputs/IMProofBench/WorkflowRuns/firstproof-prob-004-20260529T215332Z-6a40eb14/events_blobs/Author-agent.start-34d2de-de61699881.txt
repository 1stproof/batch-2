My instinct is that the literal pointwise current lemma is stronger than you need and probably the wrong object to prove directly. I would try to prove a **dual/cellular averaged version** instead.

A useful replacement target is: for every relative \(2\)-form/cochain \(\omega\) with
\(\|d\omega\|_\infty\le1\), \(\omega|_{\partial R}=0\),
\[
 \int_{Z_y}\omega
 \le C(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1)
   +C\int_{B_y\cap {\mathcal B}_z}|d\omega| . \tag{*}
\]
Taking the supremum over \(\omega\) gives the filling estimate, and the bad term is bounded by \(\Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\). This is essentially the dual of your \(P_z,H_z\) lemma, but it avoids sharp restriction seams: no \(\partial(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\) ever appears.

A rigorous route to \((*)\) might be:

1. **PL/cubicalize first.** Approximate \(f\) by a PL map affine on a fine cubulation. For fixed \(z\), define a *cellular bad carrier* using only the coordinate two-plaques that actually occur in a prescribed Manhattan carrier. Do not thicken. On the good carrier, \(F\) avoids \(z\), so \(F\) factors into \(Q\setminus\{z\}\), hence has a cellular null-carrier after retracting to \(\partial Q\). The acyclic-carrier theorem should then give a cochain homotopy supported on the good complex. Bad cells are carried unchanged. Passing mesh \(\to0\) after averaging over \(z\) should recover exactly the plaque Fubini bound, because the relevant tracks are coordinate two-plaques, not stars or neighborhoods.

2. **Prove the energy part as a target-grid deformation theorem.** For a two-current \(T=Z_y\) equipped with \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), prove a weighted deformation estimate:
\[
\text{action of the good cochain homotopy on }T
 \lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1 .
\]
I would try this by a random grid in the \(G\)-rectangle. Coarea controls the expected mass of the preimage of grid lines by \(E_y\). Pushing cells to an \(f_1\)-skeleton costs \(R_1E_y\); the remaining one-dimensional seams in the \(G\)-plane fill with planar cost \(\lesssim E_y^2/S_1\). Crucially, this is a deformation of the **degree-carrying \(G\)-cochain**, not a physical sweep of all of \(Z_y\); zero-\(G\)-energy flaps are then invisible except through the chosen filling \(B_y\) and the bad carrier.

3. **Use penalized fillings if arbitrary \(B_y\) is too rigid.** Instead of demanding the comparison for every filling, fix \(z\) and choose \(B_{y,z}\) minimizing
\[
 \Phi_{y,z}(B)=\Mass B+\Lambda\,\Mass(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z).
\]
Averaging in \(z\), the plaque estimate gives
\[
\frac1q\int_Q \Phi_{y,z}(B^0_y)\,dz
 \le (1+C\Lambda R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\Mass B^0_y
\]
for any near-minimizer \(B^0_y\). Thus in the hard regime, penalized minimizers remain mass-near-minimizing and have controlled average bad mass. This may absorb seam/interface errors that defeat the sharp restriction formulation.

So my recommendation: reformulate the missing lemma as a **dual acyclic-carrier/cochain homotopy plus weighted \(G\)-grid deformation**. I am uncertain whether the \(R_1E+E^2/S_1\) estimate closes exactly, but this seems more robust than constructing literal \(P_z,H_z\) on arbitrary currents. The cellular acyclic-carrier setup is also close to Guth/Almgren-Pitts sweepout technology and may match the unpublished Guth hard-regime argument if it exists.

### Council member: gemini-31-pro (models/gemini/gemini-31-pro) ###
This is a fascinating reduction. However, I believe the Exact-Plaque Energy Chain-Map Lemma, exactly as formulated, is mathematically impossible due to a sharpened version of your "flap" objection. You can bypass this by dualizing to differential forms or using a smoothly blended pullback deformation.

### 1. The Fatal Flap Obstruction to the Chain-Map Lemma
Suppose you pad your domain $R$ with a huge disjoint region $D$. Extend $f$ to be constant on $D$, mapping it to $y \neq z$, such that the coordinate planes in $D$ map to a small neighborhood avoiding $z$. 
By definition, $D \cap \mathcal{B}_z = \emptyset$. Since $D$ is mapped to $y$, the central slice $Z_y$ contains the entire huge flap $D$. 
Because $f$ is constant on $D$, $E_y = 0$ there. 

Now consider the proposed chain equation: $C_{y,z} = P_z(B_y) - H_z(Z_y)$. 
Since $D$ is strictly outside $\mathcal{B}_z$, $P_z(B_y)$ must be zero on $D$. Therefore, the boundary of $H_z(Z_y)$ must completely fill the huge flap $D$. The mass of $H_z(Z_y)$ must scale with $\operatorname{Area}(D) \times R_1$. This directly contradicts the requirement that $\operatorname{Mass} H_z(Z_y) \lesssim R_1E_y + E_y^2/S_1$, which is independent of the ordinary mass of $D$.

Because $P_z$ is rigidly supported on $\mathcal{B}_z$, $H_z$ is forced to *physically sweep* the flap in the source, paying ordinary mass.

### 2. The Solution: Dualize to Differential Forms
To achieve your goal of an "algebraic family-level tightening," you should abandon constructing physical filling chains. Use the dual formulation (De Rham cohomology / flat norms). 

If you bound $A = \int_Q \operatorname{Fill}_R(Z_y)dy$ by evaluating $\int_R \omega \wedge df_3 \wedge df_4$ (where $\omega$ calibrates the fillings), the "flap" naturally integrates to zero. Because $f^*$ of any target form is degenerate where $df=0$, forms natively ignore dead sheets. You never have to physically cut a chain, meaning **uncontrolled seam boundaries simply do not exist**. 

You can decompose $\omega = d\eta_{1} + \text{error}$ using a directional Poincaré lemma (integrating along the $x_1$ direction). The $\eta_1$ term gives you the $R_1$ scaling, and the error term is localized to the exact-plaque bad set, yielding the $R_3 R_4 / S_3 S_4$ bound.

### 3. Alternative: Blended Pullback Deformation
If you prefer to keep the geometric chain-map framework, you must avoid sharp cuts $B_y \llcorner \mathcal{B}_z$. Instead of a cubical $P_z$, use a **continuous target retraction**. 

Let $\rho_z: Q \setminus \{z\} \to \partial Q$ be a smooth radial retraction. You can define a deformation of $B_y$ by pulling back the target flow $\rho_z \circ F$. 
* Why this solves the flap objection: The track mass of a pulled-back target flow is bounded by the $F$-Jacobian. On flaps, $dF=0$, so the pullback of the target flow is degenerate and creates *zero 3D mass*. 
* Why this avoids seams: Use a smooth partition of unity $\chi$ to blend the $x_1$-directional source projection (on the good set) with the target-pullback flow (near the bad set $\mathcal{B}_z$). This interpolates smoothly, completely avoiding the $\partial(B_y \llcorner \mathcal{B}_z)$ seam penalty.

### Literature Connection
This blending of macroscopic source projections ($x_1$-direction) with target-based retractions (to control the bad set without paying ordinary mass) is exactly the mechanism Guth employs in *Directional isoperimetric inequalities* (arXiv:0802.3549). Specifically, look at how he combines the directional operators $P_i$ with the target macro-micro decomposition in Section 3. Applying that machinery to your exact-plaque definition should cleanly close the hard-regime gap.
