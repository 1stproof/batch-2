# Round 21 retrieval report: Guth 4D rectangle 2-dilation source

## Executive summary

I did **not** locate a copy of Guth's unpublished preprint **"On the 2-dilation of mappings between 4-dimensional rectangles"**, nor any complete theorem/proof implying the mixed lower bound
\[
 {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}
 +{R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}\over S_1\sqrt{S_3S_4}}
 +{\operatorname{Vol}(R)\over S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4}\gtrsim 1.
\]

The strongest source recovered remains Guth's MIT thesis, Chapter 8. It proves the **first alternative** in a diffeomorphism setting:
\[
 C R_1R_2^2 < S_1S_2S_3 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad R_3R_4 \gtrsim S_3S_4,
\]
using central 2-planes, filling volume, and coarea. This is exactly the mechanism already adapted in `answer.tex`, but it does **not** address the hard regime where \(R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\).

The missing preprint is explicitly cited in arXiv:0802.3549 as a preprint. Guth says the 4D rectangle problem had been solved, but that the answer is complicated and the smallest 2-dilation for degree-one maps and degree-one diffeomorphisms may differ by an arbitrary factor. I found no public copy or secondary source with the case classification.

## What the thesis proves

Relevant text is in `data/round21/thesis_pdfpages_139_149.txt`.

Thesis p.139 states Chapter 8 is only partial: Guth considers 2-dilation of diffeomorphisms between 4D rectangles and says he is not able to give a complete solution. Theorem 8.1 treats the special case where the domain is the unit 4-cube.

Proposition 8.1.2, thesis p.143 (`thesis_pdfpages_139_149.txt:149-170`): if \(C\) is an oriented relative 2-cycle in a rectangle \(R\) with area \(< (1/2)R_1R_2\), then \(C\) has an oriented filling of volume \(< C R_1R_2^2\). The OCR sometimes drops the square, but the displayed statement in this extraction has `CR1R22`, i.e. \(C R_1R_2^2\).

Proposition 8.1.3, thesis p.144 (`thesis_pdfpages_139_149.txt:175-190`): if there is a 2-contracting diffeomorphism \(R\to S\), and if
\[
 C R_1R_2^2 < S_1S_2S_3,
\]
then
\[
 R_3R_4 > cS_3S_4.
\]
Proof outline in our notation: take target planes
\([0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3,y_4\}\) with \((y_3,y_4)\) central. Their relative filling volume in \(S\) is \(\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\). Pull them back to \(R\). Since \(\operatorname{Dil}_3\le1\), the pulled-back cycles have filling volume at least that large. Proposition 8.1.2 forces their areas to be \(\gtrsim R_1R_2\). Coarea over the central \((y_3,y_4)\)-rectangle gives \(\operatorname{Vol}(R)\gtrsim R_1R_2S_3S_4\), hence \(R_3R_4\gtrsim S_3S_4\).

Section 8.2 (`data/round21/thesis_pdfpages_145_148.txt`) lists nonlinear 4D maps with bounded 2-dilation: snake map, codimension-1 snake map, short-side stretch, pinching map, and double pinching map. These are degree-one maps of pairs which can be perturbed to diffeomorphisms. The short-side/stretch technical remark is at lines 57-61 of that file. The pinching and double-pinching constructions are at lines 72-134. These constructions do not state a lower bound equivalent to the mixed estimate.

Final remark on thesis p.149 (`data/round21/thesis_pdfpage_149.txt:1-29`): Guth explicitly says the thesis techniques are "not even close" to estimating the best 2-dilation of diffeomorphisms between 4D rectangles, and that the answer cannot be determined just from the quotients \(Q_i=S_i/R_i\).

## arXiv:0802.3549 evidence for the missing preprint

Source file: `data/round21/arxiv_0802_3549_src/LINKDIL2.TEX`.

Lines 384-400 give the crucial evidence. Guth discusses the case \(n=4\), \(k=k_1=k_2=k_3=2\), then says the analogous 4D rectangle 2-dilation problem was solved in `[G4]`; the answer is complicated, near-optimal maps are far from linear, and degree-one diffeomorphisms may require arbitrarily larger 2-dilation than degree-one maps.

The bibliography entry is at lines 1588-1590:
`Guth, L., On the 2-dilation of mappings between 4-dimensional rectangles, preprint.`

This is the only exact citation I found to the missing title. The paper itself proves lower bounds for ellipses using second-order linking invariants, not the rectangle mixed-face estimate. The 4D ellipse specialization in lines 384-386 gives a monomial:
\[
\operatorname{Dil}_2(\Phi)\gtrsim |D|^{1/3}Q_1^{1/3}Q_2^{2/3}Q_3^{2/3}Q_4^{1/3},
\]
which is not the desired rectangle estimate.

The most relevant reusable tool from this paper is Proposition 2.2 (`LINKDIL2.TEX:878-894`), a relative directional isoperimetric inequality. Specializing it to \(m=2,n=4\) yields the bound already appearing as (15) in `answer.tex`:
\[
\operatorname{Fill}(Z)\lesssim
R_1(\operatorname{Vol}_{23}Z+\operatorname{Vol}_{24}Z+\operatorname{Vol}_{34}Z)
+R_2(\operatorname{Vol}_{13}Z+\operatorname{Vol}_{14}Z)
+R_3\operatorname{Vol}_{12}Z.
\]
After integrating over \(Z_y=\langle R,(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\), this gives a valid directional inequality, but not the low-energy family tightening estimate (13).

## arXiv:0710.0403 evidence

Source file: `data/round21/arxiv_0710_0403_src/EXEMB2.TEX`.

Theorem 3, lines 475-488, is Guth's relative isoperimetric profile theorem for rectangles. It is the source of the small-cycle filling estimate used in the first alternative.

Estimate 1, lines 656-665 and the nontrivial reformulation at lines 867-876, gives the standard monomial lower bounds for degree nonzero maps of pairs. For \(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=4\), it gives
\[
R_1^3R_2R_3R_4\gtrsim S_1^3S_2S_3S_4,
\]
equivalently the bound already used in `answer.tex`. This proves the desired volume alternative only when \(S_3/S_2\) is not too large. It does not imply the mixed estimate.

Note: `EXEMB2.TEX:424` and `:715` mention `[G4]`, but in this paper `[G4]` is Guth's minimax/cup-powers paper, not the missing 4D rectangle preprint.

## Attempted replacement from available Guth tools

I tried to push the available directional isoperimetric inequality toward (13). Applying Proposition 2.2 from arXiv:0802.3549 to each central fiber \(Z_y\) gives the integrated inequality recorded in `answer.tex` as (16). The bookkeeping is correct, but it is too weak: using only \(\operatorname{Dil}_2(f)\le1\), each directional integral \(M_{ij}\) is controlled at best by \(\operatorname{Vol}(R)\), giving only
\[
S_1S_2S_3\cdot S_3S_4
\lesssim (R_1+R_2+R_3)\operatorname{Vol}(R),
\]
up to constants. In the stress-test scaling \(S=(1,1,L^6,L^6)\), \(R=(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)\), this gives only a lower exponent near \(L^{13}\), well below the desired \(L^{15}\) scale. The missing information is exactly the family-level/low-energy compatibility encoded in (13), not a single-fiber directional filling bound.

The boundary ellipsoid inequality B3 in the thesis is also nearby but insufficient. In the hard regime it suggests a boundary-type lower bound of scale \(R_2R_3R_4\gtrsim S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), but multiplying by the small \(R_1\) misses the required factor \(S_1\). Moreover, Guth states it for diffeomorphisms/boundaries, not as the needed arbitrary degree-one pair-map rectangle theorem.

## Conclusion

No located public source proves the mixed 4D rectangle estimate or the hard-regime dichotomy. The available Guth sources justify:

1. the first alternative via thesis Proposition 8.1.3, after adapting diffeomorphism pullback to degree-one slicing currents;
2. the standard monomial lower bounds via arXiv:0710.0403 Estimate 1;
3. directional filling estimates via arXiv:0802.3549 Proposition 2.2.

They do **not** supply the averaged parametric tightening estimate (13). The missing `[G4]` preprint very likely contained a case classification that would be relevant, but I could not recover it from MIT DSpace, arXiv, bibliographic databases, current/archived Guth pages, or Internet Archive metadata/CDX.
