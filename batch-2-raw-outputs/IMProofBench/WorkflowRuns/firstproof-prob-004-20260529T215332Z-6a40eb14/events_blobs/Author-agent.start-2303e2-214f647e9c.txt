\begin{center}
{\bf Partial proof and reduction for the rectangle \(2\)-dilation estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I write the universal constant in the problem as \(\kappa\), to avoid
confusion with \(k\)-dilation.  Rectangles are oriented Euclidean products
\(\prod_i[0,R_i]\), and degree \(1\) means degree \(1\) on relative
\(H_4(\cdot,\partial\cdot;\mathbb Z)\).

The argument is phrased for integral currents.  A piecewise smooth map gives
well-defined push-forwards and slices on each smooth stratum.  Equivalently
one may first approximate, relative to the boundary strata, by smooth maps with
\(\Dil_2\le1+\varepsilon\); the relative degree is homotopy invariant and the
final universal constants are then changed by a harmless factor before letting
\(\varepsilon\downarrow0\).

This draft proves the standard first alternative and an unconditional weaker
volume estimate.  The desired second alternative is reduced to a precise
averaged tightening estimate, but that estimate is not proved here; therefore
the document is not yet a complete proof of the problem.

\section*{Standard inputs}

All constants below are positive and universal.  First, if \(\Dil_2(f)\le 1\),
then \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\) and \(\Dil_4(f)\le1\).  Indeed, if
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) are the singular values and
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), then \(\sigma_2\le1\), and hence
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\) for \(j\ge2\).

Second, we use Guth's lower estimate for degree-nonzero maps of pairs between
rectangles \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}.  In the case
\(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=4\), it gives
\begin{equation}\label{guth14}
        R_1^2\,\Vol(R)\ge c_0\,S_1^3S_2S_3S_4
\end{equation}
whenever \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).

Third, we use Guth's small-volume relative isoperimetric profile of a rectangle
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding}.  In the form needed here, there are constants
\(c_*,C_*\) such that, if \(Z\) is a relative \(2\)-cycle in \(R\) with
\(A=\Mass(Z)\le c_*R_1R_2\), then
\[
 \Fill_R(Z)\le C_*\max\{A^{3/2},\ A^2/R_1\}.
\]
Consequently, if \(\Fill_R(Z)\ge L\), then
\begin{equation}\label{profile-inverse}
\Mass(Z)\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,\ L^{2/3},\ (R_1L)^{1/2}\}.
\end{equation}
Also, after decreasing \(c_*\) if necessary,
\begin{equation}\label{iso}
        \Mass Z<c_*R_1R_2\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C R_1R_2^2 .
\end{equation}
The smallness condition in this paragraph is essential.

\section*{The first alternative}

\begin{lemma}[Filling a central target plane]\label{central}
Let \(Q\) be the middle half of the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle of \(S\).  For
\(y\in Q\), set
\[
P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\}.
\]
Every relative \(3\)-chain in \(S\) filling \(P_y\) has volume at least
\(cS_1S_2S_3\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle
which vanishes on its boundary and is \(\ge cS_3\) on \(Q\).  Put
\(\beta=\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2\).  The \(3\)-form \(d\beta\) has comass at
most \(1\).  If \(\partial Y=P_y\) relatively, then Stokes' theorem gives
\[
\Mass Y\ge\left|\int_Y d\beta\right|
=\left|\int_{P_y}\beta\right|
       = S_1S_2\psi(y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
The boundary-face terms vanish because either \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\) on the
\(x_1,x_2\)-faces or \(\psi=0\) on the \(x_3,x_4\)-faces.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{firstalt}
There is \(c_2>0\) such that, under the hypotheses of the problem, if
\[
        R_1R_2^2\le c_2S_1S_2S_3,
\]
then
\[
        R_3R_4\ge c_2S_3S_4 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(T=[[R]]\) be the fundamental relative current and let
\(F=(f_3,f_4)\).  Since \(f\) has relative degree \(1\), \(f_\#T\) represents
\([S]\) in \(H_4(S,\partial S)\).  In the interior of \(S\), the current
\(f_\#T\) has no boundary, so the constancy theorem gives
\(f_\#T=[[S]]\) there with multiplicity equal to the degree.  The difference
\(f_\#T-[[S]]\) is therefore supported in \(\partial S\), and no nonzero
integral \(4\)-current can be supported on the \(3\)-rectifiable set
\(\partial S\).  Hence
\[
        f_\#T=[[S]]
        \quad\hbox{as a relative }4\hbox{-current}.
\]
By slicing naturality, for a.e. \(y\in Q\),
\[
        f_\#\langle T,F,y\rangle
        =\langle [[S]],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle
        =\pm P_y
\]
in \(I_2(S,\partial S)\).  Put \(Z_y=\langle T,F,y\rangle\).  Any relative
filling of \(Z_y\) in \(R\) maps, since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), to a relative
filling of \(P_y\).  Lemma \ref{central} gives
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
Choose \(c_2\) so small that \(Cc_2<c\) in \eqref{iso}.  Then
\(\Mass Z_y\ge c_*R_1R_2\) for a.e. \(y\in Q\).  Coarea and
\(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\) give
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge \int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
        \ge cR_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Since \(\Vol(R)=R_1R_2R_3R_4\), this proves the proposition after decreasing
\(c_2\).
\end{proof}

\section*{The best bound obtained from single slices}

Assume \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\) and that the first conclusion fails with a
small constant.  Proposition \ref{firstalt} gives
\begin{equation}\label{r12large}
        R_1R_2^2\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\end{equation}
For the slices above, Lemma \ref{central} and \eqref{profile-inverse}, with
\(L=cS_1S_2S_3\), give
\[
\Mass Z_y\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,\ (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\
                         (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\}.
\]
Using \eqref{r12large} and \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\), each term in this
minimum is at least
\[
        c\,\alpha^{1/2}S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}.
\]
Integrating over \(Q\) yields
\begin{equation}\label{slicevol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,\alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
\end{equation}
On the other hand, Guth's estimate \eqref{guth14} gives
\begin{equation}\label{monovol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .
\end{equation}
Combining \eqref{slicevol} and \eqref{monovol}, and optimizing in
\(\alpha\), gives the unconditional partial bound
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4 .
\]
This misses the desired second alternative by the factor
\((S_2/S_3)^{1/10}\).

\section*{A sufficient averaged tightening estimate}

Let \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), and for \(y\in Q\) set
\(Z_y=\langle [[R]],F,y\rangle\).  Define
\[
E_y=\int \|dG|_{T Z_y}\|_{HS}^2\,d\|Z_y\|,\qquad
I=\int_QE_y\,dy,\qquad A=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy .
\]
At a rank-two point of \(dF\), put \(K=\ker dF\), let
\(\lambda=\|dG|_K\|\), and let \(\tau_1,\tau_2\) be the singular values of
\(dF\) on \(K^\perp\).  Since the \(G\)- and \(F\)-coordinates in the target
are orthogonal, \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) gives \(\lambda\tau_i\le1\).  Hence
\(\|dG|_K\|_{HS}^2J_2F\le C\).  Coarea gives
\begin{equation}\label{Icoarea}
        I\le C\Vol(R).
\end{equation}
Also Lemma \ref{central} gives
\begin{equation}\label{Alower}
        A\ge cS_1S_2S_3\,|Q|\ge cS_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .
\end{equation}

The theorem would follow from the following, still unproved, family-level
estimate:
\begin{equation}\label{sufficient}
 A\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\,A .
\end{equation}
Indeed, if \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), choose
\(\kappa\) so small that the last term in \eqref{sufficient} is absorbed.  If
\(I\le\delta S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), then, since
\(S_1\le S_2\le S_3\),
\[
R_1I\le C\kappa\delta\,S_1S_2S_3^2S_4,\qquad
\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\le C\delta^2\,S_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .
\]
Taking first \(\delta\) and then \(\kappa\) small contradicts
\eqref{Alower}.  Thus
\(I\ge cS_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), and \eqref{Icoarea} gives the desired
volume lower bound.

One possible sufficient route to \eqref{sufficient} is the following
low-energy plaque estimate.  Let \(q=|Q|\),
\(\Omega=\{y:E_y\le4I/q\}\), so \(|\Omega|\ge3q/4\), and choose
near-minimizing fillings \(B_y\) of \(Z_y\).  For \(z\in Q\), let
\({\mathcal B}_z\) be the union of points \(x\in R\) such that \(z\) lies in
the \(F\)-image of one of the six coordinate \(2\)-plaques through \(x\).  For
each fixed \(x\), the measure of such \(z\)'s is at most \(C R_3R_4\), because
each coordinate \(2\)-plaque has \(F\)-image area at most its source area and
\(R_iR_j\le R_3R_4\).  Hence
\[
 \frac{1}{q}\int_Q\Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
 \le C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\Mass(B_y).
\]
It would be enough to prove
\begin{equation}\label{lehaf}
\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
\le C\int_\Omega\left(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\right)dy
 +\frac{C}{q}\int_Q\int_\Omega
       \Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dy\,dz .
\end{equation}
After the displayed averaging bound, the last term in \eqref{lehaf} is
self-absorbed in the hard regime, and
\(\int_\Omega E_y^2dy\le(4I/q)\int_\Omega E_y\,dy\).  Since every
\(y\in\Omega\) still satisfies \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3\), this would imply
\eqref{sufficient} with \(A\) replaced by its restriction to \(\Omega\).

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The essential missing point is the averaged tightening estimate
\eqref{sufficient}, or a valid substitute such as \eqref{lehaf}.  It is not a
single-slice isoperimetric statement: an individual fiber can have large
ordinary filling volume but small \(G\)-energy because of low-energy flaps.
The proof would have to use the compatibility of the whole two-parameter family
of fibers of the same \(2\)-contracting degree-one map.  Direct sharp cutting of
a filling by \({\mathcal B}_z\) also creates uncontrolled seam terms; a
successful plaque proof would need a genuine chain-map deformation, or a
different algebraic invariant, to avoid those seams.

What has been tried is the ordinary rectangular profile, all coordinate-pair
slicings, the \(f_1\)-level weighted formulation, Guth's monomial estimates,
and elementary pre/post-compositions with the known rectangle maps.  These give
the first alternative and the weaker
\(S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4\) volume bound above, but not the missing
\((S_3/S_2)^{1/10}\) factor.  To close the proof one needs either the
unavailable rectangle-specific tightening argument from Guth's unpublished
work, or a new proof of \eqref{sufficient}/\eqref{lehaf} with precise
measurable choices of fillings and no seam or flap loss.
