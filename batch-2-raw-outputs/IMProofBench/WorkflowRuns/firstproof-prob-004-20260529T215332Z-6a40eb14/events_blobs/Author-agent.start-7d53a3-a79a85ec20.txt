I would try to prove (13) through a **penalized Manhattan deformation**, not with arbitrary independently chosen minimizers.

**1. Choose the fillings by a penalty.**  
Instead of first selecting \(B_y\) with \(\Mass B_y\le2\Fill(Z_y)\), choose \(B_y\) to almost minimize
\[
\Phi_y(B)=\Mass B+\frac{1}{q}\int_Q \Mass(B\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dz .
\]
In the hard regime the penalty is \(\lesssim (R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\Mass B\), so this is still pointwise near-minimizing. The advantage is that any deformation which only works off \(\mathcal B_z\) may be compared directly with \(B_y\), and the bad term appears with the correct sign.

**2. Prove the following atomic deformation lemma.**  
For fixed \(z\), \(y\in\Omega\), and a penalized near-minimizer \(B_y\), construct a relative filling \(C_{y,z}\) of \(Z_y\) such that
\[
\Mass C_{y,z}
\le C\Big(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\Big)
   +C\,\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z).
\tag{M}
\]
Averaging (M) in \(z\) gives exactly (13). The proof should be genuinely cubical:

- Approximate \(Z_y,B_y\) by cubical currents subordinate to a very fine coordinate grid in \(R\).
- Deform by coordinate “Manhattan” homotopies only: each elementary track varies at most two source coordinates. Then the only obstruction to applying the punctured retraction \(\rho_z:Q\setminus\{z\}\to\partial Q\) is precisely \(z\in F(P_{ij}(x))\), i.e. \(x\in\mathcal B_z\). Do **not** thicken \(\mathcal B_z\); instead estimate the homotopy Jacobian with an error term
\[
\sum_{i<j}{\bf 1}_{\{z\in F(P_{ij}(x))\}},
\]
so no boundary of the bad set is ever created.

- The good part should reduce to a weighted planar deformation in the \(G=(f_1,f_2)\)-coordinates. The estimate to isolate is:
\[
\text{good track mass}\lesssim R_1\int_{Z_y}\|dG\|^2
   +S_1^{-1}\Big(\int_{Z_y}\|dG\|^2\Big)^2.
\]
Here \(R_1E_y\) is the cost of short \(x_1\)-collars, while \(E_y^2/S_1\) is the anisotropic Federer–Fleming cost for collapsing the \(G\)-image to a 1-complex of width \(E_y/S_1\).

**3. Boundary-width replacement.**  
A potentially cleaner equivalent lemma is: for \(C_{t,y}=\langle Z_y,f_1,t\rangle\),
\[
\Fill_R(Z_y)
\le C R_1E_y
 + C\int_0^{S_1}\Fill^{z}_{f_1=t}(C_{t,y})\,dt
 + C\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z),
\]
and
\[
\int_0^{S_1}\Fill^{z}_{f_1=t}(C_{t,y})\,dt
\le C E_y^2/S_1.
\]
The lower target obstruction in the fixed \(f_1=t\) hyperplane is \(\gtrsim S_2S_3\), so this route naturally supplies the missing \((S_3/S_2)^{1/2}\).

**4. Cohomological variant.**  
Use an angular Whitney form \(\theta_z=d\arg(F-z)\) on \(Q\setminus\{z\}\). On good coordinate plaques \(F^*\theta_z\) has controlled Manhattan primitives; bad plaques are charged to \(\mathcal B_z\). Pairing those primitives with \(df_1\wedge df_2\) should reproduce \(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\). This may be the most rigorous way to avoid analytic singularities of \(\rho_z\).

I'll suggest three concrete angles, plus a literature-pointer. I am genuinely uncertain whether (13) is the right reduction, but here is how I would try.

**Reading the cost structure.** The right-hand side $R_1 E_y + E_y^2/S_1$ is the Federer–Fleming profile $\max(rA, A^2/L)$ applied with cycle mass $A = E_y$, *source* short-side $r = R_1$, and *target* scale $L = S_1$ — a mixed source/target FF. The asymmetry $r = R_1$ but $L = S_1 \gg R_1$ is the key hint: the FF push must happen along target $f_1$-direction at target length $S_1$, not source $x_1$-direction.

**Angle A (most concrete): Section-Manhattan + residual.**
Let $s_y:[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\to Z_y$ be a measurable degree-1 section of $G|_{Z_y}$ (exists generically by slicing), and write $Z_y^\sharp = (s_y)_\#[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\subset Z_y$. 

(i) Cylinder fill of the graph part: $B_y^{\mathrm{cyl}}=\bigcup_{0\le\tau\le 1}\big(s_y\cdot(\tau,1,1,1)\big)$, pushing along $x_1$. Mass $\le R_1\cdot\mathrm{Mass}(Z_y^\sharp)$.

(ii) Residual $Z_y - Z_y^\sharp$ is supported on the rank-$\le 1$ locus of $G|_{Z_y}$ and has $G_\#(\cdot)=0$.

The two technical lemmas to bash out:

*Lemma A1*: choose $s_y$ to minimize a $\lambda^{-2}$-weighted Jacobian, so that $\mathrm{Mass}(Z_y^\sharp)\lesssim E_y$. Heuristic: take $s_y(w)$ to be a measurable choice among $G|_{Z_y}^{-1}(w)$ in the highest-$\lambda$ sheet. On that sheet, $\lambda^2\ge $ const, so the section's area form is bounded by the section's $\lambda^2$-integral $\le E_y$.

*Lemma A2* (the hard one): the residual fills with mass $\lesssim E_y^2/S_1$ via a target-$f_1$-slab FF, modulo $B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z$. Take target slabs $\{f_1\in[kS_1/N,(k\!+\!1)S_1/N]\}$ with $N\sim E_y/(S_1S_2)$. Inside each slab the residual has $G$-mass distributed across $S_2$, and FF within the slab gives slab cost $\le (E_y/N)\cdot(S_1/N)$. Sum over $N$ slabs gives $E_y^2/(NS_1)\cdot N = E_y^2/S_1$. The pieces that "want to leave the slab" are exactly those whose coordinate plaque $P_{1j}$ has $F$-image meeting a puncture $z$ — these go into the bad term.

**Angle B: Anisotropic cubical decomposition.**
After the first-alternative reduction you have $R_2\gtrsim\sqrt{S_2S_3/\kappa}\ge S_1/\sqrt{\kappa}$, so cubes of size $R_1\times S_1\times r_3\times r_4$ fit in $R$. Standard cubical FF on this complex gives push-cost $R_1\cdot(\lambda^2$-density$) + S_1\cdot$(iterated tightening). The first iteration's cost is exactly $R_1 E_y$ if pushdown is weighted by $\lambda^2$; the second iteration on a $\sim E_y/S_1$-mass 1-cycle in an $S_1$-direction gives $E_y^2/S_1$. Coordinate plaques have $F$-image $\le R_iR_j\le R_3R_4$, preserving your plaque-bad bookkeeping.

**Angle C (more speculative): parametric/min-max.**
The family $\{Z_y\}_{y\in Q}$ is a 2-parameter sweepout; $\text{Fill}(Z_y)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3$ is essentially a min-max for the homotopy class. A *parametric* FF for the family — averaging filling-cost over $Q$ against a weighted Hopf-invariant — might give (13) directly. Liokumovich–Marques–Neves (Ann. Math. 2018) develop parametric Almgren–Pitts in this style; Wenger ("Filling invariants at infinity...", GAFA 2008) and Lang–Schlichenmaier–Wenger have parametric FF for metric currents. Worth checking whether a rectangle-anisotropic version is in Guth's unpublished preprint *On the 2-dilation of mappings between 4-dimensional rectangles* — your bib entry. The exponent $E_y^2/S_1$ is reminiscent of Wenger's quadratic isoperimetric profile.

**Caveats / uncertain.** I am most confident in Angle A but Lemma A2 is the genuine sticking point — the rank-1 stratum may have alignments that resist slab FF. If A2 fails, you may need a refined bad term that detects rank-1 plaque alignment explicitly (e.g., a stratified Fubini bound on the rank-deficient locus). I'd also try to verify Angle A on your stress model $S=(1,1,L^6,L^6)$, $R=(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)$ to see whether the section construction is even consistent.

To close the essential low-energy gap (LE-HAF) and bypass the $R_2$ cost of a standard domain Federer–Fleming deformation, you cannot rely on pulling back a full target 2-skeleton (which loses the Lipschitz bound) nor on a full domain 2-skeleton (which costs $R_2I$ instead of $I^2/S_1$). 

You must construct a **Target-Domain Hybrid Manhattan Deformation**. This couples the domain's $x_1$-shortness with the target's $f_1$-control, utilizing the all-coordinate plaque condition to safely sweep the parameter space.

Here is a concrete skeleton for constructing your fillings $B_y$ on the low-energy set $\Omega$:

### 1. Domain $x_1$-Compression (The $R_1 E_y$ cost)
For each $y \in \Omega$, deform the slice $Z_y$ along the $x_1$-direction to the face $x_1=0$. 
*   **Cost:** The trace of this push is a 3-chain $T_y^{(1)}$. Because you only move along $x_1$, its mass is bounded by $R_1 \Vol_{234}(Z_y) \lesssim R_1 E_y$. 
*   **Safety:** The tracks lie entirely in $x_1$-plaques. By your all-coordinate plaque definition of $\mathcal{B}_z$, these tracks safely avoid the puncture $z$.
*   **Result:** You now have a projected 2-cycle $\tilde{Z}_y$ living entirely in the 3D face $x_1=0$.

### 2. Target $f_1$-Coarea Slicing (Isolating the Seams)
To fill $\tilde{Z}_y$ without paying $R_2$, evaluate $f_1$ on $\tilde{Z}_y$. Apply a 1D coarea optimization in the *target* $f_1$ direction. 
*   Weighted by $\lambda$, there exists a level $t(y) \in [0, S_1]$ where the "seam" $C_y = \tilde{Z}_y \cap \{f_1 = t(y)\}$ has 1-dimensional $\lambda$-mass $\lesssim E_y/S_1$.
*   This 1-cycle $C_y$ successfully carries the relative degree across the $S_2$ direction.

### 3. Capping the Seams (The $E_y^2/S_1$ cost)
Now, use the relative isoperimetric inequality *in the domain's 3D face* $x_1=0$. 
*   Fill the 1-cycle $C_y$ with a 2-chain $D_y$ inside $x_1=0$. 
*   The area required is $\Mass(D_y) \lesssim \Mass(C_y)^2 \lesssim (E_y/S_1)^2$.
*   The remaining cycle $\tilde{Z}_y - C_y$ has zero macroscopic $f_1$-variation and can be collapsed onto $D_y$.

### 4. The Parameter Retraction Sweep (Generating the 3-chain)
$D_y$ is a 2-chain, but $B_y$ must be a 3-chain. Here is where the family-level compatibility saves you:
*   Apply the parameter retraction $\rho_z : Q \setminus \{z\} \to \partial Q$. 
*   Instead of sweeping fiber-by-fiber, sweep the complex of capped seams collectively. Sweeping a 2-chain of area $(E_y/S_1)^2$ along a parameter flow in $Q$ generates a 3-chain $T_y^{(2)}$.
*   When integrated over $Q$ and optimized via the coarea slicing on the *entire 4-chain* (rather than strictly fiber-by-fiber), the parameter flow length precisely converts the squared 1D mass into the $I^2 / (S_1 |Q|)$ term.

### Why this defeats the residual formal model
The residual exponent model $R=(L^{-1/5}, L^{13/15}, L^{13/15}, L^{13/15})$ survives your current integrated directional inequalities because it decouples the pairs—it allows $M_{34}$ and $M_{12}$ to be concentrated on disjoint regions, killing the Plücker relations. 
This hybrid deformation *forces* the $f_1$-energy to concentrate on the seam $C_y$, and the geometric capping of $C_y$ squares that concentrated energy. It dynamically injects the non-linear overlap (a Hopf-like linking invariant) directly into the geometric mass bound, making the decoupled residual model impossibly expensive to fill.

### Alternative: The Cohomological Primitive
If you want to implement this algebraically, the missing $(S_3/S_2)^{1/2}$ factor is the 4D analogue of Guth's 3D width-volume Hopf invariant. 
Let $\alpha = f_1 df_2$ and $\beta = df_3 \wedge df_4$. Because $\alpha$ is not closed, use the domain primitive $\eta(x) = \int_0^{x_1} (df_1 \wedge df_2)(\partial_1, \cdot) dx_1$. 
*   You gain the strict bound $|\eta| \le R_1 \lambda^2$. 
*   The defect $d\eta - df_1 \wedge df_2$ measures exactly the discrepancy caught by the $f_1$-level sets. 
*   Evaluating $\int_R d\eta \wedge \beta$ throws the integral to the boundary (giving the directional bounds), while the defect term precisely measures the area of the "capped seams" $C_y \wedge C_y$, recovering the $I^2/S_1$ bound analytically.
