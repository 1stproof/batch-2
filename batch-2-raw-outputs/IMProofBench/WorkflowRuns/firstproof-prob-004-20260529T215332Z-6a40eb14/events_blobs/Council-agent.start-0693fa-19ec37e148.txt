\begin{center}
{\bf Progress toward the rectangle estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes and a degree \(1\) map
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to (S,\partial S)\) is understood in relative homology.  The
constant called \(k\) in the problem is denoted below by \(\kappa\).  This draft records
the part of the proof that I can make rigorous at present and isolates the remaining
mixed-sweepout estimate needed to finish the argument.

\section*{Two standard consequences}

If \(\Dil_2(f)\le 1\), then \(\Dil_j(f)\le 1\) for every \(j\ge 2\).  Indeed, if the singular
values of \(df\) are \(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\), then
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le 1\), hence \(\sigma_i\le 1\) for \(i\ge 2\), and so
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le 1\).

The following elementary filling estimate is the key local input.  It is the
rectangular version of Guth's filling lemma for small relative \(2\)-cycles.

\begin{lemma}[small relative \(2\)-cycles in a rectangle]\label{lem:fill}
There is a universal constant \(C_0\) such that every oriented relative \(2\)-cycle
\(Z\) in a \(4\)-rectangle \(R_1\le R_2\le R_3\le R_4\) with
\[
        \operatorname{area}(Z)\le \frac12R_1R_2
\]
has an oriented relative filling \(Y\) with
\[
        \Vol_3(Y)\le C_0 R_1R_2^2 .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
Approximate \(Z\) by an axis-parallel polyhedral relative cycle.  Since its
projection to the \((x_1,x_2)\)-rectangle has area \(<R_1R_2\), choose a point
\((p_1,p_2)\) missed by that projection.  The usual Federer--Fleming radial filling
from a point \(p=(p_1,p_2,p_3)\), averaged over \(p_3\in[0,R_3]\), gives volume at
most \(C\,\operatorname{area}(Z)R_2\).  Under the displayed area assumption this is
\(\le C_0R_1R_2^2\).  A limiting argument removes the polyhedral approximation.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[a proved alternative]\label{prop:firstalt}
There are universal constants \(c_1,C_1>0\) with the following property.  Let
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) have degree \(1\) and \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).  If
\[
        C_1R_1R_2^2 \le S_1S_2S_3 ,
\]
then
\[
        R_3R_4 \ge c_1S_3S_4 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For a regular pair \((y_3,y_4)\) in the central subrectangle
\([S_3/3,2S_3/3]\times[S_4/3,2S_4/3]\), let
\[
 P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\}.
\]
This is a relative \(2\)-cycle in \(S\).  Any relative \(3\)-chain filling it has volume
at least \(cS_1S_2S_3\): project the filling to the \((x_1,x_2,x_3)\)-box and use the
fact that the slab \(P_y\) is distance \(\ge S_3/3\) from the two \(x_3\)-faces.

Let \(Z_y=f^{-1}(P_y)\), with its current/slicing orientation.  Since \(f\) has degree
\(1\), \(f_\#Z_y\) represents \(P_y\).  If \(Y\) fills \(Z_y\) in \(R\), then \(f_\#Y\) fills
\(P_y\) in \(S\), and \(\Vol_3(f_\#Y)\le\Vol_3(Y)\) because \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\).  Hence
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
Choosing \(C_1\) sufficiently large, Lemma \ref{lem:fill} implies
\(\operatorname{area}(Z_y)\ge cR_1R_2\) for all such regular \(y\).

Finally apply the coarea formula to \((x_3,x_4)\circ f\).  Its \(2\)-Jacobian is at
most \(1\).  Therefore
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge \int \operatorname{area}(Z_y)\,dy_3dy_4
     \ge c R_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Dividing by \(R_1R_2\) proves \(R_3R_4\ge c_1S_3S_4\).
\end{proof}

Thus, in order to prove the theorem, it remains only to handle the complementary
case
\[
        R_1R_2^2 \gtrsim S_1S_2S_3                                      \tag{1}
\]
under the additional hypothesis \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\).  In that case \(R_2^2\) is
large compared with \(S_2S_3\) if \(\kappa\) is chosen small.

\section*{The missing mixed-sweepout estimate}

The desired conclusion would follow from the following estimate.

\begin{proposition}[mixed estimate still to be proved]\label{prop:mixed}
There is a universal \(c>0\) such that every degree-one map of pairs
\(f:R\to S\) with \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) and satisfying
\(R_1R_2^2\ge c\,S_1S_2S_3\) obeys
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c\,S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
unless \(R_3R_4\ge c\,S_3S_4\).
\end{proposition}

Indeed, Proposition \ref{prop:firstalt} gives the first alternative unless (1) holds;
then Proposition \ref{prop:mixed} gives the volume alternative.  Taking
\(\kappa\) smaller than the constants in these two alternatives would prove the
problem.

A tempting proof of Proposition \ref{prop:mixed} is to pull back a three-parameter
family of \(1\)-cycles in \(S\) and use the coarea formula for
\((x_1,x_3,x_4)\circ f\).  The needed lower bound would be that each such pulled
back \(1\)-cycle has length \(\gtrsim (S_2S_3)^{1/2}\).  The local isoperimetric
inequality in \(R\) would give this length bound once the pulled-back cycles had
filling area \(\gtrsim S_2S_3\).

The difficulty is that the most obvious relative \(1\)-cycles in \(S\), the intervals
\(\{y_1\}\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\}\), do not have filling area
\(\gtrsim S_2S_3\): as relative cycles they may be filled through the \(x_1\)-faces
with area only \(O(S_1S_2)\).  Thus the argument must use a genuinely mixed
family of cycles, or an equivalent chain-level tightening, that prevents this escape
through the short \(x_1\)-direction.

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The proof is complete up to Proposition \ref{prop:mixed}.  What remains is:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Mathematically, prove the mixed-sweepout estimate
\[
        \Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
in the complementary case \(R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\) and
\(R_3R_4\lesssim S_3S_4\).

\item The obstacle in the current approach is precise: naive relative \(1\)-cycles in
\(S\) fill through the \(x_1\)-boundary with area \(O(S_1S_2)\), whereas the coarea
argument needs cycles with filling area \(\gtrsim S_2S_3\).

\item What was checked this round: the earlier proposed counterexample based on an
interpolated Guth pinching map is invalid; Guth's published degree-map estimate
rules out its final map.  Proposition \ref{prop:firstalt} above gives a rigorous first
alternative and reduces the problem to the mixed estimate.

\item What would close the proof: either a chain-level tightening argument producing
the required mixed family of \(1\)-cycles, or a reference giving Proposition
\ref{prop:mixed} for degree-one maps of pairs.
\end{enumerate}
