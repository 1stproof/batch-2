\begin{center}
{\bf The stated rectangle estimate is false}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I interpret a rectangle with side lengths \(a_1,\ldots,a_4\) as the
oriented product \(\prod_i[0,a_i]\), and a map
\((R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) of degree \(1\) as a map inducing the
usual generator of relative \(H_4\).  With this interpretation the proposed
theorem is not correct: no positive universal constant can satisfy it.  I
write the proposed constant as \(\kappa\), to avoid confusing it with the
dimension in \(k\)-dilation.

\begin{lemma}[Guth's four-dimensional constructions]
There is a universal constant \(C_0\) with the following two consequences of
Guth's Chapter 8.2 constructions \cite[pp.~144--148]{GuthThesis}.  For every
sufficiently large \(L\), there are degree-one piecewise smooth maps of pairs
with \(2\)-dilation at most \(C_0\)
\[
 [0,1]\times[0,3L^{2/3}]^3
 \longrightarrow
 [0,\tfrac12]\times[0,2]\times[0,2]\times[0,2L^2],
\tag{1}
\]
and
\[
 [0,\tfrac12]\times[0,2]\times[0,2]\times[0,2L^2]
 \longrightarrow
 [0,\tfrac12]\times[0,2]\times[0,L]\times[0,4L].
\tag{2}
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The first map is Guth's double-pinching map.  In his notation take
\[
R_1=1,\qquad R_2=R_3=R_4=3L^{2/3},\qquad A=2,\qquad B=2L^2 .
\]
The hypotheses are
\[
R_1<A,\qquad A^2<R_2R_3,\qquad A^3B<R_1R_2R_3R_4,
\]
which become \(1<2\), \(4<9L^{4/3}\), and \(16L^2<27L^2\).  Thus Guth's
construction gives a degree-one boundary-preserving map with uniformly
bounded \(2\)-dilation to
\((R_1^2/A)\times A\times A\times B=(1/2)\times2\times2\times 2L^2\).

The second map is the interpolated ``short-side stretch'' in the technical
remark following Guth's third construction.  Writing \(\lambda\) for the
stretch factor of the first side and \(A\) for the third output side, the
target is
\[
\lambda R_1\times \lambda^{-3}R_2\times A\times B,
\]
under the hypotheses
\[
\lambda^4<R_2/R_1,\qquad R_3<A<(R_3R_4)^{1/2},\qquad AB=R_3R_4 .
\]
For the source rectangle in (2), choose
\[
\lambda=1,\qquad A=L,\qquad B=4L .
\]
Then \(1<R_2/R_1=4\), \(2<L<2L=(R_3R_4)^{1/2}\), and \(AB=4L^2=R_3R_4\).
Again the dilation bound is absorbed into the same universal constant.  The
maps in Guth's construction are Lipschitz and may be taken piecewise smooth
after an arbitrarily small perturbation, increasing \(C_0\) only by a
universal factor.
\end{proof}

Now set
\[
 R^0(L)=
 [0,L^{-1/6}]\times[0,3L^{5/6}]^3,
 \qquad
 S(L)=
 [0,\tfrac12]\times[0,2]\times[0,L]\times[0,4L].
\]
The diagonal linear map \(R^0(L)\to [0,1]\times[0,3L^{2/3}]^3\) has singular
values
\[
 L^{1/6},\quad L^{-1/6},\quad L^{-1/6},\quad L^{-1/6},
\]
so its \(2\)-dilation is \(1\).  Composing it with the two maps from the
lemma gives a degree-one map of pairs
\[
F_L:R^0(L)\longrightarrow S(L)
\]
with \(\Dil_2(F_L)\le D\), where \(D=C_0^2\) is independent of \(L\).

Finally enlarge the domain by the constant factor \(\sqrt D\):
\[
 R(L)=\sqrt D\,R^0(L),
 \qquad
 f_L(x)=F_L(x/\sqrt D).
\]
Then \(f_L:(R(L),\partial R(L))\to(S(L),\partial S(L))\) has degree \(1\) and
\[
\Dil_2(f_L)\le D\cdot(\sqrt D)^{-2}=1.
\]
For \(L\ge2\), the side lengths of \(R(L)\) and \(S(L)\) are ordered
nondecreasingly.  Their relevant ratios are
\[
\frac{R_1(L)}{S_1(L)}
   =2\sqrt D\,L^{-1/6},
\]
\[
\frac{R_3(L)R_4(L)}{S_3(L)S_4(L)}
   =\frac{(3\sqrt D\,L^{5/6})^2}{4L^2}
   =\frac{9D}{4}L^{-1/3},
\]
and
\[
\frac{\Vol(R(L))}
     {S_1(L)S_2(L)^{1/2}S_3(L)^{3/2}S_4(L)}
 =\frac{D^2\cdot 27L^{7/3}}
        {(\tfrac12)\sqrt2\,L^{3/2}\cdot 4L}
 =\frac{27D^2}{2\sqrt2}\,L^{-1/6}.
\]
All three ratios tend to \(0\) as \(L\to\infty\).  Therefore, given any
positive number \(\kappa\), choosing \(L\) sufficiently large gives
\[
 R_1(L)\le \kappa S_1(L),\qquad
 R_3(L)R_4(L)\le \kappa S_3(L)S_4(L),
\]
and
\[
 \Vol(R(L))\le
 \kappa\,S_1(L)S_2(L)^{1/2}S_3(L)^{3/2}S_4(L),
\]
while \(f_L\) still has degree \(1\) and \(\Dil_2(f_L)\le1\).  Thus both
alternatives in the proposed conclusion fail.  Hence the asserted universal
constant \(\kappa>0\) does not exist.
