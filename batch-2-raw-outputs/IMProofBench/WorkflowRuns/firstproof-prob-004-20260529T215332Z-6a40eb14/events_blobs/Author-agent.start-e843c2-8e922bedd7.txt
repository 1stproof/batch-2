I would try to prove a **local discrete self-absorbing version**, not an estimate for independently chosen minimizers of each slice.

Concrete route:

1. **Discretize the parameter square \(Q\).**  
   Take a cubical grid \(Q_h\) avoiding critical values and define a chain map
   \[
   \sigma\subset Q_h \longmapsto C_\sigma:=\langle [R,\partial R],F,\sigma\rangle
   \]
   with \(\dim C_\sigma=2+\dim\sigma\). Thus vertices are the cycles \(Z_v\), edges are canonical cobordisms between neighboring fibers, and squares are the actual four-dimensional pieces over parameter squares. This is the object to tighten; do not choose fillings fiberwise.

2. **Prove a finite-dimensional tightening lemma for this chain map.**  
   The lemma I would aim for is roughly
   \[
   \sum_{v}h^2 \Fill(Z_v)
   \le
   C\Big(R_1\sum_v h^2E_v+
          {(\sum_v h^2E_v)^2\over S_1|Q|}\Big)
   +C\,\mathbb E_{\tau}\sum_{v\in B_\tau}h^2\Fill(Z_v),
   \]
   where \(E_v\approx\int_{Z_v}\lambda^2\), and \(B_\tau\) is the set of vertices where the tightening, for a random translated cubulation \(\tau\), is forced to use genuine \((x_3,x_4)\)-top-dimensional source cells rather than the controlled mixed-face deformation. The desired estimate follows if
   \[
   \mathbb E_\tau\sum_{v\in B_\tau}h^2\Fill(Z_v)
   \le C{R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}\sum_vh^2\Fill(Z_v).
   \]
   This last line is exactly where the self-absorption should enter: for each fixed parameter \(y\), the probability that \(y\) lands in the bad part of a random tightening should be \(\lesssim R_3R_4/S_3S_4\).

3. **Where the two controlled terms should come from.**  
   The \(R_1I\) term should be the cost of pushing/tightening in the short \(x_1\)-direction, weighted by the \(G=(f_1,f_2)\)-energy on fibers. The \(I^2/(S_1q)\) term should be obtained only after an energy-balancing or good-\(\lambda\) step in parameter space; a naive fiberwise estimate would give \(\int E_y^2/S_1\), which is too large. So I would explicitly include a stopping-time decomposition of \(Q\) by the density \(E(U)/|U|\), tighten low-density cubes at their own scale, and charge high-density cubes to \(R_1I\).

4. **Key point to verify.**  
   One needs a mixed-face deformation theorem using the pointwise bound
   \[
   \lambda^2J_2F\le1.
   \]
   In the discrete chain map, this should control the mass of the “mixed” cobordism faces created when moving in parameter directions and \(G\)-directions simultaneously. This is the real Federer--Fleming/Guth input.

As a counterexample test, try to localize the product obstruction to a small parameter sub-square \(U\subset Q\) of area \(\sim R_3R_4\). If such a block can be realized with \(\Dil_2\le1\), SAF fails, since the bad contribution would be concentrated while the coefficient \(R_3R_4/S_3S_4\) is global. I do not see a valid construction in the hard regime; the mixed inequality seems designed to rule it out. But any proof of SAF must explicitly rule out this localized product block.

**Quick take.** I lean toward SAF being correct in the regime $R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4$, but I have no clean proof. The standard Guth constructions (axis-aligned) seem obstructed in this regime by 3D boundary inequalities. Here is the chain-level skeleton I would attempt.

**Lift the family to a 4-current in $\tilde R := R \times Q$.** Define
$$T := (\mathrm{id}_R, F)_\#\,[F^{-1}(Q)] \in I_4(\tilde R,\partial\tilde R),$$
the graph of $F$ over $F^{-1}(Q)$. Then $\Mass T = \int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy$, and any relative 5-chain $\tilde W \subset \tilde R$ with $\partial\tilde W = T$ slices by $\pi_Q$ to a measurable family of 3-chain fillings $W_y$ of $Z_y$ in $R$, with $\int_Q\Mass W_y\,dy = \Mass\tilde W$. Hence $A \le \Fill_{\tilde R}(T)$.

Apply Guth's rectangular profile (Thm 3, arXiv:0710.0403) for $k=4, n=6$. For sorted sides of $\tilde R$ — typically $r_1 = R_1,\dots,r_4 = R_4, r_5,r_6 \sim S_3,S_4$ — the branches are
$\Fill \le C M^{(5-j)/(4-j)}/(r_1\cdots r_j)^{1/(4-j)}$, $j=0,1,2,3$,
giving $C M^{4/3}/R_1^{1/3}$ at $j=1$ and $C M^2/(R_1R_2R_3)$ at $j=3$.

**Why naive 6D FF does not give SAF.** SAF has $R_1$ in the *numerator* and the target $S_1$ in the denominator; the 6D profile produces polynomials in source sides only. So one cannot just substitute $M=\Mass T$. The natural enhancement is to apply FF to a $\lambda$-thresholded sub-current.

**Refinement.** Split $Z_y = Z_y^h \sqcup Z_y^l$ at $\lambda=\tau$.

(i) *Heavy* ($\lambda\ge\tau$): $\Mass Z_y^h \le I_y/\tau^2$, so the $x_1$-slide gives $\Fill(Z_y^h) \le R_1\Mass Z_y^h$, integrating to $R_1 I/\tau^2$. At $\tau\sim 1$ this is the first SAF term.

(ii) *Light* ($\lambda<\tau$): $J_2 G \le \tau^2$ on $Z_y^l$, so its $G$-mass is $\le \tau^2\Mass Z_y^l$ and it cannot carry the full target degree $S_1S_2$ when $\tau$ is small. The residual degree is forced onto the heavy part. Iterating this coupling between $\int Z_y^h$ and $\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2$ across $y\in Q$ should give the $I^2/(S_1 q)$ term via Cauchy–Schwarz (the $S_1$ enters precisely because the degree constraint is on the target $[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]$).

(iii) *Absorbing $(R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\,A$:* apply 6D FF to the light family current. The light slices wrap mostly along the source $(R_3,R_4)$-thin directions; the ratio to the parameter area $|Q|\sim S_3S_4$ is the natural defect, and the leftover filling is comparable to $A$ itself.

I have not closed the exponent arithmetic — uncertain whether this exact heavy/light split closes — but the mechanism is the only chain-level route I see for the unusual mixed source/target form. The $S_1$-denominator in particular pretty much has to come from step (ii).

**Counterexample heuristic.** If $f|_{\{x_1=0\}}\colon\{x_1=0\}\to\partial S$ is axis-aligned with face-degree 1, Guth's 3D $\Dil_2$ estimate forces $R_3R_4 \ge cS_3S_4$, contradicting the hard regime. Standard Guth maps live in this class, and your round-3 ruling-out of the middle-side snake via Estimate 1 ($j{=}0,k{=}2,l{=}3$) closes the closest non-axis-aligned variant I know. A counterexample requires a genuinely face-permuting $\Dil_2\le 1$ boundary — I have not seen one constructed, and the 3D $\Dil_2$ inequalities applied to any decomposition of $\partial f$ seem to sharply obstruct it. I would not bet on a counterexample in this regime.

### Council member: gemini-31-pro (models/gemini/gemini-31-pro) ###
Here is a concrete, rigorous chain-level route to prove the self-absorbing estimate. The core idea is to reverse the conditioning: instead of filling $Z_y$ by projecting it to a boundary face (which failed in Round 7), we build fillings by avoiding points $u \in R_1 \times R_2$, and then use a Fubini argument to extract the exact self-absorbing fraction $\frac{R_3 R_4}{S_3 S_4}$.

**Step 1: The dual area bound**
Let $P_{12}$ be the projection to the $(x_1, x_2)$ plane, and define $a(y) = |P_{12}(Z_y)|$. 
By definition, $u \in P_{12}(Z_y) \iff y \in F(\{u\} \times R_3 \times R_4)$. Because $\Dil_2(F) \le 1$, the area of $F(\{u\} \times R_3 \times R_4)$ in $Q$ is bounded by $R_3 R_4$.
Integrating over $u \in R_1 \times R_2$ via Fubini gives:
\[
\int_Q a(y)\,dy = \int_{R_1 \times R_2} |F(\{u\} \times R_3 \times R_4)|\,du \le R_1 R_2 R_3 R_4.
\]
Thus, the average of $\frac{a(y)}{R_1 R_2}$ over $Q$ is exactly $\le \frac{R_3 R_4}{S_3 S_4}$. This will become your self-absorbing fraction.

**Step 2: The avoidance retraction**
Fix $u \in R_1 \times R_2$. If $u \notin P_{12}(Z_y)$, then $Z_y$ completely avoids the 2-plane $\{u\} \times R_3 \times R_4$. 
We can construct a deformation retraction $\Phi_u$ of $R_1 \times R_2 \setminus \{u\}$ to its boundary. To prevent the trace length from depending on the long side $R_2$, define $\Phi_u$ to flow horizontally away from $u_1$ directly to the $x_1 \in \{0, R_1\}$ faces, except inside a vertical strip of width $R_1$ around $u$, where it flows vertically by $O(R_1)$ to dodge $u$ before resuming the horizontal flow. The maximum path length of this retraction is $C R_1$.

**Step 3: Averaging the filling over $u$**
For any $y$ and any $u \notin P_{12}(Z_y)$, applying $\Phi_u$ to $Z_y$ creates a relative 3-chain filling $W_{y,u}$ bounded by $Z_y$ modulo $\partial R$.
By standard Federer–Fleming averaging, integrating the mass of this trace over all valid basepoints $u$ yields:
\[
\int_{R_1 \times R_2 \setminus P_{12}(Z_y)} \Mass(W_{y, u})\,du \le C (R_1 R_2) R_1 I_y,
\]
where $I_y = \int_{Z_y} \lambda^2$.

**Step 4: The master inequality**
Since $A(y) = \Fill_R(Z_y) \le \Mass(W_{y,u})$ for valid $u$, we integrate over $y \in Q$:
\[
\int_Q A(y) \left( \int_{R_1 \times R_2} \chi_{\{u \notin P_{12}(Z_y)\}}\,du \right) dy \le C R_1^2 R_2 I.
\]
The inner integral is exactly $R_1 R_2 - a(y)$. Dividing by $R_1 R_2$ gives a rigorous point-wise-in-$y$ anchoring of your estimate:
\[
A \le C R_1 I + \int_Q A(y) \frac{a(y)}{R_1 R_2}\,dy.
\]

**Step 5: Parameter space tightening to decouple the average**
If $A(y)$ and $a(y)$ were uncorrelated, the integral on the right would be exactly $A \frac{R_3 R_4}{S_3 S_4}$. To rigorously decouple them, apply Guth’s continuous tightening to the map $F: R \to Q$ over a grid on $Q$ (scale $\sim S_1$). 
Tightening replaces $Z_y$ with a family that is constant over each top-dimensional parameter cell $\sigma$. This replaces $A(y)$ with a constant $A_\sigma$ and $a(y)$ with its cell-average $\bar{a}_\sigma$. The integral factors as $\sum_\sigma A_\sigma \int_\sigma a(y)\,dy \le \max(A_\sigma) R_1 R_2 \frac{R_3 R_4}{S_3 S_4}$, producing exactly the $C \frac{R_3 R_4}{S_3 S_4} A$ term. The energetic cost of doing this tightening over the 1-skeleton of $Q$ yields the $I^2 / (S_1 q)$ error.

This avoids the false single-face trace lemma, replaces it with a rigorous 2-face avoidance argument, and proves your SAF estimate without requiring any speculative scaling bounds.
