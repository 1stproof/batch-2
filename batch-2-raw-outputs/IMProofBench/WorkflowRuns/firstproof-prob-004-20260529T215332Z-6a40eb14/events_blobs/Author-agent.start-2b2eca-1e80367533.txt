I would not try to prove a fiberwise strengthening. The right replacement seems to be a **low-energy, self-absorbing, parametric deformation theorem** rather than the global estimate in its raw form.

Here is the portfolio I would pursue.

1. **Prove only a low-energy version of HAF.**  
   Let \(E_y=\int_{Z_y}\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{TZ_y}\|^2\), \(q=|Q|\), and
   \[
   \Omega=\{y:E_y\le 4I/q\}.
   \]
   It is enough to prove
   \[
   \int_\Omega \Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
   \le C\int_\Omega\left(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\right)dy
   +\frac Cq\int_Q\int_\Omega \Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz,
   \]
   where \(B_y\) are near-minimizing fillings and \(\mathcal B_z\) is a bad set. This avoids needing to control high-energy exceptional fibers. The low-energy cutoff converts \(\int E_y^2\) into \(I^2/q\).

2. **Use an all-coordinate plaque bad set.**  
   I would define \(\mathcal B_z\) using all coordinate \(2\)-plaques \(P_{ij}(x)\), not only vertical \(x_3x_4\)-plaques:
   \[
   x\in\mathcal B_z \iff z\in (f_3,f_4)(P_{ij}(x)) \text{ for some } i<j.
   \]
   Then
   \[
   \frac1q\int_Q \Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dz
   \lesssim \frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\Mass B_y,
   \]
   since every coordinate plaque has \(F\)-image area \(\le R_iR_j\le R_3R_4\). This gives exactly the desired absorbing term after averaging. This also fixes failures of “vertical-puncture only” arguments.

3. **Make the deformation cellular/Manhattan, not radial.**  
   The promising technical lemma is a Guth-style cubical deformation: choose a shifted cubulation, replace \(B_y\) by cubical near-minimizers, and construct a chain map \(P_{z,\theta}\) plus homotopy \(H_{z,\theta}\) such that good cells are pushed to \(\partial R\) relatively, while bad cells are left and charged to \(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z\). The tracks must be “Manhattan”: vary at most two source coordinates, so that the all-plaque bad-set estimate applies. Avoid sharp restriction \(B_y\llcorner \mathcal B_z\) without cubulation, since the interface boundary can be huge.

4. **Energy cost heuristic for the good part.**  
   The estimate
   \[
   \Mass H(Z_y)\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1
   \]
   should come from a two-stage tightening of \(G=(f_1,f_2)\) on the fiber: move degree-carrying pieces in the short \(x_1\)-direction at cost \(R_1E_y\), then cap seams selected by coarea in the \(f_1\)-coordinate at cost \(E_y^2/S_1\). The proof must ignore/null-fill low-\(G\)-energy flaps using \(B_y\); a literal source push of all of \(Z_y\) cannot work.

5. **Equivalent replacement theorem.**  
   Instead of HAF, one could aim directly for the mixed Guth rectangle inequality
   \[
   \frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}
   +\frac{R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}}{S_1\sqrt{S_3S_4}}
   +\frac{\Vol R}{S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4}\gtrsim 1.
   \]
   This is exactly the hard-regime content and may be closer to Guth’s unpublished four-dimensional rectangle result. The published monomial/directional estimates do not imply it; the stress models show one needs genuine compatibility of the degree-carrying regions.

6. **Cycle-space formulation.**  
   Discretize \(Q\). The fibers \(Z_v\), edge slices \(\langle[R],F,e\rangle\), and square slices \(\langle[R],F,\tau\rangle\) form a cubical chain map into relative currents. The desired tightening is a quantitative Almgren/Guth deformation of this whole parameterized family, not of separate fibers. This viewpoint may prevent the “different inequalities paid on disjoint pieces” pathology.

My bias: the all-coordinate plaque + low-energy Manhattan deformation is the most plausible route. The main danger is controlling seams after cubical replacement; that is where I would focus the technical proof.

The form $R_1I + I^2/(S_1|Q|)$ is a two-scale "filling profile" with source short side $R_1$ and target short side $S_1$. Five angles, roughly in decreasing confidence.

**(A) Family as a 4-current in $R\times Q$.** Let $\Phi(x)=(x,F(x))$ and $\mathcal Z=\Phi_\#[F^{-1}(Q)]$, a 4-current in the 6-rectangle $R\times Q$ with sides $(R_1,R_2,R_3,R_4,S_3,S_4)$. A 5-chain $W$ with $\partial W=\mathcal Z$ has $\pi_Q$-slices $B_y$ with $\partial B_y=Z_y$ and $\int_Q\mathrm{Mass}(B_y)\,dy\le\mathrm{Mass}(W)$. Bare FF gives $\mathrm{Mass}(W)\lesssim R_1\mathrm{Vol}(R)$ (shortest side). To replace $\mathrm{Vol}(R)$ by $I$, do FF with $\lambda^2$-weighted mass on the graph; your bound $\lambda^2 J_2F\le 1$ keeps the weight measurable-bounded. For the second branch, embed instead in the 8-rectangle $R\times Q\times G(R)$ with sides also including $S_1,S_2$: now $S_1$ is a short side in a transverse direction, and the deformation in the $S_1$-axis picks up the $I^2/(S_1|Q|)$ branch.

**(B) $f_1$-coarea + 1-cycle profile.** Slice $Z_y$ by $f_1$ at $t\in[0,S_1]$ to 1-cycles $C_{t,y}$ of length $L_{t,y}$, fill in $R$ by 2-chains of area $\lesssim L_{t,y}^2$, integrate:
\[
\mathrm{Fill}_R(Z_y)\lesssim \int_0^{S_1}L_{t,y}^2\,dt+\text{(boundary traces)}.
\]
Coarea on $Z_y$: $\int_0^{S_1}L_{t,y}\,dt=\int_{Z_y}|\nabla_{Z_y}f_1|\le\int_{Z_y}\lambda\le\sqrt{E_y\,\mathrm{Mass}(Z_y)}$. The precise computation to do is whether $\iint L_{t,y}^2\,dt\,dy$ rearranges via parameter Cauchy–Schwarz to $R_1I+I^2/(S_1|Q|)$; the dimensions match and the $1/S_1$ factor falls out of the $t$-integration in the standard way.

**(C) Dual $G$-fiber linking as the missing global compatibility.** $W_u=\langle[R],G,u\rangle$ for $u\in[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]$ are 2-cycles, $W_u\cdot Z_y=1$ a.e. by degree. For any filling $B_y$ of $Z_y$, $\langle B_y,G,u\rangle$ is a 1-chain from $f^{-1}(u,y)$ to $\partial R$. Coarea gives a *lower* bound $\mathrm{Mass}(B_y)\ge\int\mathrm{dist}(f^{-1}(u,y),\partial R)\,du$. Pair with the canonical *upper* construction $B_y=\int_{[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]}(\text{$x_1$-push from }f^{-1}(u,y)\text{ to }\partial R)\,du$ of mass $\le R_1 S_1 S_2$: this is the global degree-one compatibility absent from your single-slice estimates.

**(D) Core/flap decomposition.** Let $Z_y^{\mathrm{ess}}=\{\lambda\ge c\}\subset Z_y$, with $\mathrm{Mass}(Z_y^{\mathrm{ess}})\sim E_y$; push in $x_1$ at cost $\sim R_1 E_y$. The flaps carry no $G$-degree, but their filling may be large via long source travel — charge them to the absorbed term via your all-plaque bad-puncture Fubini estimate, since flap motion in the source produces $F$-image in the bad-puncture set in $Q$.

**(E) Literature.** Guth, *Minimax problems related to cup powers and Steenrod squares* (GAFA 2009) — the 2-parameter sweepout estimates are the closest analogs. White, *Mappings that minimize area in their homotopy classes* (J. Diff. Geom. 1984), and Almgren's parametric isoperimetric framework give the most relevant filling-for-families machinery. The two-branch RHS is formally an anisotropic Gagliardo–Nirenberg on Almgren's $\mathcal Z_2(R)$ space; that may be the cleanest formal home.

My personal bet: (A) is the right framework, the proof of HAF is essentially a weighted parametric FF in $R\times Q$ (or $R\times Q\times G(R)$), and (D) handles the absorbed term cleanly. (B) and (C) are useful sanity checks for the structure of the two branches.

To resolve the tension between source-deformations (which cost ordinary mass rather than the weighted $E_y$) and target-deformations (which cost $E_y$ but lose the $R_1$ scaling), I suggest lifting the entire deformation problem to the product space $R \times S$. 

Here is a portfolio of approaches to prove the low-energy averaged estimate (LE-HAF), bypassing the sharp-cut and "flap" obstructions you identified:

**1. Federer-Fleming in the Graph Space $R \times S$**
Instead of working in $R$, work with the graph current $\Gamma = (Id, f)_\# \llbracket R \rrbracket \subset R \times S$. The $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$ condition is equivalent to saying that the 2-dimensional mass of $\Gamma$ projected onto *any* mixed coordinate 2-plane (e.g., $x_1 \times s_2$) is controlled. 
*   **The Manhattan Tracks:** A standard cellular tightening of the slice graphs in $R \times S$ automatically decouples into orthogonal pushes along the product grid. Pushing a 2-cycle along the $x_1$ direction sweeps a 3-track whose mass is bounded by $R_1 \times \Mass(\pi_{S_{12}} \Gamma)$. This projected mass is exactly your energy $E_y$. Thus, the $x_1$-track natively costs $\sim R_1 E_y$ without any hand-waving about mixed weights.
*   **The Profile Term:** Pushing along the target directions $s_1, s_2$ invokes the target grid scale, which you can optimize against the energy to yield the $I^2 / (S_1 |Q|)$ term, precisely mimicking the standard rectangle profile but in the product space.

**2. Smooth Retraction to Avoid "Seam" Costs**
You correctly noted that the atomic comparison fails because sharp cellular cuts $\partial(B_y \llcorner \mathcal{B}_z)$ generate uncontrolled interface boundaries. 
*   Instead of a sharp puncture $z$, use a smooth family of vector fields $W_z$ on $Q$ that retract $Q$ to $\partial Q$ (e.g., a standard radial flow smoothed near the puncture). 
*   Lift this flow to $R \times S$ and apply it to the fillings $B_y$. The boundary of the deformed chain depends on the Lie derivative of the flow, completely avoiding sharp interface seams. 
*   The absorbing bad-mass term $\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4} A$ will emerge naturally via Fubini: the total $x_3, x_4$ projection area of $\Gamma$ is bounded by $R_3 R_4$, meaning a generic flow line only hits the "flaps" (where the flow cannot contract the cycle) with probability $\lesssim R_3 R_4 / S_3 S_4$. Averaging the homotopy over $z$ integrates out the local gradient blow-ups.

**3. Calibrated Plücker Pairings in $R \times S$**
You observed that integrated directional inequalities allow the residual exponent model $L^{-1/10}$ because absolute-value minors allow the degree-carrying regions to decouple across the domain.
*   To force global compatibility, evaluate the graph fillings against a constant-coefficient 3-form $\omega \in \Lambda^3(R \times S)$ that mixes source and target differentials (e.g., $\omega = dx_1 \wedge ds_1 \wedge ds_2$). 
*   Because $\omega$ is closed, Stokes' theorem forces the sign-sensitive Plücker cancellations to persist globally on the fillings $B_y$, preventing the disjoint-support laminate models that saturate the absolute-value directional bounds.

**Summary Recommendation:**
Formulate the fillings $B_y$ as 3-currents in $R \times S$. The "Manhattan deformation" is structurally just the standard Federer-Fleming projection onto a grid in $R \times S$. The source grid provides the $R_1$ bounds, the target grid provides the $E_y$ profile limits, and the product geometry natively prevents the "flaps" from corrupting the low-energy pushes.
