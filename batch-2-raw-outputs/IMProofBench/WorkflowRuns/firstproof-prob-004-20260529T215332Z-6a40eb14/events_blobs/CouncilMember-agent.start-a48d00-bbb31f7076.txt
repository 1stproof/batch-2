\begin{center}
{\bf Partial proof and current reduction for the rectangle estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes.  A map
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\) if
\(f_*[R,\partial R]=[S,\partial S]\) in relative homology.  I denote the
universal constant in the problem by \(\kappa\), to avoid confusing it with
\(k\)-dilation.

This draft proves the standard first alternative and reduces the remaining
case to one explicit averaged filling estimate, stated in
Proposition \ref{prop:conditional}.  That averaged estimate is the only
remaining gap in this draft.

\section*{Standard tools}

We use integral Lipschitz chains, or equivalently integral currents after the
usual approximation.  Slicing, push-forward, and coarea are used in the
Federer--Fleming sense; piecewise smooth maps are handled by applying the
estimates on each smooth piece and then passing to the associated currents.
Guth's estimates are stated for open subsets of rectangles; the closed
rectangle version used below follows by applying them to
\(\epsilon\)-interiors and then letting \(\epsilon\downarrow0\).

If \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), then \(\Dil_j(f)\le1\) for \(j=3,4\).  Indeed, if
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) are the singular values of \(df\), then
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), hence \(\sigma_i\le1\) for \(i\ge2\), and
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le1\) for \(j\ge2\).

We shall use two estimates of Guth.  The first is the small-volume
\(k=2,n=4\) part of the rectangular relative isoperimetric profile theorem
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding}.

\begin{theorem}[Guth's relative isoperimetric estimate]\label{thm:iso}
There are dimensional constants \(c_I,C_I>0\) such that if \(Z\) is a relative
integral \(2\)-cycle in the \(4\)-rectangle \(R_1\le R_2\le R_3\le R_4\) and
\(A=\Mass Z\le c_I R_1R_2\), then
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C_I
        \max\left\{ A^{3/2},\,\frac{A^2}{R_1}\right\}.
\]
In particular, \(\Fill_R(Z)\le C_I R_1R_2^2\) in this range.
\end{theorem}

The second is Guth's degree estimate \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}, applied
with \(j=1,k=2,l=4\).

\begin{theorem}[Guth's degree estimate]\label{thm:guth-degree}
There is a dimensional constant \(c_G>0\) such that every degree-one map of
pairs \(f:R\to S\) with \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) satisfies
\[
        R_1^3R_2R_3R_4\ge c_G S_1^3S_2S_3S_4 .              \tag{1}
\]
\end{theorem}

\section*{The first alternative}

\begin{lemma}[target filling lower bound]\label{lem:target-fill}
Let
\[
 P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\},
\]
where \(y_3\in[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\) and
\(y_4\in[S_4/3,2S_4/3]\).  Every relative \(3\)-chain \(Y\) in \(S\) with
\(\partial Y=P_y\) modulo \(\partial S\) obeys
\[
        \Mass Y\ge c\,S_1S_2S_3 .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle
which is zero on the boundary and satisfies
\(\psi(y_3,y_4)\ge cS_3\).  Replacing \(\psi\) by smooth functions with the
same boundary values, Lipschitz constant \(1+o(1)\), and value
\(\ge cS_3-o(1)\) at \(y\), it suffices to use Stokes' theorem for
\[
        \omega=dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge d\psi .
\]
The comass of \(\omega\) is at most \(1+o(1)\).  Moreover,
\[
 \int_Y\omega=\int_{\partial Y}\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2 .
\]
The part of \(\partial Y\) in \(\partial S\) contributes zero: on the \(x_1\)-
and \(x_2\)-faces \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\), while on the \(x_3\)- and
\(x_4\)-faces \(\psi=0\).  Letting the smoothing error tend to zero gives
\[
        \Mass Y\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[first alternative]\label{prop:first}
There are constants \(c_1,C_1>0\) such that if
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\), \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), and
\[
        C_1R_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3 ,
\]
then
\[
        R_3R_4\ge c_1S_3S_4 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(F=(x_3,x_4)\circ f\), and set
\[
Q=[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\times[S_4/3,2S_4/3].
\]
For a.e. \(y=(y_3,y_4)\in Q\), let
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\).  Naturality of slicing gives
\[
        f_\# Z_y
        =\langle [S,\partial S],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle=P_y
\]
as relative currents, up to the harmless orientation sign.  Hence any relative
filling of \(Z_y\) in \(R\) pushes forward to a relative filling of \(P_y\) in
\(S\).  Since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), Lemma \ref{lem:target-fill} implies
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .                         \tag{2}
\]
Choose \(C_1>C_I/c\).  If \(C_1R_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3\), then Theorem
\ref{thm:iso} and (2) force \(\Mass Z_y\ge c_I R_1R_2\) for a.e. \(y\in Q\).
By coarea and \(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\),
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge\int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
      \ge c R_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Since \(\Vol(R)=R_1R_2R_3R_4\), the conclusion follows.
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences of the same slicing}

The proof also gives a fiberwise profile lower bound.  For the same central
fibers,
\[
 \Mass Z_y\ge c\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\},                      \tag{3}
\]
for a.e. \(y\in Q\).  Indeed, if \(A=\Mass Z_y<c_I R_1R_2\), then (2) and
Theorem \ref{thm:iso} give
\[
 cS_1S_2S_3\le C_I\max\{A^{3/2},A^2/R_1\}.
\]
Integrating (3) over \(Q\) yields
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge cS_3S_4\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\}.                      \tag{4}
\]

The monomial degree estimate covers the mildly anisotropic range.  From (1) and
\(R_1\le\kappa S_1\),
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c_G\kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .              \tag{5}
\]
Thus (5) implies
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge \kappa S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
whenever \(S_3/S_2\le c\,\kappa^{-6}\).  The remaining range has
\(S_3/S_2\gg_\kappa1\).  Also, after choosing \(\kappa<c_1\), failure of the
first desired alternative and Proposition \ref{prop:first} imply
\[
        R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3 .                          \tag{6}
\]

\section*{Weighted coarea and the remaining averaged estimate}

The following estimate records a genuine family-level consequence of the mixed
two-Jacobian bounds and fixes the critical-set issue in the previous draft.

\begin{lemma}[weighted coarea]\label{lem:weighted}
Let \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), let \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), and set
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\).  At rank-two points of \(F\), put
\[
 \lambda(x)=\left\|dG|_{\ker dF_x}\right\|,
\]
and put \(\lambda=0\) on the rank-deficient set.  Then
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge \int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda(x)^2\,d\mathcal H^2(x)\,dy .
                                                                    \tag{7}
\]
Moreover, for a.e. \(y\in Q\),
\[
        \int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\,d\mathcal H^2\ge S_1S_2 .        \tag{8}
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
At a point with \(\operatorname{rank}dF=2\), let \(T=\ker dF_x\) and
\(N=T^\perp\).  Choose a unit vector \(v\in T\) with
\(|dG(v)|=\lambda\).  For every unit \(n\in N\),
\[
df(v)=(dG(v),0),\qquad df(n)=(dG(n),dF(n)),
\]
so
\[
        |df(v)\wedge df(n)|\ge \lambda |dF(n)|.
\]
Since \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), \(\|dF|_N\|\le\lambda^{-1}\), and therefore
\(J_2F(x)=|\det(dF|_N)|\le\lambda^{-2}\).  Equivalently
\(\lambda^2J_2F\le1\); the same inequality is trivial on the rank-deficient
set because \(J_2F=0\).  Coarea gives
\[
 \int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\,d\mathcal H^2\,dy
   =\int_{F^{-1}(Q)}\lambda^2J_2F\,dx\le \Vol(R).
\]
For a.e. central \(y\), slicing naturality gives \(f_\#Z_y=P_y\).  Projecting
\(P_y\) to the \((x_1,x_2)\)-rectangle shows that \(G|_{Z_y}\) has relative
degree \(1\).  Hence
\[
 \int_{Z_y}|df_1\wedge df_2|\ge S_1S_2.
\]
Since the square of the operator norm of \(dG|_{T Z_y}\) dominates the
absolute \(2\)-Jacobian of \(G|_{Z_y}\), (8) follows.
\end{proof}

Let
\[
        I=\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\,d\mathcal H^2\,dy,\qquad
        q=|Q|\sim S_3S_4,\qquad F_0=S_1S_2S_3 .
\]
Lemma \ref{lem:weighted} gives only \(I\gtrsim S_1S_2q\), hence only the basic
volume bound.  The following averaged filling estimate would close the proof.

\begin{proposition}[conditional averaged filling estimate]\label{prop:conditional}
Assume there is a dimensional constant \(C_A\) such that for every map as above,
\[
 \int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C_A\left(
       R_1 I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}
       +\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\,F_0q\right).                  \tag{9}
\]
Then the theorem in the problem follows.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By (2), the left hand side of (9) is at least \(cF_0q\).  If the first desired
alternative fails and \(\kappa<c/(2C_A)\), the last term on the right of (9)
can be absorbed, giving
\[
        F_0q\lesssim R_1 I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}.                 \tag{10}
\]
If, in addition,
\[
        I\le \eta\,q\,S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2},
\]
then, using \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(S_1\le S_2\le S_3\),
\[
 R_1I\le \eta\kappa\,F_0q,\qquad
 \frac{I^2}{S_1q}\le \eta^2\,F_0q .
\]
Choosing \(\eta\) and then \(\kappa\) sufficiently small contradicts (10).
Therefore
\[
        I\ge c\,q\,S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}.
\]
Finally (7) gives
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge I
   \ge c S_3S_4\,S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}
   =cS_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
\]
After decreasing \(\kappa\) below this dimensional constant, the second
desired alternative follows.
\end{proof}

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The proof is incomplete exactly because (9) has not been proved.

\begin{itemize}
\item Mathematically, the missing point is an averaged, parametric filling
inequality for the two-parameter family
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\).  Fiberwise isoperimetry alone
gives the weaker \(A^2/R_1\) branch in (3), leaving a factor
\((S_3/S_2)^{1/10}\) in the model below.
\item The gap appears in Proposition \ref{prop:conditional}; all preceding
estimates are standard current/coarea consequences of \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).
\item What has been tried: a boundary-trace filling inequality was ruled out by
central-square examples; the corrected weighted coarea lemma above gives only
\(\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4\); Guth's monomial estimate gives (5), which
handles only \(S_3/S_2\lesssim_\kappa1\).
\item What is needed: a proof of (9), probably by a Guth/Federer--Fleming
deformation applied to the whole family of fibers rather than to one fiber at a
time.  The term involving \(R_3R_4/S_3S_4\) is essential: without it the product
foliation \([0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\}\) in a long target rectangle is a
counterexample to any purely fiberwise bound.
\end{itemize}

A useful stress test is
\[
        S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad
        R=(L^{-1/5},L^{3/5},L,L),
\]
with the last two source sides reduced by a fixed small constant and \(R_2\)
multiplied by a compensating fixed constant if necessary to satisfy Guth's
monomial estimate.  It satisfies the known reductions at the exponent level,
whereas the desired volume bound is larger by a factor \(L^{1/10}\).  Thus the
missing input must use the whole ambient two-parameter family and the strict
smallness of \(R_3R_4/S_3S_4\).
