My instinct is that the literal pointwise current lemma is stronger than you need and probably the wrong object to prove directly. I would try to prove a **dual/cellular averaged version** instead.

A useful replacement target is: for every relative \(2\)-form/cochain \(\omega\) with
\(\|d\omega\|_\infty\le1\), \(\omega|_{\partial R}=0\),
\[
 \int_{Z_y}\omega
 \le C(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1)
   +C\int_{B_y\cap {\mathcal B}_z}|d\omega| . \tag{*}
\]
Taking the supremum over \(\omega\) gives the filling estimate, and the bad term is bounded by \(\Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\). This is essentially the dual of your \(P_z,H_z\) lemma, but it avoids sharp restriction seams: no \(\partial(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\) ever appears.

A rigorous route to \((*)\) might be:

1. **PL/cubicalize first.** Approximate \(f\) by a PL map affine on a fine cubulation. For fixed \(z\), define a *cellular bad carrier* using only the coordinate two-plaques that actually occur in a prescribed Manhattan carrier. Do not thicken. On the good carrier, \(F\) avoids \(z\), so \(F\) factors into \(Q\setminus\{z\}\), hence has a cellular null-carrier after retracting to \(\partial Q\). The acyclic-carrier theorem should then give a cochain homotopy supported on the good complex. Bad cells are carried unchanged. Passing mesh \(\to0\) after averaging over \(z\) should recover exactly the plaque Fubini bound, because the relevant tracks are coordinate two-plaques, not stars or neighborhoods.

2. **Prove the energy part as a target-grid deformation theorem.** For a two-current \(T=Z_y\) equipped with \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), prove a weighted deformation estimate:
\[
\text{action of the good cochain homotopy on }T
 \lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1 .
\]
I would try this by a random grid in the \(G\)-rectangle. Coarea controls the expected mass of the preimage of grid lines by \(E_y\). Pushing cells to an \(f_1\)-skeleton costs \(R_1E_y\); the remaining one-dimensional seams in the \(G\)-plane fill with planar cost \(\lesssim E_y^2/S_1\). Crucially, this is a deformation of the **degree-carrying \(G\)-cochain**, not a physical sweep of all of \(Z_y\); zero-\(G\)-energy flaps are then invisible except through the chosen filling \(B_y\) and the bad carrier.

3. **Use penalized fillings if arbitrary \(B_y\) is too rigid.** Instead of demanding the comparison for every filling, fix \(z\) and choose \(B_{y,z}\) minimizing
\[
 \Phi_{y,z}(B)=\Mass B+\Lambda\,\Mass(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z).
\]
Averaging in \(z\), the plaque estimate gives
\[
\frac1q\int_Q \Phi_{y,z}(B^0_y)\,dz
 \le (1+C\Lambda R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\Mass B^0_y
\]
for any near-minimizer \(B^0_y\). Thus in the hard regime, penalized minimizers remain mass-near-minimizing and have controlled average bad mass. This may absorb seam/interface errors that defeat the sharp restriction formulation.

So my recommendation: reformulate the missing lemma as a **dual acyclic-carrier/cochain homotopy plus weighted \(G\)-grid deformation**. I am uncertain whether the \(R_1E+E^2/S_1\) estimate closes exactly, but this seems more robust than constructing literal \(P_z,H_z\) on arbitrary currents. The cellular acyclic-carrier setup is also close to Guth/Almgren-Pitts sweepout technology and may match the unpublished Guth hard-regime argument if it exists.
