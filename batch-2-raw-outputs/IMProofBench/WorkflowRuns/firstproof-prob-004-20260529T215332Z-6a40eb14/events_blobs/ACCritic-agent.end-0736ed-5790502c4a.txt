## Paragraph-by-paragraph audit

### Title and interpretation

The new title, “Partial proof and reduction…,” is accurate. The interpretation of rectangles as oriented Euclidean products and degree one as relative homology degree is reasonable and faithful to the problem statement.

However, because the document announces itself as partial, it cannot be a complete solution to the problem.

### “Two standard inputs”

The singular-value argument is correct: if \(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), then \(\sigma_2\le1\), hence \(\sigma_3,\sigma_4\le1\), so all higher exterior products have norm at most \(1\).

The specialization of Guth’s Estimate 1 to \(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=4\) is also correct. From
\[
1\ge c\,Q_1(Q_2Q_3Q_4)^{1/3}
\]
one obtains
\[
R_1^2\Vol(R)=R_1^3R_2R_3R_4
\gtrsim S_1^3S_2S_3S_4.
\]

The quoted isoperimetric profile implication
\[
\Mass Z<c_1R_1R_2\implies \Fill_R(Z)\le C_1R_1R_2^2
\]
is consistent with Guth’s rectangle isoperimetric profile. This is acceptable as a standard input, though a fully self-contained proof would need to state the precise profile theorem and show the specialization.

### Lemma: filling a central target plane

The calibration argument is essentially correct. A \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) vanishing on the boundary of the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle and satisfying \(\psi\gtrsim S_3\) on the middle half exists because \(S_3\le S_4\).

Minor technical point: the proof suppresses orientation signs in Stokes’ theorem. The conclusion should be written using absolute values:
\[
\Mass Y\ge \left|\int_Y d\beta\right|
=\left|\int_{\partial Y}\beta\right|.
\]
This is a minor presentation issue, not a fatal mathematical error.

### Proposition: first alternative

The proof is plausible and mostly correct, assuming the standard current-slicing framework.

The key steps are valid:

- For a.e. \(y\), the slice
  \[
  Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle
  \]
  is a relative \(2\)-cycle.
- Slicing naturality gives \(f_\#Z_y=P_y\) as a relative current.
- Since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), any filling of \(Z_y\) pushes forward to a filling of \(P_y\) with no larger mass.
- The central-plane filling lemma then gives
  \[
  \Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3.
  \]
- Guth’s small-cycle isoperimetric estimate forces
  \[
  \Mass Z_y\gtrsim R_1R_2.
  \]
- Coarea and \(J_2(f_3,f_4)\le1\) give
  \[
  \Vol(R)\ge \int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
  \gtrsim R_1R_2S_3S_4.
  \]

Technical gaps remain if this were intended as a fully self-contained proof: the draft does not spell out the current-theoretic slicing/naturality theorem for piecewise smooth maps of manifolds with corners. This is standard, but it is still being invoked as a black box.

The constant bookkeeping is slightly loose: the same symbol \(c_2\) is used for both the hypothesis threshold and the final lower bound. This can likely be repaired by shrinking \(c_2\), but it should be stated more carefully in a final proof.

### “What this proves toward the stated theorem”

This section derives a legitimate partial lower bound, assuming the preceding proposition and the full rectangle isoperimetric profile.

The lower bound
\[
\Mass Z_y\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,\ (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\
(R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\}
\]
is reasonable as the inversion of the relevant filling-profile estimates, but the derivation is not shown. For a complete proof, this should be expanded.

The algebra from there is correct. From
\[
R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3,\qquad R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1,
\]
one obtains
\[
\Mass Z_y\gtrsim \alpha^{1/2}S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}.
\]
Integrating over \(Q\) gives
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim
\alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]
Combining with Guth’s estimate
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim \alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4
\]
and optimizing in \(\alpha\) indeed gives
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4.
\]

This is correctly identified as weaker than the required bound by the factor
\[
(S_2/S_3)^{1/10}.
\]

### “Remaining open issues”

This section is the main reason the answer cannot be accepted as a solution. The author explicitly states:

> “The proof above does not yet establish the stated theorem.”

The proposed averaged estimate
\[
 \int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
\]
is not proved. It is essential: without it, the draft only gives the weaker exponent \(S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}\), not the required \(S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}\).

The weighted coarea claim \(I\le \Vol(R)\) is plausible and standard, but it is not enough to close the theorem. The missing averaged tightening estimate is a genuine mathematical gap, not a minor detail.
