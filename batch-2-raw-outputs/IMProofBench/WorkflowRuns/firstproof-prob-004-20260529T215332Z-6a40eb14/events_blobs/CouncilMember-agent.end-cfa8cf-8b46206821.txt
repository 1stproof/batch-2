**The averaged form is exactly equivalent to the volume bound.** By coarea,
\[\mathrm{Vol}(R)=\int_0^{S_1}\!\!\int_{M_t}|\nabla u|^{-1}\,d\mathcal H^3\,dt,\]
so averaging your conjectured inequality over central $t$ recovers exactly $\mathrm{Vol}(R)\gtrsim T$. The $f_1$-slicing route does not decouple anything at the averaged level — it **is** the main theorem.

The per-slice form is strictly stronger. Three structural obstructions to extracting it:

**(1) Wrong exponent from 3D Guth.** \cite{GuthWidth} Thm 2 gives $b_1(b_2b_3)^{1/2}\gtrsim a_1(a_2a_3)^{1/2}$ for 2-contracting degree-one maps between 3-rectangles. With $|\nabla u|^{-1}$ playing the role of $\Dil_1$ and "effective widths" of $M_t$ as $b_i$, this yields a weighted bound of shape $S_2(S_3S_4)^{1/2}$, not $S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4$. The gap is exactly the $(S_3/S_2)^{1/2}$ obstruction you face in 4D.

**(2) Diagonal models give zero slack.** If $f_i$ depends primarily on $x_i$, then $|\nabla u|^{-1}\equiv R_1/S_1$, so $\int_{M_t}|\nabla u|^{-1}\,d\mathcal H^3=\mathrm{Vol}(R)/S_1$ — the per-slice bound is *literally* the volume bound.

**(3) Identity test.** With $M_t=S'$, $g|_{M_t}=\mathrm{id}$, $|\nabla u|\equiv c$, the bound forces $c\lesssim(S_2/S_3)^{1/2}$. In the hard regime $|\nabla u|$ averages to $\gtrsim\kappa^{-1}$, so any per-slice bound must come from *variance* of $|\nabla u|$ on $M_t$, not its size.

**The one route I would actually try.** The genuine new content of $f_1$-slicing is that *fillings can be required to stay inside $M_t$*. Your round-11 obstruction (the target $x_2$-segment fills through the short-$x_1$ face with area $O(S_1S_2)$) used filling in all of $S$. Restrict to filling inside the target 3-slice $\{x_1=t\}\subset S$: the 1-form $\eta=\psi(x_3,x_4)dx_2$, with $\psi$ vanishing on the $(x_3,x_4)$-boundary and $\psi(y_3,y_4)\gtrsim S_3$, calibrates any 2-chain in $\{x_1=t\}_S$ bounding the segment to have area $\gtrsim S_2 S_3$.

Pulling back via $g|_{M_t}$ (image in $\{x_1=t\}_S$, $\Dil_2(g|_{M_t})\le1$): every 2-chain $Y'\subset M_t$ filling $\gamma_{t,y}=\langle M_t,(f_3,f_4),y\rangle$ has $\mathrm{Mass}(Y')\gtrsim S_2 S_3$. Combine with a 3D Federer–Fleming **inside $M_t$** (filling $\lesssim\min(L^2, b\, L)$ for the appropriate thin direction $b$): the $L^2$ branch gives $L\gtrsim\sqrt{S_2 S_3}$. Triple coarea via $(f_1,f_3,f_4)$ then gives
\[\mathrm{Vol}(R)\ge\int_R|J_3|\,dx=\iiint \mathcal H^1(\gamma_{t,y})\,dy\,dt\gtrsim S_1S_3S_4\cdot\sqrt{S_2 S_3}=T.\]

The catch: the FF must operate **inside $M_t$**, not in $R$ — otherwise fillings escape and you reproduce the round-11 obstruction. The hard-regime hypothesis $R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4$ bounds the $(x_3,x_4)$-projection area of $M_t$ and should be the natural input for a relative 3D isoperimetric inequality intrinsic to $M_t$, but the relative-to-$\partial M_t$ statement (with constants surviving the hard-regime balance) is the nontrivial step. Uncertain whether this version of FF is already in the literature or needs to be proved by hand using the structure $M_t=f_1^{-1}(t)$ in $R$.

This is the only route I see where $f_1$-slicing genuinely uses the level-set constraint to block the short-$x_1$ escape rather than restating the volume bound. It is *not* a strict improvement over your LE-HAF program — it's a parallel approach with the same hard ingredient (a custom relative isoperimetric inequality) but in 3D instead of via parametric tightening.
