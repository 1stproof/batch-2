\begin{center}
{\bf Partial proof and trace reduction for the rectangle estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes.  A map
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\) if
\(f_*[R,\partial R]=[S,\partial S]\) in relative homology.  I denote the
universal constant in the problem by \(\kappa\), to avoid confusing it with
\(k\)-dilation.

The argument below proves the standard first alternative and adds a
boundary-trace reduction for the remaining case.  Thus the document is still
not yet a complete proof of the stated problem.

\section*{Standard tools}

We work with integral Lipschitz chains, equivalently with integral currents
after the usual approximation.  Masses are counted with multiplicity.  Slicing,
push-forward, and coarea are used in the standard Federer--Fleming sense; for a
piecewise smooth map the dilation bounds hold on each smooth piece and hence
almost everywhere.

If \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), then \(\Dil_j(f)\le1\) for \(j=3,4\).  Indeed, if
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) are the singular values of \(df\) at a smooth
point, then \(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), so \(\sigma_i\le1\) for \(i\ge2\), and
therefore \(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le1\) for every \(j\ge2\).

We shall use two estimates of Guth.  The first is the \(k=2,n=4\) small-volume
part of the rectangular relative isoperimetric profile theorem
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding}.

\begin{theorem}[Guth's relative isoperimetric estimate]\label{thm:iso}
There are dimensional constants \(c_I,C_I>0\) such that if \(Z\) is a relative
integral \(2\)-cycle in the \(4\)-rectangle \(R_1\le R_2\le R_3\le R_4\) and
\(A=\Mass Z\le c_I R_1R_2\), then
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C_I
        \max\left\{ A^{3/2},\,\frac{A^2}{R_1}\right\}.
\]
In particular, \(\Fill_R(Z)\le C_I R_1R_2^2\) in this range.
\end{theorem}

The second is Guth's degree estimate \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}, applied
with \(j=1,k=2,l=4\).

\begin{theorem}[Guth's degree estimate]\label{thm:guth-degree}
There is a dimensional constant \(c_G>0\) such that every degree-one map of
pairs \(f:R\to S\) with \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) satisfies
\[
        R_1^3R_2R_3R_4\ge c_G S_1^3S_2S_3S_4 .              \tag{1}
\]
\end{theorem}

\section*{The first alternative}

\begin{lemma}[target filling lower bound]\label{lem:target-fill}
Let
\[
 P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\},
\]
where \(y_3\in[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\) and
\(y_4\in[S_4/3,2S_4/3]\).  Every relative \(3\)-chain \(Y\) in \(S\) with
\(\partial Y=P_y\) modulo \(\partial S\) obeys
\[
        \Mass Y\ge c\,S_1S_2S_3 .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle such
that \(\psi=0\) on its boundary and \(\psi(y_3,y_4)\ge cS_3\); for instance use
a fixed small multiple of the distance to the boundary.  Approximate \(\psi\)
from within by smooth functions, or use Stokes for Lipschitz forms by
approximation.  The \(3\)-form
\[
        \omega=dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge d\psi
\]
has comass at most \(1\).  Stokes' theorem gives
\[
 \int_Y\omega=\int_{\partial Y}\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2 .
\]
The portion of \(\partial Y\) lying in \(\partial S\) contributes nothing: on
the \(x_1\)- and \(x_2\)-faces \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\), while on the \(x_3\)- and
\(x_4\)-faces \(\psi=0\).  Hence
\[
        \Mass Y\ge \left|\int_Y\omega\right|
        = \psi(y_3,y_4) S_1S_2 \ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[first alternative]\label{prop:first}
There are constants \(c_1,C_1>0\) such that if
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\), \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), and
\[
        C_1R_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3 ,
\]
then
\[
        R_3R_4\ge c_1S_3S_4 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(F=(x_3,x_4)\circ f\).  For almost every regular
\(y=(y_3,y_4)\) in
\[
Q=[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\times[S_4/3,2S_4/3],
\]
let \(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\) be the relative slice.  Naturality
of slicing and \(f_\#[R,\partial R]=[S,\partial S]\) give
\[
        f_\# Z_y=\langle [S,\partial S],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle=P_y
\]
as relative currents.  Thus, if \(W\) fills \(Z_y\) in \(R\), then \(f_\#W\)
fills \(P_y\) in \(S\).  Since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), Lemma
\ref{lem:target-fill} implies
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .                       \tag{2}
\]

Choose \(C_1>C_I/c\).  Under the hypothesis,
\(C_IR_1R_2^2\le (C_I/C_1)S_1S_2S_3<cS_1S_2S_3\).  Therefore Theorem
\ref{thm:iso} and (2) force
\[
        \Mass Z_y\ge c_I R_1R_2
\]
for almost every \(y\in Q\).  By the coarea formula and
\(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\),
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge \int_Q \Mass Z_y\,dy
      \ge c R_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Since \(\Vol(R)=R_1R_2R_3R_4\), the proposition follows.
\end{proof}

\section*{Sharper information from the same slicing}

The same proof gives a useful profile bound for the central slices.

\begin{proposition}[profile consequence]\label{prop:profile}
Assume \(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\) and
\(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).  For \(Z_y=f^{-1}(P_y)\) as above and for almost every
\(y\in Q\),
\[
 \Mass Z_y\ge c\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\}.
\]
Consequently
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge cS_3S_4\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\}.                 \tag{3}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(A=\Mass Z_y\).  Equation (2) gives
\(\Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3\).  If \(A\ge c_IR_1R_2\), this gives the first
term.  If \(A<c_IR_1R_2\), Theorem \ref{thm:iso} gives
\[
        cS_1S_2S_3\le C_I\max\{A^{3/2},A^2/R_1\},
\]
which gives one of the other two terms.  Integrating over \(Q\) and using coarea
gives (3).
\end{proof}

The monomial degree estimate also handles the mildly anisotropic range.  From
(1) and \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\),
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c_G\kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .           \tag{4}
\]
Hence (4) implies the desired volume alternative
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge \kappa S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
whenever \(S_3/S_2\le c\,\kappa^{-6}\).

\section*{A boundary-trace refinement}

The following elementary estimate records the extra information carried by the
boundary of a relative fiber.  It is not yet strong enough, by itself, to finish
the theorem, but it is the main additional reduction obtained in this round.

\begin{lemma}[filling with one boundary trace]\label{lem:trace-fill}
Let \(Z\) be a relative integral \(2\)-cycle in \(R\), let
\(A=\Mass Z\), and let \(L_i\) denote the mass of the part of \(\partial Z\)
lying in the two faces \(x_i=0,R_i\) (modulo the edges of those faces).  Then
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C\bigl(R_iA+A\,L_i\bigr)              \tag{5}
\]
for each \(i=1,\ldots,4\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
It is enough to prove two standard upper bounds and take their minimum.  First,
doubling \(R\) across its boundary and using the Euclidean isoperimetric
inequality gives
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C A^{3/2}\le C\bigl(R_iA+A^2/R_i\bigr).       \tag{6}
\]
Second, fill the trace \(\Gamma\) of \(\partial Z\) on the two \(x_i\)-faces
inside those faces by relative \(2\)-chains of area at most \(CL_i^2\), using
the two-dimensional Federer--Fleming inequality in the faces.  After adding
these face chains, which are zero in the relative chain group, the new
representative \(Z'\) has no boundary on the two \(x_i\)-faces and
\(\Mass Z'\le A+CL_i^2\).  Linearly push \(Z'\) to the face \(x_i=0\).
Because the remaining boundary of \(Z'\) lies in the other faces, its track stays
in \(\partial R\).  The track has mass at most \(CR_i(A+L_i^2)\), so
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C\bigl(R_iA+R_iL_i^2\bigr).                  \tag{7}
\]
Taking the better of (6) and (7), and using
\(\min\{A^2/R_i,R_iL_i^2\}\le A L_i\), proves (5).
\end{proof}

Apply this to the central fibers \(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\)
from Proposition \ref{prop:first}.  Let \(A_y=\Mass Z_y\) and let \(L_{i,y}\) be
the trace of \(\partial Z_y\) on the two \(x_i\)-faces of \(R\).  Lemma
\ref{lem:target-fill}, slicing naturality, and \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\) give
\(\Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cF\), where
\[
        F=S_1S_2S_3 .
\]
Therefore Lemma \ref{lem:trace-fill} implies, for every \(i\) and a.e.
\(y\in Q\),
\[
        A_y(R_i+L_{i,y})\ge cF .                                  \tag{8}
\]
On the other hand, coarea on the two \(x_i\)-faces and the bound
\(J_2(f_3,f_4)\le1\) give
\[
        \int_Q L_{i,y}\,dy\le C\,\frac{\Vol(R)}{R_i}.               \tag{9}
\]
Indeed the left hand side is the integral, over the central target rectangle
\(Q\), of the lengths of the slices of the two \(x_i\)-faces by
\((f_3,f_4)\).

Combining (8), (9), and Jensen's inequality yields the quantitative trace
constraint
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge
 c\,\frac{F\,S_3S_4}{R_i+\Vol(R)/(R_iS_3S_4)}
        \qquad (i=1,\ldots,4).                                    \tag{10}
\]
Equivalently,
\[
        R_i\Vol(R)+\frac{\Vol(R)^2}{R_iS_3S_4}
        \ge c\,S_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .                                  \tag{11}
\]
This family of inequalities is stronger than the single-fiber profile bound in
the regime where a small fiber filling would have to use long traces on source
faces.  What remains is to combine (11), the side ordering, and Guth's monomial
estimate in the extreme anisotropic range.

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The preceding results and the trace inequalities leave the highly anisotropic complementary case.
After taking \(\kappa<c_1\), the failure of the first desired alternative and
Proposition \ref{prop:first} imply
\[
        R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3,\qquad R_1\le\kappa S_1,
\]
and hence \(R_2^2\gtrsim \kappa^{-1}S_2S_3\).  The missing statement is the
family-level mixed estimate
\[
\boxed{\quad
 R_1\le\kappa S_1,\quad R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 \Vol(R)\ge cS_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
 \quad}
\]

A single-slice improvement cannot be the whole solution.  The model cycle
\([0,r]\times[0,A/r]\) can map with Jacobian at most \(1\) and degree \(1\) onto
\([0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\), while its filling volume is of order
\(S_1S_2S_3\) for \(A\sim(rS_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\).  This realizes the bad
\(A^2/R_1\) branch of the rectangular isoperimetric profile.  Thus the missing
argument must use that the cycles \(Z_y\) occur as a two-parameter family of
fibers of one ambient map \(f\), together with the mixed two-Jacobian bounds.
The known monomial estimates and the fiberwise filling estimate above do not
supply the extra factor \((S_3/S_2)^{1/2}\).
