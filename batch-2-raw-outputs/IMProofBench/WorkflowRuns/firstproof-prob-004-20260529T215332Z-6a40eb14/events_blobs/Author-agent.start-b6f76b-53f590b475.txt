I would not bet on (13c) in its present form. The cubical replacement fixes the seam problem, but I think the flap problem survives even for whole-cell bad sets.

Here is the countermodel I would use to test it. Work inside any hard-regime box, say with \(R_1\ll S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\), and put a tiny tube \(U\) in the interior. Let \(D\subset Q\) be a very small parameter disk. On \(U\), arrange \(F=(f_3,f_4)\) to be a \(1\)-Lipschitz submersion onto \(D\), and make \(G=(f_1,f_2)\) constant. For each \(y\in D\), the slice then contains an extra closed component \(W_y\) with \(G_\#W_y=0\) and
\[
E_y(W_y)=\int_{W_y}\|dG|_{\ker dF}\|^2=0.
\]
Take \(W_y\) to be the boundary of a calibrated thin 3-chain \(V_y\), with \(\Fill(W_y)\sim M\). By taking many disjoint copies or multiplicity, \(M\) can be made large while the tube occupies only volume \(\sim M|D|\). This is a null bubble: it does not affect degree, and it can be grafted into a map without changing the target slice current except by a component whose pushforward is zero.

Now choose \(z\in Q\setminus D\). For a cubulation fine relative to the tube, every prescribed Manhattan coordinate-plaque track in \(U\) has \(F\)-image contained in \(D\). Hence no cell of \(V_y\) is bad for such \(z\):
\[
\Mass(B_{y,\theta}\llcorner \mathcal B^{\rm cell}_{z,\theta})=0
\]
for the natural filling \(B_{y,\theta}\supset V_y\). But any competitor filling the full slice \(Z_y\) must also fill \(W_y\), so it has mass at least \(\Fill(W_y)\sim M\). The low-energy term sees none of this bubble. Thus, after adding the ordinary degree-carrying slice, one can choose \(M\) much larger than
\[
R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1
\]
without increasing the bad term for most punctures \(z\). Averaged in \(z\), the bad probability is only \(|D|/|Q|\), so the same obstruction persists at the family level if \(D\) is tiny.

This is not a counterexample to the rectangle theorem; it is a counterexample to the proposed cellular comparison as a statement about \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\). The issue is that \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\) includes null-homologous garbage invisible to \(G\)-energy and invisible to puncture badness. A Manhattan homotopy cannot turn a positive filling-volume bubble into cost \(0\) merely because \(dG=0\).

So I think the estimate must be modified. Two possible directions:

1. Replace \(Z_y\) by an “essential” representative carrying \(G_\#Z_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\), after quotienting/filling \(G\)-null bubbles separately.

2. Keep \(B_y\), but add a term charging good-cell null fillings, not only bad cells. That term would need a new averaging mechanism; it will not self-absorb by the plaque probability alone.

In short: cellularization removes seams, but not flaps. The desired \(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\) bound can only plausibly hold for the degree-carrying part of the slice, not for the raw current \(Z_y\) or its full filling volume.

I don't have a complete construction or a clean hard-regime countermodel, but the structural shape of the bound tells you a lot.

**The cost form is target-side.** $R_1 E_y + E_y^2/S_1$ is sharper than the source FF profile $E_y^{3/2}$ by exactly $\alpha^{1/2}$ where $\alpha = R_1/S_1 \ll 1$. The appearance of the target dimension $S_1$ in a source filling estimate is the key tell: no purely source-side push of $Z_y$ can give this — it has to factor through a target-side step that operates on $G_\# Z_y$ (whose uncancelled mass is $\int J_2 G \le E_y$) and is pulled back.

**Proposed framework.** Rather than constructing $H$ and $P$ separately, build the competitor as target-FF pulled back through a $G$-section, plus a flap correction:

(i) Cubulate the target plane $[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]$ at scale $\sqrt{E_y/S_1}$.

(ii) Choose a measurable section $\sigma_y$ of $G|_{Z_y}$ on the **good** set (cells whose all-coordinate plaques avoid $z$). The decisive claim: the cellular puncture criterion *is exactly* the criterion for unambiguous local sheet count of $G|_{Z_y}$. Plaques missing $z$ means no $G$-sheet-structure jump locally; the section exists cellularly.

(iii) The $R_1 E_y$ term arises as the source push of the *target-side* deformation of $G_\#Z_y$ in the $x_1$-direction, pulled back via $\sigma_y$; the cost is weighted by $J_2 G$ rather than ambient mass — this is exactly the mechanism that avoids the flap cost.

(iv) The $E_y^2/S_1$ term: residual 2D FF in $[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]$ of the target-null part. (The 2D FF profile in a rectangle of short side $S_1$ gives $A^2/S_1$.)

(v) Flap = where $\sigma_y$ jumps = where the puncture criterion fails. Cost: $\Mass(B_{y,\theta} \llcorner \mathcal{B}^{\rm cell}_{z,\theta})$.

**The genuine obstacle.** The real content of (13c) is showing that the $G$-section's branch locus is concentrated in the all-plaque puncture-bad set. The "if" direction is plausible (missing $z$ on every plaque → no sheet jump); the "enough overlap" direction is what makes the absorbing fraction $R_3R_4/(S_3S_4)$ correct. You are absolutely correct to be suspicious of the literal cellular tightening estimate. A purely spatial source-grid formulation must fail in the hard regime due to the exact "flap" issue you identified.

**The Hard-Regime Countermodel (The Flap Obstruction)**
Let $Z_y$ be a valid central slice carrying the correct degree. Append to it a massive "flap"—a highly folded 2-sheet in the source $R$ on which $G=(f_1,f_2)$ is constant. On this flap, $\lambda=0$, so its addition leaves the weighted energy $E_y = \int_{Z_y} \lambda^2$ completely unchanged. 

However, any physical coordinate-plaque homotopy $H_{z,\theta}$ defined on a *source* grid will blindly sweep this flap across a distance $\sim R_1$. This produces a 3-chain of mass $\gtrsim R_1 \Mass(\text{flap})$. Since $\Mass(\text{flap})$ can be arbitrarily large independently of $E_y$, the bound $\Mass H_{z,\theta}(Z_y) \lesssim R_1 E_y + E_y^2/S_1$ is geometrically impossible. Source-domain Federer–Fleming projections pay unweighted ordinary mass and cannot distinguish between the degree-carrying core and null-energy flaps.

**How to Modify the Construction (Thick-Thin Core Extraction)**
To achieve the $R_1 E_y + E_y^2/S_1$ scaling, you must decouple the low-energy bound from the raw slice $Z_y$ and instead leverage the target structure. Here is how I would approach it:

1. **Absorb Flaps via the Minimal Filling:** Do not project $Z_y$ directly. Because $B_y$ is an almost-minimizing filling, it already efficiently fills any massive flaps of $Z_y$ that bound small volumes. By using a flat-norm/isoperimetric truncation, you can extract an energy-dense "core" cycle $Z_y^*$ that is homologous to $Z_y$ within $B_y$, such that its ordinary mass satisfies $\Mass(Z_y^*) \lesssim E_y$. The 3-mass used to homotope $Z_y$ to $Z_y^*$ is bounded by a fraction of $\Mass B_y$, which feeds cleanly into your self-absorbing term $(R_3 R_4 / S_3 S_4)\Mass B_y$.

2. **Target-Level Slicing for the Core:** To get the $E_y^2/S_1$ term, apply Guth's profile strategy in the *target* variables to the core $Z_y^*$. Slice $Z_y^*$ by the target levels $f_1 = t$. For a generic $t$, the 1-cycle $C_t = Z_y^* \cap \{f_1 = t\}$ maps to the $S_2$ interval. Weighted coarea guarantees there are "good" levels where the ordinary length of $C_t$ is $\lesssim \Mass(Z_y^*)/S_1 \lesssim E_y/S_1$.

3. **The Hybrid Homotopy:** Construct your low-energy competitor by first pushing the core $Z_y^*$ along the short $x_1$-direction to these good $f_1$-levels. Because you are only moving the core, this costs $R_1 \Mass(Z_y^*) \lesssim R_1 E_y$. Then, cap these $f_1$-levels (which costs $\sim (\text{length})^2 \lesssim E_y^2/S_1$). 

**A Related Strategic Angle**
If resolving the boundary seams of this chain-level core extraction proves too unwieldy, I strongly suggest abandoning atomic chain comparisons and looking at Guth's cohomological arguments (e.g., in the *Width-Volume* or *Minimax* papers). Cup products and Steenrod squares are topologically invariant and automatically ignore null-energy flaps. A coordinate-specific DGA invariant that explicitly leverages the asymmetric $x_1$ shortness against the $x_2$ target span is highly likely to be the missing mechanism from the unlocated 2-dilation preprint.
