\begin{center}
{\bf Partial proof and the remaining averaged-tightening gap}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I denote the universal constant in the problem by \(\kappa\), to avoid
confusion with \(k\)-dilation.  Rectangles are oriented Euclidean products
\(\prod_i[0,R_i]\), and degree \(1\) means relative degree \(1\) on
\(H_4(\cdot,\partial\cdot;\mathbb Z)\).

The arguments below are written for smooth transverse maps.  This is harmless
for the proved reductions: a piecewise smooth pair map can be smoothed relative
to the boundary strata with \(C^1\)-control on strata, preserving the relative
degree and increasing \(\Dil_2\) by \(1+o(1)\); rescaling the target by the
corresponding fixed factor only changes universal constants.  Equivalently, the
same statements may be read in the language of integral currents and slicing.

This document gives a complete proof of the first-alternative mechanism and
isolates the precise remaining input needed for the hard case.  The previous
primal averaged estimate has been replaced by the quotient/dual formulation
below, because an ordinary source-side deformation need not preserve the
\(f\)-null boundary discrepancies of an essential filling.

\section*{Standard inputs}

All constants below are positive and universal.  If \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), then
\(\Dil_3(f)\le1\) and \(\Dil_4(f)\le1\): if
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) are the singular values of \(df\) and
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), then \(\sigma_2\le1\) and
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le\sigma_1\sigma_2\) for \(j\ge2\).

We also use Guth's map-of-pairs estimate \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}.
For \(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=4\) it gives
\begin{equation}\label{guth14}
        R_1^2\,\Vol(R)\ge c_0\,S_1^3S_2S_3S_4
\end{equation}
whenever \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).  Finally, Guth's relative isoperimetric profile
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding} implies that, if \(Z\) is a relative
\(2\)-cycle in \(R\) with \(A=\Mass Z\le c_*R_1R_2\), then
\[
 \Fill_R(Z)\le C_*\max\{A^{3/2},A^2/R_1\}.
\]
In particular,
\begin{equation}\label{profile-inverse}
\Fill_R(Z)\ge L\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\Mass Z\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,L^{2/3},(R_1L)^{1/2}\},
\end{equation}
and, after decreasing \(c_*\),
\begin{equation}\label{iso}
        \Mass Z<c_*R_1R_2\quad\Rightarrow\quad
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C R_1R_2^2 .
\end{equation}

\section*{The first alternative}

Let \(Q\) be the concentric subrectangle of
\([0,S_3]\times[0,S_4]\) with side lengths \(S_3/2,S_4/2\), and put
\[
P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\},\qquad y\in Q.
\]

\begin{lemma}[central target plane]\label{central}
Every relative \(3\)-chain in \(S\) whose boundary is \(P_y\), \(y\in Q\),
has mass at least \(cS_1S_2S_3\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle
which vanishes on its boundary and is \(\ge cS_3\) on \(Q\).  For
\(\beta=\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2\), the \(3\)-form \(d\beta\) has comass
\(\le1\).  If \(\partial Y=P_y\) relatively, then Stokes' theorem gives
\[
\Mass Y\ge\left|\int_Yd\beta\right|
 =\left|\int_{P_y}\beta\right|
 =S_1S_2\psi(y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
The relative boundary terms vanish because either \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\) on
the \(x_1,x_2\)-faces or \(\psi=0\) on the \(x_3,x_4\)-faces.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{firstalt}
There is \(c_2>0\) such that, under the hypotheses of the problem, if
\[
        R_1R_2^2\le c_2S_1S_2S_3,
\]
then \(R_3R_4\ge c_2S_3S_4\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(T=[[R]]\) be the fundamental relative current and set \(F=(f_3,f_4)\).
Since \(f\) has relative degree \(1\), \(f_\#T=[[S]]\) as a relative
\(4\)-current (by the constancy theorem in the interior; no nonzero
\(4\)-current is supported on \(\partial S\)).  For a.e. \(y\in Q\), slicing
naturality gives
\[
        f_\#\langle T,F,y\rangle
        =\langle [[S]],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle=\pm P_y .
\]
Write \(Z_y=\langle T,F,y\rangle\).  Any relative filling of \(Z_y\) in \(R\)
pushes forward, since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), to a filling of \(P_y\) in \(S\).
Lemma \ref{central} therefore gives
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
If \(c_2\) is small enough, \eqref{iso} forces
\(\Mass Z_y\ge c_*R_1R_2\).  Coarea and \(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\) yield
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge\int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
        \ge cR_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Cancelling \(R_1R_2\) proves \(R_3R_4\ge cS_3S_4\).
\end{proof}

\section*{What the single-slice argument proves}

Assume \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\) and that the first conclusion fails with a
small constant.  Proposition \ref{firstalt} gives
\begin{equation}\label{r12large}
        R_1R_2^2\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\end{equation}
For the same slices, Lemma \ref{central} and \eqref{profile-inverse}, with
\(L=cS_1S_2S_3\), imply
\[
\Mass Z_y\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,(S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},
                         (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\}.
\]
Using \eqref{r12large} and \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\), each term in the
minimum is at least \(c\alpha^{1/2}S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\).  Hence
\begin{equation}\label{slicevol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
\end{equation}
On the other hand, \eqref{guth14} gives
\begin{equation}\label{monovol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .
\end{equation}
Optimizing \eqref{slicevol} and \eqref{monovol} in \(\alpha\) yields the
unconditional bound
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge cS_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4 .
\]
This misses the desired \(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) only in the high-aspect
range \(S_3\gg S_2\).

\section*{The corrected hard-case target}

Let \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), and \(Z_y=\langle [[R]],F,y\rangle\).
Define
\[
E_y=\int \|dG|_{T Z_y}\|_{HS}^2\,d\|Z_y\|,\qquad
I=\int_QE_y\,dy,\qquad q=|Q|.
\]
The pointwise inequality
\(\|dG|_{\ker dF}\|_{HS}^2J_2F\le C\), which follows from
\(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), gives by coarea
\begin{equation}\label{Icoarea}
        I\le C\Vol(R).
\end{equation}
Let \(\Omega=\{y\in Q:E_y\le4I/q\}\), so \(|\Omega|\ge3q/4\).

For real normal currents, define the relaxed essential filling norm
\[
\EFill_f(y)=\inf\{\Mass C:\ C\in N_3(R,\partial R;\mathbb R),\
        f_\#(\partial C)=\pm P_y\}.
\]
By Lemma \ref{central}, \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), and duality for the mass norm,
\begin{equation}\label{EFilllower}
        \EFill_f(y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3
\end{equation}
for a.e. \(y\in Q\).  Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{AElower}
A_\Omega:=\int_\Omega\EFill_f(y)\,dy
        \ge cS_1S_2S_3\,q
        \ge cS_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .
\end{equation}

For \(z\in Q\), let \({\mathcal B}_z\) be the set of points \(x\in R\) such
that \(z\) lies in the \(F\)-image of at least one coordinate \(2\)-plaque
through \(x\).  Since each such plaque has source area at most \(R_3R_4\) and
\(J_2F\le1\), Fubini gives, for every normal \(3\)-current \(B\),
\begin{equation}\label{badfubini}
 \frac1q\int_Q\Mass(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
 \le C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\Mass B .
\end{equation}

The missing estimate should be formulated in the quotient by \(f\)-null
boundaries.  Equivalently, for measurable near-minimizers
\(\Mass C_y^0\le2\EFill_f(y)\), one needs
\begin{equation}\tag{QEAT}\label{QEAT}
 A_\Omega
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
 +\frac{C}{q}\int_Q\int_\Omega
       \Mass(C_y^0\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dy\,dz .
\end{equation}
The quotient formulation is essential: if \(W_y=\partial C_y^0\), then
\(f_\#W_y=f_\#Z_y\), but an ordinary cellular deformation of \(W_y\) and
\(Z_y\) need not preserve the null difference \(W_y-Z_y\).  In the quotient
complex \(N_\ast(R,\partial R;\mathbb R)/\ker f_\#\), this boundary mismatch
disappears.

Assuming \eqref{QEAT}, the theorem follows.  Indeed, \eqref{badfubini} and
\(\Mass C_y^0\le2\EFill_f(y)\) give
\[
A_\Omega\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
  +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}A_\Omega .
\]
If \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), choose \(\kappa\) so
small that the last term is absorbed.  If also
\(I\le\delta S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), then \(q\sim S_3S_4\) and
\(S_1\le S_2\le S_3\) imply
\[
R_1I\le C\kappa\delta S_1S_2S_3^2S_4,\qquad
\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\le C\delta^2S_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .
\]
For sufficiently small \(\delta,\kappa\) this contradicts \eqref{AElower}.
Thus \(I\ge cS_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), and \eqref{Icoarea} gives the
desired second alternative.

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The proof is not complete until \eqref{QEAT} is proved.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Mathematically, \eqref{QEAT} is an averaged quotient tightening theorem
for the two-parameter family \(Z_y\).  It must construct, or dualize against,
controlled representatives of the degree-carrying part of \(Z_y\), while
charging the uncontrolled part only to the exact-plaque bad sets
\({\mathcal B}_z\).

\item The gap occurs in the last section: the first alternative, the
single-slice estimates, the lower bound \eqref{AElower}, the bad-set Fubini
estimate, and the absorption algebra are proved above, but the quotient
tightening estimate itself is not.

\item What was tried: ordinary Federer--Fleming or acyclic-carrier
deformations of source chains fail to respect \(f\)-null boundary differences;
fiberwise ``essentialization'' of \(Z_y\) runs into seam and flap obstructions;
known Guth estimates give the monomial bound \eqref{guth14} and the restricted
first alternative, but not the high-aspect hard case.

\item What would close the proof is a quotient-equivariant cellular/cochain
homotopy, or an equivalent parametric isoperimetric theorem, proving
\eqref{QEAT}.  In a dual form, it should test only cochains descending to
\(N_\ast(R,\partial R)/\ker f_\#\), so that the discrepancy
\(\partial C_y^0-Z_y\) is invisible.
\end{enumerate}
