My strongest recommendation is to **downgrade HAF-avg to a low-energy/contradiction version first**. It looks both more plausible and still closes the theorem.

Let
\[
E(y)=\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2,\qquad I=\int_Q E(y)\,dy .
\]
If \(I\) is below the desired scale, Markov gives a large set
\[
\Omega=\{y:E(y)\le 4I/q\},\qquad |\Omega|\ge 3q/4
\]
(after adjusting constants). Since \(\Fill(Z_y)\gtrsim F_0:=S_1S_2S_3\), it is enough to prove, for such low-energy slices and after averaging in \(z\),
\[
\int_\Omega \Fill(Z_y)\,dy
\le C\int_\Omega\left(R_1E(y)+\frac{E(y)^2}{S_1}\right)dy
+\frac Cq\int_Q\int_\Omega \Mass(B_y\!\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz.
\tag{LE-HAF}
\]
On \(\Omega\),
\[
\int_\Omega E(y)^2dy\le (4I/q)I,
\]
so LE-HAF gives exactly the desired \(R_1I+I^2/(S_1q)\) bound. The all-plaque averaging also restricts to \(\Omega\):
\[
\frac1q\int_Q\int_\Omega \Mass(B_y\!\llcorner\mathcal B_z)
\lesssim \frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\int_\Omega \Mass B_y
\lesssim \kappa \int_\Omega \Fill(Z_y),
\]
so it still self-absorbs. This avoids having to prove the globally convex-looking \(I^2/q\) estimate directly.

On localization of Guth: I think the right target is a **masked Federer–Fleming/Guth deformation lemma**, not a literal pointwise cycle-space tightening. Fix \(z\). On \(R\setminus\mathcal B_z\), every coordinate two-plaque through a point avoids \(z\), so coordinate-by-coordinate deformation tracks can be composed with the punctured retraction
\[
\rho_z:Q\setminus\{z\}\to\partial Q.
\]
This is exactly why the all-coordinate plaque definition is the right one: FF deformation tracks sweep coordinate two-plaques, not just vertical \(x_3x_4\)-plaques. I would try to prove a polyhedral lemma first: after cubulating \(R\), deform the good part of \(B_y\) through good coordinate plaques; the undeformed remainder is \(B_y\cap\mathcal B_z\). The mass bookkeeping should be done only for low-energy slices, giving \(R_1E(y)+E(y)^2/S_1\). This is closer to Guth’s tightening plus Almgren’s relative cycle-space isomorphism than to an isolated filling inequality.

I am pessimistic about a purely differential-form/coarea proof. Closed-form identities using \(df_i\wedge df_j\) recover the degree/basic-volume bound, but the missing \((S_3/S_2)^{1/2}\) is a minimax/filling obstruction. Singular angular forms around \(z\), e.g. \(d\arg(F-z)\), might be useful as certificates inside the punctured deformation argument, but probably not as a standalone proof.

I do **not** know a hard-regime counterexample to all-plaque HAF. The earlier linear/permutation failures are precisely charged by all-coordinate plaques or violate \(R_1\ll S_1\). A genuine counterexample under both \(R_1/S_1\ll1\) and \(R_3R_4/S_3S_4\ll1\) would likely be close to a counterexample to the theorem itself.

So my proposed bypass is: prove LE-HAF or even its contradiction form, only for \(E(y)\le C I/q\), with an \(\varepsilon\)-neighborhood of \(\mathcal B_z\) if needed for transversality. This seems materially easier than HAF-avg and still gives the same final lower bound.

**Framing.** HAF-avg is a *parametric Federer–Fleming* statement on $\{Z_y\}_{y\in Q}$ viewed as the 4-current $\langle T,F,Q\rangle$ in $R\times Q$ (the graph of $F$). The two terms $R_1 I$ and $I^2/(S_1q)$ are the linear-push and Cauchy–Schwarz signatures of cubical FF; the bad-mass piece is the FF defect on cells where the puncture is hit.

**Q1 — Yes, the tightening should localize.** Sketch:

(a) Cubically discretize $Q$ at scale $\rho$ and $R$ at scale $r$.
(b) At each parameter 0-cell $v$, FF-project $Z_v$ to the $r$-skeleton. The natural weighted cost is $\int_{Z_v}\lambda^2$, since $\lambda^2 J_2 F\le1$ makes $\lambda^2$ the correct "non-degenerate area density."
(c) Glue across 1-cells $e=[v,v']$ using $T_e=\langle T,F,e\rangle$.
(d) Use the retract $\rho_z:Q\setminus\{z\}\to\partial Q$ on the subcomplex of cells whose plaque images avoid $z$. The pulled-back filling mass decomposes into:
  – $R_1$-push of the $r$-skeleton: $\sim R_1 I$;
  – $f_1$-coarea optimization on 1-skeleton seams: $\sim I^2/(S_1 q)$ via Cauchy–Schwarz; the $1/S_1$ appears because the *target* side of $f_1$ controls the optimization length, not the source;
  – cells whose plaque images hit $z$: charged to $\mathcal B_z$.

Closest precedent I know is the chain-tightening in **Guth**, *The width–volume inequality* (GAFA 2007), which builds a complex of cycles and tightens simultaneously; multi-parameter cubical infrastructure appears in **Liokumovich–Marques–Neves**, *Weyl law for the volume spectrum* (Ann. Math. 2018), §2–4. Neither does HAF-avg, but the machinery is there. (Uncertain whether either treats the punctured-parameter case explicitly.)

**Q2 — pure coarea/level-set.** Skeptical. The $1/S_1$ in the Cauchy–Schwarz term is intrinsically *target-side*; coarea on $f_1$ pays $|df_1|$, not $S_1$. Your Round 12 level-slice obstruction is the prototype: a single-fiber argument cannot prevent simultaneous use of the short $f_1$-direction across an $S_1$-thick family. The $(S_3/S_2)^{1/2}$ factor is *intrinsically parametric*. Single calibrations max out at $S_1S_2S_3S_4$.

**Q3 — counterexamples.** Under both $R_1\ll S_1$ and $R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4$, I don't see one. Two sanity checks:

– Compute both sides of HAF-avg on Guth's short-side-stretch and pinching constructions in the hard regime — $\lambda^2$ has clean closed form there, and HAF-avg should be tight.
– Cross-check that the all-coordinate plaque definition is essential: your Round 9 product example $R=(1,M,L,L)$ shows the vertical-only plaque version fails, while the all-coordinate version correctly absorbs that example. Confirm in your write-up that the bad-mass lemma genuinely uses all $\binom{4}{2}$ pairs.

**Q4 — weaker family-level estimates.** Two genuine weakenings:

(i) **Direct $I$-bound, bypassing $A$.** Prove $I\ge cqS_1(S_2 S_3)^{1/2}$ without going through filling identification. This decouples the weighted-energy lower bound from the FF tightening; you'd only need the coarea identity $I\le\Vol(R)$. A Plücker pointwise estimate combined with directional FF on a different cycle family (e.g., 1-cycles obtained from $(f_1,f_3,f_4)$-slicing, with $\lambda$ appearing as a weight) might do it.

(ii) **Sup–avg version.** Replace $A$ by $|Q|\cdot\sup_y\Fill_R(Z_y)$. Weaker, but turns the question into a minimax/width–volume statement, which is closer to Almgren–Pitts territory and may be more accessible: extremal slices are easier to identify than averaged behavior. The conclusion still closes the theorem after the absorbing argument.

Of these, **(i) is more promising** — it cleanly isolates "where does the parametric obstruction live?" from "how do we deform the family?", and the second is precisely where your previous rounds have been stuck. The directional inequality + an averaged Plücker identity may be enough; this also bypasses the measurable-selection bookkeeping for $B_y$.

Here is how I would approach the current wall. The struggle with `HAF-avg` and the failure of absolute-value directional integrals are symptoms of the same underlying obstruction: you are trying to force global overlap of stretchings using unsigned metric tools.

**1. Why `HAF-avg` is structurally problematic:**
Guth's complex-of-cycles tightening cannot be easily localized to weight the failure set by the *original* fillings $B_y$. Tightening is a non-linear deformation that pushes parameterized families of cycles onto the source skeleton. You cannot bound the bad mass of the tightened cycles using the intersection of the *initial* fillings with $\mathcal{B}_z$, because the deformation inherently displaces the currents. The failures of your random-puncture lemma (RP) reflect this fundamental incompatibility.

**2. The Plücker/Sign Obstruction (Cleaner diff-form argument?):**
You correctly observed that integrated absolute minors allow laminate/Young-measure models that distribute directional costs onto disjoint domain regions. Unsigned metric tools (like current mass and standard coarea) cannot rule this out. Differential forms force overlap specifically because the wedge product ($\int \omega_1 \wedge \dots \wedge \omega_4$) naturally encodes the sign-sensitive Plücker relations. To rule out the laminates and force compatibility, you must use intersection theory or cup products.

**3. The Face-Preservation Bifurcation (Counterexamples vs. Closing the Theorem):**
Your ability to close this theorem depends entirely on a subtle topological distinction: **does your degree-one map of pairs preserve the cubical face structure** ($f(\text{face}_i) \subset \text{face}_i$)?

A generic degree-one map of pairs $(R, \partial R) \to (S, \partial S)$ only requires $f(\partial R) \subset \partial S$. The boundary $\partial S$ is topologically just $S^3$, whose standard cohomology has no intermediate cup products. The rich boundary rational-homotopy inequalities (like $R_2 R_3^2 R_4 \gtrsim S_2 S_3^2 S_4$) that reliably rule out the "middle-side snake" rely strictly on the Differential Graded Algebra (DGA) of forms vanishing on specific faces (see Guth's thesis, pp. 128–130).

This leaves you with two actionable paths:

*   **Path A (If the map preserves faces):** Bypass `HAF-avg` entirely. You don't need a new parametric current tightening. Instead, use the Sullivan minimal model of the cubical boundary. By constructing differential forms that vanish on specific boundary faces, you can write down the exact cup-product invariants that reproduce the boundary area obstructions (yielding the missing boundary-constrained $(S_2S_3)^{1/2}$ factor). 
*   **Path B (If the map does *not* preserve faces):** The theorem is likely false, and `HAF-avg` has counterexamples even in the hard regime. Your stress-test scaling $S=(1,1,L,L)$, $R=(L^{-1/5}, L^{13/15}, L^{13/15}, L^{13/15})$ satisfies all of Guth's interior monomial bounds (published Estimate 1). The main obstacle to building a degree-one map for this scaling was the mixed 2-dilation of the middle-side snake. Without face-preservation, you can freely map the long faces of $R$ into the short faces of $S$ along the boundary, wrapping the boundary to absorb the mixed derivatives. I strongly suggest actively trying to construct this bounded-2-dilation counterexample.

**Summary:** I recommend abandoning `HAF-avg`. If your problem setup requires face-preservation, use boundary differential forms (DGA). If it does not, exploit the flexibility of $\partial S$ to build the counterexample.
