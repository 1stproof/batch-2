\begin{center}
{\bf Partial proof and current reduction for the rectangle estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes.  A map
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\) if
\(f_*[R,\partial R]=[S,\partial S]\) in relative homology.  I denote the
universal constant in the problem by \(\kappa\), to avoid confusing it with
\(k\)-dilation.

This draft proves the standard first alternative and records two valid
reductions for the remaining case.  The final mixed estimate in the highly
anisotropic range is still open in this draft.

\section*{Standard tools}

We use integral Lipschitz chains, or equivalently integral currents after the
usual approximation.  Slicing, push-forward, and coarea are used in the
Federer--Fleming sense; piecewise smooth maps are handled by applying the
estimates on each smooth piece and then passing to the associated currents.

If \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), then \(\Dil_j(f)\le1\) for \(j=3,4\).  Indeed, if
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) are the singular values of \(df\), then
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), hence \(\sigma_i\le1\) for \(i\ge2\), and
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le1\) for \(j\ge2\).

We shall use two estimates of Guth.  The first is the small-volume
\(k=2,n=4\) part of the rectangular relative isoperimetric profile theorem
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding}.

\begin{theorem}[Guth's relative isoperimetric estimate]\label{thm:iso}
There are dimensional constants \(c_I,C_I>0\) such that if \(Z\) is a relative
integral \(2\)-cycle in the \(4\)-rectangle \(R_1\le R_2\le R_3\le R_4\) and
\(A=\Mass Z\le c_I R_1R_2\), then
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C_I
        \max\left\{ A^{3/2},\,\frac{A^2}{R_1}\right\}.
\]
In particular, \(\Fill_R(Z)\le C_I R_1R_2^2\) in this range.
\end{theorem}

The second is Guth's degree estimate \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}, applied
with \(j=1,k=2,l=4\).

\begin{theorem}[Guth's degree estimate]\label{thm:guth-degree}
There is a dimensional constant \(c_G>0\) such that every degree-one map of
pairs \(f:R\to S\) with \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) satisfies
\[
        R_1^3R_2R_3R_4\ge c_G S_1^3S_2S_3S_4 .              \tag{1}
\]
\end{theorem}

\section*{The first alternative}

\begin{lemma}[target filling lower bound]\label{lem:target-fill}
Let
\[
 P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\},
\]
where \(y_3\in[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\) and
\(y_4\in[S_4/3,2S_4/3]\).  Every relative \(3\)-chain \(Y\) in \(S\) with
\(\partial Y=P_y\) modulo \(\partial S\) obeys
\[
        \Mass Y\ge c\,S_1S_2S_3 .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle
which is zero on the boundary and satisfies
\(\psi(y_3,y_4)\ge cS_3\).  A smooth approximation gives the same estimate, so
we may use Stokes' theorem for
\[
        \omega=dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge d\psi .
\]
The comass of \(\omega\) is at most \(1\).  Moreover,
\[
 \int_Y\omega=\int_{\partial Y}\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2 .
\]
The part of \(\partial Y\) in \(\partial S\) contributes zero: on the \(x_1\)-
and \(x_2\)-faces \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\), while on the \(x_3\)- and
\(x_4\)-faces \(\psi=0\).  Thus
\[
        \Mass Y\ge\left|\int_Y\omega\right|
        =\psi(y_3,y_4)S_1S_2\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[first alternative]\label{prop:first}
There are constants \(c_1,C_1>0\) such that if
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\), \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), and
\[
        C_1R_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3 ,
\]
then
\[
        R_3R_4\ge c_1S_3S_4 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(F=(x_3,x_4)\circ f\), and set
\[
Q=[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\times[S_4/3,2S_4/3].
\]
For a.e. \(y=(y_3,y_4)\in Q\), let
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\).  Naturality of slicing gives
\[
        f_\# Z_y
        =\langle [S,\partial S],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle=P_y
\]
as relative currents.  Hence any filling of \(Z_y\) in \(R\) pushes forward to
a filling of \(P_y\) in \(S\).  Since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), Lemma
\ref{lem:target-fill} implies
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .                         \tag{2}
\]
Choose \(C_1>C_I/c\).  If \(C_1R_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3\), then Theorem
\ref{thm:iso} and (2) force \(\Mass Z_y\ge c_I R_1R_2\) for a.e. \(y\in Q\).
By coarea and \(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\),
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge\int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
      \ge c R_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Since \(\Vol(R)=R_1R_2R_3R_4\), the conclusion follows.
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences of the same slicing}

The proof also gives a fiberwise profile lower bound.  For the same central
fibers,
\[
 \Mass Z_y\ge c\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\},                      \tag{3}
\]
for a.e. \(y\in Q\).  Indeed, if \(A=\Mass Z_y<c_I R_1R_2\), then (2) and
Theorem \ref{thm:iso} give
\[
 cS_1S_2S_3\le C_I\max\{A^{3/2},A^2/R_1\}.
\]
Integrating (3) over \(Q\) yields
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge cS_3S_4\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\}.                      \tag{4}
\]

The monomial degree estimate covers the mildly anisotropic range.  From (1) and
\(R_1\le\kappa S_1\),
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c_G\kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .              \tag{5}
\]
Thus (5) implies
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge \kappa S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
whenever \(S_3/S_2\le c\,\kappa^{-6}\).  The remaining range has
\(S_3/S_2\gg_\kappa1\).  Also, after choosing \(\kappa<c_1\), failure of the
first desired alternative and Proposition \ref{prop:first} imply
\[
        R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3 .                          \tag{6}
\]

\section*{A valid weighted coarea refinement}

The following estimate replaces the invalid one-trace filling lemma from the
previous draft.  It records a genuine family-level consequence of the mixed
two-Jacobian bounds, although it has not yet been combined with (6) to close the
proof.

\begin{lemma}[weighted coarea]\label{lem:weighted}
Let \(F=(f_3,f_4)\) and let \(Z_y=F^{-1}(y)\) be a smooth fiber at a regular
point.  Let
\[
 \lambda(x)=\left\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{T_xZ_y}\right\|
\]
be the operator norm of the tangent differential to the fiber.  Then
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda(x)^2\,d\mathcal H^2(x)\,dy .
                                                                    \tag{7}
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
At a smooth point with \(\operatorname{rank} dF=2\), choose a unit vector
\(v\in T_xZ_y\) such that \(|d(f_1,f_2)(v)|=\lambda\).  If \(n\) is a unit
normal vector to the fiber, then \(dF(v)=0\).  Hence
\[
 |df(v)\wedge df(n)|\ge \lambda |dF(n)|.
\]
Since \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), this gives \(|dF(n)|\le\lambda^{-1}\).  Thus the
normal \(2\)-Jacobian \(J_F(x)\) is at most \(C\lambda^{-2}\).  The coarea
formula in the form
\[
 \int_{F^{-1}(Q)}1\,dx
 =\int_Q\int_{Z_y}J_F(x)^{-1}\,d\mathcal H^2(x)\,dy
\]
therefore gives (7), after ignoring the null critical set and absorbing the
dimensional constant.
\end{proof}

For each central \(y\), the map
\((f_1,f_2):Z_y\to[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\) has degree \(1\) in the relative
sense, and \(|d f_1\wedge d f_2|\le1\).  Consequently
\[
 \int_{Z_y} |d f_1\wedge d f_2| \ge S_1S_2,\qquad
 \int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\,d\mathcal H^2\ge cS_1S_2 .              \tag{8}
\]
Together with Lemma \ref{lem:weighted}, this recovers the basic volume lower
bound \(\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4\).  Closing the theorem would require a
sharper lower bound for the weighted energy in (7), using also the filling lower
bound (2) and the fact that the fibers form one ambient family.

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The core missing implication is
\[
 R_1\le\kappa S_1,\qquad R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 \Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .                  \tag{9}
\]
The established reductions prove this when \(S_3/S_2\lesssim_\kappa1\) and prove
the first alternative when \(R_1R_2^2\lesssim S_1S_2S_3\).  The hard case is
therefore (6) together with \(S_3/S_2\gg_\kappa1\).

The independent boundary-trace strategy from the previous draft cannot be used:
an arbitrary relative \(2\)-cycle of small area in a long rectangle may have very
large filling volume even when its trace on a chosen coordinate face is short.  For
example, in
\([0,1]^2\times[0,L]^2\) the central square
\([0,1]^2\times\{(L/2,L/2)\}\) has area \(1\) and short trace on an
\(x_1\)-face, but the calibration of Lemma \ref{lem:target-fill} gives filling
volume \(\gtrsim L\).  Thus any successful argument must couple the whole
two-parameter family of fibers, as in Lemma \ref{lem:weighted}, rather than
estimate each face trace independently.

A useful stress test for any proposed algebraic closure is
\[
        S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad
        R=(L^{-1/5},L^{3/5},L,L),
\]
with the last two source sides reduced by a fixed small constant.  It saturates
the short-side-stretch scaling and the known monomial estimates up to constants,
while (9) would require an additional factor \(L^{1/10}\).  The missing input
should rule out this scaling when \(R_3R_4\) is a sufficiently small fixed
multiple of \(S_3S_4\).
