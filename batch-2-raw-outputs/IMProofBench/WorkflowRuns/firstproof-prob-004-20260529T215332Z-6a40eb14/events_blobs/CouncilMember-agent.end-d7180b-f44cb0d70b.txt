My opinion: the listed alternatives will not close the hard regime unless they are upgraded to a genuinely parametric/compatibility statement essentially like (13). There is a clean exponent obstruction to the scalar inequalities they seem to produce.

Use the residual model
\[
S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad
R=(L^{-1/5},L^{13/15},L^{13/15},L^{13/15}).
\]
Then
\[
R_1/S_1=L^{-1/5},\qquad R_3R_4/(S_3S_4)=L^{-4/15},
\]
but
\[
\Vol R=L^{12/5},\qquad
S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4=L^{5/2}.
\]
So it misses the desired bound by \(L^{-1/10}\).

For all target-coordinate-pair slicings, the strongest profile-type consequence I see is: if slicing by target coordinates \(a,b\), with complement \(c,d\), the target filling lower bound is
\[
F_{ab}\sim S_cS_d\min(S_a,S_b),
\]
and the rectangular source profile gives
\[
\Vol R\gtrsim S_aS_b\,
\min\{R_1R_2,\ F_{ab}^{2/3},\ (R_1F_{ab})^{1/2}\}.
\]
For the above model the exponents of these six lower bounds are
\[
\begin{array}{c|c}
(a,b)&\text{exponent}\\ \hline
(1,2)&2/3\\
(1,3),(1,4),(2,3),(2,4)&7/5\\
(3,4)&12/5 .
\end{array}
\]
Thus the best pair-slicing bound is exactly \(\Vol R\gtrsim L^{12/5}\), still below \(L^{5/2}\). Directional filling estimates for all pairs do not appear stronger at the exponent level: the integrated absolute minors can be paid for on disjoint “laminate” regions, so Plücker relations do not force the overlap needed to gain the missing \(L^{1/10}\).

The 3D \((n-1)\)-dilation/width-volume theorem has the same issue. Even granting the boundary-style estimate
\[
R_2R_3R_4\gtrsim S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
and its natural variants, the model satisfies it:
\[
R_2R_3R_4=L^{13/5}>L^{5/2}.
\]
Multiplying by \(R_1=L^{-1/5}\) only gives \(L^{12/5}\). To get the desired \(S_1\)-factor one would need to replace \(R_1\) by \(S_1\), and ordinary \(f_1\)-level slicing does not do that: coarea introduces uncontrolled \(|\nabla f_1|\), while target intervals can escape through the short \(S_1\)-direction. That missing replacement is exactly a parametric compatibility phenomenon.

Known rational-homotopy/DGA inequalities also do not rule out this model. For instance the usual Guth-type 4D inequalities give exponents such as
\[
R_2R_3^2R_4=L^{52/15}\ge L^3,\qquad
R_1R_2R_3^2R_4=L^{49/15}\ge L^3,
\]
and the relevant monomial \(j=1,k=2,l=4\) estimate is saturated:
\[
\Vol R\gtrsim (S_1/R_1)^2\,S_1S_2S_3S_4=L^{12/5}.
\]
So standard DGA/minimax invariants reproduce known monomial or boundary constraints, not the missing square-root gain.

Thus I would treat this exponent model as a formal obstruction to any proof using only scalar outputs from pair slicings, 3D width-volume on levels/boundaries, and known rational-homotopy invariants. A successful alternative would need to force overlap among the degree-carrying regions across the two-parameter family of slices; that is essentially the role of the low-energy averaged tightening estimate.
