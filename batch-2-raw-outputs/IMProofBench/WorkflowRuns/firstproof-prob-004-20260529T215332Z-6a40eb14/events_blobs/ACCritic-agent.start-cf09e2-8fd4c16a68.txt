## Section-by-section mathematical audit

### Problem statement and interpretation

The interpretation of degree via relative homology is reasonable. Renaming the problem’s small constant \(k\) to \(\kappa\) is harmless.

However, this section explicitly says the document is a “sharp reduction rather than a complete proof.” Under the stated grading instructions, this alone prevents answer-readiness.

The newly added partial bound
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4
\]
is later justified correctly from the available estimates, but it is only a partial replacement for the desired
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]

### Standard tools

The singular-value argument proving \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\Rightarrow \Dil_j(f)\le1\) for \(j=3,4\) is correct.

The PL approximation/transversality paragraph is improved from earlier versions by explicitly scaling the target by \((1+\varepsilon)^{-1/2}\), but it remains somewhat compressed. A fully rigorous final proof would still need precise citations or details for:

- relative PL approximation preserving the pair condition;
- preservation of relative degree;
- control of \(\Dil_2\) under approximation;
- passage to the limit using flat convergence of slices.

This is not the main fatal gap, but it would need cleanup in a complete solution.

The use of Guth’s profile theorem and Estimate 1 is plausible and consistent with the cited source.

### Central target filling lemma

This revision improves the proof by explicitly introducing
\[
\eta=\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2.
\]
The calibration/Stokes argument is now clear. The lower bound
\[
\Mass Y\ge cS_1S_2S_3
\]
is valid: since \(y_3,y_4\) are central and \(S_3\le S_4\), one can choose \(\psi\) vanishing on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-boundary and satisfying \(\psi(y)\gtrsim S_3\). Boundary contributions vanish for the reasons stated.

This addresses the previous minor concern about clarity in the calibration proof.

### First alternative proposition

The first-alternative proof remains essentially valid, modulo standard current-theoretic slicing facts.

The logic is:

1. Slice by \(F=(f_3,f_4)\).
2. Use degree one and slicing naturality to get \(f_\#Z_y=P_y\).
3. Push forward fillings of \(Z_y\) to fillings of \(P_y\), using \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\).
4. Apply the central target filling lemma.
5. Apply Guth’s small relative filling profile to force \(\Mass Z_y\gtrsim R_1R_2\).
6. Integrate over \(Q\) by coarea.

This proves the stated first-alternative proposition, assuming the quoted standard GMT tools.

### Consequences of slicing and new partial bound

The derivation of equation (4) from equations (1) and (3) is valid.

Equation (6) follows correctly from Guth’s Estimate 1:
\[
R_1^3R_2R_3R_4\ge cS_1^3S_2S_3S_4
\]
and \(R_1\le \kappa S_1\), giving
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim \kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4.
\]

The derivation of equation (8) is also correct. If the first desired alternative fails and \(\kappa<c_1\), then the contrapositive of the first-alternative proposition gives
\[
R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3.
\]
Thus
\[
R_1R_2\gtrsim (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}.
\]
Also
\[
(S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3}\ge (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}
\]
because \(R_1\le S_1\le S_2\le S_3\). Hence equation (8) follows.

The new partial bound (10a) is algebraically correct:
\[
(\alpha^{1/2}T)^{4/5}
(\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4)^{1/5}
=
S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4.
\]
This improves the exposition and gives a genuine partial result. But it still falls short by
\[
(S_3/S_2)^{1/10},
\]
which can be arbitrarily large.

Minor wording issue: the phrase “unconditional partial replacement” should be read as “unconditional relative to the missing estimate (13), under the theorem’s hypotheses and after failure of the first alternative.” Without \(R_1\le \kappa S_1\), the displayed derivation would not apply as stated.

### Weighted coarea

The weighted coarea argument is sound.

At a rank-two point of \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), if \(T=\ker dF_x\), \(N=T^\perp\), and \(v\in T\) realizes \(\lambda=\|dG|_T\|\), then for \(n\in N\),
\[
|df(v)\wedge df(n)|\ge \lambda |dF(n)|.
\]
Thus \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) implies \(\lambda^2J_2F\le1\), and coarea gives
\[
I\le \Vol(R).
\]

The lower bound
\[
E_y\ge S_1S_2
\]
also follows from \(G_\#Z_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\) and the area formula.

This section addresses previous concerns and appears correct.

### Self-absorbing plaque-puncture reduction

The measurable selection of near-minimizing fillings is still under-justified. The invocation of Kuratowski–Ryll-Nardzewski is plausible, but a final proof should specify the precise measurable multifunction, compactness/mass cutoff, and closed-graph conditions. This is a technical gap, though not the main fatal one.

The all-plaque bad-mass lemma is plausible and likely correct under the PL/transversality reduction. For fixed \(x\), the set of bad punctures lies in a union of finitely many images \(F(P_{ij}(x))\), and each has area \(\lesssim R_iR_j\le R_3R_4\). Integrating against \(d\|B_y\|\) gives the stated estimate.

The revision also correctly clarifies that estimate (13) must hold for the selected near-minimizing family \(B_y\), or for a special measurable near-minimizing choice. This addresses one previous logical ambiguity, but it does not prove (13).

### Estimate (13)

This remains the fatal gap.

The proof of the second alternative depends on the unproved estimate
\[
 \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\int_\Omega\left(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\right)dy
      +\frac{C}{q}\int_Q\int_\Omega
        \Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz .
\]
The draft explicitly calls this estimate “unresolved” and says it is “not proved here.”

The subsequent absorption argument is correct **conditional on (13)**. In particular:

- \(|\Omega|\ge 3q/4\) follows from Markov’s inequality.
- The bad-mass term self-absorbs when \(R_3R_4\le \kappa S_3S_4\) and \(\kappa\) is small.
- The lower bound \(A_\Omega\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3q\) follows from equation (3).
- The two alternatives on the right side of (14) both imply \(I\gtrsim T\).
- Then \(I\le\Vol(R)\) gives the desired second alternative.

But since (13) is unproved and not cited from the literature, the theorem is not proved.

### Directional filling check

Equation (15), if the cited specialization is correct, gives a valid directional filling estimate. The complementary-minor bookkeeping in equation (16) is correct.

However, this section explicitly says the directional estimate is weaker than (13) and does not control the necessary parametric compatibility. Therefore it does not close the proof.

### Remaining open issues

This section accurately admits that the proof is incomplete at one essential point. That admission is mathematically appropriate, but it means the document cannot be accepted as a complete solution to the problem.
