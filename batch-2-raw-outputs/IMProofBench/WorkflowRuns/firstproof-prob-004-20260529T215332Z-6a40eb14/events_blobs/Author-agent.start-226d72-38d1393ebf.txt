\begin{center}
{\bf The stated rectangle assertion is false}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes and degree \(1\) means degree \(1\) as a map
of pairs.  I denote the proposed universal constant by \(\kappa\).  Under this
standard interpretation the assertion is false.  The counterexamples below are built
from Guth's standard pinching maps; after one fixed rescaling of the domain their
\(2\)-dilation is at most \(1\), while the two conclusions in the problem both fail.

\section*{The two Guth maps used}

I use the following standard constructions from Chapter 8.2 of Guth's thesis
(\emph{Area-contracting maps between rectangles}).  They are the maps called
``a map that stretches the short side of the domain'' (with the technical remark
following it) and the ``pinching map''.  The constant \(C\) is universal.  I state the hypotheses with non-strict
inequalities; Guth writes some of them strictly, and the non-strict form follows by
an arbitrarily small perturbation of the side lengths, changing only the universal
constant.

\begin{lemma}[Guth pinching maps]\label{lem:guthmaps}
The following degree-one maps of pairs have \(\Dil_2\le C\).
\begin{enumerate}
\item If \(A\ge1\) and \(A^4\le R_2/R_1\), then there is a map
\[
 R_1\times R_2\times R_3\times R_4
 \longrightarrow
 AR_1\times A^{-3}R_2\times R_3\times R_4 .
\]
Moreover, if also \(R_3\le A\le (R_3R_4)^{1/2}\) and \(B=R_3R_4/A\), the same
construction gives a map
\[
 R_1\times R_2\times R_3\times R_4
 \longrightarrow
 AR_1\times A^{-3}R_2\times A\times B .
\]

\item If \(a\le R_1\) and \(a^2b\le R_2R_3R_4\), then there is a map
\[
 R_1\times R_2\times R_3\times R_4
 \longrightarrow a\times a\times a\times b .
\]
\end{enumerate}
\end{lemma}

The construction is by a quasi-isometric folding in three coordinates, followed by
a retraction and a final pinching of the exceptional set.  The pinching sends the
part where the retraction has large \(2\)-Jacobian into a curve or into a plane on
which the final linear map has compensating \(2\)-contraction.  Thus the maps have
uniformly bounded \(2\)-dilation, send boundary to boundary, and have degree \(1\).

\section*{Counterexample}

Let \(L\) be large.  Put
\[
 R^0=(L^{-7},\,1,\,L,\,L),
 \qquad
 S=(L^{-13/2},\,L^{-13/2},\,1,\,L^{11/2}).
\]
These side lengths are already in nondecreasing order.

First apply Lemma \ref{lem:guthmaps}(1) to \(R^0\) with \(A=L\).  Since
\(A^4=L^4\le R^0_2/R^0_1=L^7\), we get a degree-one map with bounded
\(2\)-dilation
\[
 (L^{-7},1,L,L)\longrightarrow (L^{-6},L^{-3},L,L).
\]
Next apply Lemma \ref{lem:guthmaps}(2) with
\[
 a=L^{-13/2},\qquad b=L^{12}.
\]
Indeed,
\[
 a\le L^{-6},\qquad a^2b=L^{-13}L^{12}=L^{-1}
      =(L^{-3})L\,L .
\]
This gives
\[
 (L^{-6},L^{-3},L,L)\longrightarrow
 (L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{12}).
\]
Finally apply the second part of Lemma \ref{lem:guthmaps}(1) with \(A=1\).  Here
\[
 R_3=L^{-13/2}\le1\le (R_3R_4)^{1/2}
       =(L^{-13/2}L^{12})^{1/2}=L^{11/4},
\]
and \(B=R_3R_4/A=L^{11/2}\).  Hence
\[
 (L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{12})
 \longrightarrow
 (L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},1,L^{11/2})=S .
\]
Composing the three maps gives a degree-one map of pairs
\[
 F_L:R^0\longrightarrow S
\]
with \(\Dil_2(F_L)\le C_0\), where \(C_0\) is independent of \(L\).

Now enlarge the domain by the fixed factor \(\lambda=C_0^{1/2}\):
\[
 R=\lambda R^0=(\lambda L^{-7},\,\lambda,\,\lambda L,\,\lambda L),
\]
and let \(f_L=F_L\circ H\), where \(H:R\to R^0\) is the homothety \(H(x)=x/\lambda\).
Then \(f_L:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\) and
\[
 \Dil_2(f_L)\le C_0\lambda^{-2}=1.
\]

For these rectangles,
\[
 \frac{R_1}{S_1}=\lambda L^{-1/2},\qquad
 \frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}
 =\frac{\lambda^2L^2}{L^{11/2}}
 =\lambda^2L^{-7/2},
\]
and
\[
 \frac{\Vol(R)}{S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4}
 =\frac{\lambda^4L^{-5}}
        {L^{-13/2}L^{-13/4}L^{11/2}}
 =\lambda^4L^{-3/4}.
\]
Given any proposed \(\kappa>0\), choose \(L\) so large that all three displayed
quantities are \(<\kappa\).  Then \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\), but
\[
 R_3R_4<\kappa S_3S_4
\]
and
\[
 \Vol(R)<\kappa\,S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
\]
Thus the hypotheses of the problem hold while both alternatives in its conclusion
fail.  Consequently no universal positive constant \(\kappa\) with the stated
property exists.
