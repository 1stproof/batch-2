{\bf Rigorous partial proof and the remaining quotient tightening estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

The small universal constant in the problem is denoted by \(\kappa\), to avoid
confusion with \(k\)-dilation.  Rectangles are oriented Euclidean products, and
degree \(1\) means relative degree \(1\) on
\(H_4(R,\partial R;\mathbb Z)\to H_4(S,\partial S;\mathbb Z)\).

This document proves the first alternative and the strongest scalar hard-case
bound obtained from the standard estimates.  It also gives a precise quotient
form of the averaged tightening estimate which would finish the theorem.  That
quotient estimate is not proved here; thus the document is still a conditional
reduction, not a complete proof of the stated dichotomy.  The arguments are
written for smooth transverse maps.  For piecewise smooth maps one first
approximates relative to the boundary strata by smooth maps with
\(\Dil_2\le1+\varepsilon\) and the same relative degree, applies the estimates
to the target scaled by \((1+\varepsilon)^{-1/2}\), and finally decreases
\(\kappa\) by a universal factor and lets \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\).

\section*{Standard inputs}

All constants below are positive and universal.  If \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), then
\(\Dil_3(f),\Dil_4(f)\le1\): for singular values
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\), the inequality
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\) implies \(\sigma_2\le1\) and
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le\sigma_1\sigma_2\) for \(j\ge2\).

We use Guth's map-of-pairs estimate \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}.  For
\(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=4\), applied to a degree-one map with \(\Dil_2\le1\), it gives
\begin{equation}\label{guth14}
        R_1^2\,\Vol(R)\ge c_0\,S_1^3S_2S_3S_4 .
\end{equation}
We also use Guth's small-volume relative isoperimetric estimate in rectangles
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding}: if \(Z\) is a relative \(2\)-cycle in \(R\)
with \(A=\Mass Z\le c_*R_1R_2\), then
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C_*\max\{A^{3/2},A^2/R_1\}.
\]
By the contrapositive, after decreasing constants,
\begin{equation}\label{profile-inverse}
\Fill_R(Z)\ge L\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\Mass Z\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,L^{2/3},(R_1L)^{1/2}\}.
\end{equation}
In particular,
\begin{equation}\label{iso}
        \Mass Z<c_*R_1R_2\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C R_1R_2^2 .
\end{equation}

\section*{The first alternative}

Let \(Q\) be the concentric subrectangle of the \((x_3,x_4)\)-face of \(S\)
with side lengths \(S_3/2,S_4/2\), and put
\[
        P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\},\qquad y\in Q.
\]

\begin{lemma}[central target plane]\label{central}
Every relative \(3\)-chain in \(S\) with boundary \(P_y\), \(y\in Q\), has
mass at least \(cS_1S_2S_3\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the
\((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle which vanishes on its boundary and satisfies
\(\psi\ge cS_3\) on \(Q\).  Put \(\beta=\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2\).  Then
\(d\beta\) has comass at most \(1\), and Stokes' theorem gives
\[
\Mass Y\ge\left|\int_Yd\beta\right|
 =\left|\int_{P_y}\beta\right|\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
The relative boundary terms vanish because \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\) on the
\(x_1,x_2\)-faces and \(\psi=0\) on the \(x_3,x_4\)-faces.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{firstalt}
There is \(c_2>0\) such that, under the hypotheses of the problem, if
\[
        R_1R_2^2\le c_2S_1S_2S_3 ,
\]
then \(R_3R_4\ge c_2S_3S_4\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(T=[[R]]\) be the fundamental relative current and set
\(F=(f_3,f_4)\).  Since \(f\) has relative degree \(1\), the constancy theorem
gives \(f_\#T=[[S]]\) as a relative \(4\)-current.  For a.e. \(y\in Q\),
slicing naturality gives
\[
        f_\#\langle T,F,y\rangle
        =\langle [[S]],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle=\pm P_y .
\]
Write \(Z_y=\langle T,F,y\rangle\).  Any relative filling of \(Z_y\) in \(R\)
pushes forward, using \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), to a filling of \(P_y\) in \(S\).
Lemma \ref{central} therefore gives
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
If \(c_2\) is sufficiently small, \eqref{iso} forces
\(\Mass Z_y\ge c_*R_1R_2\).  Since \(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\), coarea gives
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge\int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
        \ge cR_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Cancelling \(R_1R_2\) proves the proposition.
\end{proof}

\section*{The unconditional scalar hard-case bound}

Assume \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\) and that the first conclusion fails with a
small constant.  Proposition \ref{firstalt} gives
\begin{equation}\label{r12large}
        R_1R_2^2\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\end{equation}
For the slices above, \eqref{profile-inverse} with
\(L=cS_1S_2S_3\) gives
\[
\Mass Z_y\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,(S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},
                         (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\}.
\]
Using \eqref{r12large} and \(R_1=\alpha S_1\), each term in the minimum is
at least \(c\alpha^{1/2}S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\).  Hence
\begin{equation}\label{slicevol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
\end{equation}
On the other hand, \eqref{guth14} gives
\begin{equation}\label{monovol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .
\end{equation}
Optimizing between \eqref{slicevol} and \eqref{monovol} yields
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge cS_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4 .
\]
Thus purely scalar slicing and monomial estimates miss the desired hard-case
scale by the factor \((S_2/S_3)^{1/10}\).

\section*{A quotient averaged estimate sufficient for the theorem}

Keep \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), put \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), and define
\[
E_y=\int_{Z_y}\|dG|_{TZ_y}\|_{HS}^2\,d\|Z_y\|,\qquad
I=\int_QE_y\,dy,\qquad q=|Q|.
\]
At a regular point of \(F\), let \(K=\ker dF\), and let \(a\ge b\) be the
singular values of \(dF|_{K^\perp}\).  For a unit \(w\in K\) and a unit
\(v\in K^\perp\) with \(|dF(v)|=a\), the mixed \(G\)-\(F\) component of
\(df(w)\wedge df(v)\) has norm \(|dG(w)|a\).  Therefore
\(|dG(w)|a\le1\), and
\[
        \|dG|_{\ker dF}\|_{HS}^2J_2F\le2 .
\]
Coarea gives
\begin{equation}\label{Icoarea}
        I\le C\Vol(R).
\end{equation}
Let \(\Omega=\{y\in Q:E_y\le4I/q\}\); then \(|\Omega|\ge3q/4\).

To avoid the null-chain obstruction, work in the quotient complex
\[
 {\mathcal Q}_*=N_*(R,\partial R;\mathbb R)/\ker f_\# .
\]
For a central regular \(y\), define the relaxed essential filling norm
\[
\EFill_f(y)=\inf\{\Mass C:\ C\in N_3(R,\partial R;\mathbb R),\
        f_\#(\partial C)=\pm P_y\}.
\]
This is the filling norm of the degree-carrying class in \({\mathcal Q}_*\).
If \(C\) is admissible, then \(f_\#C\) fills \(P_y\) in \(S\), so Lemma
\ref{central} and \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\) imply
\begin{equation}\label{AElower}
A_\Omega:=\int_\Omega\EFill_f(y)\,dy
        \ge cS_1S_2S_3\,q .
\end{equation}

For \(z\in Q\), let \({\mathcal B}_z\) be the set of points \(x\in R\) such
that \(z\) belongs to the \(F\)-image of at least one coordinate \(2\)-plaque
through \(x\).  For fixed \(x\), the set of such \(z\)'s has measure at most
\(CR_3R_4\), because there are six plaques and each has \(F\)-area at most
\(R_3R_4\).  Consequently, for every normal \(3\)-current \(B\),
\begin{equation}\label{badfubini}
 \frac1q\int_Q\Mass(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
 \le C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\Mass B .
\end{equation}
For a quotient class \([C]\in{\mathcal Q}_3\), set
\[
 \beta_z([C])=\inf_{N\in\ker f_\#}\Mass((C+N)\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z).
\]
Then \(\beta_z([C])\le \Mass(C\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\).

The following quotient averaged tightening estimate would finish the proof:
for measurable near-minimizers \(C_y^0\), \(\Mass C_y^0\le2\EFill_f(y)\),
\begin{equation}\tag{QEAT}\label{QEAT}
 A_\Omega
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
 +\frac{C}{q}\int_Q\int_\Omega
       \beta_z([C_y^0])\,dy\,dz .
\end{equation}
Indeed, by \(\beta_z([C_y^0])\le\Mass(C_y^0\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\) and
\eqref{badfubini},
\[
A_\Omega\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
  +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}A_\Omega .
\]
If \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), the last term is absorbed for small
\(\kappa\).  If also \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and
\(I\le\delta S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), then, since \(q\sim S_3S_4\),
\[
R_1I\le C\kappa\delta S_1S_2S_3^2S_4,\qquad
\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\le C\delta^2S_1S_2S_3^2S_4 ,
\]
contradicting \eqref{AElower} for small \(\delta,\kappa\).  Thus
\(I\ge cS_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), and \eqref{Icoarea} gives the desired
second alternative.

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The document is still a conditional reduction.

\begin{enumerate}
\item The essential missing estimate is \eqref{QEAT}.  It is a quotient or dual
averaged tightening theorem for the degree-carrying part of the slices
\(Z_y=\langle[[R]],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\).

\item The quotient formulation is needed.  In a two-cell model with
\(f_\#A=f_\#A'=P\), the difference \(A-A'\) is \(f\)-null, but a source-local
restriction to a bad set containing \(A\) and not \(A'\) is no longer
\(f\)-null.  Thus an ordinary source-local deformation does not descend to
\({\mathcal Q}_*\).

\item The proved parts are the first alternative, the scalar partial hard-case
bound, the weighted coarea inequality, the essential filling lower bound, the
exact-plaque Fubini estimate, and the absorption algebra following
\eqref{QEAT}.

\item A complete proof would require a quotient-equivariant exact-plaque
tightening, or equivalently a dual target-pullback cochain homotopy, proving
\eqref{QEAT}.
\end{enumerate}

Scratchpad for the rectangle 2-dilation problem.

Interpretation: rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes; a map
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) of degree 1 means the induced map on
\(H_4(\cdot,\partial\cdot;\mathbb Z)\) sends \([R]\) to \([S]\).
The constant called \(k\) in the problem is a small universal constant;
in drafts it is denoted \(\kappa\) to avoid conflict with \(k\)-dilation.

Important correction after referee report.  The earlier proposed theorem
\[
 M_2/(S_3S_4)+M_3/(S_1S_3S_4)
 +M_4/(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4)\ge c
\]
for arbitrary two-parameter sweepouts is false.  Counterexample:
\(S=[0,1]^2\times[0,L]^2\) and cycles
\([0,1]^2\times\{u\}\times\{v\}\).  Then \(M_2=1,M_3=L,M_4=L^2\), so the
left side is \(L^{-2}+L^{-1}+L^{-1/2}\to0\).  Any valid proof must use
more of the map \(f\) than the fiber sweepout: specifically, the
\(\Dil_2\) bound controls mixed two-faces of a cubical decomposition of
\(R\).

Precise Guth estimate from arXiv:0710.0403, Estimate 1.  Let
\(Q_i=S_i/R_i\).  If \(\Phi:(U,\partial U)\to(S,\partial S)\), \(U\subset R\),
has nonzero degree, then for \(0\le j<k\le l\le n\),
\[
 \operatorname{dil}_k(\Phi)\ge c(n)Q_1\cdots Q_j
   (Q_{j+1}\cdots Q_l)^{(k-j)/(l-j)}.
\]
Equivalently, if \(\Phi\) is \(k\)-contracting and \(0<j<k<l\),
\[
 \left({R_1\cdots R_j\over S_1\cdots S_j}\right)^{(l-k)/(k-j)}
 R_1\cdots R_l \ge c(n)S_1\cdots S_l .
\]
Estimate 2 includes a factor \(D^{(k-j)/(n-j)}\) but for degree \(D=1\)
coincides with the \(l=n\) case.

Consequences for \(n=4,k=2\), plus \(\Dil_3,\Dil_4\le1\) from singular

because \(L^4\le L^7=R_2/R_1\).
Then pinching with \(a=L^{-13/2}\), \(b=L^{12}\):
\[
(L^{-6},L^{-3},L,L)
 \to (L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{12}),
\]
because \(a\le L^{-6}\) and \(a^2b=L^{-1}=L^{-3}LL\).  Guth states some inequalities strictly; equalities here can be perturbed by fixed factors and do not affect asymptotics.
Finally the interpolated short-side/stretch-split map with \(A=1\):
\[
(L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{12})
 \to (L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},1,L^{11/2}),
\]
because \(A^4=1\le R_2/R_1=1\) and
\(R_3=L^{-13/2}\le1\le(R_3R_4)^{1/2}=L^{11/4}\).

The bad ratios for \(R^0,S\):
\[
R_1/S_1=L^{-1/2},\quad
R_3R_4/(S_3S_4)=L^2/L^{11/2}=L^{-7/2},
\]
\[
\operatorname{Vol}(R^0)/(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4)
=L^{-5}/(L^{-13/2}L^{-13/4}L^{11/2})=L^{-3/4}.
\]
After scaling the domain by \(\lambda=C_0^{1/2}\), these become
\(\lambda L^{-1/2}\), \(\lambda^2L^{-7/2}\), \(\lambda^4L^{-3/4}\).  Hence they
all tend to zero.  This gives a genuine counterexample if the cited Guth maps are
accepted.



Round 3 update / current mathematical state.

The round-2 counterexample using the map

(L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},L^{12})\to
(L^{-13/2},L^{-13/2},1,L^{11/2})
\]
is invalid.  Guth's published Estimate 1 for degree-one maps of pairs applies with
\(j=0,k=2,l=3\) and gives a lower bound \(\Dil_2\gtrsim L^{13/3}\).  Therefore the
``interpolated technical remark'' from the thesis cannot be used in that broad form;

A rigorous partial result that remains useful:
For degree-one \(f:R\to S\) with \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), if
\[
 C R_1R_2^2 \le S_1S_2S_3,
\]
then
\[
 R_3R_4\ge cS_3S_4.
\]
Proof: pull back the central relative 2-planes
\([0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3,y_4\}\).  Their filling volume in \(S\) is
\(\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\).  Since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), their preimages have at least this
filling volume in \(R\).  Guth's small relative 2-cycle filling lemma says any
relative 2-cycle of area \(<cR_1R_2\) fills with volume \(\lesssim R_1R_2^2\).  Hence
the pulled-back cycles have area \(\gtrsim R_1R_2\).  Coarea for
\((x_3,x_4)\circ f\) yields \(\Vol(R)\gtrsim R_1R_2S_3S_4\), hence the conclusion.

Thus the only remaining case (assuming the first desired alternative is false) is
\[
 R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3,\qquad R_1\le\kappa S_1.
\]
For \(\kappa\) small this implies \(R_2^2\gg S_2S_3\).

Naive attempt for the remaining case:
Pull back the intervals
\[
\{y_1\}\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\}
\]
and try to use local isoperimetry to show their preimages have length
\(\gtrsim (S_2S_3)^{1/2}\); coarea for \((x_1,x_3,x_4)\circ f\) would then give
\[
 \Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_3S_4(S_2S_3)^{1/2}
 =S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]
Obstacle: those intervals are relative 1-cycles in \(S\) that can fill through an
\(x_1\)-face with area \(O(S_1S_2)\), not \(\gtrsim S_2S_3\).  Need a genuinely mixed
family of cycles or a chain-level tightening that prevents escape through the short
\(x_1\) direction.

(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)\to(1,L,L^5,L^6)\to(1,1,L^6,L^6).

Technical lemmas for the slicing approach.

I made the first-alternative argument fully current/chain based.  For a central
target plane
\[
P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\}
\]
a relative filling \(Y\) in \(S\) has volume \(\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\).  The clean
proof uses the calibration
\[
\omega=dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge d\psi,
\]
where \(\psi\) is a 1-Lipschitz function on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle, zero on
its boundary and \(\psi(y)\gtrsim S_3\) for central \(y\).  Boundary terms vanish:
on \(x_1,x_2\)-faces \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\), and on \(x_3,x_4\)-faces
\(\psi=0\).  This also fixes the previous objection that a filling might escape
through \(x_4\).

Slicing naturality: for \(F=(x_3,x_4)\circ f\) and a.e. regular \(y\),
\[
f_\#\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle
=\langle f_\#[R,\partial R],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle
=P_y
\]
as relative currents.  Hence any filling of the slice in \(R\) pushes forward,
using \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), to a filling of \(P_y\).

Guth's rectangular isoperimetric profile for \(k=2,n=4\): if a relative
2-cycle has area \(A\le cR_1R_2\), then
\[
I_R^2(A)\le C\max(A^{3/2},A^2/R_1),
\]
and in particular \(I_R^2(A)\le CR_1R_2^2\).  This gives the rigorous first
alternative:
\[
CR_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3\quad\Longrightarrow\quad R_3R_4\gtrsim S_3S_4.
\]

A useful but insufficient profile consequence for the complementary case:
For the central slices \(Z_y\),
\[
\Mass Z_y\gtrsim
\min\{R_1R_2,\ (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\ (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\}.
\]
Coarea over \(y_3,y_4\) gives the same expression times \(S_3S_4\) as a lower
bound for \(\Vol(R)\).  The term \((S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3}\) would imply the desired
fiber-area lower bound \(S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\), since
\((S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3}/(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{1/2})
=(S_2S_3/S_1^2)^{1/6}\ge1\).  The obstruction is the alternative
\((R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\), which is smaller by \((R_1/S_1)^{1/2}\).

Guth Estimate 1 with \(j=1,l=4,k=2\) gives
\[
R_1^3R_2R_3R_4\gtrsim S_1^3S_2S_3S_4.
\]
Under \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\),
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim \kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4,
\]
which proves the desired volume bound when \(S_3/S_2\lesssim \kappa^{-6}\).
Thus the hard range is \(S_3/S_2\gg_\kappa1\), and, after the first alternative
reduction, \(R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\).

Potential next attack: upgrade the profile consequence by using the degree of
the map \(Z_y\to P_y\), not merely the area and filling volume of \(Z_y\).  A
formal slicing of \(Z_y\) by the target \(x_1\)-coordinate suggests the desired
estimate \( \Fill(Z_y)\lesssim A^2/S_1\), which would imply
\(A\gtrsim S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\).  The obstacle is that \(f_1\) is not
1-Lipschitz; however on \(Z_y\) the Jacobian bound
\(|df_1\wedge df_2|\le1\) and the degree-one condition to
\([0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\) may provide the missing weighted coarea control.
This is the most concrete remaining subproblem.




\section*{Single-fiber no-go and family-level obstruction}

A proposed route was to improve the source filling upper bound for each central
fiber \(Z_y=f^{-1}([0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3,y_4\})\) from the Guth-profile
branch \(A^2/R_1\) to \(A^2/S_1\).  This is false under slice-only hypotheses.
Model: choose a relative rectangle
\[
Z=[0,r]\times[0,L]\times\{(h,h)\}\subset R,\qquad A=rL,
\]
and map it to \([0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\) by
\[
(u,v)\mapsto(S_1u/r,S_2v/L).
\]
The Jacobian is \(S_1S_2/A\le1\) if \(A\ge S_1S_2\), and the map has relative
degree one.  A calibration \(\omega=dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge d\psi\), with
\(\psi(h,h)\sim h\), shows that its filling volume in the ambient rectangle is
\(\gtrsim hA\).  Taking \(h=S_1S_2S_3/A\) gives filling volume
\(\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\) at area
\[
A\sim (rS_1S_2S_3)^{1/2},
\]
which is exactly the offending \(A^2/R_1\) branch when \(r=R_1\).  Hence a proof
of the problem must use the fact that the \(Z_y\)'s are fibers of a single
four-dimensional map, not merely the degree/Jacobian data on each fiber.

Current rigorous partial results:
\[
CR_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3 \Longrightarrow R_3R_4\gtrsim S_3S_4.
\]
If the first desired alternative fails (for small \(\kappa\)), then
\[
R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3,\qquad
R_2^2\gtrsim \kappa^{-1}S_2S_3.
\]
Guth's Estimate 1 with \(j=1,k=2,l=4\) gives
\[
R_1^2\operatorname{Vol}(R)\gtrsim S_1^3S_2S_3S_4,
\]
so \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) implies
\[
\operatorname{Vol}(R)\gtrsim \kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4.
\]
This proves the desired second alternative in the range
\(S_3/S_2\lesssim\kappa^{-6}\).  The remaining hard range is
\(S_3/S_2\gg_\kappa1\), with \(R_2^2\gg S_2S_3\), and requires a genuinely mixed
family-level estimate.

The scaling stress test remains
\[
S=(1,1,L^6,L^6),\qquad R=(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6).
\]
It saturates Guth's known monomial estimates and the boundary \(B3\)-type
ellipsoid estimate, while violating the desired alternatives by a factor \(L^{-1}\).
No valid bounded-\(2\)-dilation degree-one map \(R\to S\) is known.  The tempting
route
\[
(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)\to(1,L,L^5,L^6)\to(1,1,L^6,L^6)
\]
fails at the second arrow: the boundary/ellipsoid obstruction gives
\(R_3R_4\gtrsim S_3S_4\) in the relevant case, but \(L^{11}<L^{12}\).



\section*{Round 6 boundary-trace reduction}

New potentially useful lemma.  Let \(Z\) be a relative \(2\)-cycle in a
rectangle \(R\), \(A=\Mass Z\).  For a coordinate \(x_i\), let \(L_i\) be the
mass of the trace of \(\partial Z\) on the two faces \(x_i=0,R_i\).  Then
\[
\Fill_R(Z)\lesssim R_iA+A L_i.
\]
Proof idea:
1. General relative FF after doubling gives \(\Fill_R(Z)\lesssim A^{3/2}\).
By AM-GM, \(A^{3/2}\le (R_iA+A^2/R_i)/2\).
2. Fill the trace \(\Gamma_i\) in the two \(x_i\)-faces by relative
2-chains of area \(\lesssim L_i^2\).  Adding these face chains changes \(Z\)
only by a relative-zero chain and gives a representative \(Z'\) whose boundary
does not lie on the \(x_i\)-faces.  Translate \(Z'\) linearly to \(x_i=0\);
the remaining boundary track stays in \(\partial R\).  This fills with volume
\(\lesssim R_i(A+L_i^2)\).
Taking the minimum of the \(A^2/R_i\) and \(R_iL_i^2\) terms gives
\(\lesssim R_iA+AL_i\).

For central fibers \(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\), with
\(A_y=\Mass Z_y\) and \(L_{i,y}\) the trace on \(x_i\)-faces, the target
calibration gives \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim F:=S_1S_2S_3\).  Hence
\[
A_y(R_i+L_{i,y})\gtrsim F.
\]
Coarea on the two \(x_i\)-faces gives
\[
\int_Q L_{i,y}\,dy\lesssim \Vol(R)/R_i,
\]
because \(J_2((f_3,f_4)|_{\{x_i=0,R_i\}})\le1\).  Jensen/optimization:
for \(q=|Q|\sim S_3S_4\),
\[
\int_Q A_y\,dy\gtrsim {Fq\over R_i+\Vol(R)/(R_iq)}.
\]
Since \(\int_Q A_y\le\Vol(R)\),
\[
R_i\Vol(R)+{\Vol(R)^2\over R_iS_3S_4}\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3^2S_4
\quad\text{for all }i.
\]
This is a genuine family-level strengthening.  It recovers, for the stress-test
dimensions \(S=(1,1,L^6,L^6)\), \(R=(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)\), an obstruction
from \(i=3\) if the constants were sharp enough, but the inequality as used
still needs to be combined with \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), side ordering, and
Guth's monomial bounds.  Algebraic optimization of only
\(\Vol\gtrsim S_3S_4\sqrt{R_1S_1S_2S_3}\) and
\(\Vol\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4/(R_1/S_1)^2\) leaves a gap when
\(S_3/S_2\) is extremely large, so the face inequalities for \(i=2,3,4\) or an
additional boundary/slicing input are likely needed.

Questions to resolve next:
- Can the four trace inequalities plus \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\) algebraically
imply \(\Vol\gtrsim \kappa S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\)?
- If not, is there a sharper coarea control for the trace term, e.g. involving
\(\int A_yL_{i,y}\) or simultaneous traces on both \(x_3,x_4\) faces?
- Check whether Guth's thesis contains an exact lower bound or an explicit
snake-map counterexample with the scaling
\((L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)\to(1,1,L^6,L^6)\).




\section*{Round 7 correction: trace lemma is false; weighted coarea replacement}

The Round 6 one-face trace filling lemma
\[
 \Fill_R(Z)\lesssim R_i\Mass(Z)+\Mass(Z)L_i
\]
is false for arbitrary relative \(2\)-cycles.  Counterexample:
\(R=[0,1]^2\times[0,L]^2\), \(Z=[0,1]^2\times\{(L/2,L/2)\}\).  Then
\(\Mass Z=1\), the trace on (say) the \(x_1\)-faces has length \(O(1)\), but the
calibration \(dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge d\psi(x_3,x_4)\), with
\(\psi=0\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-boundary and \(\psi(L/2,L/2)\sim L\), gives
\(\Fill_R(Z)\gtrsim L\).  The false proof failed in two places: Euclidean
\(\Fill\lesssim A^{3/2}\) is not true for relative cycles in long rectangles, and
a short trace in a long face need not fill inside the face with area \(O(L_i^2)\)
independent of the distance to the boundary.  Do not use inequalities (8)--(11)
from the old draft.

A valid family-level replacement is the following weighted coarea observation.
Let \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), \(Z_y=F^{-1}(y)\), and
\[
 \lambda(x)=\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{T_xZ_y}\|.
\]
At a regular point, if \(v\in T_xZ_y\) realizes \(\lambda\) and \(n\) is a unit normal
to \(Z_y\), then \(dF(v)=0\) and
\[
 |df(v)\wedge df(n)|\ge \lambda |dF(n)|.
\]
Since \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), \(|dF(n)|\le\lambda^{-1}\), so the normal \(2\)-Jacobian
\(J_F\le C\lambda^{-2}\).  Coarea gives
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge c\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\,d\mathcal H^2\,dy.
\]
For each central \(y\), \((f_1,f_2):Z_y\to[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\) has relative
degree \(1\), so \(\int_{Z_y}|df_1\wedge df_2|\ge S_1S_2\) and
\(\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\gtrsim S_1S_2\).  This recovers only
\(\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4\).  A sufficient missing lemma would be a
fiber-family energy lower bound
\[
 \int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{1/2}
\]
for a positive-measure set of central \(y\), using the filling lower bound
\(\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\) and the fact that the \(Z_y\)'s are fibers of the
same ambient map.

Algebraic insufficiency of known single-fiber/profile estimates persists.  With
\(S=(1,1,L,L)\), the exponent model
\[
 r=(-1/5,\,3/5,\,1,\,1)
\]
has \(R_1R_2^2\) exactly at the first-alternative threshold and volume exponent
\(12/5\), below the desired \(5/2\).  If the last two sides are multiplied by a
small fixed constant this also models \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\) at the exponent
level.  It is the short-side-stretch scaling; Guth's short-side-stretch map realizes
the comparable case \(R_3R_4\sim S_3S_4\), so the missing argument must use the
strict smallness of the long-area ratio, not just monomial degree estimates.

Correct OCR for Guth thesis double pinching map (p.148): the target is
\[
 R_1^2/A \times A\times A\times B,
\]
not \(R_1/A\times A\times A\times B\).  Conditions:
\(R_1<A\), \(A^2<R_2R_3\), \(A^3B<R_1R_2R_3R_4\).  Earlier exponent searches
using \(R_1/A\) produce spurious counterexamples and must be ignored.  Random
searches with the corrected \(R_1^2/A\) output found no Guth-map counterexample
to the stated theorem; the best scalings saturate the first alternative rather than
violating it.




Further technical consolidation.

Correct weighted coarea identity.  Let \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), and at
rank-two points of \(F\) let
\[
\lambda(x)=\|dG|_{\ker dF_x}\|.
\]
Then the pointwise inequality is
\[
 \lambda(x)^2 J_2F(x)\le 1.
\]
Proof: if \(T=\ker dF_x\), \(N=T^\perp\), \(v\in T\) is unit with
\(|dG(v)|=\lambda\), and \(n\in N\) is unit, then
\[
df(v)=(dG(v),0),\quad df(n)=(dG(n),dF(n)),
\]
so \(|df(v)\wedge df(n)|\ge \lambda |dF(n)|\).  Since \(\Dil_2 f\le1\),
\(\|dF|_N\|\le\lambda^{-1}\), hence \(J_2F\le\lambda^{-2}\).  Coarea gives
\[
\int_Q\int_{\langle R,F,y\rangle}\lambda^2\,d{\cal H}^2\,dy
=\int_{F^{-1}(Q)}\lambda^2J_2F\,dx\le \Vol(R).
\]
This avoids dividing by \(J_2F\); critical points simply contribute zero to the
coarea integrand.  For a.e. central \(y\), slicing naturality gives
\(f_\#\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle=P_y\), so \(G\) has relative degree one on
the slice and
\[
\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\ge \int_{Z_y}|df_1\wedge df_2|\ge S_1S_2.
\]
This recovers only \(V_R\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4\).

Averaged filling estimate that would close the theorem.  Put
\[
q=|Q|\sim S_3S_4,\quad F_0=S_1S_2S_3,\quad
I=\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2 .
\]
A sufficient family-level estimate is
\[
\int_Q \Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}
+\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}F_0q\right). \tag{AF}
\]
Indeed \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim F_0\) for a.e. central \(y\), so if
\(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), the last term is absorbed for small \(\kappa\).  If also
\(I\le\eta qS_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\), then
\[
R_1I\le \eta\kappa F_0q,\qquad I^2/(S_1q)\le\eta^2F_0q
\]
(using \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(S_1\le S_2\le S_3\)), a contradiction for small
\(\eta,\kappa\).  Hence \(I\gtrsim qS_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\), and weighted coarea gives
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_3S_4\,S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}
=S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]
The defect term in (AF) is necessary: if \(R=S\) and \(Z_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\}\), the family is a product foliation with
large filling but no extra weighted energy; its obstruction is exactly
\(R_3R_4/S_3S_4\sim1\).

Why simple variants fail.  A fiberwise inequality
\[
\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\gtrsim S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}
\]
is false without ambient information.  In \(S=(1,1,L,L)\), a sheet spanning long
source directions and mapping them with degree one to the \((f_1,f_2)\)-square can
have \(\int\lambda^2\sim1\) while its filling in the source is very large.  Such a
single-fiber model violates the full ambient \(2\)-dilation bounds when one tries to
make it part of a family covering the \((f_3,f_4)\)-rectangle; the missing argument
must exploit the pointwise mixed bound \(\lambda\|dF|_N\|\le1\) together with the
limited source parameter area \(R_3R_4\).

Boundary and level-set ideas.  Guth's ellipsoid boundary estimate B3a says, in the
case \(R_2^2\gtrsim S_2S_3\), that a \(2\)-contracting boundary diffeomorphism
forces
\[
R_2R_3R_4\gtrsim S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]
The complementary first-alternative range and \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) imply
\(R_2^2\gtrsim_\kappa S_2S_3\), so this boundary estimate has the correct
\(S_2,S_3,S_4\) pattern.  However multiplying it by \(R_1\) gives only
\(V_R\gtrsim R_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), not the desired \(S_1\)-factor.  A level-set
approach using \(f_1=t\) also runs into the fact that target intervals/circles at
interior \(x_1=t\) can fill through the short \(x_1\)-direction with area \(O(S_1S_2)\),
so it does not reproduce the boundary filling area \(S_2S_3\).  These failures point
back to an averaged double/parametric version of the boundary argument, rather than
a direct boundary or single-level argument.

Short-side-stretch counterexample checks.  The tempting map
\[
(L^{-1/5},L^{3/5},\epsilon L,\epsilon L)\to
(1,1,\epsilon L^{6/5},\epsilon L^{6/5})
\]
with stretch \(A=L^{1/5}\) is not one of Guth's valid short-side-stretch maps and is
ruled out by published Estimate 1: for the proposed target the quotients are
\((L^{1/5},L^{-3/5},L^{1/5},L^{1/5})\), and the \(j=1,k=2,l=4\) lower bound gives
\(\Dil_2\gtrsim L^{2/15}\).  In the thesis, map 3 has target
\[
AR_1\times A^{-3}R_2\times AR_3\times A^{-1}R_4
\]
with the additional condition \(A\le (R_4/R_3)^{1/2}\), and its technical variant
has target \(AR_1\times A^{-3}R_2\times A\times B\) with
\(R_3<A<(R_3R_4)^{1/2}\) and \(AB=R_3R_4\).  These conditions exclude the symmetric
hard model with \(R_3\sim R_4\) and small stretch \(A=L^{1/5}\).  Thus no explicit
Guth-map counterexample to the theorem is currently known from the listed
Chapter 8 constructions.




Further consolidation of the averaged-filling route.

The non-absorbing averaged filling estimate
\[
\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}
+\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}F_0q\right)
\]
is false in this universal form.  Product counterexample:
\[
S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad R=(1,M,L,L),\qquad
f(u_1,u_2,u_3,u_4)=(u_1,u_2/M,u_3,u_4),
\]
with \(1<M<L\).  Then \(\Dil_2(f)=1\), the map has degree one, and for
\(F=(f_3,f_4)\) the central fibers are
\([0,1]\times[0,M]\times\{y_3,y_4\}\).  On these fibers
\(\lambda=\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{\ker dF}\|=1\), hence
\[
I\sim ML^2,\qquad q\sim L^2,\qquad F_0=S_1S_2S_3=L.
\]
A source calibration \(du_1\wedge du_2\wedge d\psi(u_3,u_4)\), with
\(\psi=0\) on the \((u_3,u_4)\)-boundary and \(\psi(y)\gtrsim L\), gives
\[
\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim ML,\qquad \int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy\gtrsim ML^3.
\]
The proposed right side is \(O(ML^2+M^2L^2+L^3)\), so \(M=L^{1/2}\) violates it
by \(L^{1/2}\).  This example has \(R_3R_4/S_3S_4=1\), so it does not attack the
final theorem in the hard regime, but it rules out any universal non-absorbing
AF estimate.

The natural replacement is the self-absorbing form
\[
A:=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
 + C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}A . \tag{SAF}
\]
In the hard regime \(R_3R_4\le \kappa S_3S_4\), the final term can be absorbed,
and the same algebra as before forces
\[
I\gtrsim qS_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2},
\]
hence by weighted coarea
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]
The product counterexample above is compatible with SAF because the coefficient
of \(A\) is order \(1\).

Thoughts on proving SAF.  A fiberwise filling inequality cannot work: central
product sheets have large filling caused by long normal travel while their
weighted \(G=(f_1,f_2)\)-energy is small.  The estimate must be applied to the
whole two-parameter complex of slices.  The heuristic is to discretize \(Q\) and
tighten the complex skeleton-by-skeleton as in Guth's proof of Estimate 1.  The
controlled part should contribute \(R_1I+I^2/(S_1q)\).  The cells for which the
tightening must use actual source normal area rather than target parameter area
should be charged not to the fixed lower target filling \(F_0q\) but to the
original average \(A\); their total proportion should be
\(R_3R_4/(S_3S_4)\).

One-dimensional slicing attempts do not currently close the gap.  For
\(H=(f_1,f_3,f_4)\), a central fiber maps to the \(x_2\)-segment in \(S\).
The target relative filling area of that segment is only \(\sim S_1S_2\), since
it can fill through the short \(x_1\)-direction.  Even if one could convert this
to a source length lower bound and integrate over parameters, the resulting
lower bound is at best of basic-volume scale and misses the factor
\((S_3/S_2)^{1/2}\).  Boundary ellipsoid inequality B3 gets the desired
\(\sqrt{S_2S_3}\) length scale because the circles are constrained to fill in
the boundary, where the short \(S_1\)-direction is unavailable.  An interior
argument needs an averaged/parametric way to reproduce this boundary obstruction
over an \(S_1\)-thick family, not just isolated level sets.

Guth's product-domain generalization near the end of arXiv:0710.0403 says that
for \(U\subset X^j\times Y^{l-j}\times Z^{n-l}\), a \(k\)-contracting
nonzero-degree map to \(S\) forces
\[
 |X|^{(l-j)/(k-j)}|Y|\gtrsim
 (S_1\cdots S_j)^{(l-j)/(k-j)}S_{j+1}\cdots S_l.
\]
With the standard product decomposition of \(R\) this recovers the monomial
estimates.  Attempts to choose a more favorable product by replacing the
\((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle with a square of side \(\sqrt{R_3R_4}\) run into exactly
the need for a controlled projection/fiber-volume estimate; no such projection
is available for a highly eccentric rectangle without paying the lost aspect
factor.  Thus this published product estimate does not by itself imply the
desired mixed volume bound.



Further round-10 exploration: projection concentration and level-set routes.

For central slices \(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\), \(F=(f_3,f_4)\),
define
\[
 a(y)=\mathcal L^2(\pi_{12}(\operatorname{spt}Z_y)).
\]
A rigorous Fubini estimate is
\[
 \int_Q a(y)\,dy\le R_1R_2R_3R_4.
\]
Indeed \(u\in\pi_{12}(\operatorname{spt}Z_y)\) implies
\(y\in F(\{u\}\times[0,R_3]\times[0,R_4])\), whose area is at most \(R_3R_4\)
because \(J_2F\le1\) on the vertical rectangle.  In the hard regime this says
that the set of parameters for which the slice has large \((x_1,x_2)\)-base
projection has relative measure \(O(R_3R_4/(S_3S_4))\).  This exactly matches
the desired absorbing coefficient.

Tempting but currently unproved/false strengthening.  If one could prove a
good-slice inequality
\[
 \Fill_R(C)\le C R_1\Mass(C)
\]
for every relative 2-cycle \(C\) whose base projection omits a point (or has
area at most half of the base), then the theorem would follow immediately:
good central fibers would have mass at least \(S_1S_2S_3/R_1\), and coarea over
a \(1-O(\kappa)\) fraction of \(Q\) would give a volume bound stronger than the
desired one.  The obstruction is topological: retracting a punctured
\(R_1\times R_2\) base to its boundary as a pair forces a loop around the
puncture to wind once around the long outer boundary, and Guth's averaging
argument naturally produces an \(R_2\), not \(R_1\), loss.  The model
\(C=[0,R_1]\times[0,L]\) sitting at fixed values of the other coordinates shows
how boundary tracks can force a long annular sweep even when the two-dimensional
base projection has measure zero.  Thus a naive ``short horizontal retraction''
does not supply the missing estimate.

A weaker good-slice inequality with an \(R_2\) factor gives only
\[
 V_R\,R_2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3^2S_4,
\]
the same scale as one of the double/boundary homotopy inequalities and still
compatible with the scaling test \(S=(1,1,L^6,L^6)\),
\(R=(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)\).  It therefore cannot prove the theorem.

Level-set attempt.  Slice by \(H=(f_1,f_3,f_4)\).  If for most
\((t,y_3,y_4)\) the preimage of the \(x_2\)-segment in the level
\(\{s_1=t,s_3=y_3,s_4=y_4\}\) had length \(\gtrsim (S_2S_3)^{1/2}\), then
\(\Dil_3(f)\le1\) and coarea would give exactly
\(V_R\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\).  The available filling obstruction for
that segment in the full target is only \(S_1S_2\), because it can fill through
the short \(s_1\)-direction.  The desired \(S_2S_3\) filling area is a boundary
or fixed-level obstruction, but the source level sets \(f_1=t\) have no uniform
isoperimetric inequality.  This route therefore also seems to require a
parametric argument that keeps track of level compatibility, not just isolated
curves.

Conclusion preserved for later work: the promising rigorous data are the
uniform target filling lower bound, the weighted mixed inequality
\(\lambda^2J_2F\le1\), and the projection average above.  The missing step is a
decoupling/tightening theorem that converts the unweighted small bad-projection
fraction into the self-absorbing term
\((R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy\), while charging the good part to
\(R_1I+I^2/(S_1|Q|)\) or to an equivalent quantity strong enough to force
\(I\gtrsim |Q|S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\).



\section*{Round 11 algebra and strategy notes}

After the first-alternative reduction, the existing central-slice lower bound
is slightly stronger than previously emphasized.  Let
\(\alpha=R_1/S_1\) and
\[
T=S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4,\qquad F_0=S_1S_2S_3,\qquad q\sim S_3S_4 .
\]
If \(R_3R_4\lesssim S_3S_4\), Proposition first gives
\(R_1R_2^2\gtrsim F_0\).  In the lower bound
\[
V_R\gtrsim q\min\{R_1R_2,F_0^{2/3},(R_1F_0)^{1/2}\},
\]
the first term is then at least \((R_1F_0)^{1/2}\), and the second is also at
least this because \(R_1\le S_1\le S_2\le S_3\).  Hence
\[
V_R\gtrsim \alpha^{1/2}T.
\]
Guth's \(j=1,k=2,l=4\) monomial estimate in the sharper variable \(\alpha\) gives
\[
V_R\gtrsim \alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4.
\]
Therefore a genuine counterexample to the desired second alternative
\(V_R\gtrsim\kappa T\), under \(R_1\le \kappa S_1\), would have to satisfy
\[
\alpha\lesssim \kappa^2,\qquad {S_3\over S_2}\gtrsim \alpha^{-4}\kappa^{-2}.
\]
The unresolved range is thus not merely \(S_3/S_2\gg_\kappa1\), but the extreme
case where the first side is much smaller than the hypothesis requires and the
target aspect ratio is enormous.  This is precisely where the \(A^2/R_1\) branch
of the fiberwise filling profile loses the factor \(\alpha^{1/2}\).

Potential counterexample caveat checked this round: a tempting abstract
argument from Guth's area-expanding embeddings \(S\hookrightarrow R\) to a
degree-one pair map \(R\to S\) is not automatic.  The Pontryagin--Thom collapse
naturally gives a map \(R/\partial R\to S/\partial S\), but to lift it to an
actual map of pairs one must extend the inverse boundary map through the
complement as a map to \(\partial S\).  The boundary degree obstruction forces
the outer boundary map also to have degree one, so a constant collapse outside
the embedded copy is not a continuous pair map.  Any real counterexample must
therefore use explicit degree-one snake/pinching maps of pairs, not just an
opposite-direction embedding.

One-dimensional slicing check: slicing by \((f_1,f_3,f_4)\) gives preimage curves
mapping with degree one to \(x_2\)-segments.  In the full target those segments
fill through the short \(x_1\)-direction with area only \(\sim S_1S_2\), not
\(\sim S_2S_3\).  Thus the resulting curve-length lower bounds recover at most
basic-volume scale (or low-aspect partial ranges) and do not address the extreme
range above.  Any successful argument still appears to need a parametric
mechanism that prevents all \(x_2\)-segments, over an \(S_1\)-thick family, from
using the short \(x_1\)-escape independently.

Useful question for external help: Is there a published boundary or minimax
(Steenrod-square/cup-product) estimate for the two-parameter family of central
2-cycles \(Z_y=\langle[R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\) that gives a lower bound
\(\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\gtrsim |Q|S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\) under
\(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\)?  Equivalently, can Guth's tightening construction be
localized to prove the self-absorbing estimate (SAF) recorded above?




\section*{Round 12 notes: random-puncture reduction and level-slice check}

Council feedback converged on a self-absorbing version of the averaged filling
estimate.  The right bad term should be weighted by the actual almost-minimizing
fillings \(B_y\), not by the fixed lower target filling \(F_0=S_1S_2S_3\).  For
\(F=(f_3,f_4)\), \(Q\subset [0,S_3]\times[0,S_4]\) central, and
\(V_u=\{u\}\times[0,R_3]\times[0,R_4]\), define for a puncture \(z\in Q\)
\[
 \mathcal B_z=\{x\in R:\ z\in F(V_{\pi_{12}x})\}.
\]
For any measurable almost-minimizing fillings \(B_y\) of the central slices,
Fubini gives the rigorous estimate
\[
 {1\over |Q|}\int_Q\int_Q \Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz
 \le {R_3R_4\over |Q|}\int_Q \Mass B_y\,dy
 \lesssim {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}A,
\]
where \(A=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy\).  This is stronger than the old projection
area estimate because it is weighted by the fillings and hence has the right
self-absorbing form.

A precise sufficient lemma is now:
for every puncture \(z\),
\[
 A\le C\left(R_1I+{I^2\over S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C\int_Q\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy, \tag{RP}
\]
where \(I=\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\,d\|Z_y\|\,dy\) and
\(\lambda=\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{\ker dF}\|\).  Averaging in \(z\) gives
\[
 A\le C\left(R_1I+{I^2\over S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C{R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}A.
\]
If \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), the final term is absorbed.  Since the target
calibration gives \(A\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3|Q|\), this forces
\[
I\gtrsim |Q|\,S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}
\]
for small \(\kappa\), and weighted coarea gives the desired volume lower bound.

What remains is the deformation lemma (RP).  Heuristic: on
\(R\setminus\mathcal B_z\), each vertical rectangle \(V_u\) maps into the
punctured parameter square \(Q\setminus\{z\}\); the two-parameter vertical
obstruction is therefore null.  Guth's tightening should be applied to the
whole \(Q\)-complex of slices.  The good part should be charged to \(R_1I\)
(pushing in the short direction with mixed energy weight) and to
\(I^2/(S_1|Q|)\) (capping the \(f_1\)-level seams by a one-dimensional coarea
optimization).  The only mass where one cannot avoid the long vertical cost is
the bad mass \(B_y\cap\mathcal B_z\), which averages to the absorbing fraction.

Level-slicing attempt checked this round.  For fixed \(y\), slice
\(Z_y\) by \(f_1=t\).  Naturality says the pushforward of the curve is the
target \(x_2\)-interval at \((t,y)\).  If a filling of this curve were required
to stay in the target hyperplane \(x_1=t\), its target filling area would be
\(\gtrsim S_2S_3\), giving the desired boundary-type length scale
\((S_2S_3)^{1/2}\).  However an arbitrary source filling can leave the
\(f_1=t\) level, and the corresponding target interval fills through the short
\(x_1\)-direction with area only \(O(S_1S_2)\).  Slicing a 3-filling of \(Z_y\)
by \(f_1\) restores the target-slice lower bound but introduces uncontrolled
\(|df_1|\) in coarea.  I do not see a way to turn this into a direct proof
without essentially reproducing the parametric/random-puncture tightening.




\section*{Round 13 notes: random-puncture false; directional alternative}

The fixed-puncture deformation lemma (RP) from Round 12 is false as stated.
A clean linear counterexample is
\[
R=[0,1]\times[0,L^2]\times[0,L^2]\times[0,L^2],\quad
S=[0,1]\times[0,1]\times[0,L]\times[0,L],
\]
\[
f(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)=
\left({x_3\over L^2},{x_4\over L^2},Lx_1,{x_2\over L}\right).
\]
The singular values are \(L,L^{-1},L^{-2},L^{-2}\), so \(\Dil_2(f)=1\).
For \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), a central fiber is
\[
Z_y=\{x_1=y_3/L,\ x_2=Ly_4\}\times[0,L^2]^2,
\]
with mass \(L^4\) and relative filling mass \(\gtrsim L^4\) (calibrate by
\(d(\phi(x_1,x_2)\,dx_3\wedge dx_4)\), with \(\phi\) vanishing on the boundary
of the \((x_1,x_2)\)-rectangle).  On the fiber
\(\lambda=\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{\ker dF}\|=L^{-2}\), so the weighted energy per fiber is
\(O(1)\) and \(I\sim |Q|\sim L^2\).  For each puncture \(z\), the bad set
\(\mathcal B_z\) is a 2-dimensional vertical sheet, so every integral 3-filling has
zero mass on it.  Hence \(A\sim L^6\) but the right side of RP is only \(O(L^2)\).
This example is not in the theorem's hard regime because \(R_3R_4/S_3S_4=L^2\),
but it invalidates RP as a universal lemma.  Any future deformation statement must
either assume \(R_3R_4\lesssim S_3S_4\) explicitly and use that assumption in the
proof, or include additional terms detecting horizontal use of the parameter map
\(F\).

A second coordinate-permutation counterexample shows that even in
\(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\), RP can fail if \(R_1/S_1\) is not small.  Let
\(R=(\epsilon,1,\epsilon,1)\), \(S=(\epsilon,\epsilon,1,1)\), and
\(f(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)=(x_3,x_1,x_2,x_4)\).  Then \(\Dil_2(f)=1\), central
\(F=(x_2,x_4)\)-fibers have filling \(\sim\epsilon^2\), \(I\sim\epsilon^2\),
the \(R_1I+I^2/(S_1q)\) terms are \(O(\epsilon^3)\), and the puncture bad set is
a 3-dimensional hyperplane with zero 3-mass intersection for fillings.  This does
not satisfy \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) for small \(\kappa\), since \(R_1=S_1\).

Directional isoperimetry gives a valid replacement reduction.  For a relative
2-cycle \(Z\subset R\), Guth's directional relative filling estimate yields
\[
 \Fill_R(Z)\le C\{R_1(\Vol_{23}Z+\Vol_{24}Z+\Vol_{34}Z)
      +R_2(\Vol_{13}Z+\Vol_{14}Z)+R_3\Vol_{12}Z\}.
\]
For \(Z_y=\langle [R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\), integrate and use the target filling
lower bound \(S_1S_2S_3\) to get
\[
 S_1S_2S_3|Q|
 \le C\{R_1(M_{12}+M_{13}+M_{14})
      +R_2(M_{23}+M_{24})+R_3M_{34}\},
\]
where
\[
 M_{ij}=\int_{F^{-1}(Q)}
        \left|\frac{\partial(f_3,f_4)}{\partial(x_i,x_j)}\right|\,dx .
\]
Elementary \(M_{ij}\le \Vol(R)\) only recovers old estimates.  The remaining
possible path is to combine such inequalities for several target coordinate pairs
with pointwise Pluecker relations among the six 2-forms \(df_a\wedge df_b\) and
with the hard-regime assumptions \(R_1/S_1\ll1\), \(R_3R_4/S_3S_4\ll1\).



\section*{Round 14 notes: current sharp reduction and algebraic obstruction}

Round 14 did not close the theorem.  The answer draft was cleaned up to fix the
minor referee issues: Proposition ``first alternative'' now states the degree and
\(\Dil_2\) hypotheses; the reduction explicitly takes
\(\kappa<\min\{c_1,1\}\), so \(\alpha=R_1/S_1\le1\); and directional volumes are
defined as projected masses with multiplicity,
\[
  \Vol_{ij}(Z)=\int_Z |dx_i\wedge dx_j|\,d\|Z\|.
\]
For \(F=(f_3,f_4)\) and \(Z_y=\langle[R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\), the coarea
bookkeeping is complementary:
\[
 \int_Q\Vol_{ab}(Z_y)\,dy
 =\int_{F^{-1}(Q)}
 \left|\frac{\partial(f_3,f_4)}{\partial(x_c,x_d)}\right|\,dx,
 \qquad \{a,b,c,d\}=\{1,2,3,4\}.
\]
This explains why the integrated directional filling inequality has the terms
\[
 S_1S_2S_3|Q|\lesssim
 R_1(M_{12}+M_{13}+M_{14})+R_2(M_{23}+M_{24})+R_3M_{34}.
\]

The clean sufficient estimate is now isolated as the hard-regime averaged filling
estimate.  With
\[
 A=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy,\qquad
 I=\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\,d\|Z_y\|\,dy,\qquad |Q|\sim S_3S_4,
\]
where \(\lambda=\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{\ker d(f_3,f_4)}\|\), it would be enough to prove
\[
 A\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
      +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}A. \tag{HAF}
\]
Indeed \(A\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3|Q|\) by the target filling calibration.  If
\(R_3R_4\le \kappa S_3S_4\), absorb the last term.  If the first term on the
right dominates, then
\[
 I\gtrsim \frac{S_1S_2S_3|Q|}{R_1}
 \gtrsim \kappa^{-1} S_2S_3^2S_4
 \gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
using \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(S_1\le S_2\le S_3\).  If the quadratic term
dominates, then directly
\[
 I\gtrsim S_1|Q|(S_2S_3)^{1/2}
 \sim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]
Weighted coarea \(I\le \Vol(R)\) gives the desired second alternative.

Council/compute feedback indicates that (HAF), or an equivalent parametric
topological statement, really is stronger than the current directional and
Pluecker reductions.  Absolute-value integrated directional inequalities for all
six target coordinate pairs can be satisfied by laminate/Young-measure models
below the desired exponent.  The obstruction is that each target-pair directional
lower bound can be paid for on disjoint pieces of the domain, while the pointwise
Pluecker relations are sign-sensitive and do not survive as useful relations among
the integrated absolute values.  Thus a successful proof must force overlap or
compatibility of the degree-carrying regions with the regions paying the large
\((3,4)\)-directional filling cost.

A useful residual scaling model for testing any proposed closing lemma is
\[
 S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad
 R=(L^{-1/5},L^{13/15},L^{13/15},L^{13/15}).
\]
It has \(R_1/S_1=L^{-1/5}\), \(R_3R_4/(S_3S_4)=L^{-4/15}\), and
\[
 \Vol(R)=L^{12/5},\qquad
 S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4=L^{5/2}.
\]
It saturates the currently proved first-slicing lower bound
\(\Vol(R)\gtrsim \alpha^{1/2}T\) and the degree monomial
\(\Vol(R)\gtrsim \alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4\), but it is only an exponent/relaxation
model, not an actual map.  Any valid proof must rule out this scaling by using
global compatibility, not just the listed monomial estimates.

The earlier fixed-puncture lemma (RP) is known false without hard-regime
hypotheses.  Counterexamples have \(R_1\) comparable to \(S_1\) or
\(R_3R_4\) comparable/larger than \(S_3S_4\).  No counterexample to (HAF) under
both \(R_1\ll S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\) is known from the searches so far.
A possible next attack is to build a cubical two-parameter complex over \(Q\):
vertices carry the cycles \(Z_y\), edges carry the 3-chains
\(\langle[R],F,e\rangle\), and squares carry the 4-chains
\(\langle[R],F,\tau\rangle\).  One would need a tightening/deformation of chosen
vertex fillings whose failure set is weighted by the actual fillings and hence
produces the self-absorbing term \((R_3R_4/S_3S_4)A\).




\section*{Round 15 all-coordinate plaque puncture refinement}

For \(F=(f_3,f_4)\) and central \(Q\subset [0,S_3]\times[0,S_4]\), the old bad set
using only vertical \(x_3x_4\)-plaques misses the linear obstruction where \(F\)
varies in the \(x_1x_2\)-directions.  A better definition uses all coordinate
\(2\)-plaques.  Let \(P_{ij}(x)\) be the coordinate \(2\)-rectangle through \(x\)
obtained by varying only \(x_i,x_j\), and set
\[
 \mathcal B_z=\{x:\ z\in F(P_{ij}(x))\cap Q\text{ for some }i<j\}.
\]
For any rectifiable \(3\)-current \(B\),
\[
 {1\over |Q|}\int_Q \Mass(B\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dz
 \le C {R_3R_4\over |Q|}\Mass B.
\]
Reason: for fixed \(x\), the set of bad punctures is contained in
\(\bigcup_{i<j} F(P_{ij}(x))\cap Q\), and each image has area at most
\(R_iR_j\le R_3R_4\) because \(J_2F\le1\).  Integrate the resulting pointwise
probability bound against \(d\|B\|\).

Applied to almost-minimizing fillings \(B_y\) of the slices \(Z_y\),
\[
 {1\over |Q|}\int_Q\int_Q\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz
 \lesssim {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4} A,
 \qquad A=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy.
\]
Thus the correct self-absorbing hard estimate would follow from the fixed-puncture
parametric tightening inequality
\[
 A\le C\left(R_1I+{I^2\over S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C\int_Q\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy.
\]
This version avoids both known failures of the earlier random-puncture lemma:
(1) the linear example with \(R=(1,L^2,L^2,L^2)\), \(S=(1,1,L,L)\) is charged by
the \(x_1x_2\)-plaques; (2) any coordinate-permutation example with parameter
motion in another pair is similarly charged.  The remaining geometric task is a
genuine cubical/parametric deformation theorem on the good set.  On the good
coordinate \(2\)-skeleton, \(\rho_z\circ F\) is defined with
\(\rho_z:Q\setminus\{z\}\to\partial Q\).  One needs to convert this punctured
parameter triviality into source fillings, with \(R_1I\) coming from short-direction
pushes and \(I^2/(S_1|Q|)\) from a coarea optimization in the \(f_1\)-direction.

Exact directional isoperimetry source check: Guth's
\emph{Directional isoperimetric inequalities and rational homotopy invariants},
Proposition 2.2, for relative \(m\)-cycles in an \(n\)-rectangle says
\[
 \Vol_I(y)\lesssim \sum_{d=1}^{e-1} R_d\Vol_{I-d}(z),
\]
where \(I\) is an \((m+1)\)-tuple and \(e\) is the smallest index not in \(I\).
For \(m=2,n=4\), summing over \(I=123,124,134,234\) gives
\[
\Fill_R(Z)\lesssim
R_1(\Vol_{23}Z+\Vol_{24}Z+\Vol_{34}Z)
+R_2(\Vol_{13}Z+\Vol_{14}Z)+R_3\Vol_{12}Z.
\]
This validates the directional reduction but does not close the exponent gap:
integrated absolute minors and Pluecker relations still allow the residual
formal model \(S=(1,1,L,L)\),
\(R=(L^{-1/5},L^{13/15},L^{13/15},L^{13/15})\) at the level of all currently
proved inequalities.



\section*{Round 16 notes: averaged formulation and alternative attacks}

The fixed-puncture estimate must be stated in an averaged form, not as an
``there exists \(z\)'' statement.  The logically sufficient statement is
\[
 A=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
   +{C\over |Q|}\int_Q\int_Q
       \Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dy\,dz. \tag{HAF-avg}
\]
Together with the all-coordinate plaque estimate
\[
 {1\over |Q|}\int_Q\int_Q\Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dy\,dz
 \lesssim {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}A
\]
this gives the desired self-absorbing inequality.  This fixes the previous
logical flaw: an estimate holding only for one unspecified puncture cannot be
combined with an average bound on the bad mass.

Measurable fillings can be handled by using the flat-current topology: for a.e.
slice \(y\), the set of relative fillings \(B\) of \(Z_y\) with
\(\Mass B\le 2\Fill_R(Z_y)+\epsilon\) is nonempty closed in a Polish space after
a mass cutoff, and a standard measurable selection theorem gives a measurable
choice.  Equivalently one can choose from a countable dense family with an
\(\epsilon\)-loss and then let \(\epsilon\to0\).

Several non-parametric alternatives were rechecked.

1. Differential form approach.  Writing \(f=(f_1,\ldots,f_4)\), the forms
\(df_i\wedge df_j\) have comass at most \(1\).  The degree identity
\[
 \int_R df_1\wedge df_2\wedge df_3\wedge df_4=S_1S_2S_3S_4
\]
recovers only the basic volume bound.  Integration by parts with
\(f_1\,df_2\wedge df_3\wedge df_4\) gives boundary-type lower bounds such as
\(R_2R_3R_4\gtrsim S_2S_3S_4\), but it does not produce the missing
\((S_3/S_2)^{1/2}\) factor.

2. Slicing by \(f_1\).  For a.e. \(t\), the slice \(f_1^{-1}(t)\) maps with
degree one to the \(3\)-rectangle \(S_2\times S_3\times S_4\).  Since planes
containing the \(df_1\)-normal force tangential derivatives of
\((f_2,f_3,f_4)\) to be \(\lesssim |\nabla f_1|^{-1}\), one gets
\[
 \int_{f_1^{-1}(t)} |\nabla f_1|^{-1}\gtrsim S_2S_3S_4,
\]
and after coarea again only \(\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4\).  Applying a
three-dimensional \(2\)-dilation theorem to these level sets would require
geometric width information about the level sets which is not available.

3. Slicing by \((f_1,f_3,f_4)\).  The one-dimensional fibers map by \(f_2\) to
the \(S_2\)-interval.  The \(2\)-dilation hypothesis gives no direct bound for
stretching along a one-dimensional fiber.  The target interval can fill through
the short \(f_1\)-direction with area \(O(S_1S_2)\), so this line of attack
again gives at most the basic volume scale.

4. Area-expanding embedding caveat.  Guth's arXiv:0710.0403 sufficient theorem
for \(2\)-expanding embeddings does not immediately produce a counterexample to
the present degree-one pair-map problem: the inverse is defined only on the
embedded image, and extending it to a map \((R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\)
with controlled \(2\)-dilation requires a controlled collapse of the complement.
A naive Pontryagin--Thom collapse may have uncontrolled \(2\)-dilation along the
collar.  This remains a useful point for the literature search, because an
explicit bounded-dilation collapse for the stress-test rectangles would disprove
the problem, while a theorem forbidding such a collapse may be exactly the
missing parametric obstruction.

The stress-test exponent model remains
\[
 S=(1,1,L^6,L^6),\qquad R=(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6),
\]
which satisfies the known Guth monomial lower bounds at scale \(O(1)\) and has
\[
 R_1/S_1=L^{-1},\qquad R_3R_4/(S_3S_4)=L^{-1},\qquad
 \Vol(R)/(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4)=L^{-1}.
\]
No valid degree-one pair map with uniformly bounded \(2\)-dilation is known for
this model.  Any proof must rule it out through a global compatibility condition,
not by the known monomial estimates alone.



\section*{Round 17 notes: low-energy tightening and rational-homotopy probe}

The referee's constant objection in the approximation paragraph is fixed as follows:
if a transverse/PL theorem is proved with constant \(\kappa_0\), then state the final
theorem with \(\kappa\le \kappa_0/4\).  A PL approximation with
\(\Dil_2\le 1+\varepsilon\) is handled by scaling the target by
\((1+\varepsilon)^{-1/2}\).  For \(\varepsilon\) small,
\(R_1\le\kappa S_1\le \kappa_0(1+\varepsilon)^{-1/2}S_1\), so the scaled theorem is
applicable; the scaled alternatives imply the original alternatives after reducing
\(\kappa\) by another universal factor and then letting \(\varepsilon\to0\).

The global HAF estimate can be weakened to a low-energy estimate.  Let
\[
 E_y=\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2,\qquad I=\int_QE_y\,dy,\qquad
 \Omega=\{y:E_y\le 4I/|Q|\}.
\]
Then \(|\Omega|\ge 3|Q|/4\).  It suffices to prove
\[
 \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\int_\Omega\left(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\right)dy
 +\frac{C}{|Q|}\int_Q\int_\Omega
     \Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz. \tag{LE-HAF}
\]
Indeed, on \(\Omega\),
\[
 \int_\Omega E_y^2\,dy\le (4I/|Q|)\int_\Omega E_y\,dy\le 4I^2/|Q|.
\]
The all-coordinate plaque lemma restricted to \(\Omega\) gives
\[
 {1\over |Q|}\int_Q\int_\Omega\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz
 \lesssim {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}\int_\Omega \Mass B_y\,dy
 \lesssim {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy.
\]
In the hard regime this self-absorbs.  Since every slice has
\(\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\), the left side over \(\Omega\) is
\(\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3|Q|\), and weighted coarea then gives
\(\Vol R\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\).  This is now the narrowest
identified gap.

A possible alternate route is via rational-homotopy invariants on the quotient
\(R/\partial R\simeq S^4\).  For \(X=S^2\vee S^2\vee S^2\), an order-two
non-repeating invariant has the form
\[
 H(u)=\int_{R/\partial R} P\alpha\wedge \beta\wedge P\gamma,
\]
where \(\alpha,\beta,\gamma\) are the pullbacks of the three area forms.  Naturality
under a degree-one map of pairs says \(H(g\circ f)=H(g)\).  The standard ellipsoid
primitive estimate gives the upper factor \(R_2R_3\Vol(R)\) and the corresponding
target lower factor \(S_2S_3\Vol(S)=S_1S_2^2S_3^2S_4\), reproducing Guth's
ellipsoid/rational-homotopy inequality
\[
 R_1R_2^2R_3^2R_4\gtrsim S_1S_2^2S_3^2S_4.
\]
This does not rule out the stress family
\(S=(1,1,L^6,L^6)\), \(R=(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)\).  A rectangle-specific
directional primitive estimate might allow one primitive to be taken in the
\(x_1\)-direction and improve the upper factor, but simple candidates such as an
upper factor \(R_1R_2\Vol(R)\) are still not algebraically sufficient in all exponent
models once \(S_4/S_3\) is large.  Therefore a successful DGA/rational-homotopy
approach probably needs either several coupled invariants or a face-preserving
structure stronger than the pair-map hypothesis.

The GAFA width-volume paper was rechecked.  Theorem 2 there solves the
\((n-1)\)-dilation problem for rectangles: for \(Q_i=S_i/R_i\), \(D_{n-1}(R,S)\) is
comparable to the maximum of
\[
 Q_1\cdots Q_l(Q_{l+1}\cdots Q_n)^{(n-l-1)/(n-l)},\quad 1\le l\le n-1,
 \qquad\text{and}\qquad Q_2\cdots Q_n.
\]
For \(n=3\), this includes \(D_2(R,S)\gtrsim
Q_1Q_2^{1/2}Q_3^{1/2}\).  This explains the exponent
\(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{1/2}\) appearing per \(S_4\)-slice, but direct slicing by
\(f_4\) loses control because coarea produces \(\int |\nabla f_4|\) rather than
\(\Vol(R)\); using the \(2\)-dilation relation with the normal only recovers the
basic volume bound \(S_1S_2S_3S_4\).




\section*{Round 18 notes: critic and council consolidation}

The latest referee report accepts the first-alternative proof, weighted coarea
identity, all-coordinate plaque averaging, and the algebra after the low-energy
estimate, but correctly identifies (LE-HAF)/(13) as the sole essential gap.  Minor
technical fixes made in the draft: explicitly postcompose PL approximations by the
target homothety \((1+\varepsilon)^{-1/2}\), choose the fillings \(B_y\) pointwise
near-minimizing with \(\Mass B_y\le 2\Fill_R(Z_y)\) a.e., and avoid saying the
low-energy estimate is a consequence of the earlier global form.

Council feedback clarifies why standard cubical Federer--Fleming is insufficient.
A useful deformation must be ``Manhattan'': elementary tracks should vary at most
two source coordinates so that the all-coordinate plaque bad set protects against
the puncture.  Ordinary radial or cubical retractions change too many coordinates or
require a punctured target retraction with singular derivative; thickening the bad set
introduces near-miss terms that are not controlled by \(J_2F\le1\) (rank-one plaque
images can be \(\delta\)-dense with tiny area).  Therefore exact plaque badness is
compatible only with exact coordinate-plaque tracks, not with a smoothed radial
proof.

The desired cost \(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\) mixes source and target geometry.  A domain
deformation has track length \(R_1\) but normally costs ordinary mass or directional
mass of \(Z_y\), not the target pullback energy \(E_y\).  A target-grid deformation
naturally costs \(E_y\) and produces the \(E_y^2/S_1\) scale, but pulling it back to
source tracks risks losing the \(R_1\) factor and may require compatibility of the
degree-carrying pieces across target cells.  This is the concrete content of the
missing weighted Manhattan deformation theorem.

The bad-mass term should be tied to the particular measurable near-minimizing
family \(B_y\); it is not stable under arbitrary changes of minimizer.  The final
self-absorption uses only the pointwise near-minimality and the all-plaque Fubini
bound, so any future proof of (13) should either construct such a family directly or
allow a measurable selection minimizing a small averaged bad-mass penalty.

Compute-worker literature status remains unchanged: the cited unpublished Guth
preprint \emph{On the 2-dilation of mappings between 4-dimensional rectangles} was
confirmed as cited in arXiv:0802.3549 but not located.  The available Guth thesis
and arXiv papers supply the first alternative, monomial degree estimates, and
directional/rational-homotopy estimates, but no located statement closes LE-HAF or
the hard-regime volume inequality for arbitrary degree-one pair maps.




\section*{Round 19 notes: exponent obstruction and bankable partial bound}

Council replies made the exponent obstruction precise.  In logarithmic variables the
stress model
\[
S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad
R=(L^{-1/5},L^{13/15},L^{13/15},L^{13/15})
\]
satisfies \(R_1\ll S_1\), \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\), and
\(\Vol R=L^{12/5}\), while the desired target scale is
\(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4=L^{5/2}\).  It satisfies all currently available
scalar monomial and pair-slicing inequalities.  The binding inequalities are the
Guth degree estimate
\[
3r_1+r_2+r_3+r_4\ge 3s_1+s_2+s_3+s_4
\]
and the slicing consequence
\[
r_1+2r_2+2r_3+2r_4\ge s_1+s_2+3s_3+2s_4.
\]
With multipliers \(1/5\) and \(2/5\) (equivalently, in the variable
\(\alpha=R_1/S_1\), combining (9) with weight \(1/5\) and (8) with weight
\(4/5\)) these give the sharp unconditional partial estimate
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4
\]
whenever the first alternative fails and \(R_1\le S_1\).  This is the desired
scale times \((S_2/S_3)^{1/10}\); hence scalar pair-slicing/monomial methods
cannot close the hard-regime gap.

The compute-worker's local obstruction to a naive Manhattan proof: at a point with
fiber tangent \(T=\mathrm{span}(e_3,e_4)\), take a linear differential with
\(dF(e_1)=L\), \(dF(e_2)=L^{-1}\), \(dG(e_3)=dG(e_4)=L^{-1}\) and all other
components zero.  Then \(\Dil_2=1\) and \(\lambda^2=L^{-2}\), while sweeping the
\(e_3e_4\)-element in the \(e_1\)-direction has density \(1\).  The averaged
all-plaque bad probability over a parameter square of area \(L^2\) is also only
\(L^{-2}\).  Therefore any proof of LE-HAF/(13) must use a genuinely global
compatibility of the family \(\{Z_y\}\), not a pointwise coordinate-track estimate.




\section*{Round 20 consolidated state}

The final round did not close the essential low-energy averaged parametric
tightening estimate.  The bankable conclusions remain:

1. First alternative: if \(CR_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3\), then
\(R_3R_4\gtrsim S_3S_4\).

2. If the first alternative fails and \(R_1\le S_1\), then the central
\((f_3,f_4)\)-slicing plus Guth's \(j=1,k=2,l=4\) degree estimate gives
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim \alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4,\qquad
\Vol(R)\gtrsim \alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4,
\]
where \(\alpha=R_1/S_1\).  Interpolating with weights \(4/5,1/5\) gives the
unconditional partial bound
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4
\]
under the theorem hypotheses and failure of the first alternative.

3. Weighted coarea for \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), and
\(\lambda=\|dG|_{\ker dF}\|\) gives
\[
I:=\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\le \Vol(R),\qquad E_y\ge S_1S_2.
\]
If one could prove the low-energy averaged estimate
\[
 \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\int_\Omega\left(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\right)dy
      +\frac{C}{q}\int_Q\int_\Omega
        \Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz,
\]
then the all-coordinate plaque Fubini estimate would absorb the last term in the
hard regime \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\), force
\(I\gtrsim qS_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\), and prove the desired second alternative.

4. The \(f_1\)-level route is not a standalone shortcut.  For
\(M_t=f_1^{-1}(t)\), \(g=(f_2,f_3,f_4)\), \(w=|\nabla f_1|^{-1}\), one has
\(\|dg\|\le w\), \(\|\Lambda^2dg\|\le1\), and hence only
\[
\int_{M_t}w\ge S_2S_3S_4.
\]
The stronger pointwise bound with \(S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) is false for the
identity map; an averaged hard-regime version would be essentially equivalent to
the desired volume theorem.



\section*{Round 21 notes: status of the low-energy gap and two further probes}

The referee continues to identify (LE-HAF)/(13) as the essential unresolved
point.  I corrected the exposition of the measurable selection issue in
\texttt{answer.tex}: rather than claiming compactness of mass-bounded relative
currents without boundary-mass control, the safer formulation uses the standard
Borel structure on flat integral currents, Borelness of mass and boundary, and a
Jankov--von Neumann universally measurable selection of almost-minimizing
fillings.  This only repairs a technical side issue; it does not prove (LE-HAF).

Additional target-pair slicing check.  The central-filling argument works for any
choice of two target coordinates to slice by.  If the sliced coordinates are
\(p,q\) and the complementary coordinates are \(r,s\), then for central slice
parameters the target filling lower bound is
\[
  \Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim S_rS_s\min(S_p,S_q).
\]
For example:
\[
\begin{array}{c|c|c}
F & |Q| & \hbox{target filling lower bound}\\ \hline
(f_3,f_4) & S_3S_4 & S_1S_2S_3\\
(f_2,f_4) & S_2S_4 & S_1S_2S_3\\
(f_2,f_3) & S_2S_3 & S_1S_2S_4\\
(f_1,f_2) & S_1S_2 & S_1S_3S_4 .
\end{array}
\]
Combining these with the same rectangular profile estimate yields valid
additional lower bounds of the form
\[
 \Vol(R)\gtrsim |Q|\min\{R_1R_2,F^{2/3},(R_1F)^{1/2}\},
\]
with \(F\) the corresponding filling lower bound.  In the stress model
\(S=(1,1,L^6,L^6)\), \(R=(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)\), these extra slicings give
only much weaker exponents (e.g. \(L^{8.5}\) or \(L^3\)) than the desired
\(L^{15}\), while the existing \((f_3,f_4)\)-slicing plus Guth monomial gives
\(L^{72/5}=L^{14.4}\).  Thus all simple pair-slicings still leave the same hard
anisotropic gap.

Level-set metric reformulation.  For a regular value \(t\) of \(f_1\), let
\(M_t=\{f_1=t\}\) and \(g=(f_2,f_3,f_4)\).  If
\(w=|\nabla f_1|^{-1}\), then the singular values of \(dg\) on \(M_t\) satisfy
\(s_1\le w\) and \(s_1s_2\le1\), hence \(J_3g=s_1s_2s_3\le w\).
Pointwise one can choose an auxiliary metric \(\tilde g_t\) on \(M_t\) with
volume density \(w\,d\Mass_{M_t}\) and with \(g:(M_t,\tilde g_t)\to
[S_2]\times[S_3]\times[S_4]\) \(2\)-contracting (start from the pullback
semimetric of \(g\), whose volume density is \(J_3g\le w\), regularize, and
inflate to density \(w\)).  Therefore the desired estimate is equivalent to a
lower bound for the weighted volumes
\[
 \int_{M_t}w\,d\Mass \gtrsim S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
for many \(t\), but a general \(3\)-dimensional domain admitting a
\(2\)-contracting degree-one map to the target \(3\)-rectangle need not obey this
bound (the identity map when \(S_3\gg S_2\) is the basic obstruction).  The
missing information must be some boundary/ambient compatibility of the
\(\tilde g_t\) coming from the embedding of all levels in the same \(R\) and the
hard-regime condition \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\).  This reformulation may be useful but
does not yet close the proof.

Potential literature target.  The needed mixed-face estimate is plausibly the
unpublished result cited in Guth's later papers as \emph{On the 2-dilation of
mappings between 4-dimensional rectangles}.  The exact sufficient inequality to
look for is
\[
 {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}
 +{R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}\over S_1\sqrt{S_3S_4}}
 +{\Vol(R)\over S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4}\gtrsim 1,
\]
or an equivalent hard-regime statement.  Locating the thesis/preprint or a
precise proof of this inequality remains a high-priority task.

\section*{Round 22 notes: penalized form and failed level-slice shortcut}

Council suggestions converged on a penalized Manhattan deformation.  A useful
formal adjustment is to choose fillings by the functional
\[
 \Phi_y(B)=\Mass B+\frac{\mu}{q}\int_Q\Mass(B\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dz .
\]
For each fixed filling \(B\), the all-coordinate plaque Fubini estimate is pointwise
in \(y\):
\[
 \frac1q\int_Q\Mass(B\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dz
 \le C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\Mass B.
\]
Thus, in the hard regime \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), a near-minimizer of
\(\Phi_y\) is still a pointwise near-minimizer for mass if \(\kappa\ll\mu^{-1}\).
This removes a measurable-selection nuisance and suggests the atomic comparison
\[
 \Mass C_{y,z}\le C(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1)
        +C\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z),
\]
where \(C_{y,z}\) is a competitor filling of \(Z_y\).  Averaging in \(z\) gives
LE-HAF/(13).  The actual construction of \(C_{y,z}\) remains the hard point.

A tempting \(f_4\)- or \(f_1\)-level shortcut was checked again.  For a regular level
\(M_t=\{f_1=t\}\), the map \(g=(f_2,f_3,f_4)\) has \(\|\Lambda^2 dg\|\le1\) and
\(\|dg\|\le |\nabla f_1|^{-1}\).  If one could force preimages of the target
intervals \([0,S_2]\times\{y_3,y_4\}\) to fill inside the target slice
\(\{x_1=t\}\), the one-dimensional argument would give the desired
\(S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) per \(t\).  This is invalid for pair maps: in the full
target rectangle those intervals fill through the \(x_1\)-faces with area
\(O(S_1S_2)\), not \(O(S_2S_3)\).  The identity map shows that no unrestricted
level-wise statement can be true.  Any valid argument must prevent this escape
only in an averaged hard-regime sense; this is again equivalent to the missing
parametric tightening.

Directional rational-homotopy route to probe next: specialize Guth's
second-order linking proof for \(n=4,k_1=k_2=k_3=2\) to coordinate-pair maps
adapted to \((f_3,f_4)\) and \((f_1,f_2)\), and keep the full directional terms
instead of bounding by the sorted product \(R_2R_3\).  The generic ellipsoid
bound gives \(R_1R_2^2R_3^2R_4\gtrsim S_1S_2^2S_3^2S_4\), too weak.  A
coordinate-specific invariant would need to exploit \(R_1\ll S_1\) and
\(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\) to replace the domain factor \(R_2R_3\) by something
smaller on the hard-regime family.  No such invariant has yet been isolated.





\section*{Round 23 notes: fixed-puncture correction and current status}

The latest critic and council replies indicate that the literal atomic comparison
\[
 \Mass C_{y,z}\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1+\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)
\]
is not the right statement.  The problem is not just measurability: a chain map
cannot sharply keep \(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z\) and collapse its complement
without paying for the boundary/interface of the cut.  The term
\(\partial(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\) can be arbitrarily large while the bad mass is
small, and penalized mass-minimality with the weight
\(1+\mu q^{-1}|\{z:x\in\mathcal B_z\}|\) gives no control of those seams.

A more credible version is cellular.  Choose a shifted cubulation and first replace
\(B_y\) by a cubical near-minimizer \(B_{y,\theta}\).  Define
\(\mathcal B^{\rm cell}_{z,\theta}\) as a union of whole cells whose prescribed
Manhattan coordinate-plaque tracks have \(F\)-image containing the puncture \(z\).
Then a chain map/homotopy template would be
\[
   C_{y,z,\theta}=H_{z,\theta}(Z_y)+P_{z,\theta}(B_{y,\theta}),
\]
where \(P\) commutes with boundary and sends good cells to \(\partial R\) (hence to
zero relatively), and \(H\) is the homotopy from the identity to \(P\) on 2-chains.
The needed estimates become
\[
 \Mass H_{z,\theta}(Z_y)\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1,
\]
and
\[
 \mathbb E_{z,\theta}\Mass(B_{y,\theta}\llcorner\mathcal B^{\rm cell}_{z,\theta})
 \lesssim {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}\Mass B_y .
\]
This avoids sharp-cut seams but introduces the hard issue of defining the cubical
bad-star so that the averaged bad mass is still controlled only by coordinate
2-plaque areas \(R_iR_j\le R_3R_4\).  If badness is defined by enlarged cells or
near misses, the area bound can fail because \(F\) may have large one-dimensional
oscillation with small two-dimensional Jacobian.

The "flap" objection: a slice \(Z_y\) can contain large regions on which
\(dG=(df_1,df_2)\) is tiny or zero.  Any physical source deformation of such flaps
costs ordinary mass, not \(E_y\).  Therefore a proof of the low-energy term cannot be
a pointwise source push of all of \(Z_y\).  It must either ignore null-homologous
flaps using the chosen filling \(B_y\) or work cohomologically/parametrically so that
only the degree-carrying part of \(G_\#Z_y\) is charged to \(E_y\).  This reinforces
that a successful argument must use the whole two-parameter family and not isolated
fiber geometry.

The coordinate-specific second-order linking probe from the compute worker did not
close the gap.  Specializing Guth's \(L_2\) invariant to \(n=4,k_1=k_2=k_3=2\) and
retaining directional terms gives a domain upper bound with an unavoidable
\(R_2R_3\Vol(R)\) term.  The resulting target lower monomial is
\[
  R_1R_2^2R_3^2R_4 \gtrsim S_1S_2^2S_3^2S_4,
\]
or equivalently \(\Vol(R)R_2R_3\gtrsim \Vol(S)S_2S_3\), which is too weak.  Even
optimistic Hamilton-path coordinate choices do not imply the desired
\(\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\).  The residual exponent model
\(S=(1,1,L,L)\), \(R=(L^{-1/5},L^{13/15},L^{13/15},L^{13/15})\) still satisfies all
currently certified scalar/directional inequalities while missing the desired bound by
\(L^{-1/10}\).

The public-source status is unchanged.  Guth's arXiv:0802.3549 explicitly cites an
unpublished preprint, \emph{On the 2-dilation of mappings between 4-dimensional
rectangles}, and says the 4D rectangle mapping problem was solved there with a
complicated case distinction.  No copy or exact theorem statement has been located in
the available searches.  Quoting that preprint as a black box would make the present
problem a corollary only if the missing mixed hard-regime inequality is indeed one of
its cases; without the text, this is not a self-contained proof.




\section*{Asymptotic counterexample from Guth Chapter 8.2}

A decisive obstruction to the proposed theorem is furnished by the upper-bound
constructions in Guth's thesis, Chapter 8.2.  The theorem statement asks for a
positive universal \(\kappa\) such that, if \(R_1\le \kappa S_1\) and a
degree-one pair map has \(\Dil_2\le1\), then either
\(R_3R_4>\kappa S_3S_4\) or
\[
 \Vol(R)>\kappa S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
\]
But Guth's double-pinching and interpolated short-side-stretch maps give a
family for which all three corresponding ratios tend to zero.

The useful fixed-constant version is as follows.  Guth's double pinching map
has uniformly bounded \(2\)-dilation under
\[
R_1<A,\qquad A^2<R_2R_3,\qquad A^3B<R_1R_2R_3R_4,
\]
and maps
\[
R_1\times R_2\times R_3\times R_4
 \to (R_1^2/A)\times A\times A\times B
\]
(up to the harmless OCR ambiguity \(R_1/A\) versus \(R_1^2/A\), irrelevant
when \(R_1=1\)).  With
\[
R_1=1,\quad R_2=R_3=R_4=3L^{2/3},\quad A=2,\quad B=2L^2,
\]
the strict inequalities are \(1<2\), \(4<9L^{4/3}\), and \(16L^2<27L^2\).
Thus there is a bounded-\(2\)-dilation degree-one map
\[
X_0(L)=(1,3L^{2/3},3L^{2/3},3L^{2/3})
 \to X_1(L)=(1/2,2,2,2L^2).
\]

The interpolated short-side stretch from the technical remark maps
\[
(R_1,R_2,R_3,R_4)\to
(\lambda R_1,\lambda^{-3}R_2,A,B)
\]
with uniformly bounded \(2\)-dilation when
\[
\lambda^4<R_2/R_1,\qquad R_3<A<(R_3R_4)^{1/2},\qquad AB=R_3R_4 .
\]
For \(X_1(L)=(1/2,2,2,2L^2)\), choose
\[
\lambda=1,\qquad A=L,\qquad B=4L.
\]
Then \(1<4\), \(2<L<2L\), and \(AB=4L^2=R_3R_4\).  Hence
\[
X_1(L)\to S(L)=(1/2,2,L,4L)
\]
with uniformly bounded \(2\)-dilation.

Finally let
\[
R^0(L)=(L^{-1/6},3L^{5/6},3L^{5/6},3L^{5/6}).
\]
The diagonal linear map \(R^0(L)\to X_0(L)\) has singular values
\(L^{1/6},L^{-1/6},L^{-1/6},L^{-1/6}\), so its \(2\)-dilation is \(1\).
The composition \(F_L:R^0(L)\to S(L)\) has \(\Dil_2\le D\) for a universal
\(D\).  Replacing \(R^0(L)\) by \(R(L)=\sqrt D\,R^0(L)\) and precomposing by
the homothety \(R(L)\to R^0(L)\) gives a degree-one map \(R(L)\to S(L)\) with
\(\Dil_2\le1\).

The three ratios are
\[
R_1/S_1=2\sqrt D\,L^{-1/6},\qquad
R_3R_4/(S_3S_4)=\frac{9D}{4}L^{-1/3},
\]
and
\[
\Vol(R)/(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4)
 =\frac{27D^2}{2\sqrt2}L^{-1/6}.
\]
They tend to zero.  Therefore for any proposed positive \(\kappa\), large
\(L\) violates the theorem's conclusion.  This counterexample also explains
why attempts to prove a stronger fixed-puncture or cellular comparison were
failing: the desired inequality is simply false for arbitrary degree-one maps,
even though weaker first-alternative slicing estimates remain true in their
proper regime.



\section*{Round 25 correction after referee report}

The counterexample section immediately above is invalid.  The second asserted
Guth-map
\[
[0,\tfrac12]\times[0,2]\times[0,2]\times[0,2L^2]
\longrightarrow
[0,\tfrac12]\times[0,2]\times[0,L]\times[0,4L]
\]
would have source-target quotients
\[
Q=(1,1,L/2,2/L).
\]
Guth's published Estimate 1 for degree-nonzero maps of pairs, with
\(n=4,k=2,j=0,l=3\), gives
\[
\Dil_2\ge c(4)(Q_1Q_2Q_3)^{2/3}
     =c(4)(L/2)^{2/3}.
\]
Thus this map cannot have uniformly bounded \(2\)-dilation.  The Chapter 8.2
technical remark was over-interpreted or used outside its valid range.  The
counterexample must be discarded.

The current canonical answer has been replaced by a rigorous partial proof:
(1) the central-plane filling calibration;
(2) the first-alternative implication
\(R_1R_2^2\lesssim S_1S_2S_3\Rightarrow R_3R_4\gtrsim S_3S_4\);
(3) the two unconditional hard-regime estimates
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim \alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4,\qquad
\Vol(R)\gtrsim \alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4,
\quad \alpha=R_1/S_1,
\]
and their optimized consequence
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4.
\]
This leaves exactly the factor \((S_2/S_3)^{1/10}\) compared to the desired
second alternative.

The remaining essential open subproblem is still the hard-regime averaged
parametric tightening estimate for the family of slices
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\):
\[
 \int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy,
\]
where
\[
 I=\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{TZ_y}\|^2.
\]
Weighted coarea gives \(I\le\Vol(R)\), and the target calibration gives
\(\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\).  If this averaged estimate were proved, then
for \(R_1\ll S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\) the final term would absorb and
force \(I\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), closing the theorem.  Known
published monomial estimates and single-slice isoperimetry do not by themselves
supply this mixed anisotropic estimate; the stress family
\[
S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad
R=(L^{-1/5},L^{13/15},L^{13/15},L^{13/15})
\]
continues to saturate all currently certified scalar estimates while missing the
desired bound by \(L^{-1/10}\).



\section*{Averaged tightening reformulation and failed direct closures}

A cleaner way to package the hard estimate is via the central slices
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\), \(y\in Q\), and
\(G=(f_1,f_2)\).  Define
\[
 E_y=\int_{Z_y}\|dG|_{TZ_y}\|_{\mathrm{op}}^2\,d\mathcal H^2,\qquad
 I=\int_Q E_y\,dy,\qquad A=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy .
\]
At a regular point of \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), put \(K=\ker dF\).  If
\(\mu=\|dG|_K\|_{\mathrm{op}}\) and \(\tau_1,\tau_2\) are the singular values of
\(dF\) on a normal two-plane, then \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) gives
\(\mu\tau_i\lesssim1\) for \(i=1,2\) (use the target splitting
\(\mathbb R^4=\mathbb R^2_G\oplus\mathbb R^2_F\)).  Hence
\(\mu^2J_2F\lesssim1\).  Coarea gives
\[
 I=\int_{F^{-1}(Q)}\|dG|_{\ker dF}\|^2 J_2F\lesssim \Vol(R).
\]
Since \(f_\# Z_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\}\), each slice has
\(\int_{Z_y}J_2G\ge S_1S_2\), and hence \(E_y\ge S_1S_2\); this by itself only
recovers \(\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4\).

The desired theorem follows from the averaged tightening estimate
\[
 A\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}A. \tag{HAF}
\]
Indeed \(A\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3|Q|\sim S_1S_2S_3^2S_4\) by the target calibration.
If \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), the last term absorbs.  If
\(I\le\delta T\), \(T=S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), and \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\), then
\[
 R_1I\le \kappa\delta\, S_1^2S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
       \le \kappa\delta\, S_1S_2S_3^2S_4
\]
because \(S_1\le(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\), while
\[
 I^2/(S_1|Q|)\lesssim \delta^2 S_1S_2S_3^2S_4.
\]
Thus \(I\gtrsim T\), and weighted coarea gives \(\Vol(R)\gtrsim T\).

A tempting stronger single-slice estimate
\[
 \Fill_R(Z_y)\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1
\]
is false without using family compatibility.  Example: take a rectangle with
\(R_1\ll S_1\) but with long source directions allowing a copy of
\([0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\) to sit at distance \(\sim S_3\) from the boundary, and
let \(G\) be essentially the identity on this copy.  Then
\(E_y\sim S_1S_2\) but \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\sim S_1S_2S_3\), much larger when
\(S_3\gg S_2\).  This example cannot occur as the whole central fiber family
when \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\) without paying elsewhere; HAF is precisely the needed
averaged version.

Curve-slicing probe.  For \(t\in(0,S_1)\) and \(y\in Q\), the slice
\(C_{t,y}=\langle [R,\partial R],(f_1,f_3,f_4),(t,y)\rangle\) pushes forward to
the interval \(\{t\}\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\}\).  A target calibration implies
any relative \(2\)-chain filling this interval has area \(\gtrsim S_1S_2\) for
central \(t\).  Rectangular 1-cycle filling then suggests
\(\Mass C_{t,y}\gtrsim S_1S_2/R_1\).  However the coarea identity controlling
\(\int \Mass C_{t,y}\,dt\,dy\) involves the uncontrolled \(3\)-Jacobian of
\((f_1,f_3,f_4)\).  The controlled weighted identity
\[
 \int\!\!\int_{C_{t,y}} |df_2|\,dt\,dy=\int |df_1\wedge df_2\wedge df_3\wedge
 df_4|\le \Vol(R)
\]
only recovers the trivial volume bound \(S_1S_2S_3S_4\).  Thus curve slicing does
not close the exponent gap without an additional mechanism converting long
low-\(df_2\) curves into energy/filling cost.

Another useful algebraic check: all target-coordinate pair slicings combined
with the ordinary rectangle profile still allow the exponent model
\(S=(1,1,L,L)\), \(R=(L^{-1/5},L^{13/15},L^{13/15},L^{13/15})\), which has
\(\Vol(R)=L^{12/5}\) while the desired bound is \(L^{5/2}\).  For the central
\((f_3,f_4)\)-slicing, the profile lower bound is saturated by the
\((R_1L)^{1/2}\) branch.  Other pair slicings are weaker in this model.  Hence
the remaining obstruction is not a missed coordinate-pair slicing consequence.



\section*{Round 27 notes: profile correction and further closure attempts}

The isoperimetric-profile statement in the current answer was corrected to include
Guth's small-volume hypothesis.  The valid form used for relative \(2\)-cycles in a
4-rectangle \(R\) is: for universal \(c_*,C_*\), if
\(A=\Mass Z\le c_*R_1R_2\), then
\[
 \Fill_R(Z)\le C_*\max\{A^{3/2},A^2/R_1\}.
\]
Consequently,
\[
 \Fill_R(Z)\ge L\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 \Mass Z\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,L^{2/3},(R_1L)^{1/2}\}.
\]
The smallness condition cannot be omitted: in
\([0,1]^2\times[0,L]^2\), the central copy
\([0,1]^2\times\{(L/2,L/2)\}\) has area \(R_1R_2=1\) but filling volume
\(\gtrsim L\).

I checked several possible ways to close the remaining hard-regime gap.

1.  \(f_1\)-level reduction.  For a regular \(t\), the slice
\(M_t=\langle [R,\partial R],f_1,t\rangle\) maps by
\((f_2,f_3,f_4)\) with relative degree one to the \(3\)-rectangle
\([0,S_2]\times[0,S_3]\times[0,S_4]\).  If
\(s=|\nabla f_1|\) and \(w=s^{-1}\), then on \(M_t\) the singular values
\(\mu_i\) of \(dg=d(f_2,f_3,f_4)\) satisfy
\[
 \mu_1\le w,\qquad \mu_1\mu_2\le1.
\]
A tempting weighted 3-dimensional estimate would be
\[
 \int_{M_t}w\gtrsim S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
under \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\), which would integrate in \(t\) to the desired
volume bound.  The difficulty is that the natural 3D first-alternative proof
for maps from a 3-rectangle fills central target lines inside the level
\(M_t\).  A filling in the ambient 4-rectangle may leave \(M_t\), and then the
target filling lower bound drops from \(S_2S_3\) to only \(S_1S_2\).  Thus this
route still needs a genuine weighted/level-set isoperimetric theorem, not just
the ordinary 3D rectangle argument.

2.  Auxiliary composition with known Guth maps.  Postcomposing
\(f:R\to S\) with a degree-one map \(S\to T\) and applying Guth's monomial
Estimate 1 to \(R\to T\) can only help if \(T_1^3\Vol(T)/\Dil_2(S\to T)^3\)
is larger than \(S_1^3\Vol(S)\).  Simple linear changes do not help; stretching
one target direction forces large \(2\)-dilation unless all other directions are
shrunk.  Guth's short-side-stretch maps can improve the shortest side only when
there is room in \(S_2/S_1\) (and sometimes \(S_4/S_3\)); they do not address
the stress regime \(S=(1,1,L,L)\), where the missing factor comes from
\(S_3/S_2\).

3.  Precomposition by known maps into the hypothetical domain was also tested
at the exponent level.  The natural double-pinching predecessors of the stress
model have larger relevant monomial widths, so applying Estimate 1 to the
composite predecessor-to-\(S\) gives no contradiction.  This is consistent with
the earlier failed counterexample searches: the obstruction is not captured by a
single published Guth monomial estimate after an elementary auxiliary
composition.

4.  Boundary ellipsoid inequalities give, after adaptation to degree-one
boundary maps, a lower bound of the form
\(R_2R_3R_4\gtrsim S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) unless
\(R_3R_4\gtrsim S_3S_4\).  Multiplying by \(R_1\) yields only an
\(\alpha=R_1/S_1\) fraction of the desired 4D volume alternative, and
combining with the monomial estimate \(R_1^2\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1^3S_2S_3S_4\)
still leaves a power gap.

The current essential gap therefore remains the averaged parametric tightening
estimate
\[
 A\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}A.
\]
Any successful proof must use compatibility of the full two-parameter family of
central fibers, not merely the geometry of one fiber or one level set.



\section*{Round 28 notes: tightened target and literature status}

Easy referee objections fixed in the current answer: the slicing identity is
now justified by applying the constancy theorem to \(f_\#[R]\) in the interior
of \(S\), then slicing by \((x_3,x_4)\); and the weighted fiber energy is
defined with respect to the mass measure \(d\|Z_y\|\), not plain Hausdorff
measure.  The smoothing/PL approximation point is recorded as a relative
approximation with \(\Dil_2\le1+\varepsilon\), preserving degree.

The Advisory Council and the round-27 compute probe both agreed that two
tempting replacements for HAF do not close the gap.

1.  \(f_1=t\) level sets.  For
\(M_t=\langle [R],f_1,t\rangle\), \(g=(f_2,f_3,f_4)\), and
\(w=|\nabla f_1|^{-1}\), the robust pointwise consequences are
\[
 \|dg\|\le w,\qquad \|\Lambda^2 dg\|\le1,\qquad
 \int_{M_t}w\ge S_2S_3S_4.
\]
The stronger desired level estimate
\(\int_{M_t}w\gtrsim S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) is false in isolation: the identity
map on \((1,1,L,L)\) gives \(L^2\) on the left and \(L^{5/2}\) on the right.
Any valid level-set proof must use inter-level compatibility preventing
fillings from escaping through the short \(x_1\)-direction, i.e. essentially
the same parametric issue as HAF.

2.  Auxiliary pre/post-composition with known Guth rectangle maps.  In the
stress exponent model
\[
 S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad
 R=(L^{-1/5},L^{13/15},L^{13/15},L^{13/15}),
\]
all published Estimate-1 monomials are saturated or subcritical.  The compute
worker encoded the corrected Chapter 8.2 moves (linear, snake, codimension
snake, short-side stretch, pinch, double pinch, and the single-parameter
technical interpolation) and optimized all pre/post-composition words of depth
up to \(3\).  No positive Estimate-1 exponent appeared; the maximum remained
\(0\), usually saturating \((k,j,l)=(2,1,4)\).  This strongly suggests that a
new mixed/parametric estimate is needed rather than another scalar monomial.

A useful sufficient substitute for full HAF is the low-energy plaque version.
Let \(q=|Q|\),
\[
 \Omega=\{y:E_y\le4I/q\},\qquad |\Omega|\ge3q/4,
\]
and choose fillings \(B_y\) with \(\Mass B_y\le2\Fill_R(Z_y)\).  For \(z\in Q\)
let \({\mathcal B}_z\) be the union of points \(x\in R\) such that \(z\) lies
in the \(F\)-image of at least one coordinate \(2\)-plaque through \(x\).  Since
every coordinate plaque has \(F\)-image area \(\le R_iR_j\le R_3R_4\),
\[
 {1\over q}\int_Q \Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
 \le C {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}\Mass B_y.
\]
Thus the following would suffice:
\[
\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
\le C\int_\Omega\left(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\right)dy
 +{C\over q}\int_Q\int_\Omega\Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dy\,dz.
\tag{LE-HAF}
\]
On \(\Omega\), \(\int_\Omega E_y^2\le(4I/q)\int_\Omega E_y\le4I^2/q\).
After plaque averaging, the last term is
\(\lesssim (R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)dy\), absorbable in the hard
regime.  Since every \(y\in\Omega\) still has
\(\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\), LE-HAF gives the desired lower bound for
\(I\) and hence for \(\Vol(R)\).

The conceptual proof skeleton for LE-HAF is a Manhattan deformation: use a
source-coordinate or target-coordinate flattening to crush low-\(G\)-energy
flaps at cost \(R_1E_y\), fill the flattened cycle through the \(G\)-target at
cost \(E_y^2/S_1\), and splice arbitrary filling only inside
\({\mathcal B}_z\).  The two technical traps remain (a) controlling flaps whose
ordinary mass is large but \(G\)-energy is small, and (b) avoiding uncontrolled
seam terms when cutting by \({\mathcal B}_z\).  The plaque formulation is meant
to isolate exactly where such seam terms may be paid.

Web/literature search in this round located the arXiv page for Guth's
\emph{Directional isoperimetric inequalities and rational homotopy invariants}
(arXiv:0802.3549) and confirmed that it advertises directionally-dependent
isoperimetric inequalities, but did not locate the cited unpublished preprint
\emph{On the 2-dilation of mappings between 4-dimensional rectangles}.  A
compute-agent literature task has been requested to retrieve Guth's thesis or
the unpublished preprint and identify whether it contains exactly the missing
LE-HAF/HAF step or another proof of the stated theorem.


\section*{Round 29 notes: LE-HAF status, chain-map skeleton, and extra scalar checks}

The current answer should not call the low-energy plaque estimate equivalent to
the averaged tightening estimate.  It is only a sufficient substitute in the
hard regime after restricting to
\[
 \Omega=\{y:E_y\le 4I/|Q|\}.
\]
The implication is: LE-HAF plus the plaque Fubini bound gives
\[
 \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \lesssim R_1I+I^2/(S_1|Q|)
       +(R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy.
\]
If \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\), absorb the last term; since every \(y\in\Omega\)
still has \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\) and \(|\Omega|\gtrsim |Q|\), this
is enough.  But LE-HAF has not been proved and may require modification.

Refined diagnosis of the plaque route.  A sharp restriction
\(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z\) is not a valid chain-level operation for the
desired inequality, because
\(\partial(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\) creates an uncontrolled seam on
\(\partial{\mathcal B}_z\).  The cleaner formal skeleton is to choose a
cubulation \(K_\theta\) and, for each puncture \(z\), build a relative chain map
\[
 P_{z,\theta}:C_*(K_\theta)\to C_*(\partial R)+C_*(\hbox{bad cells})
\]
with a chain homotopy
\[
 \partial H_{z,\theta}+H_{z,\theta}\partial=P_{z,\theta}-\operatorname{Id}.
\]
Then for a filling \(B_y\) with \(\partial B_y=Z_y\),
\[
 C_{y,z,\theta}=H_{z,\theta}(Z_y)-P_{z,\theta}(B_y)
\]
fills \(Z_y\) relatively, and no seam appears.  A proof would need
\[
 \Mass H_{z,\theta}(Z_y)
   \lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1
\]
and an averaged bad-cell estimate
\[
 \mathbb E_{z,\theta}\Mass(P_{z,\theta}(B_y)_{\rm bad})
   \lesssim (R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\Mass B_y.
\]
The latter is plausible only if the tracks are controlled by exact coordinate
2-plaques; thickened bad cells or near-miss neighborhoods are dangerous because
rank-one oscillation can have tiny area but large neighborhoods in \(Q\).

Flap obstruction.  Any pointwise deformation of the whole geometric fiber
\(Z_y\) at cost \(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\) is false: a large-mass flap on which
\(dG\approx0\) can have tiny \(G\)-energy.  Such a flap must either be
algebraically invisible after pushforward by \(G\) or be absorbed statistically
through the chosen filling \(B_y\) and the bad-cell term.  Therefore a successful
proof must be ``fill first, then apply a chain-map deformation,'' not a literal
source homotopy of all of \(Z_y\).

A possible stronger obstruction to the literal LE-HAF is a multi-sheeted fiber
family: make \(F\) cover \(Q\) many times while \(G\) carries the required
degree on only one sheet and is nearly constant on the other sheets.  The dead
sheets have small \(E_y\) but may contribute to \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\).  I do not
currently have a fully verified degree-one pair map in the hard regime realizing
this model, so it is a warning about the formulation rather than a counterexample
to the theorem.  It does show why any proof should measure the degree-carrying
chain algebraically and should not rely on the ordinary mass of the whole
geometric fiber.

Extra scalar checks.  Since \(\Dil_2\le1\) implies \(\Dil_3\le1\), Guth's
Estimate 1 can also be applied with \(k=3\).  The useful new monomial is
\[
 R_1R_2\,\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1^2S_2^2S_3S_4
\]
from \(j=2,l=4\), and the \(j=1,l=4\) estimate is
\[
 (R_1/S_1)^{1/2}\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4.
\]
These do not close the hard exponent model.  In normalized exponents
\(S=(1,1,L,L)\), the stress family
\[
 R=(L^{-1/5},L^{13/15},L^{13/15},L^{13/15})
\]
satisfies the \(k=2,3,4\) monomials, the first-alternative failure condition
\(R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\), and
\(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\), while
\[
 \Vol(R)=L^{12/5}=L^{-1/10}
       \bigl(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\bigr).
\]
Thus the remaining gap is genuinely mixed/parametric, not a missed scalar
monomial.

Current polished current-theoretic point for the first alternative: from
relative degree one, \(f_\#[[R]]-[[S]]\) vanishes in the interior by the
constancy theorem and is supported on \(\partial S\); because \(\partial S\) is
3-rectifiable, it supports no nonzero integral 4-current.  Therefore equality
holds as relative 4-currents before slicing by \((x_3,x_4)\).




\section*{Exact-plaque chain-map formulation}

For the central target square \(Q\subset [0,S_3]\times[0,S_4]\), write
\(F=(f_3,f_4)\), \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), and
\(Z_y=\langle [[R]],F,y\rangle\).  The energy used in the averaged tightening
attempt is
\[
E_y=\int \|dG|_{T Z_y}\|_{HS}^2\,d\|Z_y\|.
\]
The coarea calculation gives \(\int_QE_y\,dy\lesssim \Vol(R)\): at rank-two
points of \(dF\), if \(K=\ker dF\) and \(\tau_i\) are the two singular values of
\(dF\) on \(K^\perp\), then \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) implies
\(\|dG|_K\|^2\tau_i^2\lesssim1\), hence
\(\|dG|_K\|^2J_2F\lesssim1\).

For \(z\in Q\), define the exact coordinate-plaque bad set
\[
{\mathcal B}_z=\{x\in R:\ z\in F(P_{ij}(x))\hbox{ for at least one coordinate
two-plaque }P_{ij}(x)\}.
\]
For fixed \(x\), the set of \(z\)'s for which \(x\in{\mathcal B}_z\) has measure
\(\lesssim R_3R_4\), because every coordinate two-plaque through \(x\) has area
\(\le R_3R_4\) and the \(F\)-area of its image is at most its source area.  Hence
\[
|Q|^{-1}\int_Q \Mass(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
   \lesssim (R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\Mass B.
\]

A sufficient exact-plaque chain-map lemma is the following.  For a.e. \(y,z\) and
each relative filling \(B_y\) of \(Z_y\), there should exist relative chain
operators \(P_z,H_z\) on \(B_y,Z_y\) with
\[
\partial H_z(T)+H_z(\partial T)=P_z(T)-T,\qquad
\partial P_z(T)=P_z(\partial T),
\]
\[
\Mass H_z(Z_y)\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1,\qquad
\Mass P_z(B_y)\lesssim \Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z).
\]
Then \(C_{y,z}=P_z(B_y)-H_z(Z_y)\) fills \(Z_y\) with no seam term:
\[
\partial C_{y,z}=P_zZ_y-(P_zZ_y-Z_y)=Z_y.
\]
Averaging and using the exact-plaque Fubini estimate gives
\[
\int \Fill(Z_y)\,dy
\lesssim R_1\int E_y\,dy+{(\int E_y\,dy)^2\over S_1|Q|}
 +(R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\int \Fill(Z_y)\,dy
\]
(after restricting to the good set \(E_y\le 4|Q|^{-1}\int E\)).  This is the
precise missing family-level tightening estimate.

Two obstructions to simpler proofs:
\begin{itemize}
\item Thickened bad cells are not acceptable.  A rank-one comb plaque
\(F(u,v)=\gamma_N(u)\) has exact image area zero but a \(c/N\)-neighborhood can
occupy a fixed fraction of \(Q\).  Thus an ordinary cubical deformation using
cell-stars loses the \(R_3R_4/S_3S_4\) self-absorption factor.
\item A literal pointwise sweep of all of \(Z_y\) cannot satisfy the energy bound.
In the local affine model with singular values \(L,L^{-1},L^{-1},L^{-1}\) and
\(T Z_y=\operatorname{span}(e_3,e_4)\), the \(G\)-energy density on \(T Z_y\) is
\(O(L^{-2})\), while sweeping the sheet through a source direction has track
density \(O(1)\).  Low-energy dead sheets must be killed algebraically through
\(P_z(B_y)\), not swept as ordinary mass.
\end{itemize}

The \(f_1\)-level reformulation remains useful but incomplete.  For
\(u=f_1\), a central regular level \(M_t=\langle [[R]],u,t\rangle\) maps by
\((f_2,f_3,f_4)\) with relative degree \(1\) to the \(3\)-rectangle
\([0,S_2]\times[0,S_3]\times[0,S_4]\).  With weight
\(w=|\nabla u|^{-1}\), the pointwise singular-value inequalities give only
\(\int_{M_t} w\gtrsim S_2S_3S_4\) in general; the identity map on
\((1,1,L,L)\) shows that a stronger unconditioned level estimate
\(\int w\gtrsim S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) is false.  Any valid level proof must use
the hard-regime hypothesis \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\) to prevent filling from escaping
through the ambient \(x_1\)-direction.


\section*{Round 31 notes: restricted averaged estimate and next search}

The referee's latest objection was correct: the current draft cannot be answer-ready

theorem, is proved or replaced by a cited theorem.  I rewrote the reduction in the
canonical answer so that the logically sufficient estimate is the restricted
low-energy form

 A_\Omega:=\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
    +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}A_\Omega,

where

after absorbing the last term in the hard regime \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\).

 \lesssim {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}\Mass B,
\]
with \({\mathcal B}_z\) defined using all six coordinate \(2\)-plaques.  The

\Mass H_z(Z_y)\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1,\qquad

\]
A standard cubical deformation is not enough unless the bad cells are defined by
exact coordinate-plaque tracks; thickened stars lose the area-ratio

Potentially decisive literature target: Guth's unpublished preprint

arXiv:0802.3549, reportedly solves the 4D rectangle mapping problem by a complicated

\[
 {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}
 +{R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}\over S_1\sqrt{S_3S_4}}
 +{\Vol(R)\over S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4}\gtrsim1,
\]
or a theorem immediately implying it for degree-one maps of pairs with

\section*{Round 32 notes: exact-plaque lemma demoted and alternative targets}

The pointwise exact-plaque chain-map lemma in the previous answer is too strong
as a literal statement.  The obstruction is not merely a technical seam issue.
One can add, at least locally and after a small perturbation, a large sheet on
which \(F=(f_3,f_4)\) and \(G=(f_1,f_2)\) are nearly constant and whose image
avoids a fixed puncture \(z\).  Such a sheet may appear in a fiber \(Z_y\) with
arbitrarily small \(G\)-energy and with no mass in the exact plaque bad set
\({\mathcal B}_z\).  Any physical chain homotopy of the whole current \(Z_y\)
would have to sweep this null sheet and pay ordinary mass, contradicting an
estimate depending only on \(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\).  Therefore the missing
tightening cannot be a pointwise source deformation of every component of
\(Z_y\).

The robust target is the restricted averaged estimate
\[
 A_\Omega:=\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
 +\frac{C}{|Q|}\int_Q\int_\Omega
   \Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dy\,dz. \tag{AT}
\]
Here \(B_y\) are chosen near-minimizing fillings of \(Z_y\).  After the exact
plaque Fubini estimate this gives
\[
A_\Omega\le C(R_1I+I^2/(S_1|Q|))
  +C(R_3R_4/S_3S_4)A_\Omega,
\]
which is absorbable in the hard regime.  The answer has been rewritten to state
\((\mathrm{AT})\) directly as the missing averaged input rather than presenting
the false pointwise chain map as an ordinary lemma.

A plausible correct formulation should work in a quotient or dual sense.  The
extra low-energy null sheets should be algebraically cancelled by \(B_y\), while
only the part of \(B_y\) carried by exact coordinate-plaque bad sets is charged.
Equivalently, for every relative \(2\)-cochain or smooth \(2\)-form \(\omega\)
with \(\|d\omega\|_\infty\le1\), one would like an inequality of the form
\[
 \int_{Z_y}\omega
 \lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1
       + \int_{B_y\cap{\mathcal B}_z}|d\omega|
\]
after averaging in \(y,z\), with no sharp-restriction seam.  This is the dual of
a cellular acyclic-carrier tightening but avoids physically sweeping the whole
fiber.

A second possible replacement is an ``essential filling'' norm.  Instead of
\(\Fill_R(Z_y)\), define the least mass of a \(3\)-chain \(C\) whose boundary
maps under \(f\) to the target plane \(P_y\).  This norm is still bounded below
by \(cS_1S_2S_3\) because \(f_\#C\) fills \(P_y\) and \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), but it
ignores null sheets in \(Z_y\).  The difficulty is to construct such a
degree-carrying boundary in \(R\) from the current \(Z_y\) with cost controlled
by \(E_y\); selecting one sheet of \(G:Z_y\to[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\) creates
uncontrolled switching boundaries unless an averaged/cellular selection theorem
is available.

The level-set route remains another possible formulation.  For regular
\(t\), \(M_t=f_1^{-1}(t)\) maps by \((f_2,f_3,f_4)\) with degree one to the
\(3\)-rectangle \([0,S_2]\times[0,S_3]\times[0,S_4]\), and
\[
 \Vol(R)=\int_0^{S_1}\int_{M_t}|\nabla f_1|^{-1}\,d\mathcal H^3\,dt
\]
(up to the usual current-theoretic multiplicity interpretation).  A sufficient
weighted three-dimensional statement would be
\[
 \int_{M_t}|\nabla f_1|^{-1}\,d\mathcal H^3
   \gtrsim S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
for most central \(t\), under \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\).  The unconditioned version
is false (identity on \((1,1,L,L)\)), so any proof must use the small source
\((x_3,x_4)\)-plaque area.  This may be a cleaner way to express the same
parametric obstruction as \((\mathrm{AT})\).



\section*{Essential filling formulation}

For central regular \(y\), set
\[
 \EFill_f(y)=\inf\{\Mass C:\ C\in I_3(R,\partial R),\
 f_\#(\partial C)=\pm P_y\hbox{ in }I_2(S,\partial S)\}.
\]
This is weaker than \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\) and intentionally ignores components of
the geometric fiber \(Z_y=\langle [R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\) that are null after
applying \(f\).  The calibration lower bound survives:
if \(f_\#(\partial C)=P_y\), then \(f_\#C\) is a target filling of \(P_y\);
since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\),
\[
 \Mass C\ge\Mass(f_\#C)\ge cS_1S_2S_3.
\]
Thus \(A_\Omega^{\rm ess}:=\int_\Omega\EFill_f(y)\,dy
\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3|Q|\).

An essential averaged tightening estimate sufficient for the theorem is
\[
 A_\Omega^{\rm ess}
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
 +\frac{C}{|Q|}\int_Q\int_\Omega
      \Mass(C_y^0\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz,       \tag{EAT}
\]
where \(C_y^0\) are measurable near-minimizers for \(\EFill_f(y)\).  The
standard exact-plaque Fubini estimate gives
\[
 {|Q|}^{-1}\int_Q\Mass(C_y^0\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dz
 \lesssim (R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\Mass C_y^0,
\]
so in the hard regime the bad term is absorbed and the same algebra as for
\((\mathrm{AT})\) yields
\(I\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), hence the desired volume lower bound by
weighted coarea.

Important caveat for future proof attempts: the energy \(E_y\) is attached to
the actual slice \(Z_y\), whereas a near-minimizing essential chain \(C_y^0\)
need not satisfy \(\partial C_y^0=Z_y\).  Therefore the naive chain-map identity
\(P(C_y^0)-H(Z_y)\) does not automatically have the desired boundary or
essential boundary.  A proof of \((\mathrm{EAT})\) must either:
\begin{enumerate}
\item choose essential chains with boundaries related to \(Z_y\) by a controlled
\(f\)-null correction and show that the carrier preserves this relation; or
\item work in a dual formulation where a cochain homotopy acts on the target
degree functional rather than on sharply restricted source chains; or
\item build \(C_y^0\) directly from an averaged selection of the degree-carrying
part of \(Z_y\), with all switching seams paid by the \(E_y^2/S_1\) term.
\end{enumerate}
This boundary-compatibility issue is separate from the older null-sheet
obstruction and must be resolved to turn the essential formulation into a proof.



\section*{Round 34 notes: quotient essential tightening formulation}

Latest referee objection: the document was conditional on EAT.  Council and compute
feedback identified a sharper boundary-compatibility problem.  If
\(C_y^0\) is an essential filling with \(W_y=\partial C_y^0\), then
\(f_\#W_y=f_\#Z_y=P_y\), but \(N_y=W_y-Z_y\) is only \(f\)-null.  A source
cellular projection \(P_z\) does not generally preserve \(f\)-nullity:
\(f_\#N_y=0\) need not imply \(f_\#P_zN_y=0\).  Thus the primal chain-level
EAT cannot be proved by a standard Federer--Fleming deformation of
\(C_y^0\) combined with a homotopy of \(Z_y\).

Corrected target: work in the quotient current complex
\[
  \mathcal Q_\ast=N_\ast(R,\partial R;\mathbb R)/\ker f_\# .
\]
The relaxed essential filling norm is the filling norm of the class \([Z_y]\)
in \(\mathcal Q_\ast\).  The desired averaged estimate becomes
\[
 A_\Omega
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
 +\frac{C}{q}\int_Q\int_\Omega
       \Mass(C_y^0\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dy\,dz ,
\]
for quotient/relaxed near-minimizers \(C_y^0\).  The absorption algebra is the
same as before, using the exact-plaque Fubini estimate
\[
 q^{-1}\int_Q\Mass(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
 \le C(R_3R_4/S_3S_4)\Mass B .
\]
What remains is a quotient-equivariant exact-plaque cochain homotopy.  A dual
Hahn--Banach approach may be cleaner: for quotient 2-cochains \(\phi\) with
\(\|\delta\phi\|\le1\), prove after averaging in punctures \(z\) a homotopy
identity whose \(K_z\delta\phi\) term is bounded by \(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\) and
whose residual \(\Pi_z\phi\) has coboundary supported in \({\mathcal B}_z\);
then pair \(\delta\Pi_z\phi\) with \(C_y^0\).  This is not currently proved.

\section*{Technical consolidation: essential averaged tightening and quotient obstruction}

A precise sufficient hard-case estimate is the following.  Let
\(F=(f_3,f_4)\), \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), \(Q\subset[0,S_3]\times[0,S_4]\) be the
central parameter rectangle, \(Z_y=\langle [[R]],F,y\rangle\), and
\[
E_y=\int_{Z_y}\|dG|_{TZ_y}\|_{HS}^2\,d\|Z_y\|,\qquad
I=\int_QE_y\,dy,\qquad q=|Q|.
\]
For regular \(F\)-points,
\[
\|dG|_{\ker dF}\|_{HS}^2J_2F\le 2.
\]
Proof: if \(a\ge b\) are the singular values of \(dF|_{(\ker dF)^\perp}\),
then \(J_2F=ab\).  For a unit vector \(w\in\ker dF\) and a unit vector \(v\)
with \(|dF(v)|=a\), the mixed component of \(df(w)\wedge df(v)\) has norm
\(|dG(w)|a\), so \(|dG(w)|a\le \Dil_2(f)\le1\); hence
\(|dG(w)|^2ab\le b/a\le1\), and summing over two orthonormal \(w\)'s gives
the displayed bound.  Coarea therefore gives \(I\lesssim\Vol(R)\).

Define the relaxed essential filling
\[
\EFill_f(y)=\inf\{\Mass C:C\in N_3(R,\partial R;\mathbb R),\
f_\#(\partial C)=\pm P_y\}.
\]
It is nonempty by filling the actual slice.  The central calibration in the
target gives \(\EFill_f(y)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\), because \(f_\#C\) fills
\(P_y\) and \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\).

For \(z\in Q\), let \({\mathcal B}_z\) be the exact coordinate-plaque bad set:
\(x\in{\mathcal B}_z\) iff \(z\) belongs to the \(F\)-image of one of the six
coordinate \(2\)-plaques through \(x\).  Exact plaques satisfy
\[
{1\over q}\int_Q \Mass(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
\lesssim {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}\Mass B
\]
for every normal \(3\)-current \(B\), since for each fixed \(x\) the set of
bad \(z\)'s has measure at most the sum of the six \(F\)-areas of coordinate
plaques through \(x\), each at most \(R_3R_4\).

A sufficient estimate (EAT) is:
\[
\int_\Omega \EFill_f(y)\,dy
\le C\left(R_1I+{I^2\over S_1q}\right)
 +{C\over q}\int_Q\int_\Omega
       \Mass(C_y^0\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dy\,dz,
\]
where \(\Omega=\{E_y\le4I/q\}\) and \(C_y^0\) are near-minimizing essential
fillings.  Together with the exact-plaque Fubini estimate this absorbs the bad
term when \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\).  If also \(R_1\ll S_1\), then a low value of
\(I\) makes \(R_1I+I^2/(S_1q)\) smaller than the calibration lower bound
\(\int_\Omega\EFill_f\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3q\); hence
\(I\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), and coarea gives the desired volume
alternative.

The obstruction to a standard quotient proof is finite-dimensional.  Take two
oriented \(2\)-cells \(A,A'\) with \(f_\#A=f_\#A'=P\).  Then
\(N=A-A'\in\ker f_\#\).  If an exact source bad set contains \(A\) but not
\(A'\), the source-local projection \(P_{\rm bad}\) satisfies
\(P_{\rm bad}A=A\), \(P_{\rm bad}A'=0\), and
\(f_\#P_{\rm bad}N=P\ne0\).  Dually, quotient cochains are vectors \((x,x)\)
on the ordered basis \((A,A')\), while restriction to the nonsaturated bad set
produces \((x,0)\), no longer a quotient cochain.  Thus a quotient-compatible
homotopy cannot at the same time have residual support in an arbitrary
unsaturated exact-plaque bad set unless a new nonlocal correction is supplied.
Any eventual proof of EAT must overcome exactly this source-local versus
\(f\)-saturation conflict, or replace the source bad term by a different
quantity for which an equally strong Fubini absorption remains true.




\section*{Round 36 notes: further direct attacks and requests}

This round rechecked several possible ways to bypass the averaged essential
tightening estimate.

1. Packing-width and ordinary target-subrectangle arguments reproduce Guth's
published monomial estimates.  Choosing many target blocks in the \((x_3,x_4)\)
directions with area comparable to \(R_3R_4\) only gives the known
\(P_{2,N}(R)\) bounds (the \(l=0,1,2\) packing-width branches); it never
generates the missing factor \(S_3^{1/2}S_2^{-1/2}\).  Using \(3\)-width and
\(\Dil_3\le1\) gives the monomial
\((R_1R_2)^2R_3R_4\gtrsim (S_1S_2)^2S_3S_4\), again insufficient.

2. Slicing by one target coordinate, e.g. \(f_4\), gives central target
\(3\)-planes with filling volume of order \(S_1S_2S_3S_4\), but the coarea
formula for \(f_4\) involves the uncontrolled one-Jacobian \(|\nabla f_4|\).
Weighted variants collapse back to the basic volume estimate.  Similarly,
\(f_1\)-level sets lead to a weighted \(3\)-dimensional problem whose
unconditioned version is false for the identity map on \((1,1,L,L)\).

3. Differential-form integration by parts with the coordinate forms
\(df_i\) recovers boundary-type estimates such as
\(R_2R_3R_4\gtrsim S_2S_3S_4\) and the basic volume lower bound, but no
form-level Poincare estimate found so far uses \(R_1\ll S_1\) and
\(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\) strongly enough to imply the desired mixed scale.

4. Boundary \(2\)-dilation estimates for \(\partial R\to\partial S\) have the
right \(S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) pattern for the large boundary face, but
multiplying by \(R_1\) loses the factor \(R_1/S_1\).  Optimizing this with
Guth's \(j=1,l=4\) monomial still leaves a high-aspect power gap.

Conclusion: no scalar, one-level, or simple form argument found this round
closes the gap.  The remaining target is still an averaged/quotient-compatible
parametric tightening theorem such as (EAT), or locating Guth's unpublished
4-dimensional rectangle preprint/thesis argument that proves an equivalent
mixed hard-regime estimate.

\section*{Round 37 notes: quotient bad seminorm and dual target}

The raw essential averaged tightening estimate should be replaced by a quotient
bad-seminorm version.  Let
\[
 {\mathcal Q}_*=N_*(R,\partial R;\mathbb R)/\ker f_\# .
\]
For an essential filling class \([C]\in{\mathcal Q}_3\) and puncture \(z\in Q\),
define
\[
 \beta_z([C])=\inf_{N\in\ker f_\#}
       \Mass((C+N)\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z),
\]
where \({\mathcal B}_z\) is the exact all-coordinate \(2\)-plaque bad set.  This is
the right residual term for any source-local tightening in the quotient: it is
invariant under changing representatives by \(f\)-null chains, unlike the sharp
restriction \(C\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z\).  The absorption algebra is unchanged
because for any chosen representative \(C\),
\[
 \beta_z([C])\le \Mass(C\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z),
\]
and the exact-plaque Fubini estimate gives
\[
 {1\over q}\int_Q \beta_z([C])\,dz
 \le {1\over q}\int_Q \Mass(C\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
 \lesssim {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}\Mass C .
\]
Thus a sufficient hard-case estimate is
\[
 A_\Omega
 \le C\left(R_1I+{I^2\over S_1q}\right)
 +{C\over q}\int_Q\int_\Omega \beta_z([C_y^0])\,dy\,dz. \tag{QEAT}
\]
The two-cell obstruction to raw EAT disappears at the level of the statement:
if \(A-A'\in\ker f_\#\), the seminorm may choose the representative \(A'\) when
\(A\) alone is bad.

The main unresolved issue is now purely constructive/dual: prove QEAT.  A
plausible Hahn--Banach dual form is as follows.  For a quotient \(2\)-cochain
\(\phi\) with \(\|\delta\phi\|\le1\), construct after averaging in \(z\) an
identity on the class \([Z_y]\)
\[
 \phi([Z_y])=K_z(\delta\phi;Z_y)+\Pi_z(\delta\phi;[C_y^0]),
\]
where
\[
 |K_z(\delta\phi;Z_y)|\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1
\]
and \(\Pi_z(\delta\phi)\) pairs only with the quotient bad seminorm
\(\beta_z([C_y^0])\).  The cochains should be target-pullback or quotient
cochains from the start; otherwise restricting to exact bad sets destroys
\(f\)-nullity.

A smooth target-side Green operator on the punctured parameter square remains a
possible route.  For \(z\in Q\), choose a \(1\)-form \(\eta_z\) on \(Q\) with
\(d\eta_z=\delta_z-q^{-1}dy_3dy_4\) and controlled integral kernels.  Pulling
\(\eta_z\) back by \(F=(f_3,f_4)\) gives a quotient-compatible homotopy because it
is defined through \(f\)-coordinates, not through a source-local cutoff.  The hard
estimate is to bound the regular part by \(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\) while localizing the
singular residual to the exact plaque bad seminorm.  No complete kernel estimate
has been proved.

Additional scalar checks this round again do not close the gap.  Combining:
\[
 R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3,\qquad
 R_1^2\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1^3S_2S_3S_4,\qquad
 (R_1R_2)^2R_3R_4\gtrsim(S_1S_2)^2S_3S_4,

 S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad

which has \(\Vol(R)=L^{12/5}\) while the desired hard scale is \(L^{5/2}\).
Thus the missing estimate must use family-level compatibility of the slices, not
