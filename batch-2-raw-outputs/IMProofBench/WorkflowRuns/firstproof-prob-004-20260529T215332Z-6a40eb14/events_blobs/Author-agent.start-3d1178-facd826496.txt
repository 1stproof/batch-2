A=(rF_0)^{1/2}

### Council member: gemini-31-pro (models/gemini/gemini-31-pro) ###
Here is how I would approach the roadblocks you are hitting. 

**1. The single-fiber upgrade is blocked (and your bound is sharp)**
You cannot prove $\Fill(Z_y) \lesssim \Mass(Z_y)^2 / S_1$, because the inequality is geometrically false for cycles inside $R$. Your current lower bound $\Mass(Z_y) \gtrsim (R_1 S_1 S_2 S_3)^{1/2}$ is actually the best you can extract from a single 2D slice.

To see why, construct a hypothetical flat-ish cycle $Z_y$ in $R$ of dimensions $R_1 \times L_2$, where $L_2 = A/R_1$. To map this to $S_1 \times S_2$ with degree one, we stretch the $x_1$-direction by a factor of $S_1/R_1$ and contract the $x_2$-direction by $S_2/L_2$. The 2-dilation of this map is $(S_1/R_1)(S_2/L_2) = S_1 S_2 / A$. As long as $A \ge S_1 S_2$, this 2-dilation is $\le 1$. 

The filling volume of this cycle in $R$ is naturally bounded by $R_1 L_2^2 = A^2/R_1$. If we set $A = (R_1 S_1 S_2 S_3)^{1/2}$, the filling volume is exactly $S_1 S_2 S_3$ (matching the target filling bound). This cycle perfectly satisfies the 2-dilation and filling constraints but achieves your exact bottleneck mass. The geometry of a single 2D fiber simply cannot "see" the $R_3R_4$ constraint.

**2. The counterexample does not exist (The 1-Dilation Trade-off)**
You can rest easy about the scaling test map: it is rigorously impossible to construct. The $\Dil_2 \le 1$ bound forces a rigid trade-off between 1-dilations that kills the snake map you are worried about. 

Algebraically, for any vectors $v_1, v_3, v_4$, we have the identity:
\[
|v_1 \wedge v_3 \wedge v_4| \le \frac{|v_1 \wedge v_3| \cdot |v_1 \wedge v_4|}{|v_1|}
\]
Let $v_i = \nabla f_i$. Since $\Dil_2 \le 1$, the numerators are at most 1. This means the 3-Jacobian $J_3(f_1, f_3, f_4)$ is bounded by $1/|\nabla f_1|$. 

Because $R_1 \le \kappa S_1$, the map *must* stretch heavily in the $x_1$-direction on average ($|\nabla f_1| \ge \kappa^{-1}$). The identity above forces the map to become incredibly sluggish in the orthogonal $x_3, x_4$ directions. To still cover the massive target area $S_3 \times S_4$ with such sluggish gradients, the domain lengths $R_3$ and $R_4$ would have to be enormous. But your hypothesis specifically states $R_3 R_4 \le \kappa S_3 S_4$. This algebraic bottleneck strictly forbids the necessary target-covering, meaning no such map exists.

**3. Proposed Strategy**
Since 2D slices are too flexible, the rigidity you need must come from the 3D or 4D structure. I suggest two alternatives:

*   **Move up a dimension:** Instead of pulling back 2-planes, pull back the 3-planes $Y_{y_4} = f^{-1}([0, S_1] \times [0, S_2] \times [0, S_3] \times \{y_4\})$. The 3D isoperimetric profile of $Y_{y_4}$ in $R$ will interact with $R_1$ and $R_2$ differently, and mapping it to a 3D target will heavily exploit the 3-Jacobian bound derived above.
*   **Anisotropic Tightening:** Apply Guth's cubical approximation (chain-level tightening) directly to $R$, but using a highly anisotropic grid with dimensions $R_1 \times R_2 \times R_3 \times R_4$. The initial 2-skeleton of this grid will have mixed faces of area exactly $R_i R_j$. The hypothesis $R_3 R_4 \le \kappa S_3 S_4$ ensures that the $34$-faces are exceptionally small. When you bound the 3-width of this complex, the smallness of the $34$-faces will naturally inject the missing $(S_3/S_2)^{1/2}$ factor into your volume estimate.
