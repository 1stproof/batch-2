```latex
\begin{center}
{\bf A four-dimensional rectangle problem for \(2\)-dilation}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes and a map
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to (S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\) if
\(f_*[R,\partial R]=[S,\partial S]\) in relative homology.  The constant
called \(k\) in the problem will be denoted by \(\kappa\).

The preceding draft used a two-parameter sweepout estimate which is not
correct for arbitrary sweepouts.  The following is the precise standard
input from Guth's rectangle theorem which I will use below.  If
\(Q_i=S_i/R_i\) and \(\Phi:(U,\partial U)\to(S,\partial S)\), with
\(U\subset R\), has non-zero degree, then for
\(0\le j<k\le l\le n\),
\[
 \Dil_k(\Phi)\ge c(n)Q_1\cdots Q_j
       (Q_{j+1}\cdots Q_l)^{(k-j)/(l-j)}.       \tag{G}
\]
Equivalently, if \(\Phi\) is \(k\)-contracting and \(0<j<k<l\), then
\[
 \left(\frac{R_1\cdots R_j}{S_1\cdots S_j}\right)^{(l-k)/(k-j)}
 R_1\cdots R_l \ge c(n)S_1\cdots S_l .        \tag{G'}
\]
This is Estimate 1 in Guth's ``Area-expanding embeddings of rectangles'';
it is proved by the complex-of-cycles tightening construction, not by a
naive continuous-family statement.

\section*{Consequences which are rigorous but not yet sufficient}

Assume \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).  At every differentiability point, if the
singular values of \(df\) are \(s_1\le\cdots\le s_4\), then
\(s_3s_4\le1\), and hence \(s_2s_3s_4\le1\) and
\(s_1s_2s_3s_4\le1\).  Thus \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\) and \(\Dil_4(f)\le1\).

Applying (G') with \(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=3,4\) gives
\[
 R_1^2R_2R_3\ge c\,S_1^2S_2S_3,                 \tag{1}
\]
\[
 R_1^2R_1R_2R_3R_4\ge c\,S_1^2S_1S_2S_3S_4.     \tag{2}
\]
Applying (G') to the same map regarded as \(3\)-contracting, with
\(k=3,j=2,l=4\), gives
\[
 (R_1R_2)^2R_3R_4\ge c\,(S_1S_2)^2S_3S_4.       \tag{3}
\]
These are genuine necessary conditions.  For instance, (2) implies, under
\(R_1\le\kappa S_1\),
\[
 R_1R_2R_3R_4\ge c\kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4.      \tag{4}
\]
This proves the desired volume alternative whenever
\(S_3/S_2\le C\kappa^{-6}\), after making \(\kappa\) sufficiently small
in terms of \(c\).  The remaining case is the high-aspect range
\(S_3/S_2\gg\kappa^{-6}\).

The high-aspect range is exactly where the estimates (G) alone lose the
factor \((S_3/S_2)^{1/2}\) appearing in the problem.  To see that this is
not just a cosmetic algebraic issue, set, for a small parameter
\(\varepsilon\),
\[
 S=(1,1,\varepsilon^{-6},\varepsilon^{-6}),\qquad
 R=(\varepsilon,\varepsilon^{-4},\varepsilon^{-5},\varepsilon^{-6}).
\]
Then \(R_1=\varepsilon S_1\), \(R_3R_4=\varepsilon S_3S_4\), and
\[
 \Vol(R)=\varepsilon\,S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]
Thus both desired alternatives fail if \(\kappa=\varepsilon\) and the
inequalities are non-strict.  Nevertheless all monomial inequalities (G)
for \(k=2\), and also the corresponding inequalities obtained from
\(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), are satisfied up to constants independent of
\(\varepsilon\).  Therefore the desired theorem needs an additional
mixed-skeleton input which uses the fact that \(\Dil_2\) controls all
mixed two-faces of the cubical decomposition of \(R\).  This is the point
not captured by the false sweepout theorem in the previous draft.

\section*{The missing mixed-face estimate}

A sufficient statement would be the following anisotropic tightening
estimate.

\begin{proposition}[mixed-face tightening estimate needed]\label{P}
There is a dimensional constant \(c_0>0\) such that every degree-one
piecewise smooth \(2\)-contracting map \(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\)
between four-rectangles satisfies
\[
 \frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}
 +\frac{R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}}{S_1\sqrt{S_3S_4}}
 +\frac{\Vol(R)}{S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4}
 \ge c_0 .                                      \tag{5}
\]
\end{proposition}

If Proposition \ref{P} is available, the problem follows immediately.
Indeed, choose
\[
 \kappa=\min\{10^{-2},c_0/4\}.
\]
Assume \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and suppose that the first desired alternative
fails.  Then \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), and the first two terms in (5)
are at most \(\kappa\) and \(\kappa^{3/2}\).  Hence the last term in (5) is
at least \(c_0-\kappa-\kappa^{3/2}\ge c_0/2\), and therefore
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge \frac{c_0}{2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
          >\kappa S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 ,
\]
which is the second desired alternative.

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The essential remaining gap is Proposition \ref{P}.  The standard Guth
Estimate (G) is not strong enough in the high-aspect regime, as the
explicit scaling example above shows.  What is needed is a version of the
complex-of-cycles tightening argument in which the \(2\)-skeleton keeps
track not only of the areas of the \(x_3x_4\)-faces but also of the mixed
\(x_1x_3\) and \(x_1x_4\) faces, whose geometric mean is controlled by
\(R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}\).  The previous two-parameter sweepout statement
ignored these mixed faces and is false.

\end{document}
```

```latex
\documentclass[12pt]{article}
\usepackage{amsmath,amssymb}
\newcommand{\Dil}{\operatorname{Dil}}
\begin{document}
Scratchpad for the rectangle 2-dilation problem.

Interpretation: rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes; a map
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) of degree 1 means the induced map on
\(H_4(\cdot,\partial\cdot;\mathbb Z)\) sends \([R]\) to \([S]\).
The constant called \(k\) in the problem is a small universal constant;
in drafts it is denoted \(\kappa\) to avoid conflict with \(k\)-dilation.

Important correction after referee report.  The earlier proposed theorem
\[
 M_2/(S_3S_4)+M_3/(S_1S_3S_4)
 +M_4/(S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4)\ge c
\]
for arbitrary two-parameter sweepouts is false.  Counterexample:
\(S=[0,1]^2\times[0,L]^2\) and cycles
\([0,1]^2\times\{u\}\times\{v\}\).  Then \(M_2=1,M_3=L,M_4=L^2\), so the
left side is \(L^{-2}+L^{-1}+L^{-1/2}\to0\).  Any valid proof must use
more of the map \(f\) than the fiber sweepout: specifically, the
\(\Dil_2\) bound controls mixed two-faces of a cubical decomposition of
\(R\).

Precise Guth estimate from arXiv:0710.0403, Estimate 1.  Let
\(Q_i=S_i/R_i\).  If \(\Phi:(U,\partial U)\to(S,\partial S)\), \(U\subset R\),
has nonzero degree, then for \(0\le j<k\le l\le n\),
\[
 \operatorname{dil}_k(\Phi)\ge c(n)Q_1\cdots Q_j
   (Q_{j+1}\cdots Q_l)^{(k-j)/(l-j)}.
\]
Equivalently, if \(\Phi\) is \(k\)-contracting and \(0<j<k<l\),
\[
 \left({R_1\cdots R_j\over S_1\cdots S_j}\right)^{(l-k)/(k-j)}
 R_1\cdots R_l \ge c(n)S_1\cdots S_l .
\]
Estimate 2 includes a factor \(D^{(k-j)/(n-j)}\) but for degree \(D=1\)
coincides with the \(l=n\) case.

Consequences for \(n=4,k=2\), plus \(\Dil_3,\Dil_4\le1\) from singular
values:
\[
R_1R_2\gtrsim S_1S_2,\quad R_1R_2R_3\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3,\quad
V_R\gtrsim V_S,
\]
\[
R_1^2R_2R_3\gtrsim S_1^2S_2S_3,\quad
R_1^2V_R\gtrsim S_1^2V_S,
\]
and from \(k=3,j=2,l=4\),
\[
(R_1R_2)^2R_3R_4\gtrsim (S_1S_2)^2S_3S_4.
\]
The \(k=2,j=1,l=4\) inequality gives, if \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\),
\[
V_R\gtrsim \kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4.
\]
This implies the desired volume lower bound
\(V_R\gtrsim \kappa S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) only when
\(S_3/S_2\lesssim \kappa^{-6}\).  The difficult range is
\(S_3/S_2\gg \kappa^{-6}\).

Scaling test showing Guth monomial estimates alone are insufficient:
take
\[
S=(1,1,\varepsilon^{-6},\varepsilon^{-6}),\quad
R=(\varepsilon,\varepsilon^{-4},\varepsilon^{-5},\varepsilon^{-6}).
\]
Then \(R_1=\varepsilon S_1\), \(R_3R_4=\varepsilon S_3S_4\), and
\[
V_R=\varepsilon\, S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]
The quotients are \(Q=(\varepsilon^{-1},\varepsilon^4,\varepsilon^{-1},1)\).
All Estimate 1 monomials for \(k=2\),
\[
Q_1Q_2,\ (Q_1Q_2Q_3)^{2/3},\ (Q_1Q_2Q_3Q_4)^{1/2},\
Q_1(Q_2Q_3)^{1/2},\ Q_1(Q_2Q_3Q_4)^{1/3},
\]
are \(O(1)\) or smaller.  The \(k=3\) monomial
\(Q_1Q_2(Q_3Q_4)^{1/2}\) is also small.  Thus the desired result needs a
genuinely mixed-face strengthening, not just the known monomial theorem.

A plausible sufficient mixed-face estimate is
\[
 {R_3R_4\over S_3S_4}
 +{R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}\over S_1\sqrt{S_3S_4}}
 +{V_R\over S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4}\ge c.
\]
Under \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), the first two
terms are \(\le\kappa\) and \(\le\kappa^{3/2}\), yielding the desired
volume lower bound.  Need prove or locate this estimate.  It should come
from an anisotropic version of Guth's tightening construction where the
initial \(2\)-skeleton records all mixed \(2\)-faces.  The geometric mean
\(R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}\) is controlled even though the individual mixed face
areas \(R_1R_3\), \(R_1R_4\) need not both be small when \(S_4/S_3\) is
large.

Ideas tried:
1. Packing-width estimates for \(2\)-width and \(3\)-width of preimages of
target subrectangles reproduce standard Guth monomial lower bounds but so
far do not generate the factor \((S_3/S_2)^{1/2}\).
2. Applying Estimate 1 to \(\Dil_3\le1\) gives
\((R_1R_2)^2R_3R_4\gtrsim(S_1S_2)^2S_3S_4\), but combining with
\(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\) gives only \(R_1R_2\gtrsim\kappa^{-1/2}S_1S_2\).
3. Coarea on \((f_3,f_4)\) gives the basic volume lower bound
\(V_R\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4\), but does not use \(R_1\) small.
4. Product constructions from 3D snake maps fail after crossing with an
interval because the mixed \(2\)-dilation involves the large \(1\)-dilation
of the snake map.  This supports the idea that mixed faces are the missing
obstruction.

\end{document}
```
