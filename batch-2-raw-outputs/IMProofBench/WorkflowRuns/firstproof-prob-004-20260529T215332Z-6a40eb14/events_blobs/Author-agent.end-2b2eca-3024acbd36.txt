\begin{center}
{\bf Slicing reduction for the rectangle \(2\)-dilation estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I write the universal constant in the problem as \(\kappa\), to avoid
confusion with \(k\)-dilation.  Rectangles are oriented Euclidean products
\(\prod_i[0,R_i]\), and degree \(1\) means degree \(1\) on relative
\(H_4(\cdot,\partial\cdot;\mathbb Z)\).  The argument is phrased in the
language of relative integral currents; for piecewise smooth maps this is just
the usual slicing/coarea theory after the standard harmless smoothing or PL
approximation.

The proof below establishes the standard first alternative and isolates the
only remaining missing ingredient, an averaged parametric tightening estimate.
Thus the document is a rigorous reduction, not yet a complete proof of the
stated theorem.

\section*{Standard inputs}

First, if \(\Dil_2(f)\le 1\), then \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\) and \(\Dil_4(f)\le1\).
Indeed, if \(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) are the singular values and
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), then \(\sigma_2\le1\), and hence
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\) for \(j\ge2\).

Second, we use Guth's lower estimate for degree-nonzero maps of pairs between
rectangles \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}.  In the case
\(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=4\), it gives
\begin{equation}\label{guth14}
        R_1^2\,\Vol(R)\ge c_0\,S_1^3S_2S_3S_4                  \tag{1}
\end{equation}
whenever \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).

Third, we use Guth's small-volume relative isoperimetric profile of a rectangle
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding}.  In the form needed here, there are constants
\(c_*,C_*\) such that, if \(Z\) is a relative \(2\)-cycle in \(R\) with
\(A=\Mass(Z)\le c_*R_1R_2\), then
\[
 \Fill_R(Z)\le C_*\max\{A^{3/2},\ A^2/R_1\}.
\]
Consequently, if \(\Fill_R(Z)\ge L\), then
\begin{equation}\label{profile-inverse}
\Mass(Z)\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,\ L^{2/3},\ (R_1L)^{1/2}\}.           \tag{2}
\end{equation}
Also, after decreasing \(c_*\) if necessary,
\begin{equation}\label{iso}
        \Mass Z<c_*R_1R_2\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C R_1R_2^2 .                              \tag{3}
\end{equation}
The smallness condition in this paragraph is essential.

\section*{The first alternative}

\begin{lemma}[Filling a central target plane]\label{central}
Let \(Q\) be the middle half of the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle of \(S\).  For
\(y\in Q\), set
\[
P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\}.
\]
Every relative \(3\)-chain in \(S\) filling \(P_y\) has volume at least
\(cS_1S_2S_3\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle
which vanishes on its boundary and is \(\ge cS_3\) on \(Q\).  Put
\(\beta=\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2\).  The \(3\)-form \(d\beta\) has comass at
most \(1\).  If \(\partial Y=P_y\) relatively, then Stokes' theorem gives
\[
\Mass Y\ge\left|\int_Y d\beta\right|
=\left|\int_{P_y}\beta\right|
       = S_1S_2\psi(y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
The boundary-face terms vanish because either \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\) on the
\(x_1,x_2\)-faces or \(\psi=0\) on the \(x_3,x_4\)-faces.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{firstalt}
There is \(c_2>0\) such that, under the hypotheses of the problem, if
\[
        R_1R_2^2\le c_2S_1S_2S_3,
\]
then
\[
        R_3R_4\ge c_2S_3S_4 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(F=(f_3,f_4)\).  For a.e. \(y\in Q\), the slice
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\) is a relative \(2\)-cycle in \(R\).
Naturality of slicing for relative currents gives
\[
        f_\# Z_y=\pm [0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\}
\]
in \(I_2(S,\partial S)\).  Hence any relative filling of \(Z_y\) in \(R\)
maps, since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), to a relative filling of \(P_y\).  Lemma
\ref{central} gives
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
Choose \(c_2\) so small that \(Cc_2<c\) in \eqref{iso}.  Then
\(\Mass Z_y\ge c_*R_1R_2\) for a.e. \(y\in Q\).  By coarea and
\(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\),
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge \int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
        \ge cR_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Since \(\Vol(R)=R_1R_2R_3R_4\), this proves the claim after decreasing \(c_2\).
\end{proof}

\section*{The best bound from single-slice information}

Assume \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\) and that the first conclusion fails with a
small constant.  Proposition \ref{firstalt} gives
\begin{equation}\label{r12large}
        R_1R_2^2\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .                                  \tag{4}
\end{equation}
For the slices above, Lemma \ref{central} and \eqref{profile-inverse}, with
\(L=cS_1S_2S_3\), give
\[
\Mass Z_y\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,\ (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\
                         (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\}.
\]
Using \eqref{r12large} and \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\), each term in this
minimum is at least
\[
        c\,\alpha^{1/2}S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}.
\]
Integrating over \(Q\) yields
\begin{equation}\label{slicevol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,\alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .                 \tag{5}
\end{equation}
On the other hand, Guth's estimate \eqref{guth14} gives
\begin{equation}\label{monovol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .                              \tag{6}
\end{equation}
Combining \eqref{slicevol} and \eqref{monovol}, and optimizing in
\(\alpha\), gives the unconditional partial bound
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4 .
\]
This misses the desired second alternative by the factor
\((S_2/S_3)^{1/10}\).

\section*{The remaining averaged estimate}

Let \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), and for the central slices set
\[
E_y=\int_{Z_y}\|dG|_{T Z_y}\|^2\,d\mathcal H^2,\qquad
I=\int_QE_y\,dy,\qquad A=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy .
\]
At a rank-two point of \(dF\), put \(K=\ker dF\) and
\(\lambda=\|dG|_K\|\).  If \(N=K^\perp\), then for each unit
\(v\in K\) with \(|dG(v)|=\lambda\) and each unit \(n\in N\),
\[
        1\ge |df(v)\wedge df(n)|\ge \lambda |dF(n)|.
\]
Thus \(J_2F\le \lambda^{-2}\), with the rank-deficient case contributing zero.
Coarea gives
\[
        I=\int_{F^{-1}(Q)}\lambda^2J_2F\le C\Vol(R).
\]
Also Lemma \ref{central} gives
\[
        A\ge cS_1S_2S_3\,|Q|\ge cS_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .                 \tag{7}\label{Alower}
\]

The theorem would follow from the following family-level tightening estimate:
\begin{equation}\label{missing}
 A\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\,A .                                \tag{8}
\end{equation}
Indeed, if \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), choose
\(\kappa\) so small that the last term in \eqref{missing} is absorbed.  If
\(I\le\delta S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), then, since
\(S_1\le S_2\le S_3\),
\[
R_1I\le \kappa\delta\,S_1S_2S_3^2S_4,\qquad
\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\le C\delta^2\,S_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .
\]
Taking first \(\delta\) and then \(\kappa\) small contradicts \eqref{Alower}.
Thus \(I\ge cS_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), and weighted coarea gives the desired
volume lower bound.

\section*{Remaining open issue}

The estimate \eqref{missing} is not proved in this draft.  It is not a
single-slice isoperimetric statement: single fibers can have small energy and
large filling volume by sitting far from the boundary of the long directions.
The missing point is that the \(Z_y\)'s are fibers of one four-dimensional
\(2\)-contracting map; an averaged tightening must use this compatibility.  The
published monomial estimates and the ordinary rectangular isoperimetric profile
give only \eqref{slicevol}--\eqref{monovol}, hence the weaker
\(S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}\) exponent above.
