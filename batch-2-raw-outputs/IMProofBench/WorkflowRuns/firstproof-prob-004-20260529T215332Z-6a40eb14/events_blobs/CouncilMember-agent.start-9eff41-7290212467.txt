\begin{center}
{\bf Partial proof and corrected reduction for the rectangle \(2\)-dilation estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes.  A map
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\) if
\(f_*[R,\partial R]=[S,\partial S]\) in relative homology.  I denote the
small constant in the problem by \(\kappa\), reserving \(k\) for dilation
dimension.  Constants below are positive dimensional constants.  Strict
inequalities in the problem follow from non-strict inequalities after decreasing
\(\kappa\).

The argument below proves the standard first alternative.  Under the hypotheses
of the problem and after failure of the first alternative, it also proves the
partial replacement
\[
   \Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4 .
\]
This misses the requested second alternative by the factor
\((S_3/S_2)^{1/10}\) in the hard anisotropic range.  The remaining factor is
reduced to a family-level cellular tightening estimate, stated below as
\((13c)\).  The point of the present revision is that the earlier
sharper pointwise comparison obtained by cutting a filling along a fixed bad set
is not used: such a cut has uncontrolled seams.  Thus the document is still a
reduction and partial proof, not a complete proof of the stated theorem.

\section*{Standard tools}

We use integral Lipschitz currents.  Let \(T=[R,\partial R]\).  It is enough,
after decreasing the final constant \(\kappa\) by a fixed factor, to argue for PL
maps for which the coordinate projections below are transverse to the relevant
planes.  Indeed, approximate a piecewise smooth map relative to a boundary collar
by PL maps \(f_\varepsilon\) of the same relative degree and with
\(\Dil_2(f_\varepsilon)\le1+\varepsilon\).  Postcompose \(f_\varepsilon\) with the
homothety \(H_\varepsilon:S\to (1+\varepsilon)^{-1/2}S\).  Then
\(H_\varepsilon\circ f_\varepsilon\) is \(2\)-contracting.  If the transverse theorem is
known with constant \(\kappa_0\), choose the final constant
\(\kappa\le\kappa_0/4\); for \(\varepsilon\) small,
\(R_1\le\kappa S_1\le\kappa_0(1+\varepsilon)^{-1/2}S_1\), so the theorem applies to
the scaled target.  The alternatives for the scaled target imply the stated
alternatives with a slightly smaller constant, and \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\) plus
flat convergence of slices and lower semicontinuity of mass passes to the original
map.  For a projection
\(\pi\) and \(F=\pi\circ f\), slicing naturality gives, for a.e. regular value
\(y\),
\[
        f_\#\langle T,F,y\rangle
        =\langle [S,\partial S],\pi,y\rangle
\]
as relative currents.

If \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), then \(\Dil_j(f)\le1\) for \(j=3,4\): if
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) are the singular values of \(df\), then
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), hence \(\sigma_i\le1\) for \(i\ge2\), and
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le1\) for \(j\ge2\).

We use two estimates of Guth.  First, Theorem 3 of
\emph{Area-expanding embeddings of rectangles} gives the following relative
profile bound for a rectangle: if \(V\le c R_1\cdots R_k\) and
\(V=cR_1\cdots R_j\rho^{k-j}\) with \(R_j\le\rho\le R_{j+1}\), then every relative
\(k\)-cycle of mass \(V\) has a relative filling of mass
\(O(R_1\cdots R_j\rho^{k-j+1})\).  For \(k=2,n=4\) this implies that, if \(Z\) is a
relative integral \(2\)-cycle in \(R\) and
\(A=\Mass Z\le c_I R_1R_2\), then
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C_I
        \max\left\{ A^{3/2},\,\frac{A^2}{R_1}\right\}.        \tag{1}
\]
Second, Guth's Estimate 1 states that if
\(Q_i=S_i/R_i\), \(0\le j<k\le l\le n\), and a degree-one map of pairs has
\(k\)-dilation \(D_k\), then
\[
        D_k\ge c(n)Q_1\cdots Q_j
        (Q_{j+1}\cdots Q_l)^{(k-j)/(l-j)} .
\]
With \(n=4,j=1,k=2,l=4\) and \(D_2\le1\), this gives
\[
        R_1^3R_2R_3R_4\ge c_G S_1^3S_2S_3S_4 .              \tag{2}
\]

\section*{The first alternative}

\begin{lemma}[central target filling]\label{lem:target-fill}
Let
\[
 P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\},
\]
where \(y_3\in[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\) and
\(y_4\in[S_4/3,2S_4/3]\).  Every relative \(3\)-chain \(Y\) in \(S\) with
\(\partial Y=P_y\) in \(I_2(S,\partial S)\) obeys
\[
        \Mass Y\ge c\,S_1S_2S_3 .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a smooth \(1+o(1)\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the
\((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle, zero on its boundary and satisfying
\(\psi(y_3,y_4)\ge cS_3-o(1)\).  Put
\(\eta=\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2\).  Then
\(d\eta=d\psi\wedge dx_1\wedge dx_2\) has comass at most \(1+o(1)\).  Stokes
for relative currents gives
\[
 \int_Y d\eta=\int_{\partial Y}\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2 .
\]
The part of \(\partial Y\) lying in \(\partial S\) contributes zero: on the
\(x_1,x_2\)-faces \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\), while on the \(x_3,x_4\)-faces
\(\psi=0\).  The remaining contribution is
\(\psi(y_3,y_4)S_1S_2\), up to sign.  Letting \(o(1)\to0\) proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[first alternative]\label{prop:first}
There are constants \(c_1,C_1>0\) such that the following holds.  Let
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) have degree \(1\) and \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).
If
\[
        C_1R_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3 ,
\]
then \(R_3R_4\ge c_1S_3S_4\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(F=(f_3,f_4)\) and
\(Q=[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\times[S_4/3,2S_4/3]\).  For a.e. \(y\in Q\), put
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\).  Slicing naturality gives
\(f_\#Z_y=P_y\), up to the harmless orientation sign.  Any relative filling of
\(Z_y\) pushes forward to a relative filling of \(P_y\), and \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\).
Lemma \ref{lem:target-fill} gives
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .                         \tag{3}
\]
If \(C_1\) is large enough, then (1) and (3) force
\(\Mass Z_y\ge cR_1R_2\) for a.e. \(y\in Q\).  Coarea and \(J_2F\le1\) give
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge\int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
      \ge c R_1R_2S_3S_4 ,
\]
and hence \(R_3R_4\ge cS_3S_4\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences of the same slicing}

The preceding argument also gives, for a.e. \(y\in Q\),
\[
 \Mass Z_y\ge c\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\}.                       \tag{4}
\]
Indeed, if \(A=\Mass Z_y<c_I R_1R_2\), then (1) and (3) imply
\[
 cS_1S_2S_3\le C_I\max\{A^{3/2},A^2/R_1\}.
\]
After integration over \(Q\),
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge cS_3S_4\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\}.                      \tag{5}
\]
Also, (2) and \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) imply
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c_G\kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .              \tag{6}
\]
Thus (6) proves the desired second alternative when
\(S_3/S_2\le c\,\kappa^{-6}\).

Assume now that the first desired alternative fails and take
\(\kappa<\min\{c_1,1\}\).  Proposition \ref{prop:first} gives
\[
        R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3 .                          \tag{7}
\]
Writing \(\alpha=R_1/S_1\le1\) and
\(T=S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), (5) gives
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c\,\alpha^{1/2}T,                          \tag{8}
\]
because (7) implies \(R_1R_2\ge (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\), while
\((S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3}\ge (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\) follows from
\(R_1\le S_1\le S_2\le S_3\).  On the other hand, (2) gives
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c\,\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .                \tag{9}
\]
Consequently, if the second desired alternative also fails, then necessarily
\[
        \alpha\lesssim \kappa^2,\qquad
        \frac{S_3}{S_2}\gtrsim \alpha^{-4}\kappa^{-2}.          \tag{10}
\]
Thus any remaining counterexample must be extremely anisotropic.

Combining (8) and (9) with weights \(4/5\) and \(1/5\) eliminates \(\alpha\) and gives
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c\,S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4 .             \tag{10a}
\]
Thus the proved estimates miss the desired second alternative only by the factor
\((S_3/S_2)^{1/10}\) in the remaining hard regime.

\section*{Weighted coarea}

Let \(G=(f_1,f_2)\).  At rank-two points of \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), set
\[
        \lambda(x)=\left\|dG|_{\ker dF_x}\right\|,
\]
and set \(\lambda=0\) on the rank-deficient set.  If
\(E_y=\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\,d\|Z_y\|\) and \(I=\int_QE_y\,dy\), then
\[
        I\le \Vol(R),                                      \tag{11}
\]
and, for a.e. \(y\in Q\),
\[
        E_y\ge S_1S_2 .                                    \tag{12}
\]
For (11), write \(T_x=\ker dF_x\), \(N_x=T_x^\perp\).  If
\(\lambda>0\), choose a unit \(v\in T_x\) with \(|dG(v)|=\lambda\).  For every unit
\(n\in N_x\),
\[
        |df(v)\wedge df(n)|\ge \lambda |dF(n)|.
\]
Since \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), the two singular values of \(dF|_{N_x}\) are at most
\(\lambda^{-1}\), so \(\lambda^2J_2F\le1\).  Coarea gives (11).  For (12),
slicing naturality gives \(G_\#Z_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\) as a relative current; the
area formula and \(J_2(G|_{Z_y})\le\lambda^2\) give the claim.

\section*{Self-absorbing bad mass}

Let
\[
        A=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy,\qquad q=|Q|\sim S_3S_4 .
\]
We shall use measurable almost-minimizing fillings of the slices.  On \(Q\),
(3) gives \(\Fill_R(Z_y)>0\) for a.e. \(y\).  The space of relative integral
currents in the compact polyhedron \(R\), with the flat metric and with currents
supported in \(\partial R\) quotiented out, is a standard Borel space; the boundary
map and the mass functional are Borel, and the slice map \(y\mapsto Z_y\) is Borel
on the set of regular values.  Hence the set of pairs \((y,B)\) satisfying
\[
        \partial B=Z_y\quad\hbox{in }I_2(R,\partial R),\qquad
        \Mass B<2\Fill_R(Z_y)
\]
is analytic with non-empty sections.  The Jankov--von Neumann selection theorem,
followed by changing the choice on a null set, gives a measurable family with
\[
        \partial B_y=Z_y,\qquad \Mass B_y\le2\Fill_R(Z_y)       \tag{12a}
\]
for a.e. \(y\).  In particular,
\(\int_E\Mass B_y\,dy\le2\int_E\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy\) for every measurable
\(E\subset Q\).

For \(i<j\), let \(P_{ij}(x)\) be the coordinate \(2\)-plaque through \(x\), varying
only \(x_i,x_j\).  For \(z\in Q\), define
\[
        \mathcal B_z=\{x\in R:\ z\in F(P_{ij}(x))\hbox{ for some }i<j\}.
\]

\begin{lemma}[all-plaque bad mass]\label{lem:plaque}
\[
 \frac1q\int_Q\int_Q \Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dy\,dz
 \le C\,\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\,A .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
For fixed \(x\), the set of \(z\) with \(x\in\mathcal B_z\) is contained in
\(\bigcup_{i<j}F(P_{ij}(x))\cap Q\).  Since \(J_2(F|_{P_{ij}})\le1\) and
\(R_iR_j\le R_3R_4\), this set has area at most \(C R_3R_4\).  Integrating the
indicator of \(\mathcal B_z\) with respect to \(d\|B_y\|(x)\,dy\,dz\) and using
\(\int_Q\Mass B_y\,dy\le2A\) proves the estimate.
\end{proof}

The preceding pointwise bad set is adequate for averaging, but it is not adequate
for a fixed-puncture chain construction: replacing \(B_y\) by
\(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z\) creates an uncontrolled boundary on
\(\partial\mathcal B_z\).  A chain-level proof would have to use whole cells.  More
precisely, choose a fine shifted cubulation \(\mathcal Q_\theta\) of \(R\), deform
\(B_y\) by the Federer--Fleming projection to a cubical \(3\)-chain
\(B_{y,\theta}\) with \(\Mass B_{y,\theta}\le(1+\varepsilon)\Mass B_y\), and call a
cell bad if one of the prescribed coordinate-plaque tracks used by the cubical
homotopy has \(F\)-image containing \(z\).  Let
\(\mathcal B^{\rm cell}_{z,\theta}\) be the union of these bad cells.  The cellular
version of Lemma \ref{lem:plaque} would be
\[
 \limsup_{\varepsilon\downarrow0}\,
 \frac1q\int_\Theta\int_Q\int_\Omega
 \Mass(B_{y,\theta}\llcorner\mathcal B^{\rm cell}_{z,\theta})\,dy\,dz\,d\theta
 \le C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy,       \tag{12b}
\]
for every measurable \(\Omega\subset Q\).  This is the correct seam-free
replacement for the former fixed-set cut.

The family-level cellular tightening estimate which would finish the proof is the
following.  Let
\[
        \Omega=\{y\in Q:\ E_y\le 4I/q\};
\]
then \(|\Omega|\ge 3q/4\).  One needs to construct, for a.e. \((y,z,\theta)\), a
cubical competitor filling \(C_{y,z,\theta}\) of \(Z_y\) satisfying
\[
 \Mass C_{y,z,\theta}
 \le C\left(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\right)
      +C\,\Mass(B_{y,\theta}\llcorner\mathcal B^{\rm cell}_{z,\theta}). \tag{13c}
\]
The intended chain identity is
\(C_{y,z,\theta}=H_{z,\theta}(Z_y)+P_{z,\theta}(B_{y,\theta})\), where
\(P_{z,\theta}\) is a cubical projection to \(\partial R\) on good cells and
\(H_{z,\theta}\) is its homotopy on \(2\)-chains.  The difficult estimate is the
low-energy bound
\(\Mass H_{z,\theta}(Z_y)\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\).

If \((12b)\) and \((13c)\) were available, then averaging would give
\[
 \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\int_\Omega\left(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\right)dy
      +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}
        \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy .                       \tag{13}
\]
In the hard regime \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), choosing \(\kappa\) small absorbs
the last term.  Since (3) gives
\[
 \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy\ge cS_1S_2S_3|\Omega|
        \ge cS_1S_2S_3q ,
\]
and since \(E_y\le4I/q\) on \(\Omega\), (13) implies
\[
 cS_1S_2S_3q
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right).
\]
Either \(R_1I\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3q\), which with \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) implies
\(I\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) after decreasing \(\kappa\), or
\(I^2/(S_1q)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3q\), which gives the same lower bound directly.
Then (11) gives the desired second alternative.

\section*{Directional filling check}

For a rectifiable relative \(2\)-current \(Z=\xi\,\|Z\|\subset R\), define
\[
        \Vol_{ij}(Z)=\int |\langle dx_i\wedge dx_j,\xi\rangle|\,d\|Z\|.
\]
The relative rectangular specialization of Guth's directional isoperimetric
inequality gives a relative filling \(Y\) of \(Z\) with
\[
 \Mass Y\le C\{R_1(\Vol_{23}Z+\Vol_{24}Z+\Vol_{34}Z)
      +R_2(\Vol_{13}Z+\Vol_{14}Z)+R_3\Vol_{12}Z\}.            \tag{14}
\]
Applying (14) to \(Z_y\) and integrating, (3) gives
\[
 S_1S_2S_3\,q
 \le C\{R_1(M_{12}+M_{13}+M_{14})
      +R_2(M_{23}+M_{24})+R_3M_{34}\},                       \tag{15}
\]
where
\[
        M_{ij}=\int_{F^{-1}(Q)}
        \left|\frac{\partial(f_3,f_4)}{\partial(x_i,x_j)}\right|\,dx .
\]
The bookkeeping is complementary: for example,
\(\int_Q\Vol_{12}(Z_y)\,dy=M_{34}\), because
\(dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge df_3\wedge df_4\) contains the \(x_3x_4\)-minor of \(F\).
This valid directional estimate is weaker than the low-energy cellular estimate; it
does not control the parametric compatibility of fillings.

\section*{\(f_1\)-level slicing is not a substitute}

One tempting replacement for (13) is to slice by \(u=f_1\).  For a.e. central
\(t\), \(M_t=\langle [R,\partial R],u,t\rangle\) maps by
\(g=(f_2,f_3,f_4)\) with relative degree one to the target \(3\)-rectangle.  If
\(w=|\nabla u|^{-1}\), then the same singular-value calculation gives
\(\|dg|_{TM_t}\|\le w\) and \(\|\Lambda^2 dg|_{TM_t}\|\le1\).  Hence only the
basic bound
\[
        \int_{M_t} w\,d\Mass\ge S_2S_3S_4
\]
follows from degree and the area formula.  The stronger desired bound with
\(S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\) is false without the global hard-regime hypotheses
(e.g. for the identity map).  Averaging such a stronger bound over \(t\) is
equivalent in strength to the missing cellular estimate, not an independent
shortcut.

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The proof is incomplete at one essential point: the cellular low-energy
fixed-puncture comparison \((13c)\), together with the corresponding
cellular averaging \((12b)\).  Mathematically, this would be a cubical
Federer--Fleming tightening of the two-parameter family
\(\{Z_y\}_{y\in Q}\), performed only on the low-energy set
\(E_y\le4I/q\).  The good cells must be projected to \(\partial R\) by coordinate
\(2\)-plaque tracks, while the bad cells are charged to the self-absorbing term.
The earlier sharper formula with the literal mass
\(\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\) is too optimistic because cutting a current
along \(\mathcal B_z\) has no controlled boundary.

What has been tried is the following.  Single-fiber filling improvements fail
because a slice can escape through the short \(R_1\)-direction.  Purely local
coordinate-track estimates fail in affine models: the density of a coordinate sweep
can be large while both \(\lambda^2\) and the averaged plaque-bad probability are
small.  Directional isoperimetry gives only (14), hence only the monomial estimates
and the partial bound (10a).  A coordinate-specific rational-homotopy calculation
again leaves the same exponent gap.  To close the proof one needs a genuinely
non-local cellular tightening proving \((13c)\), or an equivalent
family-level lower bound
\(I\gtrsim qS_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}\) in the regime
\(R_1\ll S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\).
