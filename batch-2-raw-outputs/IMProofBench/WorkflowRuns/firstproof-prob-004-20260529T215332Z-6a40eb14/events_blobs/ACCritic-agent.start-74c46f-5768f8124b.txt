## Paragraph-by-paragraph mathematical audit

### Title and “Problem statement and interpretation”

The interpretation of rectangles as oriented Euclidean boxes and degree as relative homology degree is reasonable and faithful. Renaming the problem’s constant \(k\) to \(\kappa\) is also sensible.

But the paragraph explicitly says this is a “draft” recording only the part currently rigorous and isolating a remaining estimate. This alone prevents readiness: the submitted document is not claiming to be a complete proof.

### “If \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), then \(\Dil_j(f)\le1\) for every \(j\ge2\)”

This argument is essentially correct for smooth points: if the singular values are ordered \(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\), then \(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\) implies \(\sigma_i\le1\) for \(i\ge2\), hence \(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le1\) for \(j\ge2\). For a piecewise smooth map, this should be stated as holding on each smooth piece or a.e. with the essential supremum. This is a minor rigor issue, not a fatal one.

### Lemma: “small relative \(2\)-cycles in a rectangle”

The lemma is plausible and consistent with Guth-type relative isoperimetric estimates, but the proof in `answer.tex` is only a sketch and is not rigorous enough for a complete solution.

Specific issues:

1. The category of cycles is not specified: integral currents, Lipschitz chains, polyhedral chains, or smooth relative cycles? The proof later uses slicing, pushforwards, and filling volumes, so this must be made precise.

2. The proof says “radial filling from a point \(p=(p_1,p_2,p_3)\)” inside a 4-rectangle. A point in \(\mathbb R^4\) needs four coordinates. If the construction is actually a radial deformation in the \((x_1,x_2)\)-plane with \(x_3,x_4\) fixed, that must be stated and proved.

3. The claimed volume bound \(C\,\operatorname{area}(Z)R_2\) is nontrivial. A naive cone to a point in the full rectangle could introduce dependence on \(R_3\) or \(R_4\). The proof needs a precise Federer–Fleming deformation/radial projection argument with averaging and control of the blow-up near the chosen center.

4. The threshold \(\frac12R_1R_2\) and the bound \(C_0R_1R_2^2\) need either a complete proof or an exact reference. Guth’s published isoperimetric theorem gives related estimates, but `answer.tex` does not cite the exact result or verify its hypotheses.

Thus Lemma `\ref{lem:fill}` is not fully proved as written.

### Proposition `\ref{prop:firstalt}`: statement

The proposition is plausible and resembles known Guth-style first-alternative estimates. It is also not by itself enough to prove the stated theorem, but it could be a useful partial result if fully justified.

### Proposition proof: lower bound for filling \(P_y\)

The claim that any relative \(3\)-chain filling
\[
P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\}
\]
has volume \(\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\) is probably true for central \(y_3,y_4\), but the proof given is incomplete.

The proposed proof says to project the filling to the \((x_1,x_2,x_3)\)-box and use the distance from the two \(x_3\)-faces. This projection argument does not rule out fillings that escape through the \(x_4\)-faces: such fillings may project with zero or cancelling \(3\)-volume to \((x_1,x_2,x_3)\). Since \(y_4\) is central and \(S_4\ge S_3\), escaping through \(x_4\) is still expensive enough, but the stated projection proof does not show it.

A rigorous proof could use a calibration-type argument with a Lipschitz function on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle vanishing on its boundary and equal to \(\gtrsim S_3\) at \((y_3,y_4)\). But that argument is not present.

### Proposition proof: slicing and degree of \(Z_y=f^{-1}(P_y)\)

The text says “Since \(f\) has degree \(1\), \(f_\#Z_y\) represents \(P_y\).” This needs a careful slicing argument.

For regular \(y=(y_3,y_4)\), \(Z_y\) should be understood as the slice of \(R\) by \((x_3,x_4)\circ f\). One must prove that the induced map \(Z_y\to P_y\) has relative degree one, or at least that \(f_\#Z_y\) pairs with the relevant relative cohomology/calibration as \(P_y\) does. This is standard but not automatic from the sentence given.

Also, if \(f_\#Z_y\) is merely homologous to \(P_y\), then the statement “\(f_\#Y\) fills \(P_y\)” is too quick. It fills \(f_\#Z_y\). The proof needs to show that the same filling lower bound applies to \(f_\#Z_y\), not only to the literal flat slice \(P_y\).

### Proposition proof: use of \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\)

This step is fine in principle: \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) implies \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), so the mass of a pushed-forward \(3\)-chain is at most the original mass. But again the current/chain category and piecewise smooth pushforward should be stated.

### Proposition proof: applying Lemma `\ref{lem:fill}`

The logic is correct modulo constants: if \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\) and \(C_1R_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3\) with \(C_1\) large enough, then a small-area \(Z_y\) would contradict the filling lemma. However, because the filling lemma itself is only sketched, this step inherits that gap.

### Proposition proof: coarea

The coarea argument is essentially correct: for \(F=(x_3,x_4)\circ f\), the \(2\)-Jacobian is bounded by \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), and integrating the masses of the fibers over the central \((y_3,y_4)\)-rectangle gives
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim R_1R_2S_3S_4.
\]
Dividing by \(R_1R_2\) gives \(R_3R_4\gtrsim S_3S_4\). The proof should explicitly restrict to regular values and use the area formula/coarea formula with multiplicity, but this is a manageable rigor issue.

### Reduction to the complementary case

The reduction is informal because it uses \(\gtrsim\) rather than fixed constants. For a final proof, the constants \(c_1,C_1,\kappa\) must be chosen in a precise order. The sentence “Taking \(\kappa\) smaller than the constants in these two alternatives” is not a proof of the required quantified statement unless all constants and strict inequalities are tracked.

### Proposition `\ref{prop:mixed}`: “mixed estimate still to be proved”

This is the fatal gap. Proposition `\ref{prop:mixed}` is not proved, and it is precisely the missing estimate needed to obtain the second alternative
\[
\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4
\]
when \(R_3R_4\) is not large.

The document explicitly labels it “still to be proved.” Therefore the main theorem is not proved.

There is also a constants issue: the complementary case from Proposition `\ref{prop:firstalt}` gives a lower bound of the form
\[
R_1R_2^2\ge C_1^{-1}S_1S_2S_3,
\]
whereas Proposition `\ref{prop:mixed}` assumes
\[
R_1R_2^2\ge c\,S_1S_2S_3.
\]
To combine them rigorously, one must ensure the threshold in the mixed proposition is compatible with \(C_1^{-1}\). This is not addressed.

### “Tempting proof” paragraphs

These paragraphs describe a possible strategy, not a proof. The proposed coarea-on-\((x_1,x_3,x_4)\circ f\) idea is plausible at a heuristic level, but the crucial lower bound on pulled-back \(1\)-cycles is not established.

The text correctly identifies an obstruction: the naive relative intervals in \(S\) can fill through the short \(x_1\)-direction with area \(O(S_1S_2)\), so they do not force filling area \(\gtrsim S_2S_3\). This is a useful diagnosis, but it confirms that the proof is missing rather than completing it.
