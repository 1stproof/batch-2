My opinion: not by a vanilla cubical Federer–Fleming deformation. The all-coordinate plaque bad set is the right *topological* object, but the quantitative step needed for (13) is a new weighted deformation lemma, not standard FF.

Here is the obstruction I would isolate.

1. **Track choice and the \(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\) cost.**  
   To use \(\mathcal B_z\), the deformation must be “Manhattan”: each point of the chain may change at most two source coordinates during the elementary homotopy. Then \(x\notin\mathcal B_z\) implies the whole elementary track has \(F\)-image avoiding \(z\). But ordinary radial projection in a cube changes three or four coordinates and is not protected by a 2-plaque bad set.

   Even with Manhattan tracks, standard FF controls track mass by directional masses of \(Z_y\), essentially leading back to Guth’s directional estimate (15). It does **not** convert to
   \[
   R_1\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2+\frac1{S_1}\left(\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\right)^2 .
   \]
   Low \(G=(f_1,f_2)\)-energy does not bound ordinary mass or directional mass of the slice. To get the displayed cost one would need a genuinely weighted/coarea deformation: choose good \(f_1\)-levels, cap \(f_1\)-seams with cost \(\sim E_y^2/S_1\), and push the remaining degree-carrying part through the \(R_1\)-direction with cost \(\sim R_1E_y\). I do not see a way to realize those caps by coordinate-plaque tracks while preserving the puncture avoidance. That is the concrete missing lemma.

2. **Near misses.**  
   If the construction is purely topological and every elementary track is exactly contained in a coordinate 2-plaque, then near misses are harmless: only actual crossing of \(z\) matters. But if one uses a punctured retraction \(\rho_z:Q\setminus\{z\}\to\partial Q\), or smooths/rounds tracks, near misses become fatal because \(|D\rho_z|\sim \operatorname{dist}(\cdot,z)^{-1}\). One would need a thickened bad set
   \[
   \mathcal B_{z,\delta}=\{x:\operatorname{dist}(z,F(P_{ij}(x)))<\delta\},
   \]
   and the required average estimate is false under only \(J_2F\le1\): a rank-one map on a plaque can have zero area image but be \(\delta\)-dense in \(Q\). Thus exact plaque badness is usable only for exact, unsmoothed Manhattan tracks; it cannot support a metric radial-retraction proof.

3. **Choice of fillings.**  
   I would not formulate (13) for arbitrary near-minimizing \(B_y\). The bad mass \(\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\) is not homological and is not stable under changing minimizers. The final argument only needs existence of a measurable near-minimizing selection, independent of \(z\), with
   \[
   \int_\Omega \Mass B_y\le C\int_\Omega \Fill_R(Z_y).
   \]
   Such a choice can be made by a measurable-selection argument, possibly minimizing a small averaged penalty involving \(q^{-1}\int_Q \Mass(B\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dz\). The all-plaque averaging lemma then still gives the self-absorbing term.

So: a localized cubical proof is plausible only after replacing standard FF by a bespoke **weighted Manhattan deformation theorem**. As written, ordinary cubical FF gives directional mass bounds, not the LE-HAF energy cost.

Reading (13) as a two-scale Federer–Fleming on the slice family, where $G=(f_1,f_2)$ acts as a "target-side calibration," is a useful organizing picture. Let me address the three subquestions in that frame, and flag the obstruction I find most serious.

To get $S_1$, you must use the $G$-degree on the slice, not just $\Mass(Z_y)$:

- *Linear term.* Push $Z_y$ in the $x_1$-direction onto $\{x_1=0\}$ but **weight the sweep density by $\lambda^2$**, not by the full mass. This requires discarding the $\lambda\approx0$ part of $Z_y$ — vertical (kernel-of-$dG$) leaves whose $f_1$-coordinate is constant, so they translate to themselves and the sweep is degenerate. The discarded mass needs to be filled separately, and that residue is exactly what should be charged to the plaque bad-set term.

- *Quadratic term.* Look at $G_\#(Z_y\!\!\restriction\!\{\lambda>0\})$ as a 2-current in the target rectangle: total mass $\le E_y$ (from $|df_1\wedge df_2|\le \lambda^2$) and degree 1 onto $[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]$. The cubical FF tightening should be performed on a target-side cubical decomposition at scale $\rho\in(0,S_1)$, pulled back to $Z_y$ via the regular-value structure of $G$. The standard FF trade-off on this 2D image gives $\Fill\lesssim \rho E_y + E_y^2/\rho$ on each $\rho$-cube, and minimizing in $\rho\le S_1$ produces $E_y^2/S_1$ when $E_y\ge S_1^2$ (i.e., in the regime where target-Federer-Fleming wins over a single target push).

So the cubical complex is naturally on the *target*, pulled back, not on the source — that is what makes $S_1$ (rather than $R_1$) appear.

**(2) Near-misses.** Replace $\mathcal B_z$ by the $\rho$-thickened set $\mathcal B_z^\rho=\{x:\mathrm{dist}(z, F(P_{ij}(x)))<\rho\text{ for some }i<j\}$ where $\rho$ is the cubical scale above. The all-coordinate plaque count survives:

since each $F(P_{ij}(x))^{(\rho)}$ has area at most $R_iR_j+C\rho(R_i+R_j)$. As long as $\rho\sum R_i\ll R_3R_4$, the absorbing fraction is still $O(R_3R_4/(S_3S_4))$. On the complement $R\setminus\mathcal B_z^\rho$, no coordinate plaque approaches $z$ within $\rho$, so $\rho_z\circ F$ (retraction of $Q\setminus B(z,\rho)$ to $\partial Q$) is well-defined and uniformly Lipschitz, which is what the cubical deformation needs.

Trying to state (13) for arbitrary near-minimizers is hopeless: the bad-mass integrand is filling-dependent.

After the puncture-avoiding decomposition, $Z_y$ is fragmented and degree-1 can disperse across cubes, the same way $\Vol_{12}(Z_y)$ can disperse below the desired $S_1S_2$ via laminate models. This is a fascinating approach, but the proposed cubical Federer–Fleming (FF) tightening strategy faces three severe, concrete obstructions. Here is a breakdown of why the localized fixed-puncture strategy fails as currently stated, followed by a constructive path forward.

### 1. The Track-Cost Mismatch ($R_1 E_y$ vs. Domain/Target Grids)
You want a deformation cost bounded by $R_1 E_y + E_y^2/S_1$, where $E_y = \int \lambda^2$ is a target-pullback energy. 
* **Domain FF:** If you construct a cubical grid in $R$ and push along the domain coordinate $x_1$, the maximum displacement is $R_1$. However, the volume swept out is governed by the physical mass $\Mass_{23}(Z_y)$, completely oblivious to the cancellation in $\lambda = \|d(f_1, f_2)|_{\ker dF}\|$. You get $R_1 \Mass(Z_y)$, not $R_1 E_y$.
* **Target FF:** If you pull back a grid from $[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]$ to drive the deformation, the sweep-out is governed by $E_y$, but the physical displacement in the domain is determined by the gradient flow of $f_1$. Since $\operatorname{Dil}_1 \le 1$, this track length is bounded only by $S_1$, yielding a cost of $S_1 E_y$, which loses the crucial $R_1$ factor. 
A single deformation vector field cannot simultaneously be bounded by $R_1$ (domain geometry) and have its normal Jacobian governed by $\lambda$ (target geometry) unless the map perfectly aligns with the axes.

### 2. The Logarithmic Divergence of Near Misses (The Fatal Obstruction)
Your bad set $\mathcal B_z$ only captures *exact hits*. However, when a 2-dimensional plaque $P$ maps to $F(P) \subset Q$, pushing $F(P)$ off a puncture $z$ requires a retraction $\rho_z : Q \setminus \{z\} \to \partial Q$. The differential scales as $|d\rho_z(w)| \sim 1/|w-z|$. 
The 2-dimensional mass of the deformation track scales as the Jacobian, $\int |dw|^2 / |w-z|^2$. When you average this cost over all punctures $z \in Q$, you must integrate $\int_Q |w-z|^{-2} dz$, which **diverges logarithmically**. 
In standard FF, we retract 1-cells off points (integrating $1/|w-z|$, which is finite), but here you are retracting 2-cells off a point in a 2D parameter space. The 2-capacity of a point is zero, meaning "near misses" will cause the expected mass of your tightened complex to explode. 

### 3. Incompatibility of Pointwise Fillings
The estimate cannot hold for arbitrary measurable choices of $B_y$. In any parametric tightening, the parameter space $Q$ is triangulated, and you must build 4-chains connecting the fillings over the "seams" between adjacent parameter values $y$ and $y'$. If the $B_y$'s are chosen fiber-by-fiber as independent almost-minimizers, the variation $B_y - B_{y'}$ is uncontrolled. The mass of the tracks connecting them will blow up.

### Constructive Path Forward
To salvage this framework, I would pivot as follows:

1. **Regularize the Puncture:** Do not retract off a point. Retract off a disk of macroscopic radius $r$. You will pay a finite topological penalty of $\sim \log(1/r)$ on the good set, and leave a "tube" of radius $r$ around $\mathcal B_z$. You then charge the volume of these tubes using a maximal function of the family $B_y$.
2. **Global Parametric Fillings:** Instead of measurable fiberwise selection, define $B_y$ as the slices of a single, globally minimizing 5-current $\mathbf{B}$ in $R \times Q$ with boundary corresponding to the family $Z_y$. This guarantees boundary compatibility across parameter seams.
3. **Change the Probe (Rational Homotopy):** The fundamental failure of mixing domain lengths ($R_1$) with target areas ($E_y$) via chains suggests this is the wrong language. As you suspected in Round 17, a DGA / rational homotopy probe is more natural. Instead of pushing currents, try to construct a differential form $\omega$ that explicitly weights the mixed planes. To beat the standard ellipsoid bounds, you need a secondary invariant (a Massey product or a relative cup product) that explicitly incorporates the boundary projection map, forcing the overlap of the degree-carrying regions with the $(3,4)$-directional cost.
