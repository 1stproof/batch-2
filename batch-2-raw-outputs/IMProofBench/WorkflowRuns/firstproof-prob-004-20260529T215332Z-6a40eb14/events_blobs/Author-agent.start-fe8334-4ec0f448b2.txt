I would not try to prove `(13')` by a pointwise Manhattan map. The chain-level version that could be rigorous is a Federer–Fleming style **chain projection/homotopy**, not a discontinuous collapse of \(B_y\setminus\mathcal B_z\).

The algebraic skeleton should be:

Construct operators \(P_z\) on relative \(3\)-chains and \(H_z\) on relative \(2\)-chains such that
\[
\partial P_z=P_z\partial,\qquad 
\partial H_z(T)=T-P_zT\quad\text{in }I_*(R,\partial R).
\]
Then, since \(\partial B_y=Z_y\),
\[
C_{y,z}:=H_z(Z_y)+P_z(B_y)
\]
fills \(Z_y\). Thus `(13')` would follow from
\[
\Mass H_z(Z_y)\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1,
\qquad
\Mass P_z(B_y)\lesssim \Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z).
\]
This is the right LaTeX-level template. The “Manhattan” condition should enter in constructing \(H_z,P_z\): tracks of cells are unions of coordinate \(2\)-plaque moves, so if a cell is good its tracks avoid the puncture and can be sent into \(\partial R\), hence become zero relatively.

However, I think the literal fixed-puncture statement with the **exact** term
\(\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\) is too sharp for this mechanism. A genuine chain map cannot decide pointwise “good outside \(\mathcal B_z\), bad inside \(\mathcal B_z\)” without producing interface seams. Equivalently, \(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z\) need not have controlled boundary; \(\partial(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\) can be huge or singular even when its mass is small. A discontinuous collapse of \(R\setminus\mathcal B_z\) to \(\partial R\) is not a pushforward of currents, and any Lipschitz smoothing introduces a transition/collar mass not charged by the exact bad term.

Penalized minimality of
\[
\Mass B+\frac{\mu}{q}\int \Mass(B\llcorner\mathcal B_z)\,dz
\]
does not fix this. That functional is just mass with a bounded measurable weight
\[
1+\mu\,p(x),\qquad p(x)=q^{-1}|\{z:x\in\mathcal B_z\}|.
\]
It gives no control on the slice/interface mass for a particular \(\mathcal B_z\). In particular it cannot justify cutting \(B_y\) sharply along \(\partial\mathcal B_z\).

So I would modify `(13')` to a **cellular/randomized bad-star** version. Choose a fine shifted cubulation and define \(\mathcal B_{z,\theta}^{\mathrm{cell}}\) as the union of cells whose prescribed Manhattan tracks have \(F\)-image containing \(z\). Prove
\[
\Mass C_{y,z,\theta}
\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1
+\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_{z,\theta}^{\mathrm{cell}}),
\]
then average in \((z,\theta)\). This avoids uncontrolled seams because whole cells are retained. The remaining task is to prove the averaged estimate
\[
\mathbb E_{z,\theta}\Mass(B_y\llcorner\mathcal B_{z,\theta}^{\mathrm{cell}})
\lesssim \frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\Mass B_y.
\]
If that cellular averaging fails, then the atomic approach really collapses.

Minor point: even formally, “every \(z\)” should probably be weakened to a.e. \(z\); the diagonal \(z=y\) is singular for punctured-parameter retractions and is irrelevant after averaging.

**The Verdict and the Concrete Obstruction**

The obstruction is the existence of **"flaps."** Suppose the map $f$ is such that the slice $Z_y$ contains a large surface region $W$ where $dG \approx 0$ (e.g., $f$ folds heavily in the domain but maps $W$ almost to a point in the $S_1 \times S_2$ projection). 
* On one hand, $\lambda \approx 0$ on $W$, so $W$ contributes almost nothing to $E_y$. 
* On the other hand, any "Manhattan" deformation performed in the source $R$ must physically transport the entire cycle $Z_y$, including $W$. Pushing $W$ along a coordinate axis by a distance $R_1$ sweeps out a 3-chain of mass $R_1 \Mass(W)$. 

Because $\Mass(W)$ can be large independently of $E_y$, the physical track cost $R_1 \Mass(Z_y)$ cannot be bounded by $R_1 E_y$. 

Conversely, if you try to bypass this by performing the deformation in the target $S$ (where the cost naturally scales as $E_y^2/S_1$) and pulling it back to $R$, you run into a Jacobian obstruction. The source mass of a pulled-back track depends on the integral of $1/J_3 f$. Your hypothesis $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$ bounds $J_3 f$ from above (by 1), but gives no lower bound. Thus, the source mass of the pullback can be arbitrarily large.

**The Core Conceptual Issue**
Inequality `(13')` commits a category error: it attempts to bound a *mass-based* geometric cost using a *target-energy* cost. Mass-minimization (Federer–Fleming or standard cubical tightening) is blind to the map's degree and penalizes all physical area equally. Your target energy $E_y$ only sees the "active" topological area. You cannot force the former to be bounded by the latter on the "good set" simply because the bad set is small.

You must abandon tightening the slices $B_y$ as geometric currents and instead apply the tightening to the **map $f$ itself** (or its associated differential forms/cochains).
