I don’t know a published theorem with exactly your normalization. But I would not try to prove Proposition P as a scalar “better sweepout” estimate. The true object should be a **typed cellular chain-map estimate with an \(\ell^2\) norm on mixed faces**. That \(\ell^2\) is where the square root should come from.

A plausible exact target is:

> Let \(F_\#:C_*(X)\to I_*(S)\) be a cubical chain map from a \(4\)-rectangle complex, carrying the fundamental class to \([S]\). Keep track of face types. Define  
> \(A\) = maximal total mass of the \(x_3x_4\)-plaquettes in one \(x_1x_2\)-slice;  
> \(B\) = the \(\ell^2\)-sum, over a square tiling of the \(x_3x_4\)-rectangle, of the masses of the mixed \(x_1x_3,x_1x_4\) plaquettes;  
> \(V=\mathbf M(F_\#[X])\).  
> Then
> \[
> \frac{A}{S_3S_4}
> +\frac{B}{S_1\sqrt{S_3S_4}}
> +\frac{V}{S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4}\ge c.
> \]
> Applying this to a \(2\)-contracting map gives \(A\lesssim R_3R_4\), \(V\lesssim\Vol(R)\), and—after tiling \(R_3\times R_4\) by roughly square pieces—  
> \[
> B\lesssim
> \Big(\frac{R_4}{R_3}\Big)^{1/2}(R_1R_3)
> =R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}.
> \]

This formulation avoids the false arbitrary-sweepout statement: the missing datum is not a bound on the maximal filling volume, but a quadratic/energy bound on the cellular connection between neighboring \(x_3x_4\)-cycles.

Proof strategy: adapt Guth’s complex-of-cycles tightening, but with coefficient norm \(\ell^2\) over the square tiling of the long \(S_3S_4\)-face.

1. Use Federer–Fleming deformation to tighten the \(2\)-skeleton. If \(A\ll S_3S_4\), every \(x_3x_4\)-plaquette can be pushed/filled without carrying the fundamental \(S_3S_4\)-class.

2. Track coherence of these fillings as one moves in the \(x_1\)-direction. The obstruction is controlled by the mixed \(x_1x_3,x_1x_4\) faces. Using the \(\ell^2\), not \(\ell^1\), norm over square tiles gives exactly the denominator \(S_1\sqrt{S_3S_4}\).

3. The remaining obstruction is the Almgren cup-square class: the degree-one condition says the relevant \(2\)-cycle class squares nontrivially to the top class. If both \(A\) and \(B\) are small, that square must be carried by the \(4\)-cells. The rectangular \(L^2\)-filling estimate over the \(S_2\times S_3\) directions then forces
\[
V\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4.
\]

I would look in Guth’s thesis/width-volume paper for the “bend-and-cancel” or complex-of-cycles tightening lemmas, but expect to prove this weighted \(\ell^2\) variant yourself. Pure coarea or packing-width arguments likely reproduce only the known monomial estimates; the square-root factor is genuinely a quadratic cup-product phenomenon.

The $(S_3/S_2)^{1/2}$ factor has the flavor of a Cauchy–Schwarz / Hölder between two dual face-directions and won't come from isotropic monomial tightening alone. Three angles, plus a caveat.

**(1) Cup-square identities with weighted Hölder.** Degree 1 gives three independent identities
$$\Vol(S)=\int_R f^*\omega_{ij}\wedge f^*\omega_{kl},\qquad (ij,kl)\in\{(12,34),(13,24),(14,23)\},$$
each with $|f^*\omega_{ij}|\le1$ pointwise. Symmetric Cauchy–Schwarz on any one gives only $\Vol(S)\le\Vol(R)$. The new content lives in *asymmetric* Hölder: coarea on $\pi_{ij}^S\circ f$ gives lower bounds on $\int|f^*\omega_{ij}|$ via fiber areas, and the target aspect $S_3/S_2$ determines the optimal split. The geometric mean $R_1\sqrt{R_3R_4}=\sqrt{(R_1R_3)(R_1R_4)}$ in your (5) is the natural output of Cauchy–Schwarz applied to the $\pi_{13}^S$ and $\pi_{14}^S$ projections; the $R_1\le\kappa S_1$ hypothesis enters as a uniform bound on the $x_1$-extent of those fibers.

**(2) Anisotropic Federer–Fleming.** Decompose source and target at non-uniform scale $b_i\propto S_i$, and run the deformation tightening *face-type-by-face-type* rather than summing all six 2-face types. The proof of Guth's Estimate 1 collapses them into one expression and produces only monomials; the variant that keeps each $(i,j)$-type separate should give a vector-valued inequality whose specialization to $\Dil_2\le1$ is (5) or a close variant. Guth's thesis (Chs. 4–5) treats the anisotropic case in some 3D/4D rectangle problems; plausibly the mixed-face version is implicit there but dropped in 0710.0403.

**(3) Direction-restricted coarea (sketch).** Sweep $R$ via $\Phi=\pi_{34}^S\circ f$. Each fiber $F_y$ is a 2-cycle, $f|_{F_y}:F_y\to S_{12}$ has degree 1 and is 2-contracting, so $\mathcal H^2(F_y)\ge S_1S_2$. Plain coarea on $\Phi$ gives only $\Vol(R)\ge\Vol(S)$. Plugging in $R_1\le\kappa S_1$: $F_y$'s $x_1$-extent is $\le R_1\ll S_1$, while $f_1|_{F_y}$ covers $S_1$. Extracting $(S_3/S_2)^{1/2}$ should require 2D-coarea inside $F_y$ plus the comass bound on $df_1\wedge df_2|_{F_y}$, but the bookkeeping is delicate — I don't see the clean version.

**Caveat — I'm uncertain (5) is sharp.** Your scaling saturates (5), but I would stress-test against

on the 6-parameter range satisfying all Guth monomials, checking that the max of the three terms in (5) stays $\ge c_0$ uniformly. If (5) fails somewhere, the worst case reveals the correct weights. If it holds, the proof likely combines (1) and (2).

**One more thought.** If proving (5) directly stalls, try the contrapositive: show no degree-1 $\Dil_2\le1$ map $R\to S$ exists for your scaling example. The natural attempt is a "snake in $x_2$ folding into $x_3$" construction; tracking why it must force $\Dil_2>1$ may explicitly reveal the missing inequality.

In Guth’s 2005 MIT Thesis (*Area-contracting maps between rectangles*, Main Theorem), he proves that if the sorted quotients $Q_i = S_i/R_i$ satisfy the monomial estimates for all $0 \le j < k \le l \le n$, there *actually exists* a degree-1 map $f: R \to S$ with $\Dil_k(f) \le C(n)$. 

Your explicit scaling satisfies all of Guth's monomial estimates for $k=2$ and $k=3$ up to uniform constants independent of $\varepsilon$. Because of this, Guth's folding construction guarantees the existence of a valid degree-1 map $f: (R, \partial R) \to (S, \partial S)$ with $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$ (after a minor rescaling by a dimensional constant, which does not fix the asymptotic gap). 

If you evaluate this specific, valid map against your desired alternatives, you have $R_1 \le \varepsilon S_1$, $R_3R_4 = \varepsilon S_3S_4$, and $\Vol(R) = \varepsilon S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4$. For $\varepsilon < \kappa$, this map strictly violates both of your target alternatives. Because a map exists that violates Proposition P, no anisotropic complex-of-cycles tightening (or any other homological argument) can prove it.

You correctly observed that the target face $S_1 \times S_3$ has area $\varepsilon^{-6}$, while the corresponding domain face $R_1 \times R_3$ has area $\varepsilon^{-4}$. If $f$ were a product map or strictly preserved the face structure, it would be forced to stretch $R_1 \times R_3$ by a factor of $\varepsilon^{-2} \gg 1$, violating $\Dil_2 \le 1$. However, a general degree-1 map is not geometrically rigid. Guth’s constructions use the massive available area in the other domain directions (e.g., $R_3 \times R_4$ has area $\varepsilon^{-11} \gg \varepsilon^{-6}$) to cover $S_1 \times S_3$ via "snaking" or folding, easily keeping the 2-dilation bounded. The topological condition of "degree 1" simply does not prevent this cross-mixing of dimensions.

3. **Variational/PDE constraints:** If $f$ arises from a specific physical or geometric process (e.g., it is harmonic or minimizes a specific energy), it may naturally resist arbitrary folding, forcing it to respect the mixed-face areas. 

I suggest revisiting the broader context that generated this sub-problem and extracting a stronger assumption on $f$. The degree-1 property alone is too flexible to enforce the mixed-face bounds you need.
