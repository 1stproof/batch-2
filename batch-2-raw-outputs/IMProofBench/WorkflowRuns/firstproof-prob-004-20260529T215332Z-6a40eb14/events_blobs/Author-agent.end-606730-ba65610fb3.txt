\begin{center}
{\bf Partial proof, and the remaining parametric gap}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Rectangles are oriented Euclidean boxes.  A map
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\) if
\(f_*[R,\partial R]=[S,\partial S]\) in relative homology.  I denote the
universal small constant in the problem by \(\kappa\), to avoid confusing it
with \(k\)-dilation.

The argument below proves the standard first alternative and all consequences of
Guth's published rectangle estimates that I currently know how to use.  It also
records a corrected obstruction to the averaged-filling route: the averaged
estimate used in the previous draft is false as stated.  The original theorem is
therefore still reduced to a missing genuinely parametric filling estimate,
stated in self-absorbing form in Proposition \ref{prop:selfabsorb}.  All
implicit constants below are positive dimensional constants.  Strict inequalities
in the statement would follow from the displayed non-strict estimates by
decreasing the final universal constant \(\kappa\).

\section*{Standard tools}

We use integral Lipschitz chains/currents.  Slicing, push-forward, and coarea
are meant in the Federer--Fleming sense; a piecewise smooth map is first treated
on the finitely many smooth pieces and then as the induced integral current.  If
\(T=[R,\partial R]\), \(\pi_{34}:S\to\RR^2\), and
\(F=\pi_{34}\circ f\), the slicing identity used below is
\[
        f_\#\langle T,F,y\rangle
        =\langle f_\#T,\pi_{34},y\rangle
\]
for a.e. regular value \(y\); it is the usual naturality of slices, first for
smooth maps by testing against forms and then by approximation.  The relative
versions for closed rectangles follow from the corresponding open statements by
collaring the boundary, applying the estimate on an exhaustion of the interior,
and letting the collar width tend to zero; the relative degree is unchanged in
this limiting process.

If \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), then \(\Dil_j(f)\le1\) for \(j=3,4\).  Indeed, if
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) are the singular values of \(df\), then
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), hence \(\sigma_i\le1\) for \(i\ge2\), and
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le1\) for \(j\ge2\).

We use the following two estimates of Guth.  First, the \(k=2,n=4\) small-volume
part of the rectangular relative isoperimetric profile theorem
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding} says that if \(Z\) is a relative integral
\(2\)-cycle in \(R\), \(A=\Mass Z\le c_I R_1R_2\), then
\[
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C_I
        \max\left\{ A^{3/2},\,\frac{A^2}{R_1}\right\}.
        \tag{1}
\]
In particular, in this range \(\Fill_R(Z)\le C_I R_1R_2^2\).  Second, Guth's
degree estimate \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}, with \(j=1,k=2,l=4\), gives
\[
        R_1^3R_2R_3R_4\ge c_G S_1^3S_2S_3S_4                  \tag{2}
\]
for every degree-one map of pairs with \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).

\section*{The first alternative}

\begin{lemma}[central target filling]\label{lem:target-fill}
Let
\[
 P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\},
\]
where \(y_3\in[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\) and
\(y_4\in[S_4/3,2S_4/3]\).  Every relative \(3\)-chain \(Y\) in \(S\) with
\(\partial Y=P_y\) modulo \(\partial S\) obeys
\[
        \Mass Y\ge c\,S_1S_2S_3 .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a smooth \(1+o(1)\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the
\((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle which is zero on the boundary and satisfies
\(\psi(y_3,y_4)\ge cS_3-o(1)\).  Apply Stokes' theorem to
\[
        \omega=dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge d\psi .
\]
The comass of \(\omega\) is at most \(1+o(1)\), so
\(\Mass Y\ge(1+o(1))^{-1}|\int_Y\omega|\), and Stokes' theorem gives
\[
 \int_Y\omega=\int_{\partial Y}\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2 .
\]
The part of \(\partial Y\) lying in \(\partial S\) contributes zero: on the
\(x_1\)- and \(x_2\)-faces \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\), while on the \(x_3\)- and
\(x_4\)-faces \(\psi=0\).  Letting \(o(1)\to0\) proves the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[first alternative]\label{prop:first}
There are constants \(c_1,C_1>0\) such that if
\(f:(R,\partial R)\to(S,\partial S)\) has degree \(1\), \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\), and
\[
        C_1R_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3 ,
\]
then
\[
        R_3R_4\ge c_1S_3S_4 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(F=(x_3,x_4)\circ f\), and set
\[
Q=[S_3/3,2S_3/3]\times[S_4/3,2S_4/3].
\]
For a.e. \(y\in Q\), put \(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\).  Slicing
naturality gives
\[
        f_\# Z_y
        =\langle [S,\partial S],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle=P_y
\]
as relative currents, up to orientation.  Thus any relative filling of \(Z_y\)
in \(R\) pushes forward to a relative filling of \(P_y\) in \(S\).  Since
\(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), Lemma \ref{lem:target-fill} implies
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .                         \tag{3}
\]
Choose \(C_1\) large compared with \(C_I/c\).  If
\(C_1R_1R_2^2\le S_1S_2S_3\), then (1) and (3) force
\(\Mass Z_y\ge c_I R_1R_2\) for a.e. \(y\in Q\).  Coarea and \(J_2F\le1\) give
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge\int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
      \ge c R_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Since \(\Vol(R)=R_1R_2R_3R_4\), the proposition follows.
\end{proof}

\section*{Consequences of the same slicing}

For the same central fibers,
\[
 \Mass Z_y\ge c\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\}                       \tag{4}
\]
for a.e. \(y\in Q\).  Indeed, if \(A=\Mass Z_y<c_I R_1R_2\), then (1) and
(3) imply
\[
 cS_1S_2S_3\le C_I\max\{A^{3/2},A^2/R_1\}.
\]
After integration over \(Q\),
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge cS_3S_4\min\left\{R_1R_2,\,
          (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\,
          (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\right\}.                      \tag{5}
\]

The monomial estimate (2) covers the mildly anisotropic target range.  From
\(R_1\le\kappa S_1\),
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c_G\kappa^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .              \tag{6}
\]
Therefore (6) implies the desired second alternative whenever
\(S_3/S_2\le c\,\kappa^{-6}\).  In the remaining range
\(S_3/S_2\gg_\kappa1\), if \(\kappa<c_1\) and the first desired alternative
fails, Proposition \ref{prop:first} gives the complementary lower bound
\[
        R_1R_2^2\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3 .                          \tag{7}
\]


\section*{A sharper algebraic reduction}

It is useful to record exactly what the preceding proved estimates leave
unsettled.  Put
\[
        \alpha=\frac{R_1}{S_1},\qquad
        T=S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
\]
Assume from now on that the first desired alternative fails, with
\(\kappa<c_1\).  Then Proposition \ref{prop:first} gives
\(R_1R_2^2\gtrsim F_0=S_1S_2S_3\).  Consequently the three terms in (5) are all
at least a constant multiple of \((R_1F_0)^{1/2}\): the first by
\(R_1R_2^2\gtrsim F_0\), the second because \(S_1\le S_2\le S_3\) and
\(R_1\le S_1\).  Hence (5) improves in the reduced case to
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c\,S_3S_4(R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}
        =c\,\alpha^{1/2}T .                                  \tag{13}
\]
On the other hand Guth's monomial estimate gives the sharper form
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge c\,\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .                \tag{14}
\]
Indeed (2) is \(R_1^2\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1^3S_2S_3S_4\).

Therefore, if the second desired alternative also fails, namely
\(\Vol(R)<\kappa T\), then (13) and (14) force
\[
        \alpha\lesssim \kappa^2,\qquad
        \frac{S_3}{S_2}\gtrsim \alpha^{-4}\kappa^{-2}.          \tag{15}
\]
Thus the remaining case is more special than just ``high aspect ratio'': the
first side of \(R\) must be quadratically smaller than the allowed bound, and
the target aspect ratio \(S_3/S_2\) must be correspondingly enormous.  This is
the regime in which the single-fiber rectangular isoperimetric estimate loses
the factor \(\alpha^{1/2}\), and it is exactly the regime for which a
family-level tightening estimate such as (11) is still needed.

\section*{Weighted coarea}

Let \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), and at rank-two points of \(F\) set
\[
        \lambda(x)=\left\|dG|_{\ker dF_x}\right\|,
\]
with \(\lambda=0\) on the rank-deficient set.  Then
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge I:=\int_Q\int_{Z_y}\lambda(x)^2\,d\mathcal H^2(x)\,dy,
                                                                    \tag{8}
\]
and, for a.e. \(y\in Q\),
\[
        \int_{Z_y}\lambda^2\,d\mathcal H^2\ge S_1S_2 .        \tag{9}
\]
To prove (8), let \(T=\ker dF_x\) and \(N=T^\perp\).  If \(v\in T\) is unit
and \(|dG(v)|=\lambda\), then for every unit \(n\in N\),
\[
        |df(v)\wedge df(n)|\ge \lambda |dF(n)|.
\]
Thus \(\lambda^2J_2F\le1\), and coarea gives (8).  For (9), slicing
naturality shows that \(G|_{Z_y}\) has relative degree \(1\) onto the
\(S_1\times S_2\) rectangle; since \(\lambda^2\) dominates the absolute
\(2\)-Jacobian of \(G|_{Z_y}\), (9) follows.  This recovers only the basic
bound \(\Vol(R)\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3S_4\).

\section*{The false averaged estimate and a corrected target}

Let \(q=|Q|\sim S_3S_4\) and \(F_0=S_1S_2S_3\).  The previous draft used the
estimate
\[
 \int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\left(R_1 I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}
       +\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\,F_0q\right).                  \tag{10}
\]
This estimate is false.

\begin{proposition}[counterexample to (10)]\label{prop:af-false}
For arbitrarily large \(L\), there are degree-one maps of pairs with
\(\Dil_2\le1\) for which the left side of (10) is larger than the right side by
a positive power of \(L\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(S=(1,1,L,L)\), \(R=(1,M,L,L)\), \(1<M<L\), and
\[
        f(u_1,u_2,u_3,u_4)=(u_1,u_2/M,u_3,u_4).
\]
The singular values are \(1,1,1,1/M\), so \(\Dil_2(f)=1\), and the map has
relative degree \(1\).  For \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), the central slices are
\[
        Z_y=[0,1]\times[0,M]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\},
\]
and \(\lambda\equiv1\) on them.  Hence \(I\sim ML^2\) and \(q\sim L^2\).

On the other hand \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\gtrsim ML\).  Indeed, choose a
\(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the \((u_3,u_4)\)-rectangle, zero on its
boundary and with \(\psi(y)\gtrsim L\), and calibrate by
\(du_1\wedge du_2\wedge d\psi\).  Boundary terms vanish on the \(u_1,u_2\)
faces because \(du_1\wedge du_2=0\), and on the \(u_3,u_4\) faces because
\(\psi=0\).  Therefore
\[
        \int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy\gtrsim ML^3 .
\]
The three terms on the right of (10) are
\[
        ML^2,\qquad M^2L^2,\qquad L^3 .
\]
Taking \(M=L^{1/2}\) gives \(L^{7/2}\) on the left and \(O(L^3)\) on the
right.
\end{proof}

\section*{A rigorous projection fact}

There is one useful averaged fact behind the coefficient in (11).  Let
\(\pi_{12}:R\to[0,R_1]\times[0,R_2]\), and for a.e. \(y\in Q\) set
\[
 a(y)=\mathcal L^2\bigl(\pi_{12}(\operatorname{spt}Z_y)\bigr).
\]
Then
\[
        \int_Q a(y)\,dy\le R_1R_2R_3R_4 .                  \tag{10a}
\]
Indeed, by Fubini and by writing \(V_u=\{u\}\times[0,R_3]\times[0,R_4]\),
\[
\begin{aligned}
 \int_Q a(y)\,dy
 &=\int_{[0,R_1]\times[0,R_2]}
      \mathcal L^2\{y\in Q:Z_y\cap V_u\ne\varnothing\}\,du       \\
 &\le\int_{[0,R_1]\times[0,R_2]}
      \mathcal L^2(F(V_u))\,du
 \le R_1R_2R_3R_4 ,
\end{aligned}
\]
because \(F=(f_3,f_4)\) has \(2\)-Jacobian at most \(1\) on each vertical
rectangle \(V_u\).  Thus, in the hard regime \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\), most
central fibers miss most points of the \((x_1,x_2)\)-base.  What is still
missing is a way to combine (10a) with the filling lower bound (3) without
assuming any false independence between \(a(y)\) and \(\Fill_R(Z_y)\).  This is
precisely the role of the self-absorbing estimate below.

A self-absorbing replacement would still close the theorem.

\begin{proposition}[conditional self-absorbing estimate]\label{prop:selfabsorb}
Assume that there is a dimensional constant \(C_A\) such that, in the reduced
central-slice situation above,
\[
 A:=\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C_A\left(R_1 I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
       +C_A\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\,A .                       \tag{11}
\]
Then the theorem in the problem follows.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
By (3), \(A\ge cF_0q\).  If the first desired alternative fails and
\(\kappa<1/(2C_A)\), the last term in (11) is absorbed, so
\[
        F_0q\lesssim R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}.                  \tag{12}
\]
If
\[
        I\le \eta\,q\,S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2},
\]
then \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(S_1\le S_2\le S_3\) give
\[
        R_1I\le \eta\kappa F_0q,\qquad
        \frac{I^2}{S_1q}\le \eta^2F_0q .
\]
Choosing \(\eta\) and then \(\kappa\) small contradicts (12).  Hence
\[
        I\ge c\,q\,S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}.
\]
Finally (8) yields
\[
 \Vol(R)\ge I
   \ge c S_3S_4\,S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}
   =cS_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 ,
\]
which is the second desired alternative after decreasing \(\kappa\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The proof is incomplete because (11), or an equivalent parametric filling
estimate, has not been proved.

\begin{itemize}
\item Mathematically, the missing point is a two-parameter
Federer--Fleming/Guth tightening estimate for the family
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],(f_3,f_4),y\rangle\), using not only the
individual cycles but also the mixed \(2\)-Jacobian information
\(\lambda^2J_2F\le1\).
\item The gap appears exactly in Proposition \ref{prop:selfabsorb}.  The older
non-absorbing estimate (10) cannot be repaired by changing constants, by
Proposition \ref{prop:af-false}.
\item What has been tried: fiberwise isoperimetry gives (4) and (5); after the
first-alternative reduction it yields (13), so a counterexample to the desired
second alternative would have to satisfy the sharper restrictions (15).  The
weighted coarea estimate gives only the basic volume bound; Guth's monomial
estimate gives (14), which combines with (13) to leave only the extreme range
(15); the projection average (10a) gives the correct bad-fiber fraction but not
the needed decoupling; and the false boundary-trace and false averaged
estimates have now been ruled out by explicit product examples.
\item What is needed: a proof of the self-absorbing estimate (11), probably by
applying Guth's tightening construction to the whole two-parameter complex of
slices and leaving the uncontrolled top-dimensional parameter cells as a
fraction \(\sim R_3R_4/(S_3S_4)\) of the original average filling.
\end{itemize}
