\begin{center}
{\bf Partial proof and reduction for the rectangle \(2\)-dilation estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I write the universal constant in the problem as \(\kappa\), to avoid
confusion with \(k\)-dilation.  Rectangles are oriented Euclidean products
\(\prod_i[0,R_i]\), and degree \(1\) means degree \(1\) on relative
\(H_4(\cdot,\partial\cdot;\mathbb Z)\).

The argument below proves the standard first alternative and the best
unconditional estimate I can currently justify from the published
rectangle inequalities and slicing.  It also isolates precisely the remaining
hard estimate needed for the stated theorem.

\section*{Two standard inputs}

We use two standard facts.  First, if \(\Dil_2(f)\le 1\), then
\(\Dil_3(f)\le1\) and \(\Dil_4(f)\le1\).  This follows pointwise from
singular values: if \(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) and
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), then \(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le1\) for \(j\ge2\).

Second, we use Guth's published lower estimate for degree-nonzero maps of
pairs between rectangles \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}.  In the case
\(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=4\), it gives
\begin{equation}\label{guth14}
        R_1^2\,\Vol(R)\ge c_0\,S_1^3S_2S_3S_4                  \tag{1}
\end{equation}
whenever \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).  We also use the rectangular relative
isoperimetric profile: there are constants \(c_1,C_1>0\) such that every
relative \(2\)-cycle \(Z\) in \(R\) with
\(\Mass Z<c_1R_1R_2\) satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{iso}
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C_1R_1R_2^2 .                              \tag{2}
\end{equation}
This is the \(2\)-cycle case of Guth's rectangle isoperimetric profile.

\section*{The first alternative}

\begin{lemma}[Filling a central target plane]\label{central}
Let \(Q\) be the middle half of the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle of \(S\).  For
\(y\in Q\), set
\[
P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\}.
\]
Every relative \(3\)-chain in \(S\) filling \(P_y\) has volume at least
\(cS_1S_2S_3\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle
which vanishes on its boundary and is \(\ge cS_3\) on \(Q\).  Put
\(\beta=\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2\).  The \(3\)-form \(d\beta\) has comass at
most \(1\).  If \(\partial Y=P_y\) relatively, then Stokes' theorem gives
\[
\Mass Y\ge\int_Y d\beta=\int_{P_y}\beta
       = S_1S_2\psi(y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
The boundary-face terms vanish because either \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\) on the
\(x_1,x_2\)-faces or \(\psi=0\) on the \(x_3,x_4\)-faces.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{firstalt}
There is \(c_2>0\) such that if
\[
        R_1R_2^2\le c_2S_1S_2S_3,
\]
then necessarily
\[
        R_3R_4\ge c_2S_3S_4 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(F=(f_3,f_4)\).  For a.e. \(y\in Q\), the slice
\(Z_y=\langle [R,\partial R],F,y\rangle\) is a relative \(2\)-cycle in \(R\),
and slicing naturality gives \(f_\#Z_y=P_y\).  Therefore any filling of
\(Z_y\) in \(R\) maps, because \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), to a filling of \(P_y\).
By Lemma \ref{central},
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
Choosing \(c_2\) small compared to \(c/C_1\), the isoperimetric estimate
\eqref{iso} implies \(\Mass Z_y\ge c_1R_1R_2\) for a.e. \(y\in Q\).
By the coarea formula and \(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\),
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge \int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
        \ge cR_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Since \(\Vol(R)=R_1R_2R_3R_4\), this gives the result.
\end{proof}

\section*{What this proves toward the stated theorem}

Assume now that \(R_1\le S_1\) and that the first conclusion fails with a
small constant, so \(R_3R_4\le c_2S_3S_4\).  Proposition \ref{firstalt}
then gives
\[
        R_1R_2^2\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .                                  \tag{3}
\]
The full isoperimetric profile of a rectangle gives, for the same slices,
\[
\Mass Z_y\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,\ (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\
                         (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\}.
\]
Using \((3)\) and \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\), this lower bound is at least
\[
        c\,\alpha^{1/2}S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}.
\]
Integrating over \(Q\) yields
\begin{equation}\label{slicevol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,\alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .                 \tag{4}
\end{equation}
On the other hand, Guth's estimate \eqref{guth14} gives
\begin{equation}\label{monovol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .                              \tag{5}
\end{equation}
Combining \eqref{slicevol} and \eqref{monovol}, and optimizing in
\(\alpha\), gives the rigorous partial bound
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4 .
\]
Equivalently, this is the desired second alternative times the
loss factor
\[
        (S_2/S_3)^{1/10}.
\]

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The proof above does not yet establish the stated theorem.  The missing
mathematical point is a genuinely four-dimensional, family-level tightening
estimate for the slices \(Z_y\) of \(F=(f_3,f_4)\).  In the notation
\[
E_y=\int_{Z_y}\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{T Z_y}\|^2,\qquad
I=\int_QE_y\,dy ,
\]
weighted coarea gives \(I\le\Vol(R)\), and each \(Z_y\) has filling volume
\(\gtrsim S_1S_2S_3\).  The stated theorem would follow from an averaged
estimate of the form
\[
 \int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1|Q|}\right)
     +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy .
\]
When \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), the last term would
absorb and force
\[
        I\gtrsim S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4,
\]
hence the desired volume lower bound.  Standard single-slice isoperimetry and
Guth's published monomial estimates do not prove this averaged tightening;
the gap is exactly the anisotropic factor \((S_3/S_2)^{1/10}\) above.
