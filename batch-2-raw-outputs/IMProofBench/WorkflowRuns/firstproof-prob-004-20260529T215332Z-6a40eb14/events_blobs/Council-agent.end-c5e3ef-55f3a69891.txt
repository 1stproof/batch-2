My take: this is not a viable *fiberwise 3D theorem* in the stated data. There is a sharp basic bound, but it is only
\[
\int_{M_t}|\nabla u|^{-1}\gtrsim S_2S_3S_4,
\]
not the stronger \(S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\).

Indeed, put \(w=|\nabla u|^{-1}\). If \(\mu_1\ge\mu_2\ge\mu_3\) are the singular values of \(dg|_{T M_t}\), then \(\mu_1\mu_2\le1\) and the mixed normal estimate gives \(\mu_1\le w\). Hence
\[
J_3 g=\mu_1\mu_2\mu_3\le \mu_1\le w.
\]
Since \(g\) has degree one,
\[
S_2S_3S_4\le \int_{M_t} |J_3g|\le \int_{M_t}w.
\]
This is sharp for the product/identity map \(R=S\), \(f=\mathrm{id}\): then \(w=1\) and \(M_t\) is exactly the target \(3\)-rectangle, so the left side is \(S_2S_3S_4\), smaller than the proposed bound by \((S_3/S_2)^{1/2}\). This is a genuine global \(2\)-contracting degree-one map, though of course not in the hard regime.

Under both hard assumptions \(R_1\ll S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4\), I do not know a compatible counterexample. But an averaged version is essentially equivalent to the missing hard-regime volume estimate, because coarea gives
\[
\int_0^{S_1}\left(\int_{M_t}|\nabla u|^{-1}\,dM_t\right)dt=\Vol(R).
\]
Thus proving the proposed lower bound on a central interval of \(t\)’s would already prove the desired second alternative. It is not a shortcut unless one can inject the hard-regime global compatibility of the whole \(f_1\)-sweep.

The obstruction to a direct proof is the usual “escape through the short \(x_1\)-direction.” On a fixed target hyperplane \(x_1=t\), one would like boundary-type \(3\)D width-volume behavior, but source fillings of level-slice objects are not constrained to remain in \(f_1=t\). Once they leave the level, target intervals/surfaces can fill through the short \(S_1\)-direction, giving only the basic-volume scale. Controlling that escape is precisely a parametric issue involving all \(t\)’s, not a single-slice estimate.

So I would phrase this route as a reformulation of the missing LE-HAF/Manhattan tightening, not as an independent weighted 3D lemma. The stress model
\[
S=(1,1,L^6,L^6),\qquad R=(L^{-1},L^4,L^5,L^6)
\]
would have averaged \(W(t)\sim \Vol(R)/S_1=L^{14}\), while the proposed scale is \(L^{15}\). A bounded \(2\)-dilation degree-one map for that model would kill the bound; none is currently known.
