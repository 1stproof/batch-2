\begin{center}
{\bf Verified reduction for the rectangle \(2\)-dilation estimate}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I write the universal constant in the problem as \(\kappa\), to avoid
confusion with \(k\)-dilation.  Rectangles are oriented Euclidean products
\(\prod_i[0,R_i]\), and degree \(1\) means degree \(1\) on relative
\(H_4(\cdot,\partial\cdot;\mathbb Z)\).

The proof below is stated for smooth maps transverse to the slicing maps.  This
loses no generality for the verified parts: a piecewise smooth pair map may be
smoothed on a subdivision, relative to the boundary strata and with \(C^1\)
control on each stratum, so that the relative degree is unchanged and
\(\Dil_2\le1+\varepsilon\).  Postcomposing the target with the homothety
\((1+\varepsilon)^{-1/2}\) reduces to the stated bound at the cost of changing
universal constants only; then \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\).  Equivalently, all
statements may be read in the language of integral currents and Ambrosio--Kirchheim
slicing.

This document gives a complete proof of the first alternative and a sharp
reduction of the second alternative to one family-level tightening estimate.  The
tightening estimate is formulated precisely at the end; it remains the sole
unproved point in this draft.

\section*{Standard inputs}

All constants below are positive and universal.  First, if \(\Dil_2(f)\le 1\),
then \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\) and \(\Dil_4(f)\le1\).  Indeed, if
\(\sigma_1\ge\cdots\ge\sigma_4\) are the singular values and
\(\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\), then \(\sigma_2\le1\), and hence
\(\sigma_1\cdots\sigma_j\le\sigma_1\sigma_2\le1\) for \(j\ge2\).

Second, we use Guth's lower estimate for degree-nonzero maps of pairs between
rectangles \cite[Estimate 1]{GuthExpanding}.  In the case
\(n=4,k=2,j=1,l=4\), it gives
\begin{equation}\label{guth14}
        R_1^2\,\Vol(R)\ge c_0\,S_1^3S_2S_3S_4
\end{equation}
whenever \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\).

Third, we use Guth's small-volume relative isoperimetric profile of a rectangle
\cite[Theorem 3]{GuthExpanding}.  In the form needed here, there are constants
\(c_*,C_*\) such that, if \(Z\) is a relative \(2\)-cycle in \(R\) with
\(A=\Mass(Z)\le c_*R_1R_2\), then
\[
 \Fill_R(Z)\le C_*\max\{A^{3/2},\ A^2/R_1\}.
\]
Consequently, if \(\Fill_R(Z)\ge L\), then
\begin{equation}\label{profile-inverse}
\Mass(Z)\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,\ L^{2/3},\ (R_1L)^{1/2}\}.
\end{equation}
Also, after decreasing \(c_*\) if necessary,
\begin{equation}\label{iso}
        \Mass Z<c_*R_1R_2\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
        \Fill_R(Z)\le C R_1R_2^2 .
\end{equation}

\section*{The first alternative}

\begin{lemma}[Filling a central target plane]\label{central}
Let \(Q\) be the concentric subrectangle of
\([0,S_3]\times[0,S_4]\) with side lengths \(S_3/2,S_4/2\).  For
\(y\in Q\), set
\[
P_y=[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]\times\{y\}.
\]
Every relative \(3\)-chain in \(S\) filling \(P_y\) has volume at least
\(cS_1S_2S_3\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Choose a \(1\)-Lipschitz function \(\psi\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-rectangle
which vanishes on its boundary and is \(\ge cS_3\) on \(Q\).  Put
\(\beta=\psi\,dx_1\wedge dx_2\).  The \(3\)-form \(d\beta\) has comass at
most \(1\).  If \(\partial Y=P_y\) relatively, then Stokes' theorem gives
\[
\Mass Y\ge\left|\int_Y d\beta\right|
=\left|\int_{P_y}\beta\right|
       = S_1S_2\psi(y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
The boundary-face terms vanish because either \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\) on the
\(x_1,x_2\)-faces or \(\psi=0\) on the \(x_3,x_4\)-faces.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{firstalt}
There is \(c_2>0\) such that, under the hypotheses of the problem, if
\[
        R_1R_2^2\le c_2S_1S_2S_3,
\]
then
\[
        R_3R_4\ge c_2S_3S_4 .
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(T=[[R]]\) be the fundamental relative current and let
\(F=(f_3,f_4)\).  Since \(f\) has relative degree \(1\), \(f_\#T\) represents
\([S]\) in \(H_4(S,\partial S)\).  In the interior of \(S\), the current
\(f_\#T\) has no boundary, so the constancy theorem gives
\(f_\#T=[[S]]\) there with multiplicity equal to the degree.  The difference
\(f_\#T-[[S]]\) is therefore supported in \(\partial S\), and no nonzero
integral \(4\)-current can be supported on the \(3\)-rectifiable set
\(\partial S\).  Hence \(f_\#T=[[S]]\) as a relative \(4\)-current.

By slicing naturality, for a.e. \(y\in Q\),
\[
        f_\#\langle T,F,y\rangle
        =\langle [[S]],(x_3,x_4),y\rangle
        =\pm P_y
\]
in \(I_2(S,\partial S)\).  Put \(Z_y=\langle T,F,y\rangle\).  Any relative
filling of \(Z_y\) in \(R\) maps, since \(\Dil_3(f)\le1\), to a relative
filling of \(P_y\).  Lemma \ref{central} gives
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\]
Choose \(c_2\) so small that \(Cc_2<c\) in \eqref{iso}.  Then
\(\Mass Z_y\ge c_*R_1R_2\) for a.e. \(y\in Q\).  Coarea and
\(J_2F\le\Dil_2(f)\le1\) give
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge \int_Q\Mass Z_y\,dy
        \ge cR_1R_2S_3S_4 .
\]
Since \(\Vol(R)=R_1R_2R_3R_4\), this proves the proposition after decreasing
\(c_2\).
\end{proof}

\section*{The best bound obtained from single slices}

Assume \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\) and that the first conclusion fails with a
small constant.  Proposition \ref{firstalt} gives
\begin{equation}\label{r12large}
        R_1R_2^2\ge cS_1S_2S_3 .
\end{equation}
For the slices above, Lemma \ref{central} and \eqref{profile-inverse}, with
\(L=cS_1S_2S_3\), give
\[
\Mass Z_y\ge c\min\{R_1R_2,\ (S_1S_2S_3)^{2/3},\
                         (R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2}\}.
\]
Using \eqref{r12large} and \(R_1=\alpha S_1\le S_1\), each term in this
minimum is at least
\[
        c\,\alpha^{1/2}S_1(S_2S_3)^{1/2}.
\]
Integrating over \(Q\) yields
\begin{equation}\label{slicevol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,\alpha^{1/2}S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4 .
\end{equation}
On the other hand, Guth's estimate \eqref{guth14} gives
\begin{equation}\label{monovol}
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,\alpha^{-2}S_1S_2S_3S_4 .
\end{equation}
Combining \eqref{slicevol} and \eqref{monovol}, and optimizing in
\(\alpha\), gives the unconditional partial bound
\[
        \Vol(R)\ge
        c\,S_1S_2^{3/5}S_3^{7/5}S_4 .
\]
This is short of the desired second alternative by the factor
\((S_2/S_3)^{1/10}\).

\section*{The averaged estimate which would finish the proof}

Let \(G=(f_1,f_2)\), \(F=(f_3,f_4)\), and for \(y\in Q\) set
\(Z_y=\langle [[R]],F,y\rangle\).  Define
\[
E_y=\int \|dG|_{T Z_y}\|_{HS}^2\,d\|Z_y\|,\qquad
I=\int_QE_y\,dy,\qquad q=|Q|.
\]
At a point where \(dF\) has rank two, put \(K=\ker dF\), let
\(\lambda=\|dG|_K\|\), and let \(\tau_1,\tau_2\) be the singular values of
\(dF\) on \(K^\perp\).  Since the \(G\)- and \(F\)-coordinates in the target
are orthogonal, \(\Dil_2(f)\le1\) gives \(\lambda\tau_i\le1\).  Hence
\(\|dG|_K\|_{HS}^2J_2F\le C\).  The rank-deficient set contributes zero to
the coarea integral, so
\begin{equation}\label{Icoarea}
        I\le C\Vol(R).
\end{equation}

Let
\[
        \Omega=\{y\in Q:E_y\le4I/q\}.
\]
Then \(|\Omega|\ge3q/4\).  Lemma \ref{central} gives, for a.e. \(y\in Q\),
\[
        \Fill_R(Z_y)\ge cS_1S_2S_3,
\]
and hence
\begin{equation}\label{AlowerOmega}
        \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
        \ge cS_1S_2S_3\,q
        \ge cS_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .
\end{equation}

The following restricted averaged estimate is sufficient:
\begin{equation}\label{sufficientOmega}
 \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
 \le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
     +C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}
        \int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy .
\end{equation}
Indeed, if \(R_1\le\kappa S_1\) and \(R_3R_4\le\kappa S_3S_4\), choose
\(\kappa\) so small that the last term in \eqref{sufficientOmega} is absorbed.
If \(I\le\delta S_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), then, since
\(q\sim S_3S_4\) and \(S_1\le S_2\le S_3\),
\[
R_1I\le C\kappa\delta\,S_1S_2S_3^2S_4,\qquad
\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\le C\delta^2\,S_1S_2S_3^2S_4 .
\]
Taking first \(\delta\) and then \(\kappa\) small contradicts
\eqref{AlowerOmega}.  Thus
\(I\ge cS_1S_2^{1/2}S_3^{3/2}S_4\), and \eqref{Icoarea} gives the desired
volume lower bound.

\section*{The exact-plaque lemma which would imply \eqref{sufficientOmega}}

For \(z\in Q\), let \({\mathcal B}_z\) be the set of points \(x\in R\) such
that \(z\) lies in the \(F\)-image of at least one of the six coordinate
\(2\)-plaques through \(x\).  For fixed \(x\), the measure of such \(z\)'s is
at most \(C R_3R_4\), because each coordinate \(2\)-plaque has \(F\)-image
area at most its source area and every coordinate \(2\)-plaque has area
\(\le R_3R_4\).  Consequently, for every integral \(3\)-chain \(B\),
\begin{equation}\label{badfubini}
 \frac{1}{q}\int_Q\Mass(B\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z)\,dz
 \le C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\Mass B .
\end{equation}

\begin{lemma}[Exact-plaque energy chain-map lemma]\label{plaquelemma}
There is a universal \(C\) with the following property.  For a.e. regular
\(y\in Q\), for every relative integral \(3\)-chain \(B_y\) with
\(\partial B_y=Z_y\), and for a.e. \(z\in Q\), there are relative chain
operators \(P_z,H_z\), defined on \(B_y\) and \(Z_y\), such that
\[
\partial H_z(T)+H_z(\partial T)=P_z(T)-T,\qquad
\partial P_z(T)=P_z(\partial T),
\]
and
\[
\Mass H_z(Z_y)\le C\left(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\right),\qquad
\Mass P_z(B_y)\le C\,\Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z).
\]
\end{lemma}

Assuming Lemma \ref{plaquelemma}, put
\(C_{y,z}=P_z(B_y)-H_z(Z_y)\).  The chain-homotopy identity gives
\[
\partial C_{y,z}=P_zZ_y-(P_zZ_y-Z_y)=Z_y,
\]
so \(C_{y,z}\) is a relative filling of \(Z_y\).  Therefore
\[
\Fill_R(Z_y)\le
C\left(R_1E_y+\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\right)
+C\,\Mass(B_y\llcorner{\mathcal B}_z).
\]
Choose \(B_y\) measurably with \(\Mass B_y\le2\Fill_R(Z_y)\) for a.e. \(y\)
(for instance by the Jankov--von Neumann selection theorem applied to the
standard Borel space of flat integral currents).  Average the last display over
\(z\) and \(y\in\Omega\).  Using \eqref{badfubini} and
\(E_y\le4I/q\) on \(\Omega\), we obtain
\[
\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
\le C R_1I
  + C\int_\Omega\frac{E_y^2}{S_1}\,dy
  + C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\int_\Omega\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
\]
and
\[
 \int_\Omega E_y^2\,dy
 \le \frac{4I}{q}\int_\Omega E_y\,dy
 \le \frac{4I^2}{q}.
\]
This is precisely \eqref{sufficientOmega}.

\section*{Remaining open issue}

The first-alternative proof and the algebra reducing the second alternative to
\eqref{sufficientOmega} are complete.  The essential missing point is Lemma
\ref{plaquelemma}.  Standard cubical deformation does not by itself prove this
lemma: thickening the bad set destroys the exact plaque averaging
\eqref{badfubini}, while sharply cutting \(B_y\) by \({\mathcal B}_z\) creates
uncontrolled seam boundaries.  Moreover, a literal sweep of all of \(Z_y\) cannot
be bounded only by \(R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1\), because low-\(G\)-energy null sheets may
have large ordinary mass.  A proof must therefore be an algebraic family-level
tightening, not a pointwise source homotopy of each fiber.
