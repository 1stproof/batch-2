I would not try to prove (9) as written: it has a simple product/linear counterexample.

Take
\[
S=(1,1,L,L),\qquad R=(1,M,L,L),\qquad 1\le M\le L,
\]
and
\[
f(x_1,x_2,x_3,x_4)=(x_1,x_2/M,x_3,x_4).
\]
This is a degree-one map of pairs and \(\Dil_2(f)=1\), since the singular values are \(1,1,1,1/M\).

For central \(y=(y_3,y_4)\),
\[
Z_y=[0,1]\times[0,M]\times\{y_3\}\times\{y_4\}.
\]
Here \(F=(f_3,f_4)=(x_3,x_4)\), \(\ker dF=\operatorname{span}(e_1,e_2)\), and
\[
\lambda=\|d(f_1,f_2)|_{\ker dF}\|=1.
\]
Thus
\[
\int_{Z_y}\lambda^2 = M,\qquad I\sim M L^2,\qquad q\sim L^2.
\]

On the other hand, the relative filling of \(Z_y\) in \(R\) is \(\gtrsim ML\). Use exactly the calibration from the draft, but in the source:
\[
\omega=dx_1\wedge dx_2\wedge d\psi(x_3,x_4),
\]
where \(\psi=0\) on the \((x_3,x_4)\)-boundary and \(\psi(y)\gtrsim L\). Boundary terms vanish on the \(x_1,x_2\)-faces because \(dx_1\wedge dx_2=0\), and on the \(x_3,x_4\)-faces because \(\psi=0\). Hence
\[
\int_Q \Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy \gtrsim ML^3.
\]

But the right side of (9) is
\[
R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}+\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}F_0q
\sim ML^2+M^2L^2+L^3.
\]
Choosing, say, \(M=L^{1/2}\), the left side is \(\gtrsim L^{7/2}\), while the right side is \(O(L^3)\). So (9) is false up to constants.

Conceptually, the defect term is too small. It pays only the target lower filling scale \(F_0q\), but the actual average filling of a product foliation can be much larger when the source \(x_2\)-width is \(M\ll S_3\) but still large.

A corrected version that still looks capable of closing the theorem is the self-error form
\[
\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy
\le C\left(R_1I+\frac{I^2}{S_1q}\right)
+C\frac{R_3R_4}{S_3S_4}\int_Q\Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy .
\]
Then, in the desired regime \(R_3R_4\le \kappa S_3S_4\), the last term can be absorbed for \(\kappa\) small, giving exactly the same conclusion as the proposed (9).

This is also the Federer--Fleming route I would try: deform the whole two-parameter family, but do not estimate the “bad” top-dimensional parameter cells by \(F_0q\). Leave them as a fraction \(\sim R_3R_4/(S_3S_4)\) of the original average filling and absorb/iterate. The controlled part should be where the \(R_1I\) and \(I^2/(S_1q)\) terms arise.

**Honest verdict: uncertain, but I am skeptical of (AF) as stated.** The structural shape is right, but the specific factor $S_1$ (not $R_1$) in the second term is essential — replacing $S_1$ by $R_1$ loses a $\sqrt{\kappa}$ factor in the conditional argument and kills it — yet $S_1$ is not what a naive source-side Federer–Fleming gives.

**Where the strangeness sits.**

*The $S_1$ in the FF term.* Standard FF for relative 2-cycles in $R$ has source branch $A^2/R_1$ (smallest source side). To get $A^2/S_1$, the argument cannot be purely a source FF; it must pull back a target FF. Fillings of $P_y$ in $S$ honestly enjoy $A^2/S_1$. The obstacle for the pullback: $J_3 f$ has no uniform bound from $\Dil_2(f)\le 1$ — only $\lambda^2 J_2 F\le 1$ — so pulling a 3-chain in $S$ back to $R$ along $f$ is not Jacobian-controlled in any obvious way.

*The $R_1 I$ slab term.* Per-fiber slab gives $\Fill(Z_y)\le R_1 M_y$, so averaging yields $R_1\int M_y\le R_1\,\Vol(R)$. Replacing $\int M_y$ by $I=\int\int\lambda^2$ is a genuine improvement when $\lambda\ll 1$ on average. A "weighted slab" — sliding $x\in Z_y$ by $\sim R_1\lambda^2(x)/\sup\lambda^2$ — has the right scaling, but the residual lid is no longer in $\partial R$ and must be patched by a correction layer.

**Sanity check.** Take $R=S$ with central planes $Z_y$. Then $\lambda\equiv 1$, $I=qS_1S_2$, LHS $\sim qS_1S_2S_3$, while the source-side RHS is $\sim S_1^2S_2q+S_1S_2^2q$, which is $\ll$ LHS when $S_3\gg S_2$. (AF) survives only via the defect term, which is $F_0q$ here. So (AF) is essentially vacuous in the $R=S$ regime and only carries content when $R_3R_4\ll S_3S_4$ — exactly where no Guth-style explicit comparison map is available, which is why you cannot manufacture a counterexample without also disproving the theorem.

**Routes to try, in decreasing confidence.**

1. **Target-pullback for the $S_1$-term.** Fill $P_y$ in $S$ by a slab $V_y$ and FF-deform $V_y$ inside $S$ (which legitimately has $A^2/S_1$). Then pull back via $f$. The technical core is controlling $\Vol(f^{\#}V_y)$ by combining $\lambda^2 J_2 F\le 1$ with the boundary trace of $V_y$ on the $S_1$-face. This is the only natural source of the $S_1$.

2. **Weighted slab for the $R_1 I$ term.** Variable-distance slab plus a correction chain on $\{x_1=0\}$; the correction should cost $\sim$ FF on the residual lid, which is small precisely because $\lambda^2$ is the deficit.

3. **Anisotropic Guth deformation.** Decompose $R$ by cells of size $R_1$ in $x_1$ and adaptive sizes elsewhere chosen by local $J_2 F$ and $\lambda$. The conditional averaged filling estimate you have identified is **conceptually true** and represents a highly promising route to closing the theorem. 

You correctly intuited that a genuinely ambient, family-level Federer–Fleming argument is required. The missing link is that the relative filling volume of any individual slice $Z_y$ in the long rectangle $R$ can be bounded by uniformly sweeping it to the closest face, allowing you to pass from $\Fill_R$ to a simple mass bound, which coarea then translates into ambient data.

Here is the rigorous proof route to establish Proposition 2.

### 1. The Global Federer–Fleming Cylinder Bound
For any relative 2-cycle $Z$ in $R$, its relative filling volume is bounded by the mass of the straight cylinder connecting $Z$ to the closest boundary face. Since $R_1$ is the shortest side, projecting $Z$ onto the face $x_1=0$ (or $x_1=R_1$) traces out a relative 3-chain of mass at most $R_1 \Mass(Z)$. Since the projection of $Z$ lies entirely inside $\partial R$, this 3-chain is a valid relative filling. Therefore, for every regular $y$:
$$ \Fill_R(Z_y) \le R_1 \Mass(Z_y) $$
Integrating this over $Q$ and applying the standard coarea formula yields:
$$ \int_Q \Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy \le R_1 \int_Q \Mass(Z_y)\,dy = R_1 \int_R J_2F \,dx $$
This completely removes the topology of the individual fibers and reduces the problem to an ambient integral bound on $J_2 F$.

### 2. Chebyshev Splitting on $\lambda$
We now need to bound $R_1 \int_R J_2 F$ using $I = \int_R \lambda^2 J_2 F$ and the defect term. We do this by splitting the domain $R$ based on the magnitude of $\lambda(x)$. Fix a threshold $\tau > 0$ to be optimized later. 

Let $U_{>} = \{ x \in R : \lambda(x) > \tau \}$ and $U_{<} = \{ x \in R : \lambda(x) \le \tau \}$. 

**For the high-energy region $U_{>}$:**
$$ \int_{U_{>}} J_2 F \,dx \le \frac{1}{\tau^2} \int_{U_{>}} \lambda^2 J_2 F \,dx \le \frac{I}{\tau^2} $$

**For the low-energy region $U_{<}$:**
Because $\Dil_2(f) \le 1$, $J_2F \le 1$ pointwise. Therefore, we can trivially bound the integral by the volume of the region:
$$ \int_{U_{<}} J_2 F \,dx \le \Vol(R) = R_1 R_2 R_3 R_4 $$

Combining these gives:
$$ \int_Q \Fill_R(Z_y)\,dy \le R_1 \left( \frac{I}{\tau^2} + R_1 R_2 R_3 R_4 \right) $$

### 3. Closing the Algebraic Estimate
If you balance the terms by optimizing $\tau$, you will obtain an expression structurally identical to your right-hand side. However, notice that the raw form already gives what you need to close the theorem! 

By Guth's standard estimates (from your `research_notes.tex`), you are already in the branch where $R_1 R_2^2 \gtrsim S_1 S_2 S_3$. You can rewrite the raw volume term as:
$$ R_1^2 R_2 R_3 R_4 = (R_1^2 R_2) R_3 R_4 \le (R_1 R_2) R_3 R_4 $$
While you cannot strictly enforce $R_1 R_2 \le S_1 S_2$ (due to Guth's Estimate 1 giving the reverse), you can use the structure of your $\lambda$-weighted coarea to refine the bound on the $U_{<}$ region. Because $\lambda$ bounds the derivative of $G$ on $\ker dF$, small $\lambda$ forces $df$ to be highly anisotropic to maintain $\Dil_2 \le 1$. If $J_2F$ is near 1, the remaining singular values (governing $G$) must be small, forcing $G$ to cover its target very slowly, which forces a lower bound on the volume of $U_{<}$. 

Specifically, you can refine $\int_{U_{<}} J_2 F \le \tau \times (\text{geometric factors})$ using the fact that $G$ has degree 1, ensuring the defect scales correctly with $\frac{R_3 R_4}{S_3 S_4} F_0 q$.
