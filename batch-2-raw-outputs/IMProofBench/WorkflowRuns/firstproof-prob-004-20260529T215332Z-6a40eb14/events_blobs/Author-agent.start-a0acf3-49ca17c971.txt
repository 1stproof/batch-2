### Council member: gpt-55-pro (models/openai/gpt-55-pro) ###
My opinion: a **pure target-pullback Green-operator proof is not viable with the residual measured by the unsaturated exact-plaque seminorm \(\beta_z\)**. The obstruction is structural, not just technical.

The key conflict is this. A quotient cochain is a functional depending only on \(f_\# C\). A target Green operator produces residuals of the form “pull back a target cochain/current singular at \(z\),” so its pairing with \(C\) also depends only on \(f_\# C\). But
\[
\beta_z([C])=\inf_{N\in\ker f_\#}\Mass((C+N)\llcorner {\mathcal B}_z)
\]
is deliberately **not** a target-side norm; it allows changing the source representative to avoid the unsaturated bad set. In the two-lift model \(A,A'\) with \(f_\#A=f_\#A'=P\), if \({\mathcal B}_z\) contains \(A\) but not \(A'\), then \(\beta_z([A])=0\), while any target-pullback residual detecting \(P\) pairs equally with \(A\) and \(A'\). Hence an estimate
\[
|\langle f^*\theta_z,C\rangle|\lesssim \beta_z([C])
\]
cannot hold unless that residual vanishes on the whole target class. Replacing \(\beta_z\) by a saturated \(f^{-1}f({\mathcal B}_z)\)-seminorm would restore compatibility, but then the plaque Fubini absorption is likely destroyed.

There is also a geometric mismatch. A Green kernel on \(Q\setminus\{z\}\), e.g. \(d\eta_z=\delta_z-q^{-1}dy_3dy_4\), has nonlocal tails or singular level/ray sets. Pulling it back by \(F\) localizes to \(F^{-1}\) of those tails, not to the exact coordinate-plaque bad set. The exact plaque set is strong precisely because it charges only zero-thickness Manhattan tracks; any thickened or analytic near-miss set can be huge under rank-one comb behavior.

Finally, the good term
\[
R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1
\]
does not naturally come from an \(F\)-Green operator. It is a \(G=(f_1,f_2)\)-side planar/Manhattan cost: \(R_1E_y\) is the short-direction sweep weighted by \(G\)-energy, and \(E_y^2/S_1\) is the one-dimensional coarea/planar filling cost in the \(G\)-rectangle. An \(F\)-Green estimate would more naturally give kernel integrals like \(\int_{Z_y}E_y|\nabla\eta_z(F)|^2\) or logarithmic terms, not the stated scale.

A viable formulation, if it exists, should be **cellular/quotient-dual rather than smooth target-Green**. One would need: for every quotient \(2\)-cochain \(\phi\) with \(\|\delta\phi\|\le1\),
\[
\phi([Z_y])=K_y(\delta\phi)+\Psi_{z,\phi}([C_y^0]),
\]
with
\[
|K_y(\delta\phi)|\lesssim R_1E_y+E_y^2/S_1,\qquad
|\Psi_{z,\phi}([C])|\lesssim \beta_z([C]).
\]
Here \(K_y\) should be the dual of a \(G\)-Manhattan tightening, while \(\Psi_{z,\phi}\) must lie in the polar of the quotient bad seminorm. That residual cannot be an ordinary target-pullback cochain.

So I would not pursue a smooth Green-operator proof except perhaps as a heuristic for choosing the puncture. The actual proof of QEAT seems to require a quotient cellular acyclic-carrier construction whose residual is built directly in the \(\beta_z\)-dual norm.

### Council member: opus_47_max (models/anthropic/opus_47_max) ###
**Viability of the dual target-pullback / Green-operator approach to QEAT.**

The dual route has the right structural features — target pullbacks $f^*\omega$ vanish on $\ker f_\#$ (quotient-compatibility is free), and $F^*$-singularities at $z$ are naturally supported on $\mathcal B_z$. But a *pure* target-cochain proof seems infeasible to me, because $R_1 E_y + E_y^2/S_1$ mixes source ($R_1$) and target ($S_1$) lengths, forcing a hybrid. Here is the closest viable mechanism I see, plus a bottleneck I would call near-decisive.

**Hybrid three-piece chain homotopy.**

(i) *Single-sheet quotient representative.* On the $F$-regular set, choose a $z$-averaged measurable section $\sigma_z : P_y \to Z_y$ of $G|_{Z_y}$, with singularities only on $F^{-1}(z)$. This is where the Green 1-form $\eta_z$ on $Q\setminus\{z\}$ enters — as a $z$-averaged choice of which sheet of $f^{-1}$ to pick. Set $\tilde Z_y := \sigma_{z\#}P_y$. Then $f_\#\tilde Z_y = P_y$, so $[\tilde Z_y] = [Z_y]$ in $\mathcal Q_2$.

(ii) *Source $x_1$-sweep* of $\tilde Z_y$ to $\{x_1=0\}\subset\partial R$. The 3-chain $H^{(1)}_z(Z_y)$ has mass $\le R_1\mathrm{Mass}(\tilde Z_y)$ — the source contribution of $H_z$.

(iii) *Target $(s_1,s_2)$ Federer–Fleming.* Fill $\partial G_\#\tilde Z_y$ inside the 2-strip $[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]$ at $\{s_3,s_4\}=y$ via the 2D F–F profile $L^2/S_1$ ($L\le \mathrm{Mass}(G_\#\tilde Z_y)$), then lift back via $\sigma_z$. The lifted 3-chain $H^{(2)}_z(Z_y)$ is the target contribution.

Both pieces are quotient-compatible because target-pullback information is used throughout; the exact-plaque residual lives where $\sigma_z$ degenerates, i.e., on $F^{-1}(z)\subset\mathcal B_z$.

**Bottleneck.** The construction gives $R_1 E_y + E_y^2/S_1$ provided
$$\mathrm{Mass}(\tilde Z_y) = \int_{P_y}\frac{1}{J_2 G(\sigma_z)} \,d\mathrm{vol} \le C E_y. \tag{$\star$}$$
Both sides are $\ge S_1S_2$ (degree, and Cauchy–Schwarz via $E_y\ge 2\int J_2 G \ge 2S_1S_2$). The pointwise mixed bound $\lambda^2 J_2 F\le1$ relates $J_2 G$ and $\|dG\|^2$ on the *whole* of $Z_y$, but Cauchy–Schwarz goes the wrong way:
$$\mathrm{Mass}(Z_y)^2 \le \int_{Z_y}\tfrac{1}{J_2 G}\cdot\int_{Z_y}\|dG\|^2.$$
$(\star)$ asks for a *good* sheet — a measurable section picking $J_2 G$ at-or-above its average over $Z_y$. I am genuinely uncertain whether this holds in general. Note that pointwise-flat regions of $Z_y$ ($\|dG\|=0$) lie in $\ker f_\#$ (constant $G$ and constant $F$ make $f$ constant on that piece, so $f_\#$ vanishes there), so the flap obstruction from your Round 23 notes does *not* directly obstruct $(\star)$ — flaps are already invisible in the quotient.

**Recommended test before committing.** Verify or refute $(\star)$ in clean toys:
- $f=(g_1(x),g_2(x),x_3,x_4)$, a graph over $(x_3,x_4)$. Section is canonical.
- Degree-$k$ branched cover composed with a graph.

If $(\star)$ holds in these, the program likely closes. If not, the right replacement is something like $R_1\mathrm{Mass}(\tilde Z_y) + \mathrm{Mass}(\tilde Z_y)^2/S_1$ with $\tilde Z_y$ defined as the minimizer of $\int 1/J_2 G(\sigma)$ — and the absorption algebra has to be re-checked with the modified energy.

**Closest model in the literature.** §6 of Guth's thesis (the rectangular profile) is the analog to keep in mind: source fill = (source $R_1$-sweep) $+$ (target $A^2$-fill), giving $\min(R_1 A,A^2/R_1)$. Your desired swap $R_1\to S_1$ in the denominator is precisely the source-target trade enabled by lifting through $\sigma_z$; whether it survives is exactly $(\star)$. Guth's *Directional isoperimetric inequalities* (arXiv 0802.3549) also builds source fillings by pulling back target primitives — the right ambient setup, though not formulated in the quotient.

### Council member: gemini-31-pro (models/gemini/gemini-31-pro) ###
Here is a decisive obstruction to the dual target-pullback/quotient cochain strategy, followed by a proposed primal alternative that resolves the core issue without requiring the global quotient complex.

### The Decisive Obstruction
You cannot obtain the $R_1 E_y + E_y^2/S_1$ term from a target-pullback quotient cochain homotopy. The required geometric properties are mutually exclusive in this dual setup:

1. **Target-pullback vanishing:** A Green operator on the parameter space $Q$ produces forms generated by $df_3, df_4$. Because $Z_y$ is supported on an $F$-fiber, its tangent space is in $\ker dF$. Any pullback form from $Q$ restricts to zero on $Z_y$, providing no bounding information for the fiber itself.
2. **Metric blindness to the source:** If you instead pull back a Green operator from the full target $S$, the resulting metric bounds depend entirely on target dimensions ($S_1, S_2, S_3, S_4$) and the dilation bound $\Dil_k \le 1$. It is blind to the source dimensions and will never yield the anisotropic $R_1$ advantage.
3. **Quotient breaking via source translation:** To extract the $R_1$ factor, your homotopy *must* integrate along the short source $x_1$-direction. However, translating a chain along $x_1$ changes its $x_3$ and $x_4$ coordinates. Consequently, translating an $f$-null chain yields a chain that is no longer $f$-null. A source-translation operator does not preserve the annihilator of $\ker f_\#$, meaning the resulting cochain fails to be a quotient cochain.

### The Constructive Alternative: Hybrid Primal Cellular Tightening
You moved to the quotient complex to avoid the "two-cell seam" problem caused by sharply restricting an arbitrary filling $C_y^0$ to the exact-plaque bad set $\mathcal{B}_z$. 

You can bypass the quotient complex entirely by constructing a *canonical* primal filling whose geometry is explicitly built from coordinate plaques. If your filling is a sum of discrete cells, you can define the bad set by **cellular inclusion** rather than sharp restriction.

Here is the proposed mechanism:
1. **$G$-Pullback Grid:** Pull back a regular grid from the target $[0,S_1]\times[0,S_2]$ via $G = (f_1, f_2)$ to $Z_y$. This isolates the degree-carrying part of the slice and effectively collapses "flaps" (where $dG \approx 0$), making them algebraically invisible without needing the formal quotient.
2. **Capping and Pushing (The Manhattan Tracks):** Construct the filling $C_y$ by deforming $Z_y$ over this grid. 
   - Slice $Z_y$ by $f_1$-levels and cap the 1-cycles; the coarea cost of capping these levels inside the slice produces the $E_y^2/S_1$ term. 
   - Push the remaining degree-carrying 1-skeleton along the source $x_1$-direction to the boundary $x_1=0$. Because the track spans the short source direction, its cost is bounded by $R_1 E_y$. 
3. **Cellular Bad Set Sorting:** Your filling $C_y$ is now a discrete sum of structural cells (the $x_1$-tracks and $f_1$-caps). Instead of cutting via $C_y \llcorner \mathcal{B}_z$, define a projection $P_z(C_y)$ that dumps the *entire cell* into the bad term if its $F$-image contains the puncture $z$. 
4. **Seam Elimination:** Because you select whole valid cells, $P_z$ acts as a chain map on this specific complex, generating no uncontrolled boundary seams. Since the cells are generated by 1D source translations or target-level caps, their $F$-images are subsets of exact coordinate 2-plaques. Thus, the Fubini probability $\mathbb{P}(z \in F(\text{cell}))$ remains rigorously bounded by $\lesssim R_3R_4 / S_3S_4$.

By building the filling constructively from a hybrid grid, you guarantee that the boundary operator commutes with the bad-set allocation, achieving the EAT estimate without the algebraic contradictions of the quotient cohomology.
