My strongest reaction: I would *not* try to prove  
\[
\Fill(Z_y)\lesssim \Mass(Z_y)^2/S_1
\]
from only “degree one to \(P_y\)” plus \(|df_1\wedge df_2|\le 1\). That statement has a very concrete model counterexample, and it reproduces exactly the bad Guth-profile branch.

Let \(F_0=S_1S_2S_3\), fix a short physical width \(r\ll S_1\), and choose an area \(A\ge S_1S_2\) with \(A\ll (S_1F_0)^{1/2}\). Put
\[
L=A/r,\qquad h=F_0/A,
\]
and take an ambient rectangle containing
\[
Z=[0,r]\times[0,L]\times\{(h,h)\}
\]
centrally in the last two coordinates. Define
\[
g:Z\to [0,S_1]\times[0,S_2],\qquad
g(u,v)=\big(S_1u/r,\; S_2v/L\big).
\]
Then \(g\) has relative degree one and
\[
J_2g=\frac{S_1S_2}{rL}=\frac{S_1S_2}{A}\le 1,
\]
while \(\Mass Z=A\). But a calibration exactly like the one already used, with
\(\beta=\psi(x_3,x_4)\,dx_1\wedge dx_2\), \(\psi=0\) on the boundary and
\(\psi(h,h)\sim h\), gives
\[
\Fill(Z)\gtrsim hA=F_0.
\]
Thus \(F_0\) can be much larger than \(A^2/S_1\). Taking
\[
A=(rF_0)^{1/2}
\]
when \(A\ge S_1S_2\) gives precisely the offending profile term
\[
A=(R_1S_1S_2S_3)^{1/2},
\]
and also \(R_1R_2^2\sim F_0\). So the loss is not an artifact of applying the ordinary profile; it is compatible with the fiber-level degree/Jacobian data.

This also pinpoints why slicing by target \(x_1\) is dangerous: in the model \(|dg_1|=S_1/r\) is huge and \(|dg_2|\) is tiny. The two-Jacobian is controlled, but the one-dimensional coarea cost for \(g_1\)-slices is not.

So if the mixed estimate is true, the proof has to use the fact that the \(Z_y\)’s are fibers of one four-dimensional map, not just improved filling of each \(Z_y\). A plausible route is a balanced/strip-like dichotomy: balanced fibers might admit a target-width filling argument; strip-like fibers force huge \(f_1\)-stretch, and then the singular-value condition suppresses normal \(f_3,f_4\) derivatives. That suppression would have to be converted, via coarea/degree over the whole family, into the missing volume.

On the counterexample side: yes, the Guth expanding-embedding/inverse-extension mechanism is serious, but the extension/retraction is exactly the hard point. The inverse on a snaked embedded image need not extend with bounded \(2\)-dilation or the correct boundary degree. I would use published Estimate 1 and the boundary/rational-homotopy inequalities as sanity checks; the “middle-side snake” remains the decisive test case.
