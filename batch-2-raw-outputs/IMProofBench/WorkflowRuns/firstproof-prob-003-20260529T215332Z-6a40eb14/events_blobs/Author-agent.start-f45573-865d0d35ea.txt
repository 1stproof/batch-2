# Round 7 compute report: unit-gap small-\(p\) lemma

I read the current `answer.tex` and `research_notes.tex`, focusing on the new
unit-gap formulation
\[
 \Pr[S\ge p(B+1)]\ge {p\over q}\Pr[S<p(B+1)-1],
 \quad S=\sum_i b_i\zeta_i,\quad 0\le b_i\le1,\quad q=1-p.
\]
I did not find a theorem that directly implies this statement with the strict
lower threshold, coefficient cap \(b_i\le1\), and sharp constant \(p/q\).

## Bellman/martingale attempt

Write
\[
 R=p+\sum_i b_i(p-\zeta_i).
\]
Then \(S\ge p(B+1)\) is \(R\le0\), and
\[
 S<p(B+1)-1 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad R>1.
\]
Thus (UG) is
\[
 \Pr(R\le0)\ge {p\over q}\Pr(R>1). \tag{B}
\]

There is a rigorous obstruction to the most natural one-function Bellman
certificate.  Put
\[
 g(r)=\mathbf 1_{\{r\le0\}}-\lambda\mathbf 1_{\{r>1\}},
 \qquad \lambda={p\over q}.
\]
Suppose one tries to prove (B) by finding a locally bounded function
\(\phi\le g\), \(\phi(p)\ge0\), satisfying the subharmonic condition
\[
 \phi(r)\le q\,\phi(r+pb)+p\,\phi(r-qb)
 \qquad(0\le b\le1). \tag{*}
\]
For \(x<y\) with \(y-x\le1\), take \(b=y-x\) and
\(r=px+qy\).  Then (*) becomes the fixed-weight Jensen inequality
\[
 \phi(px+qy)\le p\phi(x)+q\phi(y).
\]
Since \(\phi\le g\) is locally bounded above, the usual fixed-weight
Bernstein--Doetsch/Jensen-convexity argument forces \(\phi\) to be convex
locally, hence convex.  But \(\phi\le g\) is bounded above on both tails
(\(\phi\le1\) on the left and \(\phi\le-\lambda\) on the right), so a convex
minorant on all of \(\mathbb R\) is constant and in fact \(\phi\le-\lambda\).
Therefore \(\phi(p)\ge0\) is impossible.

I also ran a finite-grid LP for this Bellman minorant; it agrees with the
obstruction.  With grid spacing \(0.025\), the maximum possible \(\phi(p)\)
was:

| \(p\) | grid max \(\phi(p)\) |
|---:|---:|
| 0.20 | -0.050000 |
| 0.25 | -0.083333 |
| 0.30 | -0.128571 |
| 1/3 | -0.162500 |

Conclusion: a proof cannot be a scalar subharmonic minorant of
\(\mathbf1_{(-\infty,0]}-\lambda\mathbf1_{(1,\infty)}\).  It must use a
distributional/two-function invariant or exploit the nonadaptive fixed
coefficient sequence.

## Induction attempt

Exposing a coefficient \(x\) gives the exact four-tail identity.  Let
\(T=\sum_{i<n} b_i\zeta_i\), \(D=\sum_{i<n}b_i\), and
\(\tau=p(D+x+1)\).  Then
\[
\Psi(D,x)=q\Pr[T\ge\tau]+p\Pr[T\ge\tau-x]
-\lambda q\Pr[T<\tau-1]-\lambda p\Pr[T<\tau-1-x].
\]
The residual unit-gap statement for \(T\) only controls the pair of thresholds
\(p(D+1)\) and \(p(D+1)-1\).  The exposed identity needs simultaneous control
at shifted thresholds
\[
p(D+1)+px,\quad p(D+1)-qx,\quad p(D+1)-1+px,\quad p(D+1)-1-qx.
\]
So the scalar induction still does not close.  A plausible strengthened state
would be a two-parameter comparison of these shifted upper/lower tails, but I
did not find a closed invariant.

## Extremal/chamber computations

New files:

- `code/round7_unitgap.py`
- `data/round7_unitgap_output.txt`

The script computes
\[
V(b)={p\over q}\Pr[S<p(B+1)-1]-\Pr[S\ge p(B+1)].
\]
A positive \(V\) is a counterexample.  I forced the nontrivial region
\(B>q/p\), since otherwise the lower tail is empty.  Direct differential
evolution and random searches up to \(n=12\) found no positive \(V\).

I also encoded exact strict threshold chambers as MILPs for rational \(p\) and
small \(n\).  The strict chamber MILP uses an \(\varepsilon=10^{-5}\) separation
from both thresholds, so it avoids the false boundary ambiguity caused by the
strict lower tail and non-strict upper tail.  No strict chamber with positive
violation was found for the grid below, \(n\le8\).

Best (largest) strict violations found:

| \(p\) | \(n\) tested | best \(V\) | typical extremal chamber |
|---:|---:|---:|---|
| \(1/6\) | 6--8 | \(-0.196244856\) | \(B=5+\varepsilon\), nearly equal/split cap |
| \(1/5\) | 5--8 | \(-0.180800000\) | \(B=4+\varepsilon\) |
| \(1/4\) | 4--8 | \(-0.156250000\) | \(B=3+\varepsilon\) |
| \(2/7\) | 3--8 | \(-0.052478134\) | \(B=2.5+\varepsilon\), e.g. \(1,1,1/2+\varepsilon\) or equal split |
| \(3/10\) | 3--8 | \(-0.069000000\) | \(B=7/3+\varepsilon\), e.g. \(1,1,1/3+\varepsilon\) |
| \(1/3\) | 3--8 | \(-0.111111111\) | \(B=2+\varepsilon\), e.g. \(1,1,\varepsilon\) |

The non-strict outer MILP is too loose on boundary chambers: at \(p=1/3\),
\(B=2\) it can label equality adversarially and reports a fake positive outer
violation, while direct evaluation and the strict perturbation both give
margin \(1/9\).  Thus strict/non-strict handling is essential.

## Current status

No proof or counterexample was found.  The clearest new mathematical fact is
the scalar Bellman obstruction above.  The computations support the conjecture
that extremal strict chambers occur at the lower-tail activation boundary
\(B=q/p+\varepsilon\), with coefficients collapsing to \(\{0,1\}\) plus one
fractional/equal-split block, but I do not have a theorem reducing to those
patterns.

Remaining obstacles:

1. Need a genuine two-threshold invariant for the four shifted residual tails.
2. Need an extremal theorem justifying reduction to boundary chambers
   \(B=q/p+\varepsilon\) and few fractional coefficients.
3. FMP-style cyclic averaging still looks relevant, but published theorems do
   not fill the strict hypotenuse strip needed here.
