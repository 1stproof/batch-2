Here is a concrete, non-circular route that builds directly on your "weighted random quantile" intuition and elegantly bypasses the separating-hyperplane/convex-hull dualities. 

You can rigorously decouple the Bernoulli randomness from the weight distribution by mapping the problem to a uniform random permutation. 

### The Permutation-Decoupling Identity
Generate your variables $v_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ by drawing i.i.d. uniforms $U_i \sim U[0,1]$ and setting $v_i = \mathbf{1}_{U_i \le p}$. 

Let $B = \sum_{i=1}^m v_i$ be the total number of successes. Clearly, $B \sim \text{Bin}(m, p)$. 
Conditioned on the event $B = b$, the set of successful indices $S = \{i : v_i = 1\}$ is exactly a uniformly random subset of $\{1, \dots, m\}$ of size $b$. 

Now, a uniformly random subset of size $b$ has the exact same distribution as the first $b$ elements of a uniformly random permutation of $\{1, \dots, m\}$. 
Let $W_1, W_2, \dots, W_m$ be the original weights $w_i$ reordered by a uniformly random permutation. Define the random hitting index $K$ as the minimum number of items needed to reach the threshold $p$:
\[
    K = \min \left\{ k \in \{1, \dots, m\} : \sum_{j=1}^k W_j \ge p \right\}.
\]
Because $S$ acts like the first $b$ elements of this permutation, the subset sum $w(S)$ reaches $p$ if and only if $K \le b$. Therefore, we have the exact identity:
\[
    \Pr\left[\sum w_i v_i \ge p\right] = \sum_{b=0}^m \Pr[B = b] \Pr[K \le b] = \mathbb{E}_K \big[ \Pr(B \ge K \mid K) \big].
\]

### The Target Inequality
Let $G(k) = \Pr(\text{Bin}(m, p) \ge k)$. The survival function $G(k)$ is strictly decreasing. 
Notice a beautiful property of $G(k)$: its sum is the expectation of the Binomial!
\[
    \sum_{k=1}^m G(k) = \mathbb{E}[B] = m p.
\]
If $U$ is a uniformly distributed random variable on $\{1, 2, \dots, m\}$, then:
\[
    \mathbb{E}[G(U)] = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{k=1}^m G(k) = p.
\]
Thus, your entire missing small-$p$ lemma is strictly equivalent to proving:
\[
    \mathbb{E}[G(K)] \ge \mathbb{E}[G(U)].
\]

### Why this structure is so powerful
1. **It avoids circularity:** You have completely eliminated the down-set/LP duality. The problem is now a pure study of $K$ (the hitting time in a sampling-without-replacement process) against a fixed, convex-like decreasing sequence $G$.
2. **It perfectly explains your numerical infima:** You observed that the infimum is approached by one large weight $\approx p$ and a dust of finely split residuals. Under this decoupling, if $w = (p, \epsilon, \dots, \epsilon)$, the hitting time $K = 1$ if the large weight is chosen first (probability $p$), and $K \approx pm$ otherwise (probability $1-p$). 
Then $\mathbb{E}[G(K)] \approx p \cdot G(1) + (1-p) \cdot G(pm)$. Since $G(1) \approx 1$ and the median $G(pm) \approx 1/2$, the expectation evaluates to $p(1) + (1-p)(1/2)$, which bounds the probability strictly away from crashing below $p$. 

### Suggested Next Steps
To finish the proof from here, you no longer need to worry about arbitrary Boolean up-sets. You just need to show that $K$ is stochastically dominated by (or convexly ordered relative to) the uniform distribution $U$ under the specific weight $G$. Analyze the sequence $\Pr(K \le k)$ — which is simply the probability that a random sample of $k$ weights without replacement has sum $\ge p$. Since the sample mean is exactly $k/m$, you can attack this with standard concentration/Markov bounds for sampling without replacement.
