This is a fascinating reduction. The coalescence strategy is mathematically extremely natural and strikes at the heart of the problem. Here is how I would approach the proof, combining your coalescence formulation with an averaging strategy inspired by Boolean Fourier analysis and continuous density heuristics.

### 1. The Supermodularity / Copula Angle
Your coalescence gain $\Delta_{ij}$ exactly measures whether the threshold function $H(\eta) = \mathbf{1}_{\sum w_k \eta_k > q}$ is "supermodular on average" under the $q$-biased measure. 

Distributionally, merging weights $w_i$ and $w_j$ is identical to taking independent $\eta_i, \eta_j \sim \mathrm{Bern}(q)$ and forcing them to be completely positively correlated (i.e., replacing both with a single $\eta_{\mathrm{new}} \sim \mathrm{Bern}(q)$) while preserving their marginals. This operation increases the expectation of any function that has positive second-order interactive differences. Specifically, your gain identity can be written exactly as:
\[
\Delta_{ij} = pq \cdot \mathbb{E}[D_i D_j H(\eta)],
\]
where $D_i f(\eta) = f(\eta \mid \eta_i=1) - f(\eta \mid \eta_i=0)$.

### 2. Continuous Intuition: Why $\Delta_{ij} \ge 0$
To see why this gain should be robustly positive, consider a continuous approximation. Let $Z_{ij}$ be the contribution of the remaining weights, and assume it has a smooth density $f_Z$. The difference operator $D_i D_j H$ acts on the step function $H$, so its expectation approximates the negative second derivative of the CDF of $Z_{ij}$. 

By Taylor expansion, $\Delta_{ij} \approx -pq \cdot w_i w_j \cdot f_Z'(q)$. 
Where does $q$ sit relative to the distribution of $Z_{ij}$? 
The mean of $Z_{ij}$ is $\mu = q(1 - w_i - w_j)$. Since $q \ge 2/3$, $\mu \ll q$, meaning $q$ is situated firmly in the **right tail** of $Z_{ij}$. Because Poisson-binomial distributions (and their continuous limits) are strongly log-concave, their density is strictly decreasing in the right tail. Therefore, $f_Z'(q) < 0$, which implies $\Delta_{ij} > 0$. 

### 3. Defeating Lattice Effects via Averaging
You noted that arbitrary or prescribed pairs (e.g., the two smallest) can fail and decrease $\Phi_q$. In light of the continuous heuristic, these failures are purely discrete **lattice effects**. Because $Z_{ij}$ is discrete, the positive interval $(q-w_i-w_j, q-\max(w_i, w_j)]$ might happen to fall between the atoms of $Z_{ij}$, while the negative interval catches a large atom.

To rigorously bypass these local lattice traps, I strongly suggest abandoning a deterministic pair-selection rule. Instead, prove that a specific **weighted average** of the coalescence gains is non-negative:
\[
\Sigma = \sum_{i < j} w_i w_j \Delta_{ij} \ge 0.
\]
If $\Sigma \ge 0$, by the pigeonhole principle, there *must* exist some pair with $\Delta_{ij} \ge 0$.

### 4. Connection to Second-Order Russo-Margulis
Why this specific sum? In the Boolean Fourier / Hoeffding decomposition of $H$, $\Sigma$ extracts exactly the total weight of the degree-2 (and higher) interactive coefficients evaluated in the direction of the weight vector $w$. 

By a second-order analogue of the Russo-Margulis lemma, this sum of mixed directional differences governs the global convexity of the tail probability. Specifically, $\Sigma$ acts as a discrete approximation to:
\[
- \frac{d^2}{dt^2} \Pr\left(\sum w_k \eta_k > t\right) \Bigg|_{t=q}
\]
Because $q$ is to the right of the mean $E[S] = q$, the survival function $\Pr(S > t)$ is locally concave at $t=q$ (since the density is decreasing). This global concavity guarantees that the second derivative is negative, forcing $\Sigma \ge 0$. 

**Proof Strategy:** Express $\Sigma = \mathbb{E}\big[ \sum w_i w_j D_i D_j H \big]$ globally in terms of the total sum $S$. By smoothing $H$ slightly or applying a discrete integration-by-parts (like the Stein-Chen method for Poisson-binomials), you can show that $\Sigma$ is strictly governed by the downward slope of the discrete density of $S$ at $q$, averaging out the microscopic lattice violations.
