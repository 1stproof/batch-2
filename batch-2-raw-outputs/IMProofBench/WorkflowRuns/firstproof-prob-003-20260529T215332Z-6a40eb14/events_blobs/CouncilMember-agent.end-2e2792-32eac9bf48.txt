I would not try to rescue the one-parameter shifted induction; the failure you found looks structural. The cleanest “short LaTeX” route, if available, is probably through the convex-hull/partition-function formulation rather than martingales.

The precise black box I would look for/cite is:

\[
\tag{K}
0<\lambda\le \tfrac12,\quad p={\lambda\over1+\lambda},\quad
\mathcal D\subseteq 2^{[n]}\text{ a down-set},\quad
Z_\lambda(\mathcal D)>(1+\lambda)^{n-1}
\Longrightarrow
p{\bf1}\in \operatorname{conv}\{1_A:A\in\mathcal D\}.
\]

Equivalently, if \(p{\bf1}\notin P(\mathcal D)\), then
\[
Z_\lambda(\mathcal D)\le (1+\lambda)^{n-1}.
\]
This is exactly your partition-function target and would imply the unit-gap inequality in a few lines. I would search for this under “fractional matching number of a simplicial complex”, “hereditary families hard-core partition function”, or “weakly \(p\)-small families”; it feels closer to Frankl–Füredi/Kellerer-type fractional matching than to Kanter concentration. I am uncertain whether it is explicitly in the literature, but this is the exact theorem I would ask experts about.

If proving it, I’d try induction on sections. Write
\[
\mathcal D=\mathcal E\cup\{A\cup\{n\}:A\in\mathcal F\},\qquad
\mathcal F\subseteq \mathcal E.
\]
Then
\[
Z(\mathcal D)=Z(\mathcal E)+\lambda Z(\mathcal F),
\]
and
\[
p{\bf1}_n\in P(\mathcal D)
\iff
p{\bf1}_{n-1}\in qP(\mathcal E)+pP(\mathcal F).
\]
So the induction step should be formulated as a two-section convex-geometric lemma: if the above Minkowski combination misses \(p{\bf1}\), then
\[
Z(\mathcal E)+\lambda Z(\mathcal F)\le (1+\lambda)^{n-1}.
\]
The extremal case is \(\mathcal F=\varnothing,\ \mathcal E=2^{[n-1]}\), i.e. dictatorship, giving equality. This framing avoids the bad shifted base cases entirely.

I would be cautious about Kanter/Pinelis: the standard peakedness or left-tail domination statements seem to control either interval mass or one tail, not the sharp asymmetric ratio
\[
q\,\Pr[S\ge p]\ge p\,\Pr[S<-q].
\]
Likewise Fokkink–Meester–Pelekis appears to identify exactly this hypotenuse strip as missing, so I would not cite it unless you can extract a new strict-threshold corollary.

A secondary route worth testing is a stationary Glauber-chain level-crossing proof. Let the cube chain have stationary \(\mu_p\), and take the ramp
\[
f(s)=
\begin{cases}
s+q,&s<-q,\\
0,&-q\le s<p,\\
s-p,&s\ge p.
\end{cases}
\]
Stationarity gives \(\mathbb E Lf(S)=0\). Expanding \(Lf\), jumps cannot go directly from the lower tail to the upper tail because all \(b_i\le1\). If the middle contribution has the right sign for \(p\le1/3\), this identity should collapse exactly to the desired two-tail inequality. This is the martingale/ballot version I would try before another shifted induction.
