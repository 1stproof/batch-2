I would first modify the coalescence plan: the pairwise assertion (C) is false as stated.

Take
\[
q=\frac{7}{10},\qquad p=\frac{3}{10},\qquad m=14,\qquad w_1=\cdots=w_{14}=\frac1{14}.
\]
By symmetry every pair is the same. For a chosen pair \(x=y=1/14\), the remaining contribution is
\[
Z=\frac1{14}\operatorname{Bin}(12,7/10).
\]
In the coalescence formula,
\[
(q-x-y,q-y]=(0.5571\ldots,0.6285\ldots]
\]
contains exactly \(Z=8/14\), while
\[
(q-x,q]=(0.6285\ldots,0.7]
\]
contains exactly \(Z=9/14\). Hence
\[
\Delta=pq\left(\Pr[\operatorname{Bin}(12,.7)=8]
-\Pr[\operatorname{Bin}(12,.7)=9]\right).
\]
But
\[
\frac{\Pr[\operatorname{Bin}(12,.7)=8]}
{\Pr[\operatorname{Bin}(12,.7)=9]}
=
\frac{\binom{12}{8}}{\binom{12}{9}}\frac{.3}{.7}
=
\frac{297}{308}<1,
\]
so \(\Delta<0\). There is no endpoint ambiguity here. Thus every possible pair-coalescence decreases \(\Phi_q\). The lemma itself is still fine in this example:
\[
\Phi_q=P[\operatorname{Bin}(14,.7)\ge10]\approx0.584<0.7.
\]

So I would not try to prove (C). A plausible salvage is **block coalescence** rather than pair coalescence. If a block \(B\) of total mass \(c\) is replaced by one coordinate of weight \(c\), and \(T_B=\sum_{i\in B}w_i\eta_i\), then the exact gain is
\[
\Delta_B
=
\mathbb E_Z\Big[
q\,\mathbf 1_{\{Z+c>q\}}
-\Pr(T_B>q-Z)
\Big].
\]
In the equal \(14\)-weight example, merging two loses, but merging a block of four already goes in the correct direction. This suggests choosing \(B\) by a random-permutation “first block whose mass is near \(p\)” rule and proving the average block gain is nonnegative. That would still give an induction-to-one-coordinate proof, but with adaptive block sizes.

A useful diagnostic identity for pairs is also worth recording. Fix a coordinate \(i\) of weight \(x\), let \(W_i=\sum_{k\ne i}w_k\eta_k\), and set
\[
S_i=\{q-x<W_i\le q\}.
\]
For \(j\ne i\) with \(w_j\ge x\),
\[
\Delta_{ij}
=
\Pr(S_i)\big(\Pr(\eta_j=1\mid S_i)-q\big).
\]
So pair coalescence asks for a pivotal strip in which some heavier coordinate is overrepresented. In the equal counterexample, \(S_i\) is the layer “9 successes among 13”, whose conditional marginal is \(9/13<.7\), explaining the failure. A block version should ask for an overrepresented block rather than an overrepresented coordinate.

For a non-coalescence route, I would focus on the partition-function formulation (4). It looks close to a fractional matching / weighted Erdős matching statement for down-sets, with dictatorship extremal and \(\lambda\le1/2\). I am uncertain about a black-box theorem, but Frankl–Füredi-style fractional matching results seem more aligned with (4) than biased halfspace or \(k\)-wise-intersection theorems.

Three angles, in order of perceived promise:

**(1) Coalescence gain as a second discrete difference of the survival function.** Rewrite
$$\Delta_{ij}/(pq) \;=\; F_Z(q-x-y) + F_Z(q) - F_Z(q-x) - F_Z(q-y),\qquad F_Z(t) := \Pr[Z_{ij} > t].$$
This is $\ge 0$ iff $\int_0^x [F'_Z(u+y+s) - F'_Z(u+s)]\,ds \ge 0$ with $u = q-x-y$, i.e. supermodularity of $F_Z$ at those four corners — equivalently, $F_Z$ "convex on average" over $[u, q]$. Pointwise convexity fails because $\mathbb E[Z_{ij}] = q(1{-}x{-}y) > u$, so the lower endpoint is below the mode and $F_Z$ is concave at $u$. But the integral can still be $\ge 0$.

**Concrete conjecture to test computationally:** the **most-balanced pair** (minimising $|\log(w_i/w_j)|$, breaking ties by smaller sum) always gives $\Delta \ge 0$. Heuristically the four corners then straddle the mode of $Z_{ij}$ roughly symmetrically, and one can hope to extract supermodularity from log-concavity of Bernoulli convolutions (Bender 1973; Pitman 1997). The pivot+dust extremal gives $\Delta\to 0$ for the dust–dust pair, which is consistent.

**(2) Two-parameter Bellman invariant.** Your (Piv($a$)) is a single inequality with $g = 1{-}W$. Try the augmented family
$$\Pr[Y\ge T] - \lambda\Pr[Y < T-g] \;\ge\; c\,\Pr[T-g \le Y < T],$$
for weights summing to $W$. Conditioning on a new weight $u_1$ gives the *exact* decomposition $J(Y) = q\,J(Y'; T, g) + p\,J(Y'; T-u_1, g)$, so the family is closed under conditioning *as long as Piv holds for both* $(W{-}u_1, T, g)$ *and* $(W{-}u_1, T{-}u_1, g)$. The threshold floats but $g$ doesn't. The base case (singleton $Y = v_1\cdot u_1$) pins down the largest $c$ that survives; check whether $c = \lambda$ or $c = 1$ closes the recursion on the full region $\{(W,T,g): 0 \le T\le W,\, 0\le g\le T\}$ for $p\le 1/3$. The arithmetic isn't long; the question is whether the resulting $c$ is admissible at the dictator extremal.

**(3) Dubins–Savage / static "bold play."** Your conjecture is the static one-shot version of: in *sub*-fair gambling, bold play (a single all-in bet) uniquely minimizes the probability of reaching a fortune. The sequential version is DS Ch. 5; the static analogue is exactly the hypotenuse $t = q$ in FMP. Their proof on neighboring rectangles uses convex combinations of "double-or-quits" — those should extend to the boundary by a limit/perturbation argument. Pelekis has an arXiv manuscript (look for "weighted Bernoulli" + Pelekis) that addresses the triangular gaps; worth contacting him directly.

**(4) Literature worth checking before more work.**
- Pelekis & Ramon, "A lower bound on the probability that a binomial random variable is exceeding its mean," *Stat. Prob. Letters* **105** (2015), 124–130 — the exact discrete (uniform-weight) analogue. Their proof is short and may transfer.
- Greenberg & Mohri, "Tight lower bound on the probability of a binomial exceeding its expectation," *Stat. Prob. Letters* **86** (2014) — same regime.
- Léon–Perron 2003 (already cited): their Hoeffding-style "dispersing pair" technique is structurally close to your coalescence.

**My best bet:** start with (1)'s "most balanced pair" conjecture — five minutes of computer search will tell you if it survives. If yes, the proof reduces to a clean log-concavity claim about $F_{Z_{ij}}$. (2) is the cleanest formal induction if (1) doesn't crystallise. (3) is highest payoff via known literature.

This is a fascinating reduction. The coalescence strategy is mathematically extremely natural and strikes at the heart of the problem. Here is how I would approach the proof, combining your coalescence formulation with an averaging strategy inspired by Boolean Fourier analysis and continuous density heuristics.

### 1. The Supermodularity / Copula Angle
Your coalescence gain $\Delta_{ij}$ exactly measures whether the threshold function $H(\eta) = \mathbf{1}_{\sum w_k \eta_k > q}$ is "supermodular on average" under the $q$-biased measure. 

Distributionally, merging weights $w_i$ and $w_j$ is identical to taking independent $\eta_i, \eta_j \sim \mathrm{Bern}(q)$ and forcing them to be completely positively correlated (i.e., replacing both with a single $\eta_{\mathrm{new}} \sim \mathrm{Bern}(q)$) while preserving their marginals. This operation increases the expectation of any function that has positive second-order interactive differences. Specifically, your gain identity can be written exactly as:
\[
\Delta_{ij} = pq \cdot \mathbb{E}[D_i D_j H(\eta)],
\]
where $D_i f(\eta) = f(\eta \mid \eta_i=1) - f(\eta \mid \eta_i=0)$.

### 2. Continuous Intuition: Why $\Delta_{ij} \ge 0$
To see why this gain should be robustly positive, consider a continuous approximation. Let $Z_{ij}$ be the contribution of the remaining weights, and assume it has a smooth density $f_Z$. The difference operator $D_i D_j H$ acts on the step function $H$, so its expectation approximates the negative second derivative of the CDF of $Z_{ij}$. 

By Taylor expansion, $\Delta_{ij} \approx -pq \cdot w_i w_j \cdot f_Z'(q)$. 
Where does $q$ sit relative to the distribution of $Z_{ij}$? 
The mean of $Z_{ij}$ is $\mu = q(1 - w_i - w_j)$. Since $q \ge 2/3$, $\mu \ll q$, meaning $q$ is situated firmly in the **right tail** of $Z_{ij}$. Because Poisson-binomial distributions (and their continuous limits) are strongly log-concave, their density is strictly decreasing in the right tail. Therefore, $f_Z'(q) < 0$, which implies $\Delta_{ij} > 0$. 

### 3. Defeating Lattice Effects via Averaging
You noted that arbitrary or prescribed pairs (e.g., the two smallest) can fail and decrease $\Phi_q$. In light of the continuous heuristic, these failures are purely discrete **lattice effects**. Because $Z_{ij}$ is discrete, the positive interval $(q-w_i-w_j, q-\max(w_i, w_j)]$ might happen to fall between the atoms of $Z_{ij}$, while the negative interval catches a large atom.

To rigorously bypass these local lattice traps, I strongly suggest abandoning a deterministic pair-selection rule. Instead, prove that a specific **weighted average** of the coalescence gains is non-negative:
\[
\Sigma = \sum_{i < j} w_i w_j \Delta_{ij} \ge 0.
\]
If $\Sigma \ge 0$, by the pigeonhole principle, there *must* exist some pair with $\Delta_{ij} \ge 0$.

### 4. Connection to Second-Order Russo-Margulis
Why this specific sum? In the Boolean Fourier / Hoeffding decomposition of $H$, $\Sigma$ extracts exactly the total weight of the degree-2 (and higher) interactive coefficients evaluated in the direction of the weight vector $w$. 

By a second-order analogue of the Russo-Margulis lemma, this sum of mixed directional differences governs the global convexity of the tail probability. Specifically, $\Sigma$ acts as a discrete approximation to:
\[
- \frac{d^2}{dt^2} \Pr\left(\sum w_k \eta_k > t\right) \Bigg|_{t=q}
\]
Because $q$ is to the right of the mean $E[S] = q$, the survival function $\Pr(S > t)$ is locally concave at $t=q$ (since the density is decreasing). This global concavity guarantees that the second derivative is negative, forcing $\Sigma \ge 0$. 

**Proof Strategy:** Express $\Sigma = \mathbb{E}\big[ \sum w_i w_j D_i D_j H \big]$ globally in terms of the total sum $S$. By smoothing $H$ slightly or applying a discrete integration-by-parts (like the Stein-Chen method for Poisson-binomials), you can show that $\Sigma$ is strictly governed by the downward slope of the discrete density of $S$ at $q$, averaging out the microscopic lattice violations.
