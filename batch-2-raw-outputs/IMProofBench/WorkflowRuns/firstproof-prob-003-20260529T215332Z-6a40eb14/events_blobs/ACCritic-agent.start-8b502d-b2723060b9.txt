\title{A weighted Bernoulli mean-tail inequality}
\author{}

\maketitle

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I interpret the question in the universal sense: determine those \(p\in[0,1]\)
for which
\[
        \Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^m w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p                  \tag{P}
\]
holds for every \(m\ge1\) and every non-negative probability vector
\((w_1,\ldots,w_m)\), with \(v_i\) i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(p)\).

The counterexamples in Proposition~\ref{prop:negative} show that no value
outside
\[
        [0,1/3]\cup\{1/2,1\}
\]
can work.  The natural remaining assertion is the small-\(p\) lemma
(Lemma~\ref{lem:missing}) saying that all \(0\le p\le1/3\) work.  I have not
closed that lemma; this note records the complete negative direction, several
positive families, and exact reformulations of the remaining gap.

\section*{Positive values proved here}

Let \(\mathcal G\) be the set of probabilities for which (P) holds universally.

\begin{proposition}[a coloring criterion]\label{prop:criterion}
Let \(p=a/b\), where \(1\le a\le b\) are integers.  Suppose that every
non-negative \(b\)-tuple \(x_1,\ldots,x_b\) with \(\sum_j x_j=1\) has at least
\[
        \frac{a}{b}\binom ba=\binom{b-1}{a-1}
\]
\(a\)-subsets \(J\subseteq[b]\) satisfying \(\sum_{j\in J}x_j\ge a/b\).  Then
\(a/b\in\mathcal G\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Randomly color each original index \(i\) independently and uniformly with one
of \(b\) colors, and let \(X_j\) be the total weight of color \(j\).  For every
coloring outcome, the hypothesis gives at least \(\binom{b-1}{a-1}\) good
\(a\)-subsets of colors.  Hence, for a uniformly random \(a\)-subset \(J\) of
colors,
\[
       \Pr\!\left[\sum_{j\in J}X_j\ge a/b\right]\ge
       \frac{\binom{b-1}{a-1}}{\binom ba}=\frac ab .
\]
By symmetry the same lower bound holds for any fixed \(a\)-subset \(J\).  For
such a fixed \(J\), membership of an original index in \(J\) is an independent
\(\Ber(a/b)\) event, which is exactly (P).
\end{proof}

The criterion immediately gives the reciprocal points \(p=1/k\), \(k\ge3\):
among \(k\) color classes at least one has weight at least \(1/k\).

It also gives the following second family.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:pairs}
Let \(b\ge6\), and let \(x_1,\ldots,x_b\ge0\) with \(\sum_i x_i=1\).  Then at
least \(b-1\) unordered pairs \(\{i,j\}\) satisfy
\[
       x_i+x_j\ge \frac2b .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Put \(z_i=bx_i/2\).  Then \(\sum_i z_i=b/2\), and we must find at least
\(b-1\) pairs with \(z_i+z_j\ge1\).

Maintain a remaining set \(R\) of size \(s\) and sum at least \(s/2\).  Let
\(u\) and \(a\) be the smallest and largest elements of \(R\).  Since the
average of the elements of \(R\) is at least \(1/2\), its largest element
satisfies \(a\ge1/2\).  If \(u+a\ge1\), then \(a\) makes good pairs with all
other \(s-1\) elements and we stop.  Otherwise \(u+a<1\).  Let \(h\) be the
number of other elements making a good pair with \(a\).  If \(h\le(s-2)/2\),
then the \(h\) partners of \(a\) are at most \(a\), while the other \(s-1-h\)
elements are \(<1-a\), so
\[
 \sum_{z\in R}z
   < a+ha+(s-1-h)(1-a)
   =s-1-h+a(2h-s+2)\le s/2.
\]
The last inequality uses \(a\ge1/2\) and \(2h-s+2\le0\).  This contradiction
shows that \(h\ge\lfloor s/2\rfloor\).  Count these \(h\) pairs, delete \(u\)
and \(a\), and continue.  The remaining sum is
\(>s/2-1=(s-2)/2\), so the invariant is preserved.  Pairs counted at different
stages are distinct.

If the procedure never stops early, then for \(b=2r\) it counts
\(r+(r-1)+\cdots+1\ge2r-1=b-1\), and for \(b=2r+1\) it counts
\(r+(r-1)+\cdots+1\ge2r=b-1\), using \(r\ge3\).

If it stops after \(t\) deletion steps, then \(s=b-2t\) elements remain and the
number counted is
\[
       \sum_{i=0}^{t-1}\left\lfloor \frac{b-2i}{2}\right\rfloor+(b-2t-1).
\]
For \(b=2r\) this exceeds \(b-1\) by
\[
       \frac{t(2r-t-3)}{2}\ge0\qquad(0\le t\le r-1,\ r\ge3),
\]
and for \(b=2r+1\) the same displayed expression is the excess over \(b-1\).
Hence at least \(b-1\) good pairs are always counted.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:positive}
The desired inequality holds for \(p=0\), for \(p=1/k\) with every integer
\(k\ge3\), for \(p=2/b\) with every integer \(b\ge6\), for \(p=1/2\), and for
\(p=1\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
The cases \(p=0,1\) are immediate.  The reciprocal cases follow from
Proposition~\ref{prop:criterion} with \(a=1\), and Lemma~\ref{lem:pairs} plus
Proposition~\ref{prop:criterion} gives \(p=2/b\), \(b\ge6\).

For \(p=1/2\), put \(X=\sum_i w_i v_i\).  Since \(1-v_i\) has the same law as
\(v_i\), \(X\) and \(1-X\) have the same distribution.  Hence
\(\Pr[X<1/2]=\Pr[X>1/2]\), and so \(\Pr[X\ge1/2]\ge1/2\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[multiplicative closure]\label{prop:closure}
If \(p,q\in\mathcal G\), then \(pq\in\mathcal G\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(pq=0\), the conclusion is trivial.  Otherwise generate independent
\(\Ber(pq)\) variables as \(v_i=x_i y_i\), where
\(x_i\) are i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(p)\), \(y_i\) are i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(q)\), and the two
families are independent.  Put \(T=\sum_i w_i x_i\).  With probability at least
\(p\), \(T\ge p\).  Conditional on such an outcome, the normalized weights
\(w_i x_i/T\) form a probability vector, and the \(q\)-inequality gives
\[
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i \frac{w_i x_i}{T}y_i\ge q\,\middle|\,x_1,\ldots,x_m\right]
 \ge q .
\]
On this event \(\sum_i w_i x_i y_i\ge qT\ge pq\), proving the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}[closure of the set of good probabilities]\label{prop:closed}
The set \(\mathcal G\) is closed in \([0,1]\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(p_n\in\mathcal G\) and \(p_n\to p\).  Fix a finite weight vector \(w\).
For \(r\in[0,1]\) put
\[
        F(r)=\Pr_r\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge r\right],
\]
where the subscript denotes the Bernoulli parameter.  There are only finitely
many subset sums \(w(A)\).

If infinitely many \(p_n\le p\), pass to that subsequence.  For all large \(n\),
no subset sum lies in \([p_n,p)\); hence the
event \(\{w(A)\ge p_n\}\) is eventually the same family of subsets as
\(\{w(A)\ge p\}\).  Therefore \(F(p)=\lim F(p_n)\ge\lim p_n=p\).

If instead infinitely many \(p_n>p\), pass to that subsequence.  For all large
\(n\), the event \(\{w(A)\ge p_n\}\) is the fixed family \(\{w(A)>p\}\), so
\[
       \mu_p\{A:w(A)>p\}=\lim F(p_n)\ge p .
\]
Since \(\{w(A)>p\}\subseteq\{w(A)\ge p\}\), again \(F(p)\ge p\).  As the weight
vector was arbitrary, \(p\in\mathcal G\).
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:base}
If the inequality can be proved for all \(p\in[1/6,1/3]\), then it holds for
all \(p\in[0,1/3]\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For \(0<p\le1/3\), choose \(n\ge0\) such that \(2^n p\in[1/6,1/3]\).  Since
\(1/2\in\mathcal G\), repeated use of Proposition~\ref{prop:closure} gives
\(p=(2^n p)2^{-n}\in\mathcal G\).  The point \(p=0\) is trivial.
\end{proof}

\section*{Values that cannot work}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:negative}
The desired universal inequality fails for every
\(p\in(1/3,1/2)\cup(1/2,1)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(1/3<p<1/2\), take \(m=3\) and \(w_1=w_2=w_3=1/3\).  At least two
successes are needed, so
\[
\Pr\!\left[\frac{v_1+v_2+v_3}{3}\ge p\right]
  =3p^2(1-p)+p^3
  =3p^2-2p^3 .
\]
But \(3p^2-2p^3-p=p(1-p)(2p-1)<0\) on \(1/3<p<1/2\).

If \(1/2<p<1\), take \(m=2\) and \(w_1=w_2=1/2\).  The event requires two
successes and has probability \(p^2<p\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Equivalent forms of the remaining small-\(p\) lemma}

\begin{lemma}[small-\(p\) lemma]\label{lem:missing}
If \(0\le p\le1/3\), \(w_i\ge0\), \(\sum_iw_i=1\), and the \(v_i\)'s are
i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(p)\), then
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p .
\]
\end{lemma}

The lemma is sharp even in finite examples: if one weight is at least \(p\) and
the sum of all remaining weights is \(<p\), then the event in (P) is exactly
the event that this large coordinate succeeds, and its probability is \(p\).

The nontrivial sharpness mechanism with all weights below \(p\) is a pivot plus
dust.  Fix \(0<p\le1/3\), choose \(0<\varepsilon<p\), put
\(a=p-\varepsilon\), and take
\[
   w_0=a,\qquad
   w_1=\cdots=w_N=\frac{1-a}{N}.
\]
For all sufficiently large \(N\), the dust weights \((1-a)/N\), as well as the
pivot \(a\), are below \(p\).
If \(Y_N=(1-a)N^{-1}{\rm Bin}(N,p)\), then
\[
 \Pr\!\left[w_0v_0+Y_N\ge p\right]
 =
 p\,\Pr[Y_N\ge\varepsilon]+(1-p)\Pr[Y_N\ge p].
\]
Since \(Y_N\to p(1-a)=p(1-p+\varepsilon)\) in probability and
\(\varepsilon<p(1-p+\varepsilon)<p\), this probability tends to \(p\).

A particularly useful normalization is the following ``unit-gap'' form.

\begin{proposition}[unit-gap form]\label{prop:unitgap}
For a fixed \(0<p\le1/3\), Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is equivalent to the
following assertion.  Let \(q=1-p\), let \(0\le b_i\le1\),
\(B=\sum_i b_i\), and let \(\zeta_i\) be i.i.d.\ \(\Ber(p)\).  Then
\[
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i\zeta_i\ge p(B+1)\right]
 \ge
 \frac{p}{q}\,
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i\zeta_i<p(B+1)-1\right].            \tag{UG}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Assume Lemma~\ref{lem:missing}.  Apply it to the normalized coefficient vector
\((1,b_1,\ldots,b_n)/(B+1)\), and condition on the
Bernoulli variable multiplying the coefficient \(1\).  With
\(S=\sum_i b_i\zeta_i\),
\[
 p\,\Pr[S\ge p(B+1)-1]+q\,\Pr[S\ge p(B+1)]\ge p,
\]
which rearranges to (UG).

Conversely, suppose (UG) is known.  If an original weight is at least \(p\),
then that coordinate alone proves (P).  Otherwise choose a pivot of maximal
weight \(a\in(0,p)\), write the remaining weights as \(w_i=a b_i\), so that
\(0\le b_i\le1\) and \(a(B+1)=1\).  Multiplying (UG) by \(a\) in the thresholds
gives
\[
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\right]
 \ge \frac pq
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i<p-a\right].
\]
Conditioning on the pivot variable then yields
\[
 p\,\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p-a\right]
 +q\,\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p,
\]
which is (P).
\end{proof}

Here are exact reformulations of Lemma~\ref{lem:missing}.

First, after replacing \(v_i\) by \(1-v_i\), it is equivalent to
\[
   q\ge\frac23,\quad \eta_i\stackrel{\rm iid}{\sim}\Ber(q)
   \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i\eta_i>q\right]\le q ,
   \qquad q=1-p .                                      \tag{1}
\]
The inequality is strict on the left because
\(\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\) is the same as
\(\sum_iw_i(1-v_i)\le1-p\).

Second, it is equivalent to the following two-tail pivot inequality: for every
\(0\le a\le p\), every set of weights \(u_i\ge0\) with
\(\sum_i u_i=1-a\), and i.i.d.\ \(v_i\sim\Ber(p)\),
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i\ge p\right]
   \ge
   \frac{p}{1-p}\,
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i<p-a\right].                 \tag{2}
\]
Indeed, (2) with \(a=0\) gives
\(U\ge\frac{p}{1-p}(1-U)\), hence \(U\ge p\).  Conversely, if
Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is known, add one extra weight \(a\).  Conditioning on
its Bernoulli variable gives
\[
 p\,\Pr[Y\ge p-a]+(1-p)\Pr[Y\ge p]\ge p,
\]
where \(Y=\sum_i u_i v_i\); rearranging gives (2).

Third, let \(\mu_p\) denote \(p\)-biased product measure on \(2^{[m]}\).  The
small-\(p\) lemma is equivalent to the following convex-hull statement: for
every non-empty down-set \(\mathcal D\subseteq 2^{[m]}\),
\[
       \mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p
       \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
       (p,\ldots,p)\in
       \conv\{\mathbf 1_A:\,A\in\mathcal D\}.             \tag{3}
\]
For the forward implication, take
\(\mathcal D=\{A:\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\}\).  If (3) supplied a distribution on
\(\mathcal D\) with all coordinate marginals \(p\), then
\[
 p=\sum_i p w_i
   =\mathbb E\sum_{i\in A}w_i
   <p,
\]
a contradiction.

Conversely, suppose (3) fails for a down-set \(\mathcal D\), and put
\(P(\mathcal D)=\conv\{\mathbf1_A:A\in\mathcal D\}\).  This convex set is
coordinatewise down-closed: if \(x\in P(\mathcal D)\) and \(0\le y\le x\),
represent \(x\) as the marginals of a random \(A\in\mathcal D\), then thin each
\(i\in A\) independently with probability \(y_i/x_i\) (with the obvious
convention when \(x_i=0\)).  The resulting random subset is still in
\(\mathcal D\) and has marginals \(y\).  Thus \(P(\mathcal D)\) is disjoint
from \((p,\ldots,p)+\mathbb R_{\ge0}^m\).  Separating these two convex sets
gives a non-zero normal vector \(c\).  The vector must have \(c_i\ge0\) for
each \(i\): otherwise the linear functional \(x\mapsto c\cdot x\) would be
unbounded below on the upper orthant
\((p,\ldots,p)+\mathbb R_{\ge0}^m\) and could not separate it from the compact
polytope \(P(\mathcal D)\).  Thus
\[
      \sum_{i\in A}c_i < p\sum_i c_i
      \qquad(A\in\mathcal D)
\]
for some non-zero \(c\ge0\).
After normalizing \(w_i=c_i/\sum_jc_j\), Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} would imply
\[
 \mu_p(\mathcal D)
 \le \Pr\!\left[\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\right]\le1-p,
\]
contradicting \(\mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p\).

Equivalently, with \(\lambda=p/(1-p)\), the remaining down-set statement is
the partition-function bound
\[
 p{\bf 1}\notin P(\mathcal D)
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 \sum_{A\in\mathcal D}\lambda^{|A|}
 \le (1+\lambda)^{m-1},
 \qquad 0\le\lambda\le\frac12 .                         \tag{4}
\]

Finally, the same down-set has a useful intersection-theoretic description.  If
\(\mathcal D_w=\{A:\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\}\) and
\(r=\lfloor1/p\rfloor\), then no \(r\) members of \(\mathcal D_w\) cover
\([m]\), because the weight of their union is \(<rp\le1\).  Equivalently, the
complement family
\[
   \mathcal H_w=\{B:\sum_{i\in B}w_i>1-p\}
\]
is \(r\)-wise intersecting.  Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is therefore the special
weighted-threshold assertion that such a family has \((1-p)\)-biased measure at
most \(1-p\).

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The negative direction is complete, and the positive direction is complete for
the values listed in Corollary~\ref{cor:positive}, their finite products under
Proposition~\ref{prop:closure}, and \(p=1/2,1\).

The essential missing assertion is Lemma~\ref{lem:missing}.  Mathematically, it
is the assertion that every weighted threshold down-set
\(\{A:\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\}\) has \(p\)-biased measure at most \(1-p\) when
\(p\le1/3\).  It appears in the document as the unproved implication
\([0,1/3]\subseteq\mathcal G\).  The approaches recorded above reduce it to
any one of the unit-gap inequality (UG), the strict complement inequality (1),
the pivot inequality (2), the convex-hull statement (3), or the
partition-function inequality (4).  Proposition~\ref{prop:closed} shows that it
would also be enough to prove the inequality on any dense subset of
\([0,1/3]\).  Related extremal results for these weighted threshold families
appear in the work of Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis~\cite{FMP} and
Frankl~\cite{Frankl}, but the lemma above is not yet derived here from those
results.  Earlier rounds and the computations checked the pivot/unit-gap
formulation, finite chamber linear programs, and coloring/counting reductions;
no counterexample was found.  To close the solution one still needs a proof of
one of these equivalent statements, at least on the base interval
\([1/6,1/3]\), after which Corollary~\ref{cor:base} gives all \(p\le1/3\).
