I would pivot to the following finite “Kellerer/fractional Hall” formulation. It is exactly tailored to the non-integral case and avoids the two false deterministic strengthenings.

**Finite Kellerer lemma.** Let \(0<p\le 1/3\) and let \(\mathcal D\subseteq 2^{[m]}\) be a down-set. If
\[
\mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p
\]
for the product \(p\)-measure, then there is a probability distribution \(\nu\) supported on \(\mathcal D\) such that every coordinate has marginal \(p\):
\[
\nu\{A\in\mathcal D:i\in A\}=p\qquad(i=1,\dots,m).
\]
Equivalently, \((p,\ldots,p)\in \operatorname{conv}\{\mathbf 1_A:A\in\mathcal D\}\).

Given this lemma, your desired inequality is immediate. In the normalized form, put \(p=1/L\) and
\[
\mathcal D=\left\{A\subseteq[m]:\sum_{i\in A}a_i<1\right\}.
\]
This is a down-set. If the Bernoulli inequality failed, then
\[
\mu_p(\mathcal D)=\Pr\!\left[\sum_i a_i\xi_i<1\right]>1-p.
\]
Kellerer gives \(\nu\) on \(\mathcal D\) with all marginals \(p\). Hence
\[
1=p\sum_i a_i
  =\sum_i a_i\,\nu(i\in A)
  =\mathbb E_\nu\sum_{i\in A}a_i
  <1,
\]
a contradiction. This proves all real \(L\ge3\), with no rational approximation or continuity issue.

This lemma is also sharp in exactly the right way: for \(p>1/3\), take \(\mathcal D=\{\varnothing,\{1\},\{2\},\{3\}\}\). Then \(\mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p\) for \(p<1/2\), but \((p,p,p)\notin\operatorname{conv}(\mathcal D)\) because every vector in the convex hull has coordinate sum at most \(1\).

I am fairly sure this is the “analogous regions” theorem you want from Kellerer, but I would cite only this precise convex-hull/marginal statement, not the paper vaguely. If you need self-containedness, make this lemma the main lemma and reproduce Kellerer’s finite induction/LP proof. Note, however, that by separation this lemma is essentially dual to your weighted Bernoulli inequality, so a proof that merely invokes separating hyperplanes would be circular; the useful content is Kellerer’s \(p\le1/3\) convex-hull theorem itself.

### Council member: opus_47_max (models/anthropic/opus_47_max) ###
Here is my opinion as a Council member.

**1. The most promising structural reduction: $a$-subset coloring with $a=2$.**

The rational-color *deterministic* strengthening fails in general (the author's $a=3,b=10$ example), but the failure is sensitive to $a$. For $a=2$ — that is, for $p=2/b$ with $b\ge 7$ — the relevant deterministic question becomes very clean:

> *For $W_1,\dots,W_b\ge0$ with $\sum W_j=1$, is the number $N$ of pairs $\{i,j\}$ with $W_i+W_j\ge 2/b$ at least $b-1$?*

If yes, then by exchangeability of the $b$ random color-buckets, $\Pr[W_1+W_2\ge 2/b]=E[N]/\binom{b}{2}\ge (b-1)/\binom{b}{2}=2/b=p$, closing the inequality at $p=2/b$.

My numerical experiments suggest this *does* hold for $b\ge 6$, with equality on the family $(1,0,\dots,0)$ and on "one $W_1\in[1/b,2/b)$ + the rest nearly equal". But: it **fails** for $b=5$ — the configuration $W=(0.39,0.39,0.21,0.005,0.005)$ gives $N=3<4$. So the boundary case is delicate; the proof, if true, must use $b\ge 6$ in an essential way (and presumably $p\le 1/3$ enters here). This is exactly the threshold where your overall problem becomes nontrivial, which is suggestive.

I would attempt to prove this $a=2$ lemma by a case split on $W_{(1)}$: if $W_{(1)}\ge 2/b$, the $b-1$ pairs $(1,j)$ work; otherwise show that having $W_{(1)}<2/b$ forces enough mass into the middle to compensate. A short proof along these lines would settle every $p$ of the form $2/(2k+1)$, $k\ge 3$.

**2. Density + monotonicity won't quite work, but a one-sided version might.**

The rationals $\{2/(2k+1)\}$ are dense in $(0,1/3]$, but $f_w(p):=\Pr_p[\sum w_i v_i\ge p]-p$ has *downward* jumps at subset sums $p=w(S)$, and is left-continuous. So $f_w\ge 0$ at a dense set does not immediately give $f_w\ge0$ everywhere — between rationals, $f_w$ could dip. However, for fixed $w$ the locations of those jumps are exactly the (finitely many) subset sums of $w$. A possible route: for each $w$, identify the jumps of $f_w$; show that just to the right of each jump $p_0=w(S)$, $f_w(p_0^+)\ge 0$ by a direct local argument (this is the "subset $S$ stops contributing, but the remaining open set still meets $p$" argument); and between jumps, $f_w$ is a polynomial that can be controlled by its values at the dense rationals. This may be more tractable than uniform control over all $w$ at once.

**3. Worth checking: Feige (2004/2006) and its descendants.**

Feige's "On sums of independent random variables with unbounded variance and estimating the average degree" proves $\Pr[\sum X_i<\mu+1]\ge 1/13$ for independent nonneg $X_i$. The constant has been improved (He–Zhang–Zhang, Garnett, others) and the *direction* you want — $\Pr[\sum X_i\ge \mu]$ for nonnegative bounded summands — is a sibling question. None of these give $\Pr\ge p$ in the sharp form you need, but the techniques (truncation + extremal redistribution to two-point laws) are likely to be the right toolkit. In particular, a Feige-style "minimal counterexample has all $w_i\in\{0,p\}\cup\{\text{tiny}\}$" lemma would immediately reduce you to the reciprocal coloring case, which is solved.

**4. Splitting/merging is not pointwise monotone, but the extremal configuration is identifiable.**

Your splitting note is correct (the local expression involves $g(u)+g(s-u)$ with $g$ a step function, neither convex nor concave). But the *infimum* of $\Pr[X\ge p]$ over the simplex is clearly $p$, attained in the limit $w_1=p$, $w_2,\dots,w_n\to 0$ with $n\to\infty$ (by CLT-type concentration of the residual at $p(1-p)<p$). So the question is really: *no finite $w$ undercuts this limit*. A compactness + KKT argument on the simplex, using that $\Pr[X\ge p]$ is piecewise polynomial with cells defined by subset-sum hyperplanes, may force the minimizer to have one $w_i=p$ exactly, which trivializes the inequality. The piecewise structure is finite once $n$ is fixed; an induction on $n$ along these lines feels viable.

**5. The two-parameter induction failure has a workaround.**

Your note correctly identifies that conditioning on a weight produces shifted thresholds $p/(1-a)$ and $(p-a)/(1-a)$ that escape $[0,1/3]$. But if you condition on the *smallest* weight $w_n=a\le p$ and use $H(s,t)$ with $s=$ total weight and $t=$ threshold, the shifted threshold $(p-a)/(1-a)$ stays $\le p\le 1/3$, and only the upper threshold $p/(1-a)$ escapes. Since that branch occurs with probability $p$, you can pay for it explicitly: $\Pr[X\ge p]\ge p\cdot 1+(1-p)\Pr[X'\ge (p-a)/(1-a)]$, where $X'$ is a sum over $n-1$ weights with new total $(1-a)$. Renormalizing, $X'/(1-a)$ has total weight 1 and threshold $(p-a)/(1-a)\cdot 1/(1-a)$... actually the shift is messy, but the *direction* — induct on $n$ via the smallest weight — gives a thresh that stays under $p$, which is what you need. Worth pursuing carefully.

**Recommendation.** Pursue (1) and (4) in parallel: a clean proof of the $a=2$ pair lemma for $b\ge 7$, combined with a Feige-style reduction to weights in $\{0,p\}$, would together cover a dense set of $p$ plus give the extremal characterization needed to fill in irrational $p$.

Here are four viable strategies and adjacent literature angles to unblock the non-integral $L > 3$ case.

**1. Topology of the Minimizer**
You noted the objective is piecewise polynomial. For a fixed $p$, $f(w) = \Pr(\sum w_i v_i \ge p)$ is actually piecewise *constant*—it is a sum of constants $p^{|S|}(1-p)^{n-|S|}$, changing values only when a subset sum $w(S)$ crosses $p$. 
Because the condition is $\ge p$, $f(w)$ jumps *up* at the boundaries. Consequently, the minimum over any closed cell is achieved on its *open* interior, making direct boundary induction difficult. 
*Alternative:* Look at the strict complement $g(w) = \Pr(\sum w_i \eta_i > q)$. This jumps *down* at the boundaries, meaning its extreme worst-case behavior is forced *onto* the boundaries $w(S) = q$. This partitions the variables into two groups summing to exactly $q$ and $1-q$, giving you a highly structured reduction step for a minimal counterexample.

**2. Product-Measure MMS & Margulis-Russo**
Your problem is the independent Bernoulli analogue of the Manickam–Miklós–Singhi (MMS) conjecture. You already noticed the deterministic analogue fails, which perfectly mirrors how fractional MMS bounds often require careful probabilistic averaging.
In Boolean function terms, you are bounding the $p$-biased measure of a weighted threshold upset $\mathcal{F} = \{x : w \cdot x \ge p\}$. Instead of discrete reductions, you can use the Margulis-Russo formula: $\frac{d}{dp} \Pr_p(\mathcal{F}) = \sum_i \mathrm{Inf}_i(\mathcal{F})$. If you can show that the total influence (average sensitivity) of this specific threshold function is $\ge 1$ for all $p \le 1/3$, the bound $\Pr \ge p$ follows trivially by integrating from $p=0$. This converts a global probability bound into a local structural bound on the boundary of the hypercube.

**3. Size-Biased Coupling via Flow**
Your idea to prove $\Pr \ge \Pr(I \in R) = p$ reduces to proving $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{X \ge p}] \ge \mathbb{E}[X]$ for $X = \sum w_i v_i$. 
Since $X \le 1$, you can decompose $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X \mathbf{1}_{X \ge p}] + \mathbb{E}[X \mathbf{1}_{X < p}]$. 
To bound the deficit term, construct a randomized flow (e.g., Glauber dynamics) rather than a deterministic injection. If $w(R) < p \le 1/3$, its complement has weight $w(R^c) > 2/3$. There is an enormous, strictly bounded reservoir of available weight. Define a Markov chain that adds items proportional to their weight to push mass directly from the deficit to the surplus.

**4. Continuous Circle Expectation**
You rightly noted any circle proof must average over random atom positions. 
Place items as independent variables $U_i \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(S^1)$. Let $v_i = \mathbf{1}(U_i \in [0, p])$, perfectly coupling the Bernoullis. The sliding sum $S(t) = \sum w_i \mathbf{1}(U_i \in [t, t+p])$ is stationary, and $\int_0^1 S(t) dt = p$. 
Let $M(U) = \mathrm{Leb}(\{t : S(t) \ge p\})$. While $M(U) < p$ for a *fixed* bad $U$, translation invariance guarantees $\mathbb{E}_U[M(U)] = \Pr(S(0) \ge p)$. This shifts the problem from discrete combinatorics to bounding the expected super-level measure of a random stationary step function, allowing you to deploy tools from scan statistics and coverage processes.
