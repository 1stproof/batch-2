This inequality is genuinely subtle: it sits exactly where the shifted induction fails to close, where the natural coupling has the wrong rate, and where FMP's published rectangles leave the relevant triangle open. Some thoughts on where I would push.

**1. Complement form as a left-skewed tail comparison.** With $\eta_i = 1-\zeta_i \sim \mathrm{Bern}(q)$ and $T = \sum b_i \eta_i$ (so $E[T] = qB$), the inequality becomes
$$q\,\Pr[T \le qB - p] \;\ge\; p\,\Pr[T > qB + q].$$
For $q \ge 2/3$, $T$ is left-skewed: the lower tail is at distance $p$ from the mean, the upper tail at distance $q$. Heuristically the inequality reads "lower tail dominates upper tail by at least the skewness factor $p/q$." That identifies skewness, not concentration, as the operative mechanism — which explains why moment-based (Chebyshev/Bernstein/Pinelis-left-domination) attempts have been too weak.

**2. The right scalar Bellman almost works — close it with a Lyapunov term.** Your observation that the shifted induction
$\Pr[S \ge x+p] \ge (p/q)\Pr[S < x-q]$
closes algebraically but fails at the base for $x > q$ is exactly the issue. The "debt" at the base is precisely $-p$ on the bad event $\{$ shift exits upward $\}$. Try the strengthened invariant
$$\Phi(x,B)\;:=\;q\Pr[S\ge x+p]-p\Pr[S<x-q]+p\,\Pr[\text{shift walk reaches }x>q\text{ region by depletion}].$$
The third term is computable as the probability that the time-reversed shift random walk (driven by the $b_i$'s with jumps $-qb_i$ at rate $p$ and $+pb_i$ at rate $q$) ever exceeds $q$ before all weight is consumed. It is monotone in conditioning in a controlled way, and it exactly absorbs the base-case deficit. I have not verified this closes — but the failure mode is so structural that I'd expect any working proof to look like this kind of "two-term ledger." A close analog is how Bentkus closes his sharp Bennett-type bounds with an auxiliary "ghost" Poisson term.

**3. Dichotomy: near-pivot vs. genuine dust.** The hardest configurations are not extreme — they are intermediate. But the two ends are easy:
  - *Near-pivot* ($\max b_i$ close to $1$): a single-flip coupling $\zeta \mapsto \zeta'$ on a maximal-weight $0$ converts $\{S < -q\}$ events to $\{S \ge p\}$ events with measure ratio $q/p$, giving the inequality.
  - *Genuine dust* ($\max b_i \to 0$, $\sigma^2 = pq\sum b_i^2$ small relative to $1$): Cramér's theorem gives lower-tail rate $\approx q^2/(2pq\sigma^2)$ and upper-tail rate $\approx p^2/(2pq\sigma^2)$, so the ratio diverges and the inequality is comfortable.
  
The remaining work is to interpolate. One concrete attempt: bound $\Pr[\sum b_i\zeta_i < pB - q]$ from above by truncation — separate $\zeta$-realizations with all $b_i \zeta_i \le 1/2$ (handled by Hoeffding/Cramér) from those with a "large" successful coordinate (handled by single-flip).

**4. Bentkus/Pinelis sharp martingale-tail comparisons.** I would specifically look at:
  - Bentkus, *An inequality for tail probabilities of martingales with bounded differences*, Lithuanian Math. J. 42 (2002), and his Ann. Probab. 2004 paper. These give sharp Bennett-type bounds with explicit asymmetry between up and down increments.
  - Pinelis, *On the Bennett–Hoeffding inequality* (Ann. Inst. H. Poincaré 50, 2014), which optimizes the bound in terms of individual increment asymmetries.
  
The two-sided versions of these may already imply $\Pr[S\ge p]/\Pr[S<-q] \ge p/q$ in the asymmetric Bernoulli case. It is worth grinding through the explicit form of the bound on your specific summands $b_i(\zeta_i - p)$.

**5. Anderson 1955 / Hoeffding 1956 monotone-convex reduction.** Hoeffding proved that for independent Bernoulli summands with fixed total mean, the binomial extremizes various tail functionals (this is the basis of the Hoeffding 1963 inequality). If both tails $\Pr[T \le qB - p]$ and $\Pr[T > qB+q]$ are extremized in the same direction by homogenization, the problem reduces to a pure binomial inequality where the desired bound is a clean and checkable statement about ratios of $\mathrm{Bin}(n,q)$ probabilities at distance $p$ and $q$ from the mean. The hypothesis "extremized in the same direction" is the thing to check — it is not automatic.

**6. FMP shadow argument with strict event.** Their proof at $q = t = (k+1)/(k+2)$ uses a specific weight-shuffling that I think extends to the strict diagonal $\{(q,t): t > q,\ q \ge 2/3\}$. Atoms exactly at $q$ make their argument delicate; the strict event $\sum w_i \eta_i > q$ removes that pain. I'd reread Section 4 of FMP with the strict event in mind and check whether their merge step preserves the strict comparison.

**My ranking of likely-to-work strategies, in order:**
(a) The two-term ledger Bellman of (2), if you can guess the right Lyapunov term; this is the most "structural" route.
(b) The Bentkus 2004 inequality applied directly — it is the most likely off-the-shelf hit.
(c) Anderson/Hoeffding monotonization to reduce to binomial.

You have hit a classic structural roadblock for ballot-type and ruin inequalities: your naive shifted induction fails for shifts $x > q$ because the two-tail ratio is a property of paths starting *near the mean*. Once a path has drifted far below the mean, it has nearly zero chance of crossing the upper threshold but will certainly cross the lower, breaking the required ratio. A one-parameter backward induction is structurally doomed because the conditional probability does not uniformly obey the target inequality.

Here are three constructive routes to bypass this, capitalizing on the exact structures and reductions you have already uncovered:

### 1. Size-Biasing / Stein’s Method on the Unit-Gap Form
Your unit-gap (UG) form is the perfect algebraic setting for size-biasing because the bounding of weights ($b_i \le 1$) translates into exact nested events. Let $X = \sum b_i \zeta_i$, so $\mathbb{E}[X] = pB$. Set your upper threshold $k = p(B+1)$. The standard size-bias identity gives:
$$ \mathbb{E}[X \mathbf{1}_{X < k}] = p \sum b_i \Pr[X^{(i)} < k - b_i] $$
where $X^{(i)}$ is the sum omitting coordinate $i$. 
Because $b_i \le 1$, we have $k - b_i \ge k - 1$, which strictly enforces the event containment $\{X^{(i)} < k - b_i\} \subseteq \{X^{(i)} < k - 1\}$. This allows you to rigorously bound $\mathbb{E}[X \mathbf{1}_{X < k}]$ in terms of the lower threshold $k-1$. 

Applying the analogous size-bias identity to the complementary sum $Y = B - X$ yields a system of inequalities that algebraically couple the upper tail $\Pr[X \ge k]$ and the lower tail $\Pr[X < k - 1]$. This route avoids path-induction entirely, treats macroscopic pivots and fractional dust on equal footing, and is known to yield sharp constants for Bernoulli sums (see, e.g., similar exchangeable-pair techniques in Pinelis's work).

### 2. The Differential / Total Influence Route (Russo's Formula)
In your notes, you observed numerically that $I_t(D_t) \ge 1$. This is a massive clue. For the moving-threshold measure $g(r) = \mu_r(\{A : w(A) < t\})$, Russo’s formula dictates that $g'(r) = -I_r(D_t)$. 
If you can prove that the total $r$-biased influence of this specific linear threshold down-set is bounded below by $1$ for $r \in [p, t]$, then integration yields $g(p) \le g(0) - p = 1 - p = q$. This exactly proves your complementary lemma! 
Look into the literature on **influences of linear threshold functions** (e.g., generalized KKL theorems for biased measures). Because your threshold is located exactly at the mean, the influence is maximized, making a pointwise lower bound of $1$ highly plausible.

### 3. A $B$-Dependent Bellman Invariant (The "Dust" Limit)
If you want to salvage the Bellman/martingale induction, your invariant *must* depend on the remaining capacity $B$. You astutely observed that the near-equality configurations are "pivot + dust." Crucially, in the dust limit ($\max b_i \to 0$ while $\sum b_i = B$), the variance goes to zero and the dust acts as a *deterministic shift* $pB$. 

This means your Bellman target function $V(x, B)$ must accommodate the deterministic boundary condition limit:
$$ V(x, B_{dust}) = \mathbf{1}_{x + pB \ge p(B+1)} - \frac{p}{q} \mathbf{1}_{x + pB < p(B+1) - 1} $$
By explicitly introducing $B$ into your induction state and setting up a difference equation where the "diffusion" (variance) is dominated by the "advection" (drift) for small $p$, you can establish $V(0, B) \ge 0$ via a maximum principle. 

**Recommendation:** Attack the Size-Biasing route first. It natively digests the $b_i \le 1$ UG constraint and directly relates the $k$ and $k-1$ thresholds you identified.
