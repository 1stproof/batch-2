\title{A weighted Bernoulli mean-tail inequality}
\author{}
\date{}
\maketitle

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I interpret the question in the universal sense: determine those \(p\in[0,1]\)
for which
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^m w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p
\]
holds for every \(m\ge1\) and every choice of non-negative weights
\(w_1,\ldots,w_m\) with \(\sum_i w_i=1\), where the \(v_i\)'s are i.i.d.\
\({\rm Bernoulli}(p)\).

The expected complete answer is
\[
        [0,1/3]\cup\{1/2,1\}.
\]
The counterexamples excluding all other \(p\)'s are elementary and are proved
below.  The positive endpoint \(p=1/3\) and, more generally, the full interval
\(0\le p\le1/3\), is the only genuinely nontrivial part.  In this draft I record
a complete proof for the reciprocal points \(p=1/k\), including \(p=1/3\), and
isolate the remaining missing lemma for the non-reciprocal points in
\((0,1/3)\).

\section*{Positive values proved here}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:positive}
The desired inequality holds for \(p=0\), for \(p=1/k\) with every integer
\(k\ge3\), for \(p=1/2\), and for \(p=1\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The case \(p=0\) is immediate.

Let \(p=1/k\), \(k\ge3\).  Independently color each index \(i\) uniformly with
one of the colors \(1,\ldots,k\), and let
\[
       W_j=\sum_{\{i:\,{\rm color}(i)=j\}} w_i
       \qquad (j=1,\ldots,k).
\]
For each fixed outcome of the coloring, \(\sum_{j=1}^k W_j=1\), so at least one
color satisfies \(W_j\ge1/k\).  By symmetry of the colors,
\[
       \Pr[W_1\ge1/k]\ge \frac1k.
\]
But the set of indices receiving color \(1\) is exactly a \(p\)-biased random
subset of \(\{1,\ldots,m\}\); equivalently, the indicators
\(\mathbf 1_{\{{\rm color}(i)=1\}}\) are independent Bernoulli\((1/k)\)
variables.  Thus
\[
       \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge 1/k\right]\ge \frac1k .
\]

For \(p=1/2\), put \(X=\sum_i w_i v_i\).  Since \(1-v_i\) has the same
distribution as \(v_i\), the random variables \(X\) and \(1-X\) have the same
distribution.  Therefore
\[
 \Pr[X<1/2]=\Pr[1-X<1/2]=\Pr[X>1/2].
\]
Writing \(a=\Pr[X>1/2]\) and \(b=\Pr[X=1/2]\), we get \(2a+b=1\), whence
\[
      \Pr[X\ge1/2]=a+b=\frac{1+b}{2}\ge\frac12 .
\]
Finally, if \(p=1\), then all \(v_i=1\) almost surely, so the weighted sum is
\(1\) almost surely.
\end{proof}

\section*{Values that cannot work}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:negative}
The desired universal inequality fails for every
\(p\in(1/3,1/2)\cup(1/2,1)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First suppose \(1/3<p<1/2\).  Take \(m=3\) and
\(w_1=w_2=w_3=1/3\).  Then
\[
 \sum_{i=1}^3 w_i v_i=\frac{v_1+v_2+v_3}{3}.
\]
Since \(p>1/3\), the event
\(\frac{v_1+v_2+v_3}{3}\ge p\) requires at least two successes.  Hence
\[
\Pr\!\left[\frac{v_1+v_2+v_3}{3}\ge p\right]
  =\Pr[{\rm Bin}(3,p)\ge2]
  =3p^2(1-p)+p^3
  =3p^2-2p^3 .
\]
But
\[
  3p^2-2p^3-p=p(1-p)(2p-1)<0
\]
throughout \(1/3<p<1/2\).  Thus the required inequality fails on this interval.

Now suppose \(1/2<p<1\).  Take \(m=2\) and
\(w_1=w_2=1/2\).  Then
\[
 \sum_{i=1}^2 w_i v_i=\frac{v_1+v_2}{2}.
\]
Since \(p>1/2\), the event
\(\frac{v_1+v_2}{2}\ge p\) requires two successes, and therefore has probability
\(p^2<p\).  This excludes every \(p\in(1/2,1)\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The only missing step for a complete proof of the announced answer is the
following finite weighted Bernoulli inequality.

\begin{lemma}[missing small-\(p\) lemma]\label{lem:missing}
If \(0\le p\le1/3\), \(w_i\ge0\), \(\sum_i w_i=1\), and the \(v_i\)'s are
i.i.d.\ Bernoulli\((p)\), then
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p .
\]
\end{lemma}

This lemma is exactly the sufficiency of the interval \([0,1/3]\).  A useful
equivalent normalization is obtained, when all \(w_i<p\), by setting
\(a_i=w_i/p\) and \(L=1/p\ge3\): then \(0\le a_i\le1\), \(\sum_i a_i=L\), and
one must prove
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i a_i \xi_i\ge1\right]\ge \frac1L,
   \qquad \xi_i\stackrel{\rm iid}{\sim}{\rm Bernoulli}(1/L).
\]
The reciprocal case \(L\in\mathbb Z\) is Proposition~\ref{prop:positive}; the
non-integral values \(L>3\) remain to be closed.

Several natural reductions were checked but do not by themselves prove the
lemma.  For instance, a deterministic strengthening for rational
\(p=a/b\), asserting that every \(b\)-tuple of nonnegative numbers summing to
\(1\) has at least an \(a/b\) fraction of its \(a\)-subsets of weight at least
\(a/b\), is false: for \(a=3,b=10\), seven entries equal to \(1/7\) and three
entries equal to \(0\) give only \(\binom73/\binom{10}3=35/120<3/10\) good
subsets.  Thus the remaining proof needs an argument that uses the full
independence structure of the Bernoulli variables, or a precise external theorem
which states Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} with its hypotheses.

Combining Lemma~\ref{lem:missing}, once proved, with
Propositions~\ref{prop:positive} and~\ref{prop:negative} gives exactly
\([0,1/3]\cup\{1/2,1\}\).
