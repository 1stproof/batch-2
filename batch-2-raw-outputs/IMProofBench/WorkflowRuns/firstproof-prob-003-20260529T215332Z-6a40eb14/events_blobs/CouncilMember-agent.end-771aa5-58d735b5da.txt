I do not see a counterexample; the pivot inequality looks structurally right. My strongest suggestion is to change the induction target by adding “buffer bands” around the two thresholds. The exact identity below seems important.

Write \(q=1-p\), \(\lambda=p/q\), \(L=p-a\), \(U=p\), and
\[
\Phi(Y):=\Pr[Y\ge U]-\lambda\Pr[Y<L].
\]
If \(Y=R+xv\), \(v\sim{\rm Bernoulli}(p)\), then a direct expansion gives
\[
\Phi(R+xv)
=
\Phi(R)
+p\,\Pr[U-x\le R<U]
+\lambda p\,\Pr[L-x\le R<L].
\tag{*}
\]
So after peeling off a weight \(x\), one should not try to prove the same pivot inequality for \(R\); it is false in the wrong parameter range. The peeled weight creates two compensating strips adjacent to the upper and lower thresholds. This is exactly the “same residual variable at two thresholds” issue.

I would therefore try to prove the stronger buffered invariant: for \(h\ge0\),
\[
\mathbb E F_{a,h}(R)\ge0,\qquad \sum r_i=1-a-h,
\]
where
\[
F_{a,h}(r)
=
{\bf1}_{r\ge p}
-\lambda{\bf1}_{r<p-a}
+p{\bf1}_{p-h\le r<p}
+\lambda p{\bf1}_{p-a-h\le r<p-a}.
\tag{B}
\]
For \(h=x\), \((*)\) says precisely that proving \((B)\) for the residual \(R\) proves the pivot inequality for \(R+xv\). In step-function form, away from overlaps, \(F_{a,h}\) has levels
\[
-\lambda,\ -p,\ 0,\ p,\ 1,
\]
on the successive intervals cut by
\[
p-a-h,\quad p-a,\quad p-h,\quad p.
\]
Those coefficients are not arbitrary: they are exactly the Bernoulli transition coefficients. This is the invariant I would test for pairwise splitting/merging; monotonicity may fail for \(\Phi\), but it may hold for this five-level payoff.

To close an induction, you will probably need one more parameter: allow the top threshold \(t\le p\),
\[
F_{t,a,h}(r)
=
{\bf1}_{r\ge t}
-\lambda{\bf1}_{r<t-a}
+p{\bf1}_{t-h\le r<t}
+\lambda p{\bf1}_{t-a-h\le r<t-a}.
\]
The \(t\)-parameter appears because conditioning on a selected peeled weight shifts both thresholds from \((t,t-a)\) to \((t-x,t-a-x)\). I would try proving \(\mathbb EF_{t,a,h}\ge0\) for \(a\le t\le p\), with overlaps handled literally by the indicator formula.

A second promising recast: by complementing sets, the pivot inequality is equivalent to
\[
\mu_p\{u(A)\ge p\}\ge \lambda\,\mu_q\{u(A)>q\},\qquad q=1-p.
\]
Couple \(B\sim\mu_q\) and thin it with probability \(\lambda=p/q\) to get \(A\sim\mu_p\). A pointwise thinning bound is false, but this formulation may support an averaged threshold-family proof over the weighted upper family \(\{u(A)\ge t\}\). It also connects naturally to Kanter/Kemperman-type comparison results for Bernoulli convolutions, though I’m uncertain which reference gives exactly this sharp two-threshold form.
