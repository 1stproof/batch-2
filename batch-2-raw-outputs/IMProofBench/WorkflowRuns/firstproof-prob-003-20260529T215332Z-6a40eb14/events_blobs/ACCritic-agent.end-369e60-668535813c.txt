## Literature validation

The Fokkink–Meester–Pelekis citation is relevant and accurately described. Their paper defines
\[
\pi(p,t)=\sup_\gamma \Pr(S_\gamma\ge t)
\]
for convex combinations of iid Bernoulli random variables, matching the draft’s “bold play” discussion. ([alea.impa.br](https://alea.impa.br/articles/v20/20-07.pdf)) Their Theorem 4.4 proves bold play optimality only in the region
\[
\frac{k}{k+1}<p\le \frac{k+1}{k+2}<t,
\]
and Proposition 4.5 treats special boundary points \(p=t=(k+1)/(k+2)\), not the entire near-hypotenuse region needed here. ([alea.impa.br](https://alea.impa.br/articles/v20/20-07.pdf)) Thus the draft is right not to claim that FMP proves Conjecture 1.

The Frankl citation is also bibliographically valid and concerns Sperner families with an additional condition; it does not by itself supply the weighted-threshold biased-measure bound needed in this problem. ([sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/009731657690073X?utm_source=openai))

## Mathematical audit

### Problem statement and interpretation

The universal interpretation is explicitly stated and is faithful to the problem. However, the same section immediately says that the small-\(p\) assertion remains an essential gap. Therefore the document self-identifies as incomplete.

### Coloring criterion

The coloring criterion is valid. For each coloring, the deterministic hypothesis gives enough good \(a\)-subsets; averaging over a random \(a\)-subset and then using color symmetry gives the desired probability for any fixed \(a\)-subset. Membership in that fixed subset of colors is iid Bernoulli\((a/b)\).

No issue found.

### Reciprocal points

The \(p=1/k\), \(k\ge3\), conclusion follows correctly from the \(a=1\) case of the coloring criterion. In fact the method also covers \(k=2\), but \(p=1/2\) is handled separately.

No issue found.

### Pair lemma

The pair-counting lemma appears correct. The contradiction proving
\[
h\ge \lfloor s/2\rfloor
\]
is valid, the deletion invariant is preserved, and the even/odd count formulas give at least \(b-1\) good pairs for \(b\ge6\). I also checked the displayed counting formulas computationally for small \(b\), and they are consistent.

Minor wording issue: “If one or two elements remain, the procedure simply stops” could be clearer for the two-element case, since if \(s=2\), the first stopping condition \(u+a\ge1\) follows from the invariant and contributes the final pair. This does not invalidate the proof.

### Corollary of positive values

The positive cases \(p=0,1\), \(p=1/k\), \(p=2/b\), \(p=1/2\), and \(p=1\) are correctly derived. The symmetry proof for \(p=1/2\), using \(X\stackrel d=1-X\), is valid.

No issue found.

### Multiplicative closure

The closure proof is correct. Writing Bernoulli\((pq)\) variables as products of independent Bernoulli\((p)\) and Bernoulli\((q)\) variables is legitimate, and the conditional normalization argument is sound.

No issue found.

### Closedness of \(\mathcal G\)

The proof is rigorous. The finite set of subset sums guarantees threshold-family stabilization from either side of \(p\), and the limiting argument gives \(F(p)\ge p\).

No issue found.

### Base interval reduction

The reduction from proving \([1/6,1/3]\subseteq\mathcal G\) to proving all of \([0,1/3]\subseteq\mathcal G\) is valid, using \(1/2\in\mathcal G\) and multiplicative closure.

No issue found.

### Negative direction

The counterexamples are correct:

- For \(1/3<p<1/2\), three equal weights require at least two successes, giving \(3p^2-2p^3<p\).
- For \(1/2<p<1\), two equal weights require two successes, giving \(p^2<p\).

This proves failure outside the expected set, assuming \(p\in[0,1]\).

No issue found.

### Conjecture 1: small-\(p\) assertion

This is the central missing part. The conjecture is exactly the assertion needed to complete the classification. Since it is not proved or cited from an external theorem, the draft is not answer-ready.

### Sharpness examples

The finite sharpness example with one large coordinate is correct.

The pivot-plus-dust construction is also correct: \(Y_N\to p(1-a)=p(1-p+\varepsilon)\) in probability, and
\[
\varepsilon<p(1-p+\varepsilon)<p
\]
for \(0<\varepsilon<p\). Hence the displayed probability tends to \(p\).

No issue found.

### Unit-gap and homogeneous unit-gap forms

The unit-gap equivalence is valid. The conditioning on the distinguished coefficient \(1\) is correct, and the converse using a maximal pivot weight \(a\) is also correct.

The newly added homogeneous unit-gap form is also valid. Dividing all coefficients by \(h\) transforms (HUG) exactly into (UG), including the strict lower-tail inequality.

Minor precision issue: the proposition itself assumes \(0<p\le1/3\), while the later sentence says equivalence holds for fixed \(p\le1/3\). The case \(p=0\) is trivial, so this is not a substantive problem, but the wording could be made cleaner by saying “for \(0<p\le1/3\), with \(p=0\) trivial.”

### Complement formulation

The complement form
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i\eta_i>q\right]\le q,\qquad q=1-p,
\]
is exactly equivalent to the original weak inequality because
\[
\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p
\iff
\sum_i w_i(1-v_i)\le 1-p.
\]

No issue found.

### Pivot inequality

The two-tail pivot equivalence is valid. The \(a=0\) case recovers the original inequality, and adding a pivot of weight \(a\) recovers the pivot form.

No issue found.

### Convex-hull reformulation

The equivalence is essentially correct. The down-closedness of \(P(\mathcal D)\) via thinning is valid, and the strict-separation argument produces a nonnegative separating vector.

Minor exposition issue: the labels “forward implication” and “conversely” remain slightly confusing, because the first paragraph effectively proves the convex-hull statement implies the Bernoulli inequality by contradiction, while the second proves the reverse implication. This is only a presentation issue, not a mathematical flaw.

### Partition-function form

The partition-function reformulation is correct:
\[
\mu_p(\mathcal D)
=(1+\lambda)^{-m}\sum_{A\in\mathcal D}\lambda^{|A|},
\qquad \lambda=\frac p{1-p}.
\]
Thus \(\mu_p(\mathcal D)\le1-p\) is equivalent to
\[
\sum_{A\in\mathcal D}\lambda^{|A|}
\le (1+\lambda)^{m-1}.
\]

No issue found.

### Intersection-theoretic description

The \(r\)-wise intersection observation is valid. If \(r=\lfloor1/p\rfloor\), no \(r\) members of
\[
\mathcal D_w=\{A:w(A)<p\}
\]
can cover \([m]\), since their union has weight \(<rp\le1\). Hence the complement family is \(r\)-wise intersecting.

No issue found.

### Coalescence identity

The coalescence identity is correct, including the half-open endpoint conventions for the strict event.

The example
\[
p=0.3,\quad q=0.7,\quad w_i=1/4
\]
is arithmetically correct:
\[
\Phi_q(w)=4q^3(1-q)+q^4=0.6517,
\]
while after merging a pair,
\[
q(1-(1-q)^2)=0.637.
\]

The infinite equal-weight obstruction is also correct. For \(1/n<p<1/(n-1)\), the failure set has weight \(<p\) exactly when it is empty or a singleton, and the displayed coalescence difference is negative.

No issue found.

### Remaining open issues

This section accurately states the situation: the full solution depends on Conjecture 1. Its presence is fatal for answer-readiness.
