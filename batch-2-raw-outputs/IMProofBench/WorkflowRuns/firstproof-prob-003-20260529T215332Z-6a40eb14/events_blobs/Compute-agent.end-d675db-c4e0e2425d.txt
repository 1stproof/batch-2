## 1. Literature conclusion

I did not find a known theorem that proves CTC as stated.

The closest exact framework remains Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis, *Optimizing stakes in simultaneous bets*, ALEA 20 (2023), 153--165.  They define
\[
  \pi(p,t)=\sup_{\gamma}\Pr\!\left(\sum_i c_i\beta_i\ge t\right),
\]
over convex combinations of iid Bernoulli(\(p\)) variables.  In complement notation, CTC/original small-\(p\) would follow from the strict hypotenuse theorem
\[
  \pi(q,t)\le q\qquad(q\ge2/3,\ t>q). \tag{FMP-strip}
\]
Indeed, for fixed finite weights \(w_i\) with \(\sum_iw_i=1\), the event
\[
  \sum_iw_i\eta_i>q,\qquad \eta_i\sim\mathrm{Bernoulli}(q),
\]
equals \(\sum_iw_i\eta_i\ge t_*\), where \(t_*\) is the smallest attainable subset sum strictly larger than \(q\).  Thus (FMP-strip) gives \(\Pr(\sum_iw_i\eta_i>q)\le q\), equivalently
\(\Pr(\sum_iw_iv_i\ge p)\ge p\).  The pivot/unit-gap equivalence in the draft then gives CTC.

But FMP does **not** prove (FMP-strip).  Their Theorem 4.4 proves bold play when
\[
  {k\over k+1}<p\le {k+1\over k+2}<t,
\]
and Proposition 4.5 proves diagonal endpoints \(p=t=(k+1)/(k+2)\) for \(k>1\).  The needed region is exactly the leftover near-hypotenuse strip
\[
  q<t\le {k+1\over k+2},\qquad {k\over k+1}<q<{k+1\over k+2}.
\]
FMP explicitly say an array of triangles along the hypotenuse remains open.

Other checked areas:

- Fishburn--Frankl--Freed--Lagarias--Odlyzko intersecting-system results, Frankl/Tokushige/Filmus biased-intersection theorems: useful for reciprocal/endpoints, but not enough above bias \(2/3\) without the extra weighted-threshold structure.
- Pinelis left-tail domination: sharp moment-based left-tail bounds, but does not compare the two CTC tails with factor \(p/q\), and moment-only data are too weak near pivot-plus-dust.
- Bentkus/Dzindzalieta: sharp symmetric/Rademacher comparisons, structurally mismatched because CTC increments are asymmetric \((+qb_i,-pb_i)\).
- Samuels/Feige: adjacent small-deviation extremal problems, but I found no implication to the strict convex-combination Bernoulli hypotenuse.
- Kellerer: still no finite-cube down-set convex-hull or Bernoulli halfspace inequality found.

## 2. Bellman / induction exploration

For
\[
g(y)=q1_{\{y\ge p\}}-p1_{\{y<-q\}},
\]
CTC is \(\mathbb E g(Y)\ge0\).  Removing one coefficient gives the exact identity
\[
D_{R+c}(0)=qD_R(pc)+pD_R(-qc),
\]
in the notation of the prompt.  The existing shifted-HUG induction proves the nonnegative-shift subcritical region, but the supercritical case needs information tying together the two shifted residual values.

I tested the natural Bellman state \((x,R)\), where \(x\) is the current centered sum and \(R\) is remaining total coefficient mass.  This state is too small.  The finite-grid dynamic program in `code/round16_ctc_bellman.py` computes the value of a **stronger adaptive game**, where future coefficient choices may depend on the current random partial sum.  Negative values appear, so an \((x,R)\)-only Bellman proof would prove a false adaptive statement.

Mesh \(1/40\), total mass up to \(10\):

| \(p\) | best adaptive total | adaptive Bellman value at 0 |
|---:|---:|---:|
| \(1/6\) | 0 | 0 |
| \(1/5\) | \(161/40\) | \(-4.9018\cdot10^{-4}\) |
| \(1/4\) | \(121/40\) | \(-0.0231005\) |
| \(3/10\) | \(97/40\) | \(-0.0537881\) |
| \(1/3\) | \(399/40\) | \(-0.0765753\) |

These are **not counterexamples to CTC**: they use adaptive coefficient choices after observing partial sums.  They are useful because they rule out the most naive dynamic-programming proof and explain why a successful Bellman invariant must remember more than current sum and remaining mass, e.g. nonadaptive coefficient data, boundary local time, or a convex/support-function state.

## 3. Nonadaptive computational searches

The MILP from earlier rounds was reused for HUG/CTC.  For fixed \(p,n\), binary variables encode the upper and lower threshold chambers.  The relaxed objective is
\[
  \Pr[S\ge p(B+1)]-\frac pq\Pr[S<p(B+1)-1].
\]
Because the relaxation may undercount the closed upper event and overcount the strict lower event, a nonnegative relaxed optimum certifies no counterexample for that fixed \(p,n\).

All-coefficient search, \(n=7\), no normalization \(b_1=1\):

| \(p\) | relaxed optimum | solver |
|---:|---:|---|
| \(1/6\) | 0 | optimal |
| 0.20 | 0 | optimal |
| 0.25 | 0 | optimal |
| 0.30 | 0 | optimal |
| 0.32 | 0 | optimal |
| \(1/3\) | boundary relaxation negative at eps=0; eps \(10^{-5}\) gives 0 | endpoint already known |

The zero optimum is the trivial all-zero coefficient vector; since the relaxation minimizes a lower bound for the true HUG difference, this still certifies no negative chamber for those \(p,n\).

## 4. Current proof target

The cleanest theorem that would close the draft is still:

> For every \(q\ge2/3\), every convex combination \(T=\sum_i w_i\eta_i\) of iid Bernoulli(\(q\)) variables, and every \(t>q\),  
> \[
>   \Pr(T\ge t)\le q.
> \]

This is precisely the strict FMP near-hypotenuse strip.  Existing FMP/cyclic methods prove adjacent rectangles and reciprocal endpoints; deterministic interval or fixed-size coloring strengthenings fail.  The new Bellman computation suggests that a proof by dynamic programming needs a genuinely nonadaptive state, not just \((x,R)\).
