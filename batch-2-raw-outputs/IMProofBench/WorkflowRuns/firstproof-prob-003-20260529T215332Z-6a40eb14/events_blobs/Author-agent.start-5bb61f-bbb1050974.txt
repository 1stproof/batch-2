My strongest recommendation is to pivot away from a pure \(q\)-flow or polynomial proof and make the largest-weight induction into a **weighted shadow/transport theorem**.

Take a maximal weight \(a<p\), residual weights \(u_i\le a\), \(\sum u_i=1-a\), and \(\lambda=p/q\le1/2\). The induction target is exactly
\[
Z_\lambda(\{A:u(A)\ge p\})\ \ge\ \lambda\, Z_\lambda(\{A:u(A)<p-a\}), \tag{*}
\]
where \(Z_\lambda(\mathcal A)=\sum_{A\in\mathcal A}\lambda^{|A|}\). This is just your pivot inequality with the cap \(u_i\le a\). I would try to prove (*) directly as a fractional Hall/max-flow statement: supply \(\lambda^{|A|+1}\) at each low set \(A\) with \(u(A)<p-a\), capacity \(\lambda^{|B|}\) at each high set \(B\) with \(u(B)\ge p\), and allow monotone edges \(A\subseteq B\). The required inequality follows if this flow exists. The dual Hall cuts should compress to down/up ideals; after shifting, they look very close to your capped support-function certificate. This seems the cleanest finite-dimensional formulation of the missing lemma.

For the down-set route, I would sharpen the capped slice lemma into a transport statement. In the mixed shifted slice
\[
\mathcal D=\mathcal E\cup(\mathcal F+\{n+1\}),\qquad \mathcal F\subseteq\mathcal E,\quad \partial^+\mathcal F\subseteq\mathcal E,
\]
try to prove
\[
\mu_p(\mathcal E)+\lambda\mu_p(\mathcal F)>1
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\exists x\in P(\mathcal F):\ \lambda({\bf1}-x)\in P(\mathcal E).
\]
Equivalently, rule out the dual certificate
\[
\rho^\lambda_{\mathcal E}(c)+\lambda r_{\mathcal F}(c)<\lambda c([n]).
\]
Because the pair is shifted, I expect \(c\) can be assumed nondecreasing. Then prove the inequality first for step functions \(c=1_T\); that case should reduce to a finite shadow/routing statement inside \(T\), and general \(c\) follows by layer-cake. This is the direction I would bet on.

Three angles, in decreasing order of how concrete I can make them.

**(B) Largest-weight induction with a strengthened invariant.** Your shifted-HUG recurrence is
$$F_b(\delta) = q F_{b'}(\delta + pc) + p F_{b'}(\delta - qc), \qquad F_b(\delta) \ge 0\ \text{on}\ [0, qh-pB].$$
The subcritical interval is preserved but doesn't reach the relevant point $\delta=0$ when $B$ is large, which is where the original problem sits. A one-parameter $\delta$-state can't be strengthened: I worked through the two-shift variant $F_b(s_1,s_2)$ with separate upper/lower shifts and found the recurrence moves both shifts by the *same* amount in each branch, so $s_1-s_2$ is a conserved quantity and the "two-shift" collapses to one shift. So a genuinely new invariant has to be *nonlinear in $\delta$* or *vector-valued in a way the recurrence respects*.

Concrete sub-problems worth attacking:

(i) **Convexity of $F_b(\delta)$ in $\delta$.** If $F_b$ were convex on the relevant range, then $qF_{b'}(\delta+pc)+pF_{b'}(\delta-qc) \ge F_{b'}(\delta + (pc \cdot q - qc \cdot p)) = F_{b'}(\delta)$, and the recurrence becomes a contraction. Check numerically for small $b$; this seems false in general but may hold on a sub-region.

(ii) **An invariant that subtracts a "boundary defect" term.** Try $F_b(\delta) \ge -\epsilon \cdot \Pr[\sum b_i \zeta_i \in \text{window near } p(B+1)]$ for some $\epsilon$. The local-time/window probability is exactly the influence quantity that obstructed your threshold-flow attempt — but here it appears with the *right* sign to potentially close the recurrence.

(iii) **Couple to a comparison process.** Replace $\zeta_i \sim \text{Ber}(p)$ by a coupled $\tilde\zeta_i$ that has the same marginal but is constructed via a uniform $U_i$ and the same threshold; analyze the joint two-tail statistic $(F_b(\delta), F_b(\delta'))$ at a *pair* of correlated shifts. The pair tracks more than a scalar can.

**(A) Bootstrap chain via $k$-set couplings.** Your $3\Phi_q-2 \le \Phi_{3q-2}$ extends: for $q \ge (k-1)/k$, the $k$-set "max-overlap" coupling gives
$$k\Phi_q(w) - (k-1) \le \Phi_{kq-(k-1)}(w),$$
pointwise in $w$. From any known $\alpha$, you bootstrap to $q = (\alpha+k-1)/k$. Reciprocals $\alpha = 1/n$ give sequences accumulating at $(k-1)/k$ for each $k\ge 3$.

Honest accounting: bootstrap + closedness of $\mathcal G$ produces a countable point set, never an interval, because the bootstrap *requires* uniform-over-$w$ control at $\alpha$, which is exactly the conjecture at $\alpha$. The hopeful angle is a *base-interval improvement*: prove $\Phi_\alpha(w) \le \alpha$ on a sub-interval $[0,\delta]$ for some $\delta > 0$. This propagates via the 3-set bootstrap to $[2/3, (2+\delta)/3]$, and then multiplicative closure with $1/2$ closes the remaining gaps in $[0,1/3]$. So your full conjecture reduces to the *strictly weaker* "any $\delta > 0$ interval near $\alpha=0$".

This may be tractable directly: near $\alpha = 0$, all weights below $\alpha$ make the event $\{w(A)>\alpha\}$ rare in a controllable way (essentially zero singleton failures), so an explicit Poissonization or moment computation might close a tiny interval.

**(C) Transport via the size-biased identity.** I find this the cleanest reformulation, even though it's "equivalent." From your identity
$$\Pr[W\ge p] - p = \sum_i w_i D_i, \qquad D_i := q\Pr[W^{(i)} \ge p] - p\Pr[W^{(i)} < p - w_i],$$
each $D_i$ is exactly (Piv($w_i$)) for the leave-one-out sum. Pointwise $D_i\ge 0$ is the full conjecture; but $\sum_i w_i D_i \ge 0$ is a strictly weaker *averaged* statement.

For pivot+dust I checked: $D_0 = 0$ at the pivot and $D_i\approx 0$ for dust, so the equality case is exactly the diagonal $D_i\equiv 0$. For non-extremal $w$, you expect $\sum_i w_i D_i > 0$ with deficit-and-surplus structure across coordinates.

Concrete sub-problem: pair coordinates $i,j$ and show $w_i D_i + w_j D_j \ge 0$ for any pair, by a four-tail comparison using the law of $W^{(ij)}$. The coupling between $W^{(i)}$ and $W^{(j)}$ (they differ only on coordinates $i,j$) is tight enough that an explicit inclusion-exclusion may close the pair-wise version, even when the singleton version fails.

The reductions you have achieved—specifically the shifted down-set reduction and the two-tail pivot inequality—are phenomenal. You have stripped away the unstructured geometry and isolated the core obstruction. Here is how I would approach the three routes you asked about.

**1. The Fractional Transport Route on Shifted Complexes (Most Promising)**
Your observation that the capped slice lemma is dual to a fractional transport problem is the key to cracking the convex-hull formulation. 

Because you proved it suffices to check *shifted* down-sets, the inclusion graph between $\mathcal{F}$ and $\mathcal{E}$ is highly rigid. The problem $p\mathbf{1} \in P(\mathcal{D})$ asks if we can choose a mass distribution $\nu$ on $\mathcal{F}$ (with barycenter $x$) and route "slack" mass upwards into $\mathcal{E}$ to satisfy the marginal demands $\lambda(\mathbf{1} - x)$. 

This is a network flow problem on the hypercube's Hasse diagram. The dual obstruction to this flow is exactly your capped support function inequality. However, because $\mathcal{F}$ and $\mathcal{E}$ are shifted, their shadow boundary operators behave as cleanly as possible (via Kruskal-Katona). For shifted complexes, fractional matching theorems (like the Gale-Ryser theorem for shifted bipartite graphs) dictate that a greedy, "water-filling" routing algorithm works. You no longer need to analyze arbitrary convex polytopes; you only need to verify the max-flow/min-cut condition against the shifted degree sequence, which is purely combinatorial.

**2. The Gap-Polynomial Route for the Pivot Inequality**
You correctly noted that your moment majorant $P(X) = X(X-p)(X+q)$ failed globally because the threshold $X=0$ has no gap. Any continuous minorant of the strict indicator must dip negative near $0^+$, ruining the $\mathbb{E}[P(X)] \ge 0$ property.

However, the one-coordinate induction (the pivot inequality) fundamentally changes the geometry. For the residual $Y$, the inequality is:
\[ q \Pr(Y \ge pa) \ge p \Pr(Y < -p(1-a)) \]
The step function to minorize is $S_a(y) = q \mathbf{1}_{y \ge pa} - p \mathbf{1}_{y < -p(1-a)}$. 

Crucially, peeling off the pivot $a$ *creates a macroscopic dead zone* $[-p(1-a), pa]$ of width $p$ where $S_a(y) = 0$. You now have the "runway" to fit a polynomial minorant $f_a(Y)$ tailored to $a$. The gap allows $f_a$ to absorb the necessary bend without requiring an overly negative $y$-intercept. If the residual "dust" variance is large, this polynomial will minorize $S_a(y)$ with positive expectation. If the variance is extremely small, $Y$ concentrates, and standard Berry-Esseen / Gaussian tail asymptotics safely close the gap.

**3. Why Threshold Flow Fails, but a Weight Flow Could Work**
A derivative argument from $q=2/3$ fails because differentiating the threshold moves the boundary. By Russo's formula, the derivative is the total influence, which can be $\Theta(\sqrt{m}) \gg 1$ for dust configurations. $\Phi_q$ is simply not Lipschitz in $q$. 

Instead, define a continuous gradient flow on the *weights* in the simplex, such as $\frac{dw}{dt} = w^2 - (w \cdot w)w$. This flow continuously increases the $\ell_2$ norm of the weights, agglomerating dust and flowing deterministically toward the single-atom dictatorship $(1, 0, \ldots, 0)$. Because pairwise coalescence fails by "overshooting" and causing discrete jumps, a continuous flow avoids this by infinitesimally trading mass. If you can show $\frac{d}{dt} \Phi_q(w(t)) \ge 0$ along this path, the complement inequality is proven. 

**Recommendation:** Lean heavily into the Shifted Transport network flow. It replaces analytic inequalities (which are prone to looseness near the boundary) with exact, discrete Hall-type matching conditions on shifted sets, where extremal set theory is fully on your side.
