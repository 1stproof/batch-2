# Round 1 compute report: small-\(p\) weighted Bernoulli inequality

## Bottom line

I did **not** find a literature source that states the desired finite inequality
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i\,{\rm Bernoulli}(p)_i\ge p\right]\ge p
\qquad(0\le p\le 1/3,\ \sum_iw_i=1,w_i\ge0)
\]
in this form, nor a source whose proved theorem immediately implies it for the whole interval \(0<p<1/3\). I found several close results; the closest is Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis (ALEA 2023), but their proved region leaves exactly the near-diagonal favorable-odds case needed after complementing.

The best route I see is to work with the complement formulation below and try to extend the Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis cyclic-interval argument from their Theorem 4.4/Proposition 4.5 to the strict near-diagonal case \(q>2/3,\ t=q+\epsilon\).

## Exact reduction to look for in the literature

Put \(q=1-p\), and let \(\eta_i=1-{\rm Bernoulli}(p)_i\sim{\rm Bernoulli}(q)\). Then
\[
\sum_i w_i{\rm Bernoulli}(p)_i\ge p
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\sum_i w_i\eta_i\le q.
\]
Thus the desired weak-threshold inequality is equivalent to
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i\eta_i>q\right]\le q
\qquad(q\in[2/3,1]).
\]
For finite weights the event \(>q\) is the same as \(\ge q+\epsilon\) for some \(\epsilon>0\) depending on the weight vector. Therefore any theorem proving
\[
\pi(q,t):=\sup_{\gamma}\Pr(S_\gamma\ge t)\le q
\quad\text{for all }q\ge2/3,\ t>q
\]
would prove the missing lemma.

## Literature checked

### Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis, ALEA 2023

Robbert Fokkink, Ludolf Meester, Christos Pelekis, “Optimizing stakes in simultaneous bets,” ALEA 20 (2023), 153--165. DOI: `10.30757/ALEA.v20-07`. Local files:
`papers/fokkink-meester-pelekis-2023.pdf`,
`papers/fokkink-meester-pelekis-2023.txt`.

They define
\[
\pi(p,t)=\sup_\gamma \Pr(S_\gamma\ge t),\qquad
S_\gamma=\sum_i c_i\beta_i,\quad \sum_i c_i=1,\quad \beta_i\sim{\rm Bernoulli}(p),
\]
on p. 154, eq. (1.1). This is exactly the right extremal upper-tail object for the complement formulation.

Relevant proved statements:

- Theorem 4.4, p. 159: if \(k/(k+1)<p\le (k+1)/(k+2)<t\), then “bold play” is optimal, so \(\pi(p,t)=p\). This proves only thresholds above the next rational endpoint, not all \(t>p\).
- Proposition 4.5, p. 160: if \(p=t=(k+1)/(k+2)\), then bold play is optimal for \(k>1\), with an exceptional \(k=1\) optimizer. This gives diagonal endpoint information, but not the open intervals between endpoints.
- Theorem 6.1 and Proposition 6.2 concern other regions, including \(p<1/(2k+1)<1/(k+1)<t\) and \(t=1/3,p=1/b\); they are adjacent but do not imply the lower-tail inequality here.

Conclusion: this paper is highly relevant and gives the right notation/method, but I do **not** see a complete citation for the desired \(q\ge2/3,\ t>q\) region. In fact their text explicitly says there are remaining triangles near the hypothenuse in the upper-right block.

### Liggett 1977 / EKR statistical application

Thomas M. Liggett, “Extensions of the Erdős--Ko--Rado theorem and a statistical application,” J. Combin. Theory Ser. A 23(1) (1977), 15--21. DOI: `10.1016/0097-3165(77)90075-9`.

This is the “Liggett” result mentioned on MathOverflow. It says, in the statistical application, that if \(X_i\) are independent Bernoulli with parameter \(r\ge1/2\) and \(S\) is a convex combination, then
\[
\Pr(S\ge1/2)\ge r.
\]
The MathOverflow commenter explicitly added the condition \(r\ge1/2\). This result is **not** enough for the present problem: for small \(p\), the Bernoulli parameter is \(p<1/2\), and applying the theorem to complements \(q=1-p\) gives information at threshold \(1/2\), not the required upper bound at threshold \(q\).

### Arieli--Babichenko--Peretz--Young / Feige--Garnett

Itai Arieli, Yakov Babichenko, Ron Peretz, H. Peyton Young, “The Speed of Innovation Diffusion in Social Networks,” Econometrica 88(2) (2020), 569--594. DOI: `10.3982/ECTA17007`. Local files:
`papers/speed-innovation-networks.pdf`,
`papers/speed-innovation-networks.txt`.

Lemma 1, p. 579, proves for iid Bernoulli(\(p\)) variables and nonnegative weights
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i \ell_i c_i\ge p\sum_i\ell_i\right]\ge 0.14\,p.
\]
Appendix A derives this from Feige’s inequality; Theorem A.1 is quoted on p. 589 with Feige’s constant \(\gamma\ge1/13\) and Garnett’s improvement \(\gamma\ge0.14\). This is an adjacent “above the mean” weighted Bernoulli bound, but the constant \(0.14p\) is far too weak for the desired sharp \(p\).

### Kellerer 1963

Hans G. Kellerer, “Zur Existenz analoger Bereiche,” Z. Wahrscheinlichkeitstheorie verw. Gebiete 1 (1963), 240--246. DOI: `10.1007/BF00532495`. Local files:
`papers/kellerer-analogous-areas.pdf`,
`papers/kellerer-analogous-areas.txt`.

Satz 4, p. 245, says that for a finite family of atomless normalized measures and a map \(a\) continuous for all those measures, for every \(\theta\in[0,1]\) there exists an analogous region \(\{a\in G\}\) with all measures equal to \(\theta\). This is a vector-measure/analogous-region result; I found no Bernoulli halfspace tail statement in the paper. I would not cite it as the missing inequality without an additional derivation.

## Independent proof attempts and failures

### Normalized finite form

The normalization in the current draft is correct. If some \(w_i\ge p\), then the event \(\{i\text{ succeeds}\}\) gives probability at least \(p\). Otherwise set
\[
L=1/p,\qquad a_i=w_i/p.
\]
Then \(L\ge3\), \(0\le a_i\le1\), \(\sum_i a_i=L\), and the desired statement becomes
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i a_i\xi_i\ge1\right]\ge1/L,\qquad \xi_i\sim{\rm Bernoulli}(1/L).
\]

### Fokkink-style rational/cyclic route

For rational \(q=a/b\), represent Bernoulli(\(q\)) as membership in a random cyclic interval of length \(a\) in \(\mathbb Z/b\mathbb Z\). Given a random independent allocation of weights to residues, let \(J\) be the number of length-\(a\) cyclic intervals with load \(>a/b\). Then
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i\eta_i>q\right]=\frac1b\,\mathbb E J.
\]
So the desired complement bound is exactly \(\mathbb E J\le a\).

A tempting deterministic strengthening, \(J\le a\) for every load vector when \(a/b\ge2/3\), is false. I found an LP counterexample for \(b=7,a=5\): there are load vectors with six length-5 intervals above \(5/7\). See `code/check_interval_lemma.py` and `data/interval_reduction_check_output.txt`. Therefore this route must use averaging over independent random allocations, as Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis do in Proposition 4.5, rather than a pointwise interval-count lemma.

### Coloring/induction route

For \(p\in(1/(k+1),1/k]\), one can color each index into \(k\) “full” colors with probability \(p\) each plus a leftover color with probability \(r=1-kp\). If the leftover color has weight at most \(r\), then one full color has weight at least \(p\). However this would require
\[
\Pr(W_{\rm leftover}\le r)\ge 1-r,
\]
which is exactly an upper-tail-at-the-mean statement for Bernoulli(\(r\)) and is false in general. So this does not close the interval between reciprocals.

### Hypergraph/downset route

Let \(D=\{A:w(A)<p\}\). For \(p\le1/3\), three sets in \(D\) cannot cover the positive-weight support when each has weight \(<p\). Complements therefore form a 3-wise-intersecting family. But generic biased-measure bounds for 3-wise-intersecting families are not enough; they apply in the wrong bias range after complementing, and the weighted-threshold structure is essential.

### Coupling and moment routes

The size-biased index coupling \(I\sim w\), independent of the \(p\)-random set \(R\), gives \(\Pr(I\in R)=p\), but \(\{I\in R\}\subseteq\{w(R)\ge p\}\) is false. Moment/Paley--Zygmund/Feige-type arguments only give constant multiples of \(p\), consistent with the Feige--Garnett result above, not the sharp \(p\).

Do **not** cite Kellerer, Liggett, or Feige--Garnett as proving the missing lemma. The most promising precise target is:

> Prove or find a reference for: for \(q\ge2/3\), every convex combination \(S_\gamma\) of iid Bernoulli(\(q\)) variables satisfies \(\Pr(S_\gamma>q)\le q\).

If proving it directly, start from the Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis cyclic allocation formula and aim for the averaged bound \(\mathbb E J\le a\) for all rational \(q=a/b\ge2/3\).
