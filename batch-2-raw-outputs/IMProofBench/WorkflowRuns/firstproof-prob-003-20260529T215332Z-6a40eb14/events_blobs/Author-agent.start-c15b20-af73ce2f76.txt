I would prioritize two avenues over further pointwise subharmonic attempts.

**1. Last-exit martingale/local-time proof.**  
Let \(M_k=\sum_{i\le k} b_i(\zeta_i-p)\) and \(I=(-q,p)\). Since upward jumps are \(\le q\) and downward jumps are \(\le p\), a path cannot jump directly from the upper exterior \([p,\infty)\) to the lower exterior \((-\infty,-q)\), or conversely; it must re-enter \(I\). Thus the final side is the side of the **last exit** from \(I\). Writing last-exit probabilities gives
\[
P_R=\sum_k p\,\mathbb E\big[1_{\{M_{k-1}\in[p-qb_k,p)\}}\,R_k(M_{k-1}+qb_k)\big],
\]
\[
P_L=\sum_k q\,\mathbb E\big[1_{\{M_{k-1}\in[-q,-q+pb_k)\}}\,L_k(M_{k-1}-pb_k)\big],
\]
where \(R_k,L_k\) are future “no-return then end right/left” probabilities. In CTC the prefactors become \(qP_R\) and \(pP_L\), so both have coefficient \(pq\); the issue is comparing occupation of an upper strip of width \(qb_k\) with a lower strip of width \(pb_k\). This exactly neutralizes the one-step failure near \(-q\). I would try to prove the needed comparison by optional stopping for the future Dirichlet functions \(R_k,L_k\), or via a discrete Tanaka formula. The relevant kernel is
\[
\mathbb E[(x+b_k(\zeta-p)-t)_+]-(x-t)_+
=
\begin{cases}
q(pb_k+u),& -pb_k<u<0,\\
p(qb_k-u),& 0\le u<qb_k,
\end{cases}
\quad u=t-x.
\]
This may yield a short “boundary local time” proof.

**2. Bellman, but with boundary credit.**  
The scalar \(g\) fails because it charges the lower boundary immediately. Instead define the finite-horizon value
\[
V(B,x)=\inf_{\sum c_i=B,\;0\le c_i\le1}
\mathbb E\,g\!\left(x+\sum c_i(\zeta_i-p)\right).
\]
The subcritical induction proves \(V(B,x)\ge0\) in one face of the \((B,x)\)-plane. I would numerically solve this Bellman recursion and look for a piecewise-linear certificate \(H(B,x)\le V(B,x)\) with \(H(B,0)=0\). The needed extra term should be a triangular local-time credit on the two boundary strips, not a harmonic linear correction.

**3. Convex-hull/partition route via capped support duality.**  
Your capped slice lemma looks very promising. I would attack it through the LP dual
\[
\rho^\lambda_{\mathcal E}(c)
=
\min_{\alpha_i\ge0}
\left\{\lambda\sum_i\alpha_i+
\max_{A\in\mathcal E}\sum_{i\in A}(c_i-\alpha_i)\right\}.
\]
This converts the cap \(x_i\le\lambda\) into penalties \(\alpha_i\). The hope is then to reduce the hypothesis
\[
\rho^\lambda_{\mathcal E}(c)+\lambda r_{\mathcal F}(c)<\lambda c([n])
\]
to a weighted cross-union condition to which Ahlswede–Daykin four-functions or a biased edge-isoperimetric argument applies. This is probably the cleanest combinatorial route to a self-contained proof.

**4. Extremal halfspace reduction.**  
Minimize the CTC deficit over coefficients. Since the objective is chamberwise constant, a strict minimizer should lie on many subset-sum hyperplanes \(Y=p\) or \(Y=-q\), or on \(b_i\in\{0,1\}\). A perturbation/KKT argument may force an extremal counterexample to have only unit coefficients plus at most one fractional block. That residual case reduces to binomial tail ratios and is checkable.

I would pursue (1) and (3) first; they seem most structurally aligned with the exact constants.

Here are several angles I think could close the gap, ordered by how short a proof they might give.

**1. Single-state Bellman/martingale potential is provably impossible — go multi-state.**

A sub-mean function $V:\mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ for the steps $(+qc$ w.p.\ $p$, $-pc$ w.p.\ $q)$, $c\in[0,1]$, must be convex: Taylor expand the submartingale condition at $c=0$ to get $pq c^2 V''(\delta)/2 \ge O(c^3)$, hence $V''\ge0$. But a convex $V$ with $V\le g_0(\delta)=q\mathbf{1}_{\delta\le-p}-p\mathbf{1}_{\delta>q}$ and $V(0)\ge0$ forces $V\equiv0$ on $[-p,q]$ (convex above its tangent at $0$, sandwiched by $\le 0$), and then convexity pushes $V\ge 0$ on $\delta>q$, contradicting $V\le-p$ there. This explains why your subharmonic attempt for $g(y)$ near $-q$ is not fixable by adding a globally harmonic correction.

The natural escape is a **two-state** potential $V(y,v)$ where $v=\sum_{i\le k}b_i^2$ (running variance). The Taylor expansion at $c=0$ now gives $V_{yy}+2V_v\ge0$, i.e. $V$ is a subsolution of the backward heat equation. This is much weaker. A candidate: write $V(y,v)=g_0(y)\ast G_{v_\ast-v}(y)$ (convolution with a Gaussian of the "remaining" variance budget $v_\ast - v$), tweaked so $V(0,0)\ge0$. The Gaussian limit of CTC has slack $q/p$, so there is a positive margin to absorb the smoothing.

**2. Schur-convexity (reduction to binomial).**

Quick check at $p=1/3$: the CTC gap is $2/9$ at $(b_1,b_2)=(1,0)$ and $2/27$ at $(1/2,1/2)$ — i.e., moving toward equal coefficients (down in majorization) decreases the gap. If you can prove $f(b_1,\ldots,b_n)=q\Pr[Y\ge p]-p\Pr[Y<-q]$ is Schur-convex on $[0,1]^n$, the infimum over each "level set" $\sum b_i=B$ is at the most equal configuration, and the global infimum is at all-equal small coefficients — i.e., the Gaussian regime, where the inequality is trivially $q\Phi(-p/\sigma)\ge p\Phi(-q/\sigma)$ (Mills ratio is $\ge q/p>p/q$). Schur-convexity reduces to a Hessian-cone condition that's checkable directly on the two-coefficient slice, and after the Gaussian step you only need a clean finite check for "all $b_i\in\{0,1\}$" (unweighted binomial), where log-concavity of the binomial PMF gives strong margins. This is the route I'd try first for a short proof.

**3. Read León–Perron (2003) directly.**

The paper is already in your bib and its title is literally "Extremal Properties of Sums of Bernoulli Random Variables." Their machinery (sharp two-sided extremal probabilities for $\Pr[\sum c_i\xi_i\ge t]$ given moment constraints) is plausibly close enough to give CTC almost off the shelf, or to reduce it to a low-dimensional optimization. Combined with FMP's bold-play results (which cover neighboring rectangles but explicitly leave the hypotenuse), the published literature is unusually close to your missing case. Worth a careful re-read before manufacturing a new method.

**4. Two-shift inductive invariant.**

Your shifted induction (Prop.\ 7 in the draft) propagates positivity only on $\delta\in[0,qh-pB]$. Peeling a coefficient $c$ generates shifts $\delta+pc$ (stays subcritical) and $\delta-qc$ (can fall through). The "missing" piece is precisely a *mass* bound on $R$ in the strip $[T-h-pc,\,T-h)$. Try inducting on a *paired* invariant
$$F_R(\delta_+,\delta_-)\;=\;q\Pr[R\ge p(B+h)+\delta_+]\;-\;p\Pr[R<p(B+h)+\delta_--h]$$
where $(\delta_+,\delta_-)$ are coupled in a slightly larger region than the diagonal, and choose the coupling so the recursion closes simultaneously on both shifts when one would individually escape. The right "coupling region" is what your capped-replacement lemma is trying to encode in the support-function language — perhaps it is more natural in shift-coordinates than in convex-hull coordinates.

**5. Compressions on the partition-function form.**

The equivalent statement "$p\mathbf{1}\notin P(\mathcal D)\Rightarrow Z_\lambda(\mathcal D)\le(1+\lambda)^{m-1}$" is purely combinatorial. Try Frankl-style $(i,j)$-compressions $A\mapsto A\cup\{i\}\setminus\{j\}$ ($i<j$). These preserve $|A|$ (hence $Z_\lambda$), preserve down-closedness if you compress carefully, and one can hope they preserve $p\mathbf{1}\notin P(\mathcal D)$. After full compression the family is a shifted/left-compressed down-set with very restricted structure (e.g., determined by a few thresholds), and $Z_\lambda$ at the extremals is exactly the dictatorship $(1+\lambda)^{m-1}$.

**6. A long shot: a flip/coupling proof of the size-bias identity.**

Recall $\Pr[W\ge p]-p=\mathbb{E}[(1-W)\mathbf{1}_{W\ge p}]-\mathbb{E}[W\mathbf{1}_{W<p}]$. For $p\le 1/3$ the "outside" weight $1-W\ge 2/3$ on the right tail, while $W<p$ is small on the left. A concrete injection from $\{I\in A,\,W<p\}$ to $\{I\notin A,\,W\ge p\}$ (where $I$ is the size-biased index) — possibly via flipping the index Bernoulli plus a small rearrangement of the rest — would give the result. Your worked counterexamples rule out the naive injection but not a slightly cleverer one that exploits $p\le 1/3$.

**Uncertainty notes.** I have not verified the Schur-convexity (2) past one toy example; it could fail at a non-symmetric configuration. I have not opened the León–Perron paper to confirm (3) actually contains the hypotenuse case. The two-shift invariant in (4) is informed speculation about what your subcritical proof needs. Of the six, I'd put my chips on **(2)+(3) first, (4) second, (1) for the long-form fallback.**

### Council member: gemini-31-pro (models/gemini/gemini-31-pro) ###
You have pushed this to a remarkably clean frontier. The obstruction to a 1D scalar subharmonic proof (like your $g(y)$ test function) is exactly that it forgets "remaining step capacity"—near $-q$, a jump might overshoot the threshold, but the 1D function cannot credit this without knowing the remaining mass. 

Here are three distinct, rigorous avenues I recommend exploring to close the gap, leveraging structures you have already unearthed:

### 1. The Continuous-Time Empirical Martingale
Your random weighted quantile observation $Y(t) = \sum w_i \mathbf{1}_{U_i \le t}$ hides a massive structural advantage: it can be transformed into a classic empirical martingale. 
Specifically, the process 
$$ M_t = \frac{Y(t) - t}{1-t} $$
is a continuous-time martingale on $t \in [0, 1)$ with respect to the standard filtration $\mathcal{F}_t = \sigma(\mathbf{1}_{U_i \le s} : s \le t)$. 
You want to prove $\Pr(M_p \ge 0) \ge p$.

Notice how beautifully this aligns with your thresholds: at $t=p$, the absolute lowest possible value of $M_p$ (if all $U_i > p$) is exactly $-p/(1-p) = -p/q$. This perfectly mirrors the $-q$ lower bound in your (CTC) formulation! By embedding the discrete steps into a sum of independent continuous-time jump martingales, you unlock tools from empirical processes. You can apply optional stopping at $\tau = \inf\{t : M_t \ge 0\}$, reducing the problem to bounding the positive-excursion measure of a mean-zero jump martingale. 

### 2. 2D Bellman / State Augmentation
To fix the failure of $g(y)$ near $-q$, your induction state must track the remaining available step size. You already noted the Homogeneous Unit Gap (HUG) formulation explicitly carries the gap parameter $h$. Instead of searching for a 1D subharmonic function, formulate a 2D Bellman function $V(y, h)$ (or $V(y, B)$). 

Literature parallel: Bentkus (2004) and Pinelis (2016) handle asymmetric left-tail domination exactly this way—they carry the variance or total mass as a second dimension. This allows the value function to be locally smooth and curved in $y$ (fixing the zero-gradient issue), while absorbing the boundary conditions along the $h$ axis. A successful $V$ would satisfy $V(y, 0) \le q \mathbf{1}_{y \ge p} - p \mathbf{1}_{y < -q}$, with the 2D jump condition $q V(y - pc, h - c) + p V(y + qc, h - c) \ge V(y, h)$.

### 3. Hypercube Flow / Fractional Matching
You correctly noted that pure $k$-wise cross-union freeness is too weak because it forgets threshold capacity. However, by LP duality (or max-flow min-cut on the hypercube), your desired bound $q \mu_p(Y \ge p) \ge p \mu_p(Y < -q)$ is exactly equivalent to the existence of a measure-preserving directed flow from the lower-tail configurations to the upper-tail ones. 

Because flipping a variable from $0 \to 1$ increases the sum by $b_i$, the edges of your flow graph intrinsically respect the step capacities. I suggest setting up the dual LP directly on the weighted Hamming graph: construct a fractional path-coupling where the "flow" of an edge flipping $i$ is weighted by $b_i$. This bypasses the MMS-style deterministic constraints and takes advantage of the exact threshold boundary.

All three are viable, but the **continuous-time martingale ($M_t$)** seems most likely to yield the short, self-contained proof you are hunting for, as it smoothly continuous-izes the discrete combinatorial difficulty.
