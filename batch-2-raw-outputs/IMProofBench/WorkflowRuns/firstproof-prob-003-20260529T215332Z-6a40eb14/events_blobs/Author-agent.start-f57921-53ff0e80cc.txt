\title{A weighted Bernoulli mean-tail inequality}
\author{}
\date{}
\maketitle

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I interpret the question in the universal sense: determine those \(p\in[0,1]\)
for which
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^m w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p
\]
holds for every \(m\ge1\) and every non-negative probability vector
\((w_1,\ldots,w_m)\), where the \(v_i\)'s are i.i.d.\
\({\rm Bernoulli}(p)\).

The only possible complete answer is
\[
        [0,1/3]\cup\{1/2,1\}.
\]
The counterexamples excluding all other \(p\)'s are elementary and are proved
below.  The unresolved part of this draft is the sufficiency of the full
interval \(0\le p\le1/3\).  I prove several positive subfamilies in that
interval and then record a precise equivalent convex-hull formulation of the
remaining gap.

\section*{Positive values proved here}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:positive}
The desired inequality holds for \(p=0\), for \(p=1/k\) with every integer
\(k\ge3\), for \(p=2/b\) with every integer \(b\ge6\), for \(p=1/2\), and for
\(p=1\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The cases \(p=0\) and \(p=1\) are immediate.

Let \(p=1/k\), \(k\ge3\).  Independently color each index \(i\) uniformly with
one of the colors \(1,\ldots,k\), and let
\[
       W_j=\sum_{\{i:\,{\rm color}(i)=j\}} w_i .
\]
For each coloring outcome, \(\sum_{j=1}^k W_j=1\), so at least one color has
\(W_j\ge1/k\).  By symmetry,
\[
       \Pr[W_1\ge1/k]\ge \frac1k.
\]
The indicators of membership in color \(1\) are independent
Bernoulli\((1/k)\), giving the desired inequality.

Now let \(p=2/b\), \(b\ge6\).  We need the following elementary fact.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:pairs}
Let \(b\ge6\), and let \(x_1,\ldots,x_b\ge0\) with \(\sum_i x_i=1\).  Then at
least \(b-1\) unordered pairs \(\{i,j\}\) satisfy
\[
       x_i+x_j\ge \frac2b .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Put \(z_i=bx_i/2\).  Then \(0\le z_i\), \(\sum_i z_i=b/2\), and we must show
that at least \(b-1\) pairs have \(z_i+z_j\ge1\).  We prove a slightly stronger
claim by a deletion procedure.

Start with any set \(R\) of \(s\) remaining numbers whose sum is at least
\(s/2\).  If its smallest and largest elements \(u\le a\) satisfy \(u+a\ge1\),
then the largest element forms good pairs with all other \(s-1\) elements, and
we stop.  Otherwise \(u+a<1\).  Let \(h\) be the number of other elements that
form a good pair with \(a\).  If \(h\le(s-2)/2\), then the \(h\) partners of
\(a\) are at most \(a\), while the other \(s-1-h\) elements are all \(<1-a\);
hence
\[
 \sum_{z\in R} z
   < a+ha+(s-1-h)(1-a)
   =s-1-h+a(2h-s+2)\le \frac{s}{2},
\]
because \(a\ge1/2\) and \(2h-s+2\le0\).  This contradicts the assumption
\(\sum_{z\in R}z\ge s/2\).  Thus \(h>(s-2)/2\), so \(h\ge\lfloor s/2\rfloor\).
Count these \(h\) good pairs involving \(a\), delete \(u\) and \(a\), and
continue.  The remaining set has size \(s-2\) and sum
\[
       \sum_{z\in R}z-u-a>\frac{s}{2}-1=\frac{s-2}{2}.
\]

If the procedure never stops before the final one or two elements, it counts at
least
\[
 \left\lfloor\frac b2\right\rfloor+
 \left\lfloor\frac {b-2}2\right\rfloor+
 \left\lfloor\frac {b-4}2\right\rfloor+\cdots
\]
distinct good pairs.  For \(b\ge6\) this sum is at least \(b-1\).  If the
procedure stops earlier, say after \(t\) deletion steps when \(s=b-2t\) elements
remain, then it has already counted
\(\lfloor b/2\rfloor+\cdots+\lfloor (s+2)/2\rfloor\) pairs and then counts
\(s-1\) more.  For \(b\ge6\) this total is also at least \(b-1\): writing
\(b=2r\) or \(b=2r+1\), the difference from \(b-1\) is respectively at least
\(t(2r-t-3)/2\) or \(t(2r-t-3)/2\), which is nonnegative for
\(0\le t\le r-1\) and \(r\ge3\).  Hence in all cases the number of good pairs is
at least \(b-1\).
\end{proof}

Return to \(p=2/b\).  Independently color each index uniformly with one of
\(b\) colors and let \(X_j\) be the total weight of color \(j\).  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:pairs}, for every coloring outcome at least \(b-1\) pairs of
colors have total weight at least \(2/b\).  Therefore, by symmetry,
\[
 \Pr[X_1+X_2\ge 2/b]\ge
 \frac{b-1}{\binom b2}=\frac2b.
\]
The event \(X_1+X_2\ge2/b\) is exactly the event obtained by selecting each
original index independently with probability \(2/b\).  This proves the
\(p=2/b\) cases.

Finally, for \(p=1/2\), put \(X=\sum_i w_i v_i\).  Since \(1-v_i\) has the same
distribution as \(v_i\), the random variables \(X\) and \(1-X\) have the same
distribution.  Hence
\[
 \Pr[X<1/2]=\Pr[X>1/2],
\]
and consequently \(\Pr[X\ge1/2]\ge1/2\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Values that cannot work}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:negative}
The desired universal inequality fails for every
\(p\in(1/3,1/2)\cup(1/2,1)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First suppose \(1/3<p<1/2\).  Take \(m=3\) and
\(w_1=w_2=w_3=1/3\).  Since \(p>1/3\), at least two successes are needed, so
\[
\Pr\!\left[\frac{v_1+v_2+v_3}{3}\ge p\right]
  =3p^2(1-p)+p^3
  =3p^2-2p^3 .
\]
But
\[
  3p^2-2p^3-p=p(1-p)(2p-1)<0
\]
on \(1/3<p<1/2\).

Now suppose \(1/2<p<1\).  Take \(m=2\) and
\(w_1=w_2=1/2\).  The event requires two successes and has probability
\(p^2<p\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Equivalent form of the remaining small-\(p\) lemma}

The missing assertion is the following.

\begin{lemma}[small-\(p\) lemma]\label{lem:missing}
If \(0\le p\le1/3\), \(w_i\ge0\), \(\sum_iw_i=1\), and
\(v_i\stackrel{\rm iid}{\sim}{\rm Bernoulli}(p)\), then
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p .
\]
\end{lemma}

If some \(w_i\ge p\), Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is immediate from the event
\(\{v_i=1\}\).  Otherwise, setting \(a_i=w_i/p\) and \(L=1/p\ge3\), it is
equivalent to
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i a_i \xi_i\ge1\right]\ge \frac1L,
   \qquad
   0\le a_i<1,\quad \sum_i a_i=L,\quad
   \xi_i\stackrel{\rm iid}{\sim}{\rm Bernoulli}(1/L).
\]
The reciprocal proof above is exactly the case \(L\in\mathbb Z\).

A useful exact convex-geometric reformulation is this.  Let \(\mu_p\) denote
\(p\)-biased product measure on \(2^{[m]}\).  Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is
equivalent to the statement that, for every down-set \(\mathcal D\subseteq
2^{[m]}\),
\[
       \mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p
       \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
       (p,\ldots,p)\in
       \operatorname{conv}\{\mathbf 1_A:\,A\in\mathcal D\}.
\]
Indeed, if the convex-hull statement holds and the Bernoulli inequality fails,
then the down-set
\[
       \mathcal D=\{A:\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\}
\]
has \(\mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p\).  A probability distribution on \(\mathcal D\)
with all coordinate marginals \(p\) would give
\[
 p=\sum_i p w_i
   =\mathbb E\sum_{i\in A}w_i
   <p,
\]
a contradiction.  Conversely, if the convex-hull statement fails for a down-set
\(\mathcal D\), separation from the upper set
\((p,\ldots,p)+\mathbb R_{\ge0}^m\) gives nonnegative coefficients \(c_i\), not
all zero, such that
\[
       \sum_{i\in A}c_i < p\sum_i c_i\qquad(A\in\mathcal D).
\]
With \(w_i=c_i/\sum_jc_j\), Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} implies
\(\mu_p(\mathcal D)\le\Pr[\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p]\le1-p\).

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The negative direction is complete.  The positive direction is complete for
\(p=0\), all reciprocal points \(1/k\) with \(k\ge3\), the additional rational
points \(2/b\) with \(b\ge6\), and \(p=1/2,1\).  The essential gap is still
Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} for the non-covered values of \(p\in(0,1/3)\).

This round narrowed the gap to the convex-hull/down-set formulation above and
proved the first non-reciprocal family \(p=2/b\).  The remaining proof must
establish the down-set convex-hull statement, or equivalently rule out a
separating weighted halfspace whose \(p\)-biased lower side has measure
exceeding \(1-p\).  Natural deterministic rational-subset strengthenings are
false: for example, with \(p=3/10\), seven entries \(1/7\) and three entries
\(0\) yield only \(\binom73/\binom{10}3=35/120<3/10\) good \(3\)-subsets, so a
proof must use the full independent Bernoulli mixture rather than only one
uniform subset size.
