I would try to close the lemma through a **largest-pivot + unit-cap tail-ratio lemma**. This looks cleaner than ordinary \(r\)-wise intersection.

Let \(q=1-p\), \(\lambda=p/q\). The auxiliary statement I would target is:

\[
\tag{UG}
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i \zeta_i \ge p(B+1)\right]
\;\ge\;
\lambda\,
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i \zeta_i < p(B+1)-1\right],
\]
for \(0<p\le 1/3\), \(0\le b_i\le1\), \(B=\sum b_i\), and iid
\(\zeta_i\sim\mathrm{Bernoulli}(p)\).

This “unit-gap” form is exactly adapted to the pivot inequality. Indeed, choose
\(a=\max_i w_i<p\), condition on that coordinate, and write \(Y=\sum_{i>0}w_i v_i\). Since all remaining weights are \(\le a\), set \(b_i=w_i/a\). Then \(B=(1-a)/a\), so (UG) becomes

\[
\Pr[Y\ge p]\ge \lambda \Pr[Y<p-a].
\]

Conditioning on the pivot gives

\[
\Pr[a v_0+Y\ge p]
=p\Pr[Y\ge p-a]+q\Pr[Y\ge p]
=p+q\Pr[Y\ge p]-p\Pr[Y<p-a]\ge p.
\]

So (UG) would prove the full small-\(p\) lemma, not just the base interval.

Why (UG) is the right object: define the deficit martingale

\[
R_0=p,\qquad
R_n=p+\sum_i b_i(p-\zeta_i).
\]

Then \(R_n=p(B+1)-\sum b_i\zeta_i\), and (UG) is

\[
\mathbb E\,G(R_n)\ge0,\qquad
G(r)=\mathbf 1_{\{r\le0\}}-\lambda\mathbf 1_{\{r>1\}}.
\]

Each step sends \(r\) to \(r+p b\) with probability \(q\), and to \(r-qb\) with probability \(p\), for some \(b\in[0,1]\). Thus (UG) reduces to a one-dimensional Bellman/barrier problem:

\[
V(r)\le qV(r+pb)+pV(r-qb),\qquad 0\le b\le1,
\]
with terminal minorant \(V\le G\) and \(V(p)\ge0\). The sharpness mechanism “pivot plus dust” corresponds to taking many tiny \(b\)’s, where the martingale remains inside \((0,1)\) and the value tends to \(0\). The obstruction at \(p>1/3\) also appears here: for \(p=.4\), \(b_1=b_2=1\) violates (UG).

I am uncertain of the exact citation, but this is very close to classical Kemperman/Kanter “unit concentration” inequalities for weighted Bernoulli sums. Search specifically for weighted Bernoulli inequalities of the form

\[
\Pr(S\ge \mathbb ES+p)\ge \frac p{1-p}\Pr(S<\mathbb ES-(1-p)),
\quad b_i\le1.
\]

For an FMP-style adaptation, the same deficit process is the cyclic object: at rational \(p=a/b\), its increments are \(+a\,b_i\) and \(-(b-a)b_i\). I would apply the cycle/injection argument to crossings of the strip \((0,1]\), rather than to deterministic interval-load counts.
