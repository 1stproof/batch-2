## 1. Shifted-compression proposition

I found no gap in the mass-swapping proof in `answer.tex`. The key diamond-level accounting is:

For fixed `R` outside `{i,j}`, write `Ri=R union {i}`, `Rj=R union {j}`. The only points of `C_ij D` that can fail to lie in `D` are bad `Ri` with `Rj in D` and `Ri notin D`; in such a bad diamond, `Rj` is absent from `C_ij D`. Move all mass on these bad `Ri` back to `Rj`; let total moved mass be `M`.

Before this move, equal `i`- and `j`-marginals imply total `i`-only mass equals total `j`-only mass. Since the bad `i`-only mass contributes `M` and bad `j`-only mass contributes `0`, the remaining `j`-only mass in non-bad diamonds is at least `M`. Every `j`-only point that remains in `C_ij D` has its `i`-only mate in `D`; otherwise the compression would have removed it. Move any total mass `M` from these remaining `j`-only points to their `i`-only mates. This preserves all coordinates outside `{i,j}` because each swap stays in the same diamond, and it restores the `i,j` marginals.

Edge cases are harmless: if `M=0` there is no second move; partial mass moves are allowed because we are modifying a probability measure; if `p=0`, the same argument degenerates correctly. The proof only needs the contrapositive direction: `p1 in P(C_ij D) => p1 in P(D)`, so repeated compression cannot destroy the obstruction `p1 notin P(D)`.

I also brute-checked this on all down-sets through `n=5`, all pairs `i<j`, and the grid `p in {1/6,1/5,1/4,0.3,1/3}`. There were `0` failures; see `data/round17/compression_sanity.json`.

## 2. Literature search

No theorem I found directly implies the shifted theorem.

Most relevant:

- Frankl--Tokushige, *Weighted multiply intersecting families*, Studia Sci. Math. Hungar. 40 (2003), 287--291, DOI `10.1556/sscmath.40.2003.3.2`. They prove that if `F` is `r`-wise intersecting and `0<w<=(r-1)/r`, then `mu_w(F)<=w`. This exactly explains the reciprocal endpoint cases in the complement/intersection formulation, but not the open strip `w>(r-1)/r`; for arbitrary `r`-wise intersecting families the maximum tends to `1` there.
- Frankl--Kupavskii, *Simple juntas for shifted families*, Discrete Analysis 2020:14, DOI `10.19086/da.14507`, arXiv `1901.03816`. Good shifted-family/junta technology and a multi-family Erdos matching application, but asymptotic/uniform rather than the exact nonuniform partition-function inequality needed here.
- Klivans, *Threshold graphs, shifted complexes, and graphical complexes*, Discrete Math. 307 (2007), 2591--2597, arXiv `math/0703114`. Useful background: shifted complexes are componentwise order ideals; threshold graphs are exactly one-dimensional shifted complexes.
- Edelman--Gvozdeva--Slinko, *Simplicial complexes obtained from qualitative probability orders*, arXiv `1108.3700`. Important warning: threshold complexes are strictly contained in initial-segment complexes, which are strictly contained in shifted complexes.
- Alon--Frankl--Huang--Rodl--Rucinski--Sudakov, *Large matchings in uniform hypergraphs and the conjectures of Erdos and Samuels*, JCTA 119 (2012), 1200--1215, arXiv `1107.1219`. Relevant for fractional matching/cover LP language, but the results are uniform/asymptotic and do not yield the exact nonuniform weighted statement.
- Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis, *Optimizing stakes in simultaneous bets*, ALEA 20 (2023), 153--165, DOI `10.30757/ALEA.v20-07`, remains the closest probability framework, but their proved regions still miss the strict near-hypotenuse strip needed here.

Details and local PDF paths are in `notes/round17_literature.md`.

## 4. Mixed-slice induction

For shifted
\[
\mathcal D=\mathcal E\cup(\mathcal F+(n+1)),\qquad
\mathcal E\supseteq\partial^+\mathcal F,
\]
I tested the mixed implication
\[
p1\in P(\mathcal E),\quad p1\notin P(\mathcal F),\quad
\mu_p(\mathcal E)+\lambda\mu_p(\mathcal F)>1
\Rightarrow p1\in P(\mathcal D).
\]

No counterexample was found through total dimension `n+1<=7`. For total `n+1=7`, the number of shifted mixed cases with score `>1` was:

| p | score `>1` cases | counterexamples | max score | max score among mixed cases with `p1 notin P(D)` |
|---:|---:|---:|---:|---:|
| `1/6` | 18731 | 0 | `7/6` | `1` |
| `1/5` | 18607 | 0 | `6/5` | `1` |
| `1/4` | 19193 | 0 | `5/4` | `1` |
| `3/10` | 23662 | 0 | `13/10` | `1` |
| `1/3` | 22136 | 0 | `4/3` | `1` |

The maximum-score examples have `E` full and `F` the dictatorship in the lower slice; for total `7`, facets of `D` are `123456,123457`, and `D` does contain `p1`.

Useful contrapositive form for `answer.tex`: write
\[
\alpha_{\mathcal A}(c)=\max_{A\in\mathcal A}c(A),\qquad C=c([n]).
\]
Then `p1 notin P(D)` is equivalent to the existence of `c>=0`, `d>=0` such that
\[
\alpha_{\mathcal E}(c)<p(C+d),\qquad
d+\alpha_{\mathcal F}(c)<p(C+d),
\]
or
\[
\alpha_{\mathcal E}(c)<p(C+d),\qquad
\alpha_{\mathcal F}(c)<pC-(1-p)d. \tag{slice-sep}
\]
In the mixed case `p1 in P(E)`, every `c>=0` has `alpha_E(c)>=pC`, so any separator must satisfy
\[
pC\le \alpha_{\mathcal E}(c)<p(C+d),\qquad
\alpha_{\mathcal F}(c)<pC-q d.
\]
Thus the mixed implication is equivalent to proving that no shifted pair
`F subset E`, `E superset partial^+ F` satisfying `(slice-sep)` can have
\[
\mu_p(E)+\lambda\mu_p(F)>1.
\]
This is a sharper slice target than the scalar induction and matches the capped-slice obstruction already isolated in the draft.
