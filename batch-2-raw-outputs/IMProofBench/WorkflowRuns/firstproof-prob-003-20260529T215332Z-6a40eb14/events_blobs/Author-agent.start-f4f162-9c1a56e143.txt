I would try to cross the supercritical line by adding a **boundary-strip state**, not by changing the scalar Bellman. The useful one-step identity is the following.

Work in uncentered HUG. Let \(R\) be the residual sum, all residual coefficients \(\le c\le h\), and set
\[
T=p(B_R+c+h)+\delta .
\]
After exposing a coefficient \(c\),
\[
D:=q\Pr[R+c\zeta\ge T]-p\Pr[R+c\zeta<T-h]
\]
equals
\[
G+pq(U-L),
\]
where
\[
G=q\Pr[R\ge T]-p\Pr[R<T-h-c],
\]
\[
U=\Pr[T-c\le R<T],\qquad
L=\Pr[T-h-c\le R<T-h].
\]
This is just algebra:
the zero branch gives \(G-pL\), the one branch gives \(G+qU\), and the
\(q,p\) mixture gives \(G+pq(U-L)\).

This identity isolates the exact missing object. The subcritical proof uses only
\(G\ge0\). In the supercritical region the possible deficit is precisely the
lower boundary strip \(L\) not matched by the upper strip \(U\). So I would aim to prove the strengthened two-state invariant
\[
\boxed{\quad G\ge pq(L-U)\quad}                                      \tag{*}
\]
for residual sums with max coefficient \(\le c\), in the parameter range generated by the induction. Then the exposed-coordinate induction closes immediately.

There is also a clean discharging interpretation of \((*)\). The strip \(L\) consists of residual configurations for which a zero outcome of the exposed \(c\)-coin falls just below the lower barrier; \(U\) consists of configurations for which a one outcome falls just below the upper barrier. Flip/discharge \(L\)-mass to \(U\)-mass when possible. The unpaired \(L\)-mass is exactly mass that has crossed the enlarged gap \(h+c\), and should be paid for by the surplus term \(G\). This is the rigorous place where the exposure-crossing identity should enter: applied to the interval \([T-h-c,T)\), it gives a local-time count whose terminal contribution is \(L-U\), while the entrance/exit imbalance is \(G/(pq)\).

I would not expose arbitrary coefficients; expose a current maximum. Then after removing \(c\), all remaining coefficients are \(\le c\), so the same strip invariant recurses with a smaller cap. This “max-first” feature is what the scalar Bellman misses.

A second route worth checking is literature under **red-and-black/bold play** rather than only FMP: Dubins–Savage, Ferguson, Maitra–Sudderth. In their notation the needed statement is the hypotenuse superharmonic-majorant
\[
V(c,t)\le q\quad (t>qc,\ q\ge2/3),
\]
but the correct majorant must carry exactly the above boundary-strip/local-time state. If a published “bold play on the hypotenuse” theorem exists, it should imply HUG directly.

I am uncertain whether Kellerer’s 1963 paper contains precisely this finite fractional-cover theorem, but the statement to look for is exactly your partition bound
\[
p{\bf1}\notin P(\mathcal D),\ \lambda\le1/2
\implies Z_\lambda(\mathcal D)\le(1+\lambda)^{n-1}.
\]
If available, that is the clean black-box closure. But absent that, I think the strip identity \((*)\) is the most promising non-equivalent induction target.

**Why one state is structurally hopeless.** Your kernel $\delta\mapsto\delta+pc$ (w.p. $q$) or $\delta-qc$ (w.p. $p$) is mean-zero, so kernel-subharmonic = convex on $\mathbb R$. Any convex $\phi\le F^\emptyset(\delta)=q\mathbf 1_{\delta\le-p}-p\mathbf 1_{\delta>q}$ is $\le 0$ on the middle strip $(-p,q]$ and, by convexity, $\phi(0)\le 0$. Equality forces $\phi\equiv 0$ on the strip, which is then incompatible with the downward jump to $-p$ at $\delta=q$ (any convex extension violates it). A second state is therefore unavoidable.

**Two-state Bellman: what fails, and what I'd try.** Naive piecewise-constant $H(\delta,B)=\alpha(B)\mathbf 1_{\delta\le-p}+\beta(B)\mathbf 1_{\text{mid}}-\gamma(B)\mathbf 1_{\delta>q}$ and the multiplicative ansatz $H(\delta,B)=\beta(B)g(\delta)$ with a fixed concave bump both die at the $\delta=q$ transition: the kernel step there pushes into the right strip with weight $q$ at cost $-p\gamma(B-c)$, and these ansätze can't absorb that cost while keeping $\beta\ge 0$. What might work is a barrier whose *shape itself drifts with $B$*,
$$
H(\delta,B)=u(B)\,\phi\!\bigl(\delta-\theta(B)\bigr),
$$
with $\theta(B)$ chosen so the dip near $\delta=q$ aligns with where extremal $\mathbf b$ actually concentrates mass (the pivot-plus-near-deterministic-dust configuration as $B\to(q/p)^+$). Then $(u,\theta)$ are determined by kernel-subharmonicity at the three jumps. Before betting on an analytic form, I would grid-discretize $\delta$ and run value iteration $H\leftarrow\mathcal T H$ starting from $F^\emptyset$; if $H(0,B)>0$ is even achievable, the numeric solution will suggest the right ansatz. If not, the obstruction will tell you which additional state (e.g.\ $\sum b_i^2$) is needed.

**Discharging on (10).** As stated, $\sum_i\Pr[p-w_i\le S_{i-1}<p]\ge 1$ is just a rephrasing of $\Pr[S_m\ge p]\ge p$—no new content unless you commit to an ordering. With $w_i$ in *decreasing* order it becomes concrete: if $w_1\ge p$ then index 1 contributes $1$ and you're done; otherwise let $i^\ast$ be the first index with $T_{i^\ast-1}=\sum_{j<i^\ast}w_j\ge p-w_{i^\ast}$. For $i\le i^\ast$ the path $S_{i-1}$ is supported below $p$, and the natural discharging is $w_i\mapsto w_i\cdot f_{i-1}(p^-)$ for the mass function $f_{i-1}$ of $S_{i-1}$. The induction one would want is: peeling $w_1$, the residual problem on $(w_2,\ldots)$ inherits the bound *plus* a contribution from $w_1$'s danger zone $[p-w_1,p)$. I have not verified this closes, but it is the cleanest induction the identity actually supports.

**Strongest literature angle: extend FMP's competitor argument.** Your complement statement is exactly the hypotenuse $t=q$ case of Fokkink–Meester–Pelekis. Their Proposition 4.5 settles $q=t=(k+1)/(k+2)$ by an explicit injection of bad configurations into good ones, and Theorem 4.4 settles adjacent rectangles. Crucially, *your* event is the strict $\{X>q\}$: atoms at $q$ are excluded, and atoms at $q$ are exactly what forces FMP's lattice constraint. I would dissect their injection and check whether it adapts off-lattice to the open hypotenuse strips $q\in\bigl(k/(k+1),(k+1)/(k+2)\bigr)$ once the atoms-at-$q$ pathology is gone. This is the most concrete piece of existing technology that points at your supercritical regime, and I'd put it first in line.

**Speculative: Pinelis (2016) plus a sharp lower bound.** Pinelis' optimal Poisson/binomial left-tail domination gives $\Pr[Y<p-a]$ in terms of $\mu=EY$ and $\sum a_i^2$ for $Y=\sum a_i\xi_i$, $\xi_i\sim\text{Bernoulli}(1/L)$. For the other side, a sharp lower bound on $\Pr[Y\ge p]$ at the mean—e.g.\ from a third-moment Berry–Esseen, where for $p<1/2$ the right-skewness *helps* (lower tail smaller than $1/2-O(n^{-1/2})$ for many small weights, supporting the $q/p$ ratio)—might let you close the comparison $q\Pr[Y\ge p]\ge p\Pr[Y<p-a]$ by moment matching, with no Bellman. Speculative, but it uses exactly the moments your problem provides, and the asymmetry $q>p$ is built into the skewness sign.

**A subsidiary suggestion.** Numerically, $F^{\mathbf b}(0)$ on the supercritical side seems to bottom out near $\mathbf b=(1,1,1)$ for $p=1/3$ (value $2/27$), with most other configurations comfortably higher. If the worst case really is a small all-ones block plus dust below a "sub-pivot" threshold (you can check this by enumerating extreme points of the $\{b_i\le 1,\sum b_i=B\}$ polytope and noting that $F^{\mathbf b}$ is piecewise polynomial), then the proof might decompose into: (i) explicit binomial-tail verification for small all-ones blocks, (ii) Gaussian/CLT control for spread configurations, (iii) interpolation. This is a "tail estimate + finite check" strategy that several sharp Bernoulli inequalities have yielded to.

Here are three concrete, rigorous routes to cross the supercritical wall, based directly on the mechanics you have uncovered.

**1. Rescue Block Coalescence (The Equal-Weight Traps are a Mirage)**
You abandoned the pairwise coalescence induction because of the equal-weight traps. But look closely at the value of those traps! In your $n=4, p=0.3, q=0.7$ example, $\Phi_q(w) = 0.6517 < 0.7 = q$. 

In fact, for *all* $n \ge 4$ and $p \in (1/n, 1/(n-1))$, the equal-weight functional is $\Phi_q = q^n + n(1-q)q^{n-1}$. If you maximize this over $q \in [\frac{n-2}{n-1}, \frac{n-1}{n}]$, the derivative is strictly positive, so it peaks at the right endpoint, yielding exactly $(\frac{n-1}{n})^{n-2} \frac{2n-1}{n}$. This is **strictly less than $1$** for all $n \ge 4$. 

Thus, $\Phi_q(w) < q$ for *all* these "traps." They are not counterexamples to the theorem; they only show that coalescence can decrease the functional deep inside the already-safe subcritical regime. 
**Actionable route:** Proceed by minimal counterexample. Assume there exists $w$ with $\Phi_q(w) > q$. Because you are separated from the equal-weight traps, it is highly likely that *any* strictly supercritical configuration contains at least one pair (or small block) with $\Delta \ge 0$. You only need positive coalescence gain when $\Phi_q > q$.

**2. Bridge the HUG Deficit via CDF Log-Concavity**
In your subcritical shifted homogeneous unit-gap (HUG) induction, peeling a coefficient $c$ leaves a missing comparison—exactly the probability mass in the boundary strip $[T-h-pc, T-h)$. 

This is fundamentally a discrete hazard rate problem. You need to bound the mass of a strip of width $pc$ by a fraction of the lower tail $\Pr(R < T-h)$. Because $R$ is a sum of independent weighted Bernoullis, its PMF is ultra-log-concave, which guarantees that its Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) is strictly log-concave. A purely spatial scalar Bellman function fails because it ignores the residual distribution's shape, but log-concavity of the CDF tightly restricts how much mass can hide in that local boundary strip relative to the entire tail. Applying this structural property should exactly bridge the gap in your one-coordinate induction.

**3. Discharging via Size-Biased Hypercube Flow**
Your size-biased identity gives $\Pr(W \ge p) - p = \mathbb E[(1-W)1_{W \ge p}] - \mathbb E[W 1_{W < p}]$. Proving this is $\ge 0$ is equivalent to routing "supply" from the down-set $L = \{W < p\}$ to "capacity" in the up-set $R = \{W \ge p\}$. 

Because $p \le 1/3$, any state in $L$ has at least $1-p \ge 2/3$ of its weight strictly on zeros. This grants massive upward topological capacity! You can define a flow that moves mass upward by flipping zeros to ones. 
**Actionable route:** You don't need a deterministic fractional cover (which you rightly noted fails for $a/b = 3/10$); you need a randomized matching that respects the Bernoulli measure $\mu_p$. Your Round 10 exponential-clock representation (the uniform random permutation crossing time $K_p$) is exactly the canonical path ensemble for this flow. FMP used a discrete injection for their reciprocal boundary points; constructing a martingale along the uniform permutation paths generalizes their injection to the entire hypotenuse strip.
