I would try to close the lemma through a **largest-pivot + unit-cap tail-ratio lemma**. This looks cleaner than ordinary \(r\)-wise intersection.

Let \(q=1-p\), \(\lambda=p/q\). The auxiliary statement I would target is:

\[
\tag{UG}
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i \zeta_i \ge p(B+1)\right]
\;\ge\;
\lambda\,
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i b_i \zeta_i < p(B+1)-1\right],
\]
for \(0<p\le 1/3\), \(0\le b_i\le1\), \(B=\sum b_i\), and iid
\(\zeta_i\sim\mathrm{Bernoulli}(p)\).

This “unit-gap” form is exactly adapted to the pivot inequality. Indeed, choose
\(a=\max_i w_i<p\), condition on that coordinate, and write \(Y=\sum_{i>0}w_i v_i\). Since all remaining weights are \(\le a\), set \(b_i=w_i/a\). Then \(B=(1-a)/a\), so (UG) becomes

\[
\Pr[Y\ge p]\ge \lambda \Pr[Y<p-a].
\]

Conditioning on the pivot gives

\[
\Pr[a v_0+Y\ge p]
=p\Pr[Y\ge p-a]+q\Pr[Y\ge p]
=p+q\Pr[Y\ge p]-p\Pr[Y<p-a]\ge p.
\]

So (UG) would prove the full small-\(p\) lemma, not just the base interval.

Why (UG) is the right object: define the deficit martingale

\[
R_0=p,\qquad
R_n=p+\sum_i b_i(p-\zeta_i).
\]

Then \(R_n=p(B+1)-\sum b_i\zeta_i\), and (UG) is

\[
\mathbb E\,G(R_n)\ge0,\qquad
G(r)=\mathbf 1_{\{r\le0\}}-\lambda\mathbf 1_{\{r>1\}}.
\]

Each step sends \(r\) to \(r+p b\) with probability \(q\), and to \(r-qb\) with probability \(p\), for some \(b\in[0,1]\). Thus (UG) reduces to a one-dimensional Bellman/barrier problem:

\[
V(r)\le qV(r+pb)+pV(r-qb),\qquad 0\le b\le1,
\]
with terminal minorant \(V\le G\) and \(V(p)\ge0\). The sharpness mechanism “pivot plus dust” corresponds to taking many tiny \(b\)’s, where the martingale remains inside \((0,1)\) and the value tends to \(0\). The obstruction at \(p>1/3\) also appears here: for \(p=.4\), \(b_1=b_2=1\) violates (UG).

I am uncertain of the exact citation, but this is very close to classical Kemperman/Kanter “unit concentration” inequalities for weighted Bernoulli sums. Search specifically for weighted Bernoulli inequalities of the form

\[
\Pr(S\ge \mathbb ES+p)\ge \frac p{1-p}\Pr(S<\mathbb ES-(1-p)),
\quad b_i\le1.
\]

For an FMP-style adaptation, the same deficit process is the cyclic object: at rational \(p=a/b\), its increments are \(+a\,b_i\) and \(-(b-a)b_i\). I would apply the cycle/injection argument to crossings of the strip \((0,1]\), rather than to deterministic interval-load counts.

Pivot inequality (2) is structurally the right inductive target; the 4-tail obstruction you flagged in Round 5 is real but the recursion has a clean 2-parameter strengthening worth pushing, plus two refs in your own bibliography that may shortcut the whole thing.

**Strengthened pivot family.** For $u \ge 0$ with $\sum u_i = c$ and parameters $(t,a)$, define
$$\Psi(u;c,t,a) \;=\; (1-p)\Pr[Y\ge t]-p\Pr[Y<t-a],\qquad Y=\sum u_i v_i.$$
The peel recursion has the same gap $a$ at both children:
$$\Psi(Z+xv;c,t,a)=(1-p)\Psi(Z;c-x,t,a)+p\Psi(Z;c-x,t-x,a).$$
The lemma is $\Psi(u;1-a,p,a)\ge0$ for $a\in[0,p]$. Base case $u=0$: $\Psi(0;0,t,a)=(1-p)\mathbf 1_{t\le0}-p\mathbf 1_{t>a}$ is $\ge0$ exactly when $t\le a$. The shift transform lowers $t$ in lockstep with $c$; the no-shift transform doesn't, so a uniform induction over weights fails at the no-shift branch when $t>a$ but $c=0$.

Linear lower bounds $\alpha c+\beta t+\gamma$ don't close (the no-shift constraint forces $\alpha+p\beta\le 0$, but matching the initial point forces $\alpha\ge p/(1-a)>0$). The fix I'd push is a strengthened invariant
$$\Psi(u;c,t,a)\ge f(c,t,a)+g(c,t,a)\cdot M(u),$$
where $M(u)$ is a feature of $u$ that's monotone under peeling — natural candidates are $\max_i u_i$, $\sum u_i\mathbf 1_{u_i>t-a}$, or $\sum u_i^2$. The intuition: the no-shift branch only causes trouble when $u$ has a large remaining weight (otherwise $Y$ concentrates and $\Pr[Y\ge t]$ is large), and tracking that explicitly compensates. The pivot+dust extremal has $\max_i u_i\to1-a$, $\sum u_i^2\to(1-a)^2$, both attaining their max as $\Psi\to0$.

The two-tail pivot inequality $\Phi_a(Z) = \Pr[Z \ge p] - \frac{p}{1-p} \Pr[Z < p-a] \ge 0$ is your most promising path. It cleanly isolates the residual variables from the pivot. Your empirical observation that "pivot + dust" is the near-equality mechanism is profoundly correct, and Large Deviations Theory explains exactly why it does not violate the bound.

For "dust" (a sum of many small weights), $Z$ concentrates tightly around its mean $\mu = p(1-a)$. Both $p$ and $p-a$ are in the tails, so both probabilities in $\Phi_a(Z)$ vanish. However, their exponential rates of decay differ drastically. Expanding the relative entropy $D(x \| p)$ near $p$, the rate function for the lower threshold $p-a$ is approximately $\frac{(1-p)a^2}{2p}$, while the rate for the upper threshold $p$ is $\frac{pa^2}{2(1-p)}$. 

The ratio of these rate functions is roughly $\left(\frac{1-p}{p}\right)^2$. Since $p \le 1/3$, the lower tail decays **at least 4 times faster** in the exponent than the upper tail! Thus, for dust, the negative term $\Pr[Z < p-a]$ is exponentially eclipsed by $\Pr[Z \ge p]$. $\Phi_a(Z)$ approaches $0$ safely from above. 

To formalize this for finite weights, I recommend casting the pivot inequality as a **biased isoperimetric/expansion problem on the Boolean hypercube**. 
Let $\mathcal{D} = \{A : u(A) < p-a\}$ and $\mathcal{U} = \{B : u(B) \ge p\}$. You need to prove $\mu_p(\mathcal{U}) \ge \frac{p}{1-p}\mu_p(\mathcal{D})$. 

Notice that for any $A \in \mathcal{D}$, the available unselected weight is $1 - a - u(A) > 1 - p$. Because $p \le 1/3$, this unselected reservoir is at least $2/3$ — a massive amount of weight available to push $A$ up into $\mathcal{U}$. 

By Max-Flow Min-Cut, your inequality holds if and only if there exists a fractional upward flow in the hypercube from $\mathcal{D}$ to $\mathcal{U}$, where each $A \in \mathcal{D}$ sources $\frac{p}{1-p} \mu_p(A)$ flow, and each $B \in \mathcal{U}$ sinks at most $\mu_p(B)$. 

Here are three adjacent tools to build this flow/expansion:
1. **$p$-biased Kruskal-Katona / Isoperimetry:** Ahlswede, Aydinian, and Khachatrian (1998) established exact isoperimetric theorems for $p$-biased measures, proving that "Hamming balls" minimize the upper shadow for a given lower shadow. Since your sets are weighted Hamming balls (and limit to true Hamming balls in the dust case), you can likely adapt their fractional shifting techniques.
2. **Canonical Paths / Upward Random Walks:** You can construct the flow explicitly. Consider an upward random walk from $A \in \mathcal{D}$ that adds unselected items proportional to their weights. This naturally transports the measure $\mu_p$ upward and bypasses the pointwise non-monotonicity of your pairwise merge checks.
3. **Holley’s Inequality:** This is the standard tool for proving global inequalities between upper and lower subsets in a lattice.

The fractional hypercube flow described above is the exact continuous generalization of their injection argument.
