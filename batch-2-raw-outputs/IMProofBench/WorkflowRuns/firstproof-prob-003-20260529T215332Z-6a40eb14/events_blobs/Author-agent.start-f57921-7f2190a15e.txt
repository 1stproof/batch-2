# Round 2 compute report: small-\(p\) lemma

## 1. Pair-counting lemma for \(p=2/b\)

I checked the new Lemma `lem:pairs` in `answer.tex`. I do not see a flaw. The deletion argument is sound:

- In a remaining set \(R\) of size \(s\) and sum at least \(s/2\), if min \(u\) and max \(a\) have \(u+a\ge 1\), then the max forms \(s-1\) good pairs and the process can stop.
- If \(u+a<1\), then \(a\ge 1/2\); otherwise all entries are \(<1/2\), contradicting the sum condition. If \(h\) is the number of partners of \(a\), the displayed estimate indeed forces \(h>(s-2)/2\), hence \(h\ge\lfloor s/2\rfloor\).
- Deleting \(u,a\) preserves the invariant since the remaining sum is \(>s/2-1=(s-2)/2\).

The only simplification I would suggest is replacing the final bookkeeping paragraph by the explicit formula
\[
T_t=\sum_{i=0}^{t-1}\left\lfloor {b-2i\over 2}\right\rfloor+(b-2t-1)
\]
for the number counted if the procedure stops after \(t\) deletions. For \(b=2r\),
\[
T_t-(b-1)= {t(2r-t-3)\over2},
\]
and for \(b=2r+1\),
\[
T_t-(b-1)= {t(2r-t-3)\over2}.
\]
This is nonnegative in the stated ranges. If it never stops before the last one/two entries, the sum of the \(\lfloor s/2\rfloor\)'s is already at least \(b-1\) for \(b\ge6\). The strict inequalities in the proof are harmless because the invariant needed for the next step is only \(\ge\).

## 2. Literature search

I did not find an exact published theorem implying
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i{\rm Bernoulli}(p)_i\ge p\right]\ge p,\qquad 0\le p\le 1/3,
\]
or equivalently \(\Pr[\sum_iw_i{\rm Bernoulli}(q)_i>q]\le q\) for \(q\ge2/3\).

Closest sources checked:

1. Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis, “Optimizing stakes in simultaneous bets,” ALEA 20 (2023), 153--165. Local: `papers/fokkink-meester-pelekis-2023.pdf/.txt`.
   They define \(\pi(p,t)=\sup_\gamma \Pr(S_\gamma\ge t)\) on p.154, (1.1), exactly the complement extremal object. Theorem 4.4, p.159, proves bold play if
   \[
   {k\over k+1}<p\le {k+1\over k+2}<t.
   \]
   Proposition 4.5, p.160, proves the diagonal endpoint \(p=t=(k+1)/(k+2)\) for \(k>1\), with an exceptional \(k=1\) optimizer at the non-strict threshold. Proposition 4.6 proves one nearby point. But p.161 says “an array of triangles along the hypothenuse remains to be filled in.” Our needed region is exactly \(q\ge2/3,\ t>q\), including those near-diagonal triangles. So this paper does not close the lemma.

2. Csóka, “Limit theory of discrete mathematics problems,” arXiv:1505.06984. Local: `papers/csoka-2015-limit-theory.pdf/.txt`.
   Problem 4.3 defines the upper-tail limit problem \(K(p,d)\) for \(\Pr(\sum a_i x_i>d)\). Problems 4.5 and 4.8 discuss near-diagonal/compact variants. These are conjectural frameworks, not a theorem giving the desired \(q\ge2/3,t>q\) bound.

3. Alpern--Fokkink--Kikuta, “On Ruckle’s conjecture on accumulation games,” SIAM J. Control Optim. 48(8), 5073--5083 (2010). Local: `papers/alpern-fokkink-kikuta-2010-ruckle.pdf/.txt`.
   This connects deterministic fixed-size subset tails with Hoeffding-type tail problems. Theorem 23 is Hoeffding--Shrikhande for \(s=2\); Theorem 24 quotes Samuels for i.i.d. nonnegative variables under \(\max\{4sh/n,(s-1)sh/n\}<1\). These are not the Bernoulli convex-combination theorem needed here.

4. Alon--Huang--Sudakov, “Nonnegative \(k\)-sums, fractional covers, and probability of small deviations,” JCTB 102 (2012), 784--796. Local: `papers/alon-huang-sudakov-2012-nonnegative-ksums.pdf/.txt`.
   Lemma 3.2 is a Feige/He--Zhang--Zhang small-deviation bound:
   \[
   \Pr\left[\sum_{i=1}^m X_i<m+\delta\right]\ge \min\{\delta/(1+\delta),1/13\}
   \]
   for independent nonnegative \(X_i\) with expectations at most 1. This is much too coarse and in the wrong form for the sharp \(p\) bound.

5. Alon--Frankl--Huang--Rödl--Ruciński--Sudakov, “Large matchings in uniform hypergraphs and the conjectures of Erdős and Samuels,” JCTA 119 (2012), 1200--1215. Local: `papers/alon-frankl-huang-rodl-rucinski-sudakov-2012-large-matchings.pdf/.txt`.
   Section 2 states Samuels’ conjecture and its \(l\le4\) cases; Corollary 2.1 concerns fractional matching extremals for \(l=3,4\). Again close in theme, but it does not imply the weighted Bernoulli mean-tail inequality.

Conclusion: I would not cite Kellerer, Samuels/Feige, Ruckle, or Fokkink--Meester--Pelekis as proving the missing lemma. FMP remains the most relevant roadmap.

## 3. Convex-hull/downset proof attempts

For sections \(D_0,D_1\) at the last coordinate, with \(\alpha=\mu_p(D_0)\), \(\beta=\mu_p(D_1)\),
\[
\mu_p(D)=(1-p)\alpha+p\beta.
\]
If \(\beta>1-p\), induction works cleanly: by induction, \(p{\bf1}\in\operatorname{conv}(D_1)\), hence also in \(\operatorname{conv}(D_0)\), and mixing the two section distributions with probabilities \(p,1-p\) gives all marginals \(p\).

The hard case is \(\beta\le1-p\). The natural move is to put the last coordinate in with probability \(p\) using the empty set in \(D_1\), and require
\[
{p\over1-p}{\bf1}\in\operatorname{conv}(D_0).
\]
The measure information on \(D_0\) does not imply this. Example: on three coordinates, \(D_0=\{A:|A|\le1\}\) has \(\mu_{1/3}(D_0)=20/27>2/3\), but \((1/2,1/2,1/2)\notin\operatorname{conv}(D_0)\), since every point in the convex hull has coordinate sum at most 1. So the naive section induction fails exactly where one wants to bootstrap \(p=1/3\).

The random-quantile formulation remains attractive but I found no closing stochastic order. If \(U_i\) are iid uniform and \(K\) is the first position in a random permutation whose cumulative weight reaches \(p\), then
\[
\Pr\!\left[\sum_iw_i v_i\ge p\right]=\mathbb E\,\Pr[{\rm Bin}(m,p)\ge K\mid K].
\]
The minimizers in numerical searches are near one large atom, but I did not find a proof that \(K\) is extremized by that case.

## 4. Rational/cyclic experiments

New script: `code/round2_cyclic_convex_experiments.py`. Output: `data/round2_cyclic_convex_experiments.txt`.

The deterministic cyclic interval strengthening
\[
\#\{j:\ell([j,j+a))\ge a/b\}\ge a
\]
is false for many \(a/b\le1/3\). First failure found:
\[
b=7,\ a=2,\quad \ell\approx(2/7,0,2/7,0,2/7,0,1/7),
\]
for which only one cyclic length-2 interval is strictly above \(2/7\) after a small perturbation. Thus the \(p=2/b\) proof genuinely uses all unordered pairs, not just cyclic adjacent pairs.

The deterministic all-\(a\)-subsets strengthening is also false, as already noted in `answer.tex`: for \(p=3/10\), seven loads \(1/7\) and three loads \(0\) give
\[
35/120=0.291666\ldots <0.3
\]
good 3-subsets.

Exact Bernoulli-mixture values for the same and nearby weights are safely above \(p\):

| \(p\) | weights | exact tail |
|---:|---|---:|
| \(2/7\) | \(2/7,2/7,2/7,1/7\) | 0.635568513 |
| \(3/10\) | seven \(1/7\)'s | 0.352930500 |
| \(3/10\) | four \(1/4\)'s | 0.348300000 |
| \(4/13\) | four \(1/4\)'s | 0.361892091 |
| \(3/11\) | eight \(1/8\)'s | 0.378756078 |

Heuristic minimization for \(p=1/4,2/7,3/10,1/3\) and supports \(n\le8\) again found minima numerically equal to \(p\), approached by concentrated weights. This optimizer is not reliable across discontinuity surfaces: for \(p=0.34\), the explicit equal-three-weights counterexample gives tail \(0.268192<0.34\), while the smooth search often gets stuck at concentrated local minima.

Brute-force downset verification for \(m\le5\) found no convex-hull counterexamples for \(p=1/4,2/7,3/10,1/3\). For \(p=0.34\), it immediately finds the expected counterexamples from the equal-3-weight obstruction.
