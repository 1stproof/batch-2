### Paragraph-by-paragraph mathematical audit

#### Problem statement and interpretation

The document adopts the universal interpretation: determine those \(p\in[0,1]\) for which the inequality holds for every finite nonnegative probability vector \(w\). This is a reasonable interpretation and is explicitly recorded.

However, the same paragraph says:

> “The remaining unresolved assertion is the sufficiency of the full interval \(0\le p\le1/3\).”

This alone makes the document incomplete as a solution to “For which values of \(p\)?” The answer does not establish the claimed full set.

#### Proposition: positive values

The cases \(p=0\) and \(p=1\) are correctly described as immediate.

The coloring proof for \(p=1/k\), \(k\ge3\), is valid. For each coloring, at least one color class has weight at least \(1/k\); averaging over colors gives  
\[
\Pr[W_1\ge1/k]\ge 1/k.
\]
Color membership in a fixed color is indeed independent Bernoulli\((1/k)\), so this proves the desired inequality for these reciprocal points.

#### Pair lemma for \(p=2/b\)

The deterministic pair lemma appears essentially correct. The scaling \(z_i=bx_i/2\) is appropriate, and the deletion/counting argument is plausible and, after filling in minor endpoint details, works.

Minor presentation issues:

- The stopping/counting argument uses an ellipsis in the finite sum. This is not fatal, but a polished proof should state explicitly what the last term is in the even and odd cases.
- The proof says “if the procedure never stops before the final one or two elements”; formally, the algorithm should specify what happens when \(s=1\) or \(s=2\).
- The computation of the difference from \(b-1\) is stated uniformly for \(b=2r\) and \(b=2r+1\); it is correct, but a strict proof should spell out the two cases or define the relevant range of \(t\) more carefully.

These are not the main obstruction. I did not find a counterexample to the pair lemma, and numerical sanity checks for small \(b\) were consistent with the stated bound.

The subsequent coloring argument for \(p=2/b\) is valid: by symmetry, the probability that the fixed pair of colors \(\{1,2\}\) is good is at least
\[
\frac{b-1}{\binom b2}=\frac2b,
\]
and membership in colors \(1\) or \(2\) gives independent Bernoulli\((2/b)\) selection.

#### Case \(p=1/2\)

The symmetry proof is correct. For \(p=1/2\), \(v_i\) and \(1-v_i\) have the same law, so \(X\) and \(1-X\) have the same law. Hence
\[
\Pr[X<1/2]=\Pr[X>1/2],
\]
and therefore \(\Pr[X\ge1/2]\ge1/2\), including the possible atom at \(1/2\).

#### Multiplicative closure

The closure proposition
\[
p,q\in\mathcal G\implies pq\in\mathcal G
\]
is correct. The construction \(v_i=x_i y_i\) gives independent Bernoulli\((pq)\) variables. Conditional on \(T=\sum_i w_i x_i\ge p\), the normalized weights \(w_i x_i/T\) form a probability vector, and the \(q\)-inequality applies. This proves the closure statement.

No serious gap found here.

#### Corollary reducing to \([1/6,1/3]\)

The corollary is correct as a conditional reduction. For every \(0<p\le1/3\), one can choose \(n\) such that \(2^n p\in[1/6,1/3]\), and then use multiplicative closure with \(1/2\).

But this is only conditional. The base interval \([1/6,1/3]\) is not proved.

#### Negative examples

The exclusion of \(p\in(1/3,1/2)\) using \(m=3\), equal weights, is correct:
\[
\Pr[\mathrm{Bin}(3,p)\ge2]=3p^2-2p^3,
\]
and
\[
3p^2-2p^3-p=p(1-p)(2p-1)<0
\]
for \(1/3<p<1/2\).

The exclusion of \(p\in(1/2,1)\) using \(m=2\), equal weights, is also correct: the event requires two successes and has probability \(p^2<p\).

Thus the negative direction is complete for the proposed classification.

#### Small-\(p\) lemma

This is the fatal missing part. The lemma

\[
0\le p\le1/3 \implies 
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p
\]

is exactly the remaining sufficiency statement for the interval \([0,1/3]\). It is **not proved**.

The normalization \(a_i=w_i/p\), \(L=1/p\), is correct for \(p>0\) and under the assumption \(w_i<p\). The text should explicitly exclude \(p=0\) before dividing by \(p\), though \(p=0\) is trivial. This is a minor clarity issue, not the main gap.

The statement that the reciprocal coloring proof covers integral \(L\) is correct, but nonintegral \(L\ge3\) remains open in the submission.

#### Convex-hull reformulation

The convex-hull equivalence is mostly correct.

Forward direction: if the convex-hull statement holds and the Bernoulli inequality fails, the down-set
\[
\mathcal D=\{A:\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\}
\]
has \(\mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p\), and a distribution on \(\mathcal D\) with coordinate marginals \(p\) gives the contradiction
\[
p=\sum_i p w_i=\mathbb E w(A)<p.
\]
This is valid.

Reverse direction: the proof that \(P(\mathcal D)\) is coordinatewise down-closed is valid via thinning. The separation argument is also essentially valid: because \(P(\mathcal D)\) is compact and disjoint from the closed upper orthant \((p,\ldots,p)+\mathbb R_{\ge0}^m\), strong separation gives a nonzero separating vector. The argument that the vector must be nonnegative is correct.

Minor issues:

- The phrase “assume the convex-hull statement fails for a down-set \(\mathcal D\)” should explicitly include both assumptions:
  \[
  \mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p
  \quad\text{and}\quad
  p\mathbf 1\notin P(\mathcal D).
  \]
- Empty down-sets should be excluded or handled when defining \(\operatorname{conv}\varnothing\). Under the antecedent \(\mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p\), this is automatic, but the partition-function formulation should be precise.

These are fixable exposition issues. The bigger issue is that this reformulation is equivalent to the missing lemma; it is not a proof of it.

#### Partition-function formulation

The displayed partition-function implication is indeed the contrapositive of the convex-hull statement after substituting
\[
\lambda=\frac{p}{1-p}.
\]
The algebra
\[
\mu_p(\mathcal D)
=(1+\lambda)^{-m}\sum_{A\in\mathcal D}\lambda^{|A|}
\]
is correct.

But again, this is only an equivalent target. It does not prove the inequality.
