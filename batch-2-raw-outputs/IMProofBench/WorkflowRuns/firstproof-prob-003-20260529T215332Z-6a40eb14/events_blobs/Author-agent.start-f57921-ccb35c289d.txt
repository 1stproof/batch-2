I don’t have a finished elementary proof, but I think the cleanest non-circular route is to turn the convex-hull lemma into an extremal partition-function statement.

Let \(\lambda=p/(1-p)\le 1/2\), and for a down-set \(D\) put
\[
Z_\lambda(D)=\sum_{A\in D}\lambda^{|A|}.
\]
It would suffice to prove the following purely finite extremal theorem:

\[
p\mathbf 1\notin \operatorname{conv}\{1_A:A\in D\}
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
Z_\lambda(D)\le (1+\lambda)^{m-1}.
\]

Indeed \(\mu_p(D)=Z_\lambda(D)/(1+\lambda)^m\), giving \(\mu_p(D)\le 1/(1+\lambda)=1-p\). Equality is the dictatorship \(D=\{A:i\notin A\}\), so the proposed extremal statement has the right sharp example.

The useful induction target is the two-slice version. Write a down-set on \([n+1]\) as slices \(F\subseteq E\subseteq2^{[n]}\), where \(E\) is the slice without \(n+1\) and \(F\) the slice with \(n+1\). Then
\[
Z(D)=Z(E)+\lambda Z(F),
\]
and \(p\mathbf1_{n+1}\in P(D)\) is equivalent to
\[
p\mathbf1_n\in (1-p)P(E)+pP(F),
\]
where \(P(G)=\operatorname{conv}\{1_A:A\in G\}\). So the exact induction lemma to prove is:

\[
F\subseteq E,\quad
p\mathbf1_n\notin (1-p)P(E)+pP(F)
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
Z(E)+\lambda Z(F)\le (1+\lambda)^n .
\]

This is not just the separating-hyperplane dual; it is a weighted counting/isoperimetric statement with a visible extremizer. I would try compressions or a fractional-matching layer argument here. Search terms: biased Erdős matching, fractional matching number of uniform layers, Frankl–Füredi fractional matching.

For the cyclic-coloring route, the exact rational target is this averaged lemma. For \(p=a/b\le1/3\), color each original index independently in \(\mathbb Z_b\), let \(Y_j\) be the load of color \(j\), and prove
\[
\mathbb E_{\text{coloring}}
\left|\left\{t:\sum_{r=0}^{a-1}Y_{t+r}\ge a/b\right\}\right|\ge a .
\]
A fixed interval of \(a\) colors gives independent Bernoulli\((a/b)\) selection, so this proves all rational \(p\). Then pass to real \(p\) by approximating from below, avoiding subset-sum discontinuities. This averaged statement is precisely where the false deterministic fixed-color lemma must be replaced by randomness of the coloring.

### Council member: opus_47_max (models/anthropic/opus_47_max) ###
Quick take: the convex-hull and Bernoulli forms really are LP-dual (the separating hyperplane *is* the Bernoulli statement), so a non-circular proof has to do work somewhere. What the rephrasing buys is the option of a **constructive** attack — exhibit a probability measure on $\mathcal D$ with each coordinate-marginal $p$ — instead of a certificate one.

**Most concrete attack: induct on $m$, split on the last coordinate.** With
\[
\mathcal D_0=\mathcal D\cap 2^{[m-1]},\quad \mathcal D_1=\{A\setminus\{m\}:m\in A\in\mathcal D\}
\]
(both down-sets on $[m-1]$, $\mathcal D_1\subseteq\mathcal D_0$), the hypothesis becomes
\[
(1-p)\mu_p(\mathcal D_0)+p\mu_p(\mathcal D_1)>1-p. \tag{$\ast$}
\]
A natural target is $\nu=(1-p)\nu_0\otimes\delta_0+p\nu_1\otimes\delta_1$ with $\nu_j$ supported on $\mathcal D_j$ and uniform $p$-marginals on $[m-1]$. By LP duality on $[m-1]$, such $\nu$ exists iff for every $c\ge0$ with $\sum c_i=1$,
\[
(1-p)\alpha(c)+p\beta(c)\ge p,\quad \alpha=\max_{\mathcal D_0}c,\ \beta=\max_{\mathcal D_1}c. \tag{$\diamond$}
\]
*Easy half:* $\mu_p(\mathcal D_1)>1-p$. Plain IH on each piece gives $\alpha,\beta\ge p$, done.

*Hard half:* $\mu_p(\mathcal D_1)\le1-p<\mu_p(\mathcal D_0)$. Plain IH gives only $\alpha\ge p$; $(\diamond)$ rearranges to $\alpha\ge p(1-\beta)/(1-p)$, demanding $\alpha\ge p/(1-p)>p$ when $\beta=0$. Plain IH is **not** enough.

**Strengthening that formally closes.** Set $T(\mathcal D)=\sup\{q:(q,\ldots,q)\in\operatorname{conv}(\mathcal D)\}$ and try the quantitative IH
\[
T(\mathcal D)\ge \Phi(\mu_p(\mathcal D)):=\frac{(2p-1)+(1-p)\mu_p(\mathcal D)}{p},
\]
the affine interpolation through $(\mu_p=1-p,T=p)$ and $(\mu_p=1,T=1)$. By affine-linearity of $\Phi$ and $(\ast)$,
\[
(1-p)\Phi(\mu_p(\mathcal D_0))+p\Phi(\mu_p(\mathcal D_1))=\Phi(\mu_p(\mathcal D))>\Phi(1-p)=p,
\]
which closes $(\diamond)$. **If the strengthened bound propagates, the induction works.**

**Catches.** $\Phi$ goes negative when $\mu_p<(1-2p)/(1-p)$. In the hard half $\mu_p(\mathcal D_1)$ can be tiny (e.g.\ $\mathcal D_1=\{\varnothing\}$ gives $(1-p)^{m-1}$), where strengthened IH degenerates to $T\ge0$ and propagation fails. Two repairs to try:

(a) Replace $\Phi$ by a tighter function pinned at $0$ on the small-$\mu_p$ regime, and check the additive inequality $(1-p)\Phi(a)+p\Phi(b)\ge p$ still holds under the only constraint $(1-p)a+pb>1-p$, $a,b\in[0,1]$. The optimization is two-variable and finite-dimensional.

(b) Treat the small-$\mu_p(\mathcal D_1)$ regime directly: $(\ast)$ then forces $\mu_p(\mathcal D_0)$ very close to $1$, and the up-set complement $\mathcal D_0^c$ has tiny $\mu_p$-measure. A Bonferroni/Janson-type bound on the minimal elements of $\mathcal D_0^c$ may yield $T(\mathcal D_0)\ge p/(1-p)$ directly.

I'd grind small $m$ first to identify the right $\Phi$.

**Adjacent literature worth re-checking.**

- *Pelekis beyond FMP 2023.* Pelekis has earlier papers on weighted Bernoulli tail probabilities and ballot-type theorems for biased sums. The $p\le1/3$ region aligns with his "bold-play" calculations; the statement you need may sit as a corollary or a remark.

- *Pinelis 2016 left-tail domination.* If your $\sum w_iv_i$ admits a binomial left-tail comparison in his ordering, the desired bound follows from his Theorem.

- *Sharpness check.* Your extremal $w=(p,\epsilon,\ldots,\epsilon)$ yields $\mu_p(\{w(A)<p\})\to 1-p^+$ and $T\to p^+$; verifying this confirms the lemma is *tight*, which constrains acceptable proof strategies (rules out anything giving $T\ge p+\delta$).

(Uncertain whether the strengthened-$\Phi$ induction closes outright; the small-$\mu_p(\mathcal D_1)$ regime is the genuinely hard sub-case, and the cleanest fix may be (b) rather than (a).)

### Council member: gemini-31-pro (models/gemini/gemini-31-pro) ###
Here is a concrete, non-circular route that builds directly on your "weighted random quantile" intuition and elegantly bypasses the separating-hyperplane/convex-hull dualities. 

You can rigorously decouple the Bernoulli randomness from the weight distribution by mapping the problem to a uniform random permutation. 

### The Permutation-Decoupling Identity
Generate your variables $v_i \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ by drawing i.i.d. uniforms $U_i \sim U[0,1]$ and setting $v_i = \mathbf{1}_{U_i \le p}$. 

Let $B = \sum_{i=1}^m v_i$ be the total number of successes. Clearly, $B \sim \text{Bin}(m, p)$. 
Conditioned on the event $B = b$, the set of successful indices $S = \{i : v_i = 1\}$ is exactly a uniformly random subset of $\{1, \dots, m\}$ of size $b$. 

Now, a uniformly random subset of size $b$ has the exact same distribution as the first $b$ elements of a uniformly random permutation of $\{1, \dots, m\}$. 
Let $W_1, W_2, \dots, W_m$ be the original weights $w_i$ reordered by a uniformly random permutation. Define the random hitting index $K$ as the minimum number of items needed to reach the threshold $p$:
\[
    K = \min \left\{ k \in \{1, \dots, m\} : \sum_{j=1}^k W_j \ge p \right\}.
\]
Because $S$ acts like the first $b$ elements of this permutation, the subset sum $w(S)$ reaches $p$ if and only if $K \le b$. Therefore, we have the exact identity:
\[
    \Pr\left[\sum w_i v_i \ge p\right] = \sum_{b=0}^m \Pr[B = b] \Pr[K \le b] = \mathbb{E}_K \big[ \Pr(B \ge K \mid K) \big].
\]

### The Target Inequality
Let $G(k) = \Pr(\text{Bin}(m, p) \ge k)$. The survival function $G(k)$ is strictly decreasing. 
Notice a beautiful property of $G(k)$: its sum is the expectation of the Binomial!
\[
    \sum_{k=1}^m G(k) = \mathbb{E}[B] = m p.
\]
If $U$ is a uniformly distributed random variable on $\{1, 2, \dots, m\}$, then:
\[
    \mathbb{E}[G(U)] = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{k=1}^m G(k) = p.
\]
Thus, your entire missing small-$p$ lemma is strictly equivalent to proving:
\[
    \mathbb{E}[G(K)] \ge \mathbb{E}[G(U)].
\]

### Why this structure is so powerful
1. **It avoids circularity:** You have completely eliminated the down-set/LP duality. The problem is now a pure study of $K$ (the hitting time in a sampling-without-replacement process) against a fixed, convex-like decreasing sequence $G$.
2. **It perfectly explains your numerical infima:** You observed that the infimum is approached by one large weight $\approx p$ and a dust of finely split residuals. Under this decoupling, if $w = (p, \epsilon, \dots, \epsilon)$, the hitting time $K = 1$ if the large weight is chosen first (probability $p$), and $K \approx pm$ otherwise (probability $1-p$). 
Then $\mathbb{E}[G(K)] \approx p \cdot G(1) + (1-p) \cdot G(pm)$. Since $G(1) \approx 1$ and the median $G(pm) \approx 1/2$, the expectation evaluates to $p(1) + (1-p)(1/2)$, which bounds the probability strictly away from crashing below $p$. 

### Suggested Next Steps
To finish the proof from here, you no longer need to worry about arbitrary Boolean up-sets. You just need to show that $K$ is stochastically dominated by (or convexly ordered relative to) the uniform distribution $U$ under the specific weight $G$. Analyze the sequence $\Pr(K \le k)$ — which is simply the probability that a random sample of $k$ weights without replacement has sum $\ge p$. Since the sample mean is exactly $k/m$, you can attack this with standard concentration/Markov bounds for sampling without replacement.
