\title{A weighted Bernoulli mean-tail inequality}
\author{}
\date{}
\maketitle

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I interpret the question in the universal sense: determine those \(p\in[0,1]\)
for which
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_{i=1}^m w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p
\]
holds for every \(m\ge1\) and every non-negative probability vector
\((w_1,\ldots,w_m)\), where the \(v_i\)'s are i.i.d.\
\({\rm Bernoulli}(p)\).

The counterexamples below show that no value outside
\[
        [0,1/3]\cup\{1/2,1\}
\]
can work.  The remaining unresolved assertion is the sufficiency of the full
interval \(0\le p\le1/3\).  This draft proves several positive subfamilies,
adds a useful multiplicative closure principle, and records two exact
reformulations of the remaining gap.

\section*{Positive values and closure properties proved here}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:positive}
The desired inequality holds for \(p=0\), for \(p=1/k\) with every integer
\(k\ge3\), for \(p=2/b\) with every integer \(b\ge6\), for \(p=1/2\), and for
\(p=1\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The cases \(p=0\) and \(p=1\) are immediate.

Let \(p=1/k\), \(k\ge3\).  Independently color each index \(i\) uniformly with
one of the colors \(1,\ldots,k\), and let
\[
       W_j=\sum_{\{i:\,{\rm color}(i)=j\}} w_i .
\]
For each coloring outcome, \(\sum_{j=1}^k W_j=1\), so at least one color has
\(W_j\ge1/k\).  By symmetry,
\[
       \Pr[W_1\ge1/k]\ge \frac1k.
\]
The indicators of membership in color \(1\) are independent
Bernoulli\((1/k)\), giving the desired inequality.

Now let \(p=2/b\), \(b\ge6\).  We need the following elementary fact.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:pairs}
Let \(b\ge6\), and let \(x_1,\ldots,x_b\ge0\) with \(\sum_i x_i=1\).  Then at
least \(b-1\) unordered pairs \(\{i,j\}\) satisfy
\[
       x_i+x_j\ge \frac2b .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Put \(z_i=bx_i/2\).  Then \(0\le z_i\), \(\sum_i z_i=b/2\), and we must show
that at least \(b-1\) pairs have \(z_i+z_j\ge1\).

Start with a set \(R\) of \(s\) remaining numbers whose sum is at least \(s/2\).
Let \(u\) and \(a\) be the smallest and largest elements of \(R\).  If
\(u+a\ge1\), then \(a\) forms good pairs with all other \(s-1\) elements, and
we stop.  Otherwise \(u+a<1\).  Let \(h\) be the number of other elements that
form a good pair with \(a\).  If \(h\le(s-2)/2\), then the \(h\) partners of
\(a\) are at most \(a\), while the other \(s-1-h\) elements are all \(<1-a\);
hence
\[
 \sum_{z\in R} z
   < a+ha+(s-1-h)(1-a)
   =s-1-h+a(2h-s+2)\le \frac{s}{2},
\]
because \(a\ge1/2\) and \(2h-s+2\le0\).  This contradicts the invariant
\(\sum_{z\in R}z\ge s/2\).  Thus \(h>(s-2)/2\), so
\(h\ge\lfloor s/2\rfloor\).  Count these \(h\) good pairs involving \(a\),
delete \(u\) and \(a\), and continue.  The remaining set has size \(s-2\) and
sum
\[
       \sum_{z\in R}z-u-a>\frac{s}{2}-1=\frac{s-2}{2}.
\]
Pairs counted at different stages are distinct, since each counted pair uses
the current maximum element and that element is then deleted.

If the procedure never stops before the final one or two elements, it counts at
least
\[
 \left\lfloor\frac b2\right\rfloor+
 \left\lfloor\frac {b-2}2\right\rfloor+
 \left\lfloor\frac {b-4}2\right\rfloor+\cdots \ge b-1
\]
for \(b\ge6\).  If it stops after \(t\) deletion steps, when \(s=b-2t\)
elements remain, then it has counted
\[
       \sum_{i=0}^{t-1}\left\lfloor \frac{b-2i}{2}\right\rfloor+(b-2t-1)
\]
pairs.  Writing \(b=2r\) or \(b=2r+1\), the difference between this quantity
and \(b-1\) is nonnegative in the relevant range \(0\le t\le r-1\), \(r\ge3\).
Hence at least \(b-1\) good pairs are counted.
\end{proof}

Return to \(p=2/b\).  Independently color each index uniformly with one of
\(b\) colors and let \(X_j\) be the total weight of color \(j\).  By
Lemma~\ref{lem:pairs}, for every coloring outcome at least \(b-1\) pairs of
colors have total weight at least \(2/b\).  Therefore, by symmetry,
\[
 \Pr[X_1+X_2\ge 2/b]\ge
 \frac{b-1}{\binom b2}=\frac2b.
\]
The event \(X_1+X_2\ge2/b\) is exactly the event obtained by selecting each
original index independently with probability \(2/b\).  This proves the
\(p=2/b\) cases.

Finally, for \(p=1/2\), put \(X=\sum_i w_i v_i\).  Since \(1-v_i\) has the same
distribution as \(v_i\), the random variables \(X\) and \(1-X\) have the same
distribution.  Hence
\[
 \Pr[X<1/2]=\Pr[X>1/2],
\]
and consequently \(\Pr[X\ge1/2]\ge1/2\).
\end{proof}

Let \(\mathcal G\) be the set of probabilities for which the universal
inequality holds.

\begin{proposition}[multiplicative closure]\label{prop:closure}
If \(p,q\in\mathcal G\), then \(pq\in\mathcal G\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
If \(pq=0\), the conclusion is trivial.  Otherwise generate independent
Bernoulli\((pq)\) variables as \(v_i=x_i y_i\), where the \(x_i\)'s are i.i.d.\
Bernoulli\((p)\), the \(y_i\)'s are i.i.d.\ Bernoulli\((q)\), and the two
families are independent.  Let \(T=\sum_i w_i x_i\).  With probability at least
\(p\), \(T\ge p\).  Conditional on such an outcome, the normalized weights
\(w_i x_i/T\) form a probability vector, and the \(q\)-inequality gives
\[
 \Pr\!\left[\sum_i \frac{w_i x_i}{T}y_i\ge q\,\middle|\,x_1,\ldots,x_m\right]
 \ge q .
\]
On this event \(\sum_i w_i x_i y_i\ge qT\ge pq\), proving the claim.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}\label{cor:base}
If the inequality can be proved for all \(p\in[1/6,1/3]\), then it holds for
all \(p\in[0,1/3]\).
\end{corollary}

\begin{proof}
For \(0<p\le1/3\), choose \(n\ge0\) such that \(2^n p\in[1/6,1/3]\).  Since
\(1/2\in\mathcal G\), repeated use of Proposition~\ref{prop:closure} gives
\(p=(2^n p)2^{-n}\in\mathcal G\).  The point \(p=0\) is trivial.
\end{proof}

\section*{Values that cannot work}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:negative}
The desired universal inequality fails for every
\(p\in(1/3,1/2)\cup(1/2,1)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
First suppose \(1/3<p<1/2\).  Take \(m=3\) and
\(w_1=w_2=w_3=1/3\).  Since \(p>1/3\), at least two successes are needed, so
\[
\Pr\!\left[\frac{v_1+v_2+v_3}{3}\ge p\right]
  =3p^2(1-p)+p^3
  =3p^2-2p^3 .
\]
But \(3p^2-2p^3-p=p(1-p)(2p-1)<0\) on \(1/3<p<1/2\).

Now suppose \(1/2<p<1\).  Take \(m=2\) and
\(w_1=w_2=1/2\).  The event requires two successes and has probability
\(p^2<p\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Equivalent forms of the remaining small-\(p\) lemma}

The missing assertion is the following.

\begin{lemma}[small-\(p\) lemma]\label{lem:missing}
If \(0\le p\le1/3\), \(w_i\ge0\), \(\sum_iw_i=1\), and
\(v_i\stackrel{\rm iid}{\sim}{\rm Bernoulli}(p)\), then
\[
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p .
\]
\end{lemma}

It is useful to record two equivalent forms.  First, after replacing
\(v_i\) by \(1-v_i\), Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is equivalent to
\[
   q\ge\frac23,\quad \eta_i\stackrel{\rm iid}{\sim}{\rm Bernoulli}(q)
   \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i\eta_i>q\right]\le q ,
   \qquad q=1-p .
\]
The inequality is strict on the left because
\(\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\) is the same as \(\sum_iw_i(1-v_i)\le1-p\).

Second, Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is equivalent to the following two-tail
``pivot'' inequality: for every \(0\le a\le p\) and every set of weights
\(u_i\ge0\) with \(\sum_i u_i=1-a\),
\[
\label{eq:pivot}
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i\ge p\right]
   \ge
   \frac{p}{1-p}\,
   \Pr\!\left[\sum_i u_i v_i<p-a\right].
   \tag{1}
\]
Indeed, (1) with \(a=0\) gives
\(U\ge\frac{p}{1-p}(1-U)\), hence \(U\ge p\).  Conversely, if
Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is known, add one extra weight \(a\).  Conditioning on
its Bernoulli variable gives
\[
 p\,\Pr[Y\ge p-a]+(1-p)\Pr[Y\ge p]\ge p,
\]
where \(Y=\sum_i u_i v_i\); rearranging gives (1).

The sharpness mechanism for Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is not a finite cap-vector
but a pivot plus dust.  Fix \(0<p\le1/3\), choose \(0<\varepsilon<p\), put
\(a=p-\varepsilon\), and take
\[
   w_0=a,\qquad
   w_1=\cdots=w_N=\frac{1-a}{N}.
\]
If \(Y_N=(1-a)N^{-1}{\rm Bin}(N,p)\), then
\[
 \Pr\!\left[w_0v_0+Y_N\ge p\right]
 =
 p\,\Pr[Y_N\ge\varepsilon]+(1-p)\Pr[Y_N\ge p].
\]
Since \(Y_N\to p(1-a)=p(1-p+\varepsilon)\) in probability and
\(\varepsilon<p(1-p+\varepsilon)<p\), this probability tends to \(p\).
Thus the conjectural lower bound, if true, is best possible but need not be
attained by a finite non-degenerate weight vector.

A useful exact convex-geometric reformulation is this.  Let \(\mu_p\) denote
\(p\)-biased product measure on \(2^{[m]}\).  Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} is
equivalent to the statement that, for every non-empty down-set
\(\mathcal D\subseteq 2^{[m]}\),
\[
       \mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p
       \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
       (p,\ldots,p)\in
       \operatorname{conv}\{\mathbf 1_A:\,A\in\mathcal D\}.
\]
Indeed, if the convex-hull statement holds and the Bernoulli inequality fails,
then the down-set
\[
       \mathcal D=\{A:\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\}
\]
has \(\mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p\).  A probability distribution on \(\mathcal D\)
with all coordinate marginals \(p\) would give
\[
 p=\sum_i p w_i
   =\mathbb E\sum_{i\in A}w_i
   <p,
\]
a contradiction.

Conversely, assume that the convex-hull implication fails for a down-set
\(\mathcal D\), and write
\[
 P(\mathcal D)=\operatorname{conv}\{\mathbf 1_A:\,A\in\mathcal D\}.
\]
Because \(\mathcal D\) is a down-set, \(P(\mathcal D)\) is coordinatewise
down-closed: if \(0\le y\le x\) coordinatewise and \(x\in P(\mathcal D)\), then
\(y\in P(\mathcal D)\).  Hence \(P(\mathcal D)\) is disjoint from
\((p,\ldots,p)+\mathbb R_{\ge0}^m\).  Separating these two convex sets gives a
non-zero vector \(c\ge0\) such that
\[
      \sum_{i\in A}c_i < p\sum_i c_i
      \qquad(A\in\mathcal D).
\]
After normalizing \(w_i=c_i/\sum_jc_j\), Lemma~\ref{lem:missing} would imply
\[
 \mu_p(\mathcal D)
 \le \Pr\!\left[\sum_{i\in A}w_i<p\right]\le1-p,
\]
contradicting \(\mu_p(\mathcal D)>1-p\).

Equivalently, with \(\lambda=p/(1-p)\), the remaining down-set statement can be
written as the partition-function bound
\[
 p{\bf 1}\notin P(\mathcal D)
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
 \sum_{A\in\mathcal D}\lambda^{|A|}
 \le (1+\lambda)^{m-1},
 \qquad 0\le\lambda\le\frac12 .
\]

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The negative direction is complete.  The positive direction is complete for
\(p=0\), all reciprocal points \(1/k\) with \(k\ge3\), the rational points
\(2/b\) with \(b\ge6\), all finite products of the preceding values, and
\(p=1/2,1\).

The essential gap is still Lemma~\ref{lem:missing}.  By
Corollary~\ref{cor:base}, it is enough to close the base interval
\([1/6,1/3]\).  Equivalently, one must prove the strict upper-tail complement
form, the two-tail pivot inequality (1), or the down-set partition-function
inequality displayed above for \(\lambda\le1/2\).  The pivot-plus-dust
calculation above rules out stronger finite cap-vector lower bounds and shows
why the expected sharp constant is exactly \(p\).
