The two-tail pivot inequality $\Phi_a(Z) = \Pr[Z \ge p] - \frac{p}{1-p} \Pr[Z < p-a] \ge 0$ is your most promising path. It cleanly isolates the residual variables from the pivot. Your empirical observation that "pivot + dust" is the near-equality mechanism is profoundly correct, and Large Deviations Theory explains exactly why it does not violate the bound.

For "dust" (a sum of many small weights), $Z$ concentrates tightly around its mean $\mu = p(1-a)$. Both $p$ and $p-a$ are in the tails, so both probabilities in $\Phi_a(Z)$ vanish. However, their exponential rates of decay differ drastically. Expanding the relative entropy $D(x \| p)$ near $p$, the rate function for the lower threshold $p-a$ is approximately $\frac{(1-p)a^2}{2p}$, while the rate for the upper threshold $p$ is $\frac{pa^2}{2(1-p)}$. 

The ratio of these rate functions is roughly $\left(\frac{1-p}{p}\right)^2$. Since $p \le 1/3$, the lower tail decays **at least 4 times faster** in the exponent than the upper tail! Thus, for dust, the negative term $\Pr[Z < p-a]$ is exponentially eclipsed by $\Pr[Z \ge p]$. $\Phi_a(Z)$ approaches $0$ safely from above. 

To formalize this for finite weights, I recommend casting the pivot inequality as a **biased isoperimetric/expansion problem on the Boolean hypercube**. 
Let $\mathcal{D} = \{A : u(A) < p-a\}$ and $\mathcal{U} = \{B : u(B) \ge p\}$. You need to prove $\mu_p(\mathcal{U}) \ge \frac{p}{1-p}\mu_p(\mathcal{D})$. 

Notice that for any $A \in \mathcal{D}$, the available unselected weight is $1 - a - u(A) > 1 - p$. Because $p \le 1/3$, this unselected reservoir is at least $2/3$ — a massive amount of weight available to push $A$ up into $\mathcal{U}$. 

By Max-Flow Min-Cut, your inequality holds if and only if there exists a fractional upward flow in the hypercube from $\mathcal{D}$ to $\mathcal{U}$, where each $A \in \mathcal{D}$ sources $\frac{p}{1-p} \mu_p(A)$ flow, and each $B \in \mathcal{U}$ sinks at most $\mu_p(B)$.
