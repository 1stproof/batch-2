### Council member: gpt-55-pro (models/openai/gpt-55-pro) ###
The angle I would push is to make the mixed case a **convex transport problem**, not a scalar shadow estimate.

Let
\[
R_\lambda(\mathcal E):=\{x\in[0,1]^n:\lambda({\bf1}-x)\in P(\mathcal E)\}.
\]
Then the mixed case is closed exactly if
\[
P(\mathcal F)\cap R_\lambda(\mathcal E)\ne\varnothing .
\]
Indeed, if \(x\in P(\mathcal F)\) and \(y=\lambda({\bf1}-x)\in P(\mathcal E)\), then mixing a law on \(\mathcal E\) with weight \(q=1-p\) and a law on \(\mathcal F\) with weight \(p\) gives first-coordinate marginals
\[
q y+p x=q\lambda({\bf1}-x)+px=p{\bf1},
\]
and last-coordinate marginal \(p\).

Failure of this feasibility has the clean dual you already identified:
\[
\exists c\ge0:\qquad
\rho^\lambda_{\mathcal E}(c)+\lambda r_{\mathcal F}(c)<\lambda c([n]).
\]
For shifted \(\mathcal E,\mathcal F\), I would explicitly record that \(c\) may be assumed nondecreasing, by the usual rearrangement/compression argument: shifted families prefer early coordinates, so moving weight of \(c\) toward later coordinates only improves separation. This is useful because it turns the dual certificate into a “late-coordinate deficit” certificate.

A constructive sufficient condition is also worth isolating. Suppose \(\nu\) is a law on \(\mathcal F\) with barycentre \(x\). If one can find a law \(\sigma\) on \(\mathcal E\) with barycentre \(y\ge\lambda({\bf1}-x)\), then down-closedness thins \(y\) to equality and proves the mixed case. Shiftedness gives a natural way to build \(\sigma\): from \(A\in\mathcal F\), all one-extensions \(A\cup\{j\}\) lie in \(\mathcal E\). Thus one should try to route the coordinate deficits
\[
d_i=(\lambda-(1+\lambda)x_i)_+
\]
through the bipartite graph \(A\mapsto A\cup\{j\}\). The Hall inequalities for this routing are exactly a fractional shadow/covering statement. If these Hall inequalities fail, their dual should reproduce the capped support certificate above. This gives a very concrete LP-dual proof target.

A second structural observation that may help the induction: slice once more, say
\[
\mathcal E=\mathcal E_0\cup\{A\cup\{n\}:A\in\mathcal E_1\},\qquad
\mathcal F=\mathcal F_0\cup\{A\cup\{n\}:A\in\mathcal F_1\}.
\]
Shiftedness plus upper-shadow containment gives the strong chain
\[
\mathcal F_1\subseteq \mathcal F_0\subseteq \mathcal E_1\subseteq \mathcal E_0.
\]
Moreover
\[
\mu(\mathcal E)+\lambda\mu(\mathcal F)
=q\mu(\mathcal E_0)+p\mu(\mathcal E_1)+\lambda q\mu(\mathcal F_0)+\lambda p\mu(\mathcal F_1)
=q\mu(\mathcal E_0)+p\bigl(\mu(\mathcal E_1)+\mu(\mathcal F_0)\bigr)+\lambda p\mu(\mathcal F_1),
\]
using \(\lambda q=p\). The middle two slices have equal coefficient. I would try proving a stronger four-layer induction for chains
\[
\mathcal A\subseteq\mathcal B\subseteq\mathcal C\subseteq\mathcal G
\]
with the inherited shifted-shadow relations. This may avoid the asymmetric mixed obstruction.

I would be wary of pure \(\mu(\nabla\mathcal F)\) inequalities: examples like \(\mathcal F=\{\emptyset\}\), \(\mathcal E=\{\emptyset,\{1\},\{2\}\}\) already show ordinary cross-union/shadow information is too weak. The capped/transport condition is the extra information that rules them out. Literature-wise, this sits close to fractional matching/covering theorems for shifted complexes, especially Frankl–Füredi/Kalai-style shifted-complex polytope methods.

### Council member: opus_47_max (models/anthropic/opus_47_max) ###
Looking at the mixed case, you have two unused levers: the shadow containment $\partial^+\mathcal{F}\subseteq\mathcal{E}$ from shiftedness, and the dual separator $c\ge0$ for $\mathcal{D}$ on $[n+1]$ (WLOG non-increasing, $\sum c_i=1$). Write $\alpha=c_{n+1}$. The case $\alpha=0$ closes immediately: $c|_{[n]}$ separates both slices, IH gives $\mu_p(\mathcal{E}),\mu_p(\mathcal{F})\le q$. So assume $\alpha>0$; then $\max_\mathcal{F} c<p-\alpha$ and $\max_\mathcal{E} c<p$.

**A useful structural pinch.** Let $A^*=\{1,\ldots,k\}$ be the lex-greatest prefix in $\mathcal{F}$. Shadow + shifted forces $\{1,\ldots,k+1\}\in\mathcal{E}$, hence $c(A^*)+c_{k+1}<p$. With $c(A^*)<p-\alpha$ and $c_{k+1}\ge c_{n+1}=\alpha$, this is consistent only if either (i) $c(A^*)$ has slack $\ge\alpha$ below its bound, or (ii) $c_{k+1}=\alpha$. So you get a dichotomy: either there is real slack you can convert into a deficit bound on $\mu_p(\mathcal{F})$, or $c$ has an $\alpha$-flat tail starting at coordinate $k+1$. This is the natural setting for a case-split induction.

**Three angles, in order of how strongly I'd bet:**

(a) **Shadow-driven normal form.** Iterate the pinch over *all* shifted maximals of $\mathcal{F}$ (not just the lex prefix). Each maximal $A=\{i_1<\cdots<i_k\}\in\mathcal{F}$ forces a constraint on $c_{j}$ for the smallest $j\notin A$. The extremal $c$ should reduce to "head + uniform $\alpha$-tail", i.e. the equal-weight binomial regime — which is exactly the case you've already handled via the coloring/Lemma~\ref{lem:pairs} machinery and the integer-$L$ reciprocal proof. The induction closes by reducing the genuine mixed case to the rational tail.

(b) **Stability IH.** Strengthen the IH at level $n$ to a deficit form: $\max_\mathcal{H} c\le p\beta-\delta\Rightarrow\mu_p(\mathcal{H})\le q-g(\delta)$. Applied to $\mathcal{F}$ with $\delta=q\alpha$ gives $\mu_p(\mathcal{F})\le q-g(q\alpha)$. You'd need $g(q\alpha)\ge q$ for this alone to close, which is too strong, but a Russo-formula bound $g(\delta)\gtrsim\delta\cdot I_p$ near the separating face, combined with the slack in (a), may suffice. This is the same idea as your influence/threshold-flow remark in the round-11 notes.

(c) **$p$-biased Kruskal–Katona.** Since $\mathcal{E}\supseteq\mathcal{F}\cup\partial^+\mathcal{F}$,
$$\mu_p(\mathcal{E})\ge\mu_p(\mathcal{F})+\mu_p(\partial^+\mathcal{F}\setminus\mathcal{F}).$$
For shifted complexes there are clean biased shadow inequalities (Bey, Frankl) lower-bounding the second term in terms of $\mu_p(\mathcal{F})$. If you can extract a bound like $\mu_p(\partial^+\mathcal{F}\setminus\mathcal{F})\ge \lambda\cdot \mu_p(\mathcal{F})\cdot(1+\text{deficit})$, that would directly push $\mu_p(\mathcal{E})+\lambda\mu_p(\mathcal{F})\le 1$.

**References worth mining:**
- Frankl–Tokushige, *Extremal Problems for Finite Sets* (AMS Stud. Math. 86, 2018), Chs. 6, 11–12.
- Bey, "Polynomial LYM inequalities", *Combinatorica* 25 (2005).
- Friedgut, "On the measure of intersecting families, uniqueness and stability", *CPC* 17 (2008).
- Filmus, recent work on biased Fourier analysis / biased Kruskal–Katona on the slice.
- Ahlswede–Khachatrian, "The complete intersection theorem", *Invent. Math.* 125 (1997).

A useful sanity check: in your LP enumeration for $n\le 5$, examine the dual $c$ achieving $\max_\mathcal{D} c$ at the near-extremal non-dictator shifted families. I'd bet the structural pinch in (a) shows up explicitly as $c$ having a constant tail. If so, the reduction-to-equal-weight-tail story is the right one, and (a)+FMP-style pairing at the equal-weight endpoint should close it.

### Council member: gemini-31-pro (models/gemini/gemini-31-pro) ###
Here is how I would approach the mixed slice case. By using fractional covering duality, you can show that your geometric capped-slice lemma is algebraically identical to your scalar C-HUG inequality. This means you do not have two separate inductive obstacles; you only have one.

Here is the exact dictionary between the two. The separation condition gives:
\[
  \rho^\lambda_{\mathcal E}(c) + \lambda M < \lambda C
\]
where $M = r_{\mathcal F}(c) = \max_{B \in \mathcal F} c(B)$ and $C = c([n])$. 

**1. The Dual Covering Problem**
By LP duality, the capped support function is a fractional covering problem:
\[
  \rho^\lambda_{\mathcal E}(c) = \min_{\gamma, w \ge 0} \big\{ \gamma + \lambda w([n]) \;\mid\; \gamma + w(A) \ge c(A) \text{ for all } A \in \mathcal E \big\}.
\]
Thus, the strict inequality hypothesis guarantees there exist an offset $\gamma$ and a "shadow" weight vector $w \ge 0$ such that $\gamma + \lambda w([n]) + \lambda M < \lambda C$.

Substitute $\lambda = p/q$ and multiply by $q$:
\[
  q\gamma + p w([n]) + p M < p C.
\]

**2. Reduction to Threshold Events**
Define a new coefficient vector $v_i = c_i - w_i$. Notice that:
* For $A \in \mathcal E$, the dual constraint gives $v(A) = c(A) - w(A) \le \gamma$.
* For $A \in \mathcal F$, since $w \ge 0$, $v(A) \le c(A) \le M$.

Your target is to prove $q \mu_p(\mathcal E) + p \mu_p(\mathcal F) \le q$. By the above, it suffices to bound the weighted Bernoulli tail:
\[
  q \Pr(v(A) \le \gamma) + p \Pr(v(A) \le M) \le q.
\]

**3. Recovering the C-HUG Thresholds**
Under $p$-biased measure, the expected sum is $\mu = p v([n]) = pC - pw([n])$. The dual strict inequality rearranges exactly to:
\[
  \mu > q\gamma + p M.
\]
Define the gap parameter $h = \gamma - M$. The strict inequality forces $\gamma < \mu + ph$ and $M < \mu - qh$. Therefore, to bound the slices, you need:
\[
  q \Pr(v(A) < \mu + ph) + p \Pr(v(A) < \mu - qh) \le q.
\]
Let $X = v(A) - \mu$, which is a centered sum of two-point variables. Rearranging the above yields:
\[
  p \Pr(X \le -qh) \le q \Pr(X \ge ph).
\]
This is exactly your centered C-HUG inequality!

**4. Bounding the Coefficients via Shiftedness**
C-HUG requires the step sizes to be bounded by the gap $h$. This is where the shiftedness of $\mathcal D$ comes in. Because $\mathcal D$ is shifted, $\mathcal E$ contains the upper shadow of $\mathcal F$. For any $B \in \mathcal F$ and $i \notin B$, $B \cup \{i\} \in \mathcal E$. 
The dual constraint requires $\gamma + w(B \cup \{i\}) \ge c(B \cup \{i\})$, which rearranges to:
\[
  v_i = c_i - w_i \le \gamma - c(B) + w(B).
\]
If you evaluate this at the bottleneck set $B \in \arg\max_{\mathcal F} c(B)$, you have $c(B) = M$, yielding $v_i \le h + w(B)$. Complementary slackness in the LP strongly restricts $w(B)$, tightly coupling the geometric shadow containment to the $v_i \le h$ condition of C-HUG. 

**Recommendation:**
The duality is exact. Your subcritical shifted HUG proof already implicitly proves the capped-slice lemma for subcritical geometric down-sets. I suggest abandoning the search for a separate geometric induction for the mixed slice case. Direct 100% of your effort to the scalar, supercritical C-HUG inequality—perhaps by leveraging the block-coalescence idea or a Bellman induction with a variance state—because resolving C-HUG automatically resolves the capped-slice lemma.
