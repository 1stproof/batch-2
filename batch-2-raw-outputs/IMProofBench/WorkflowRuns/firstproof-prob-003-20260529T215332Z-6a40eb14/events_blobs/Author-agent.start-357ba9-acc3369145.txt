I would not try to prove the stated cap-vector inequality as written: it is false under only \(\max_i w_i<p\).

A simple counterexample structure is “one pivot plus dust.” Fix \(p\in[1/4,1/3]\), choose \(0<\varepsilon<p\), and take
\[
w_0=p-\varepsilon,\qquad 
w_1=\cdots=w_N=\frac{1-p+\varepsilon}{N},
\]
with \(N\) large enough that all weights are \(<p\). Let
\[
Y_N=\frac{1-p+\varepsilon}{N}\operatorname{Bin}(N,p).
\]
Then
\[
\Pr[X\ge p]
=
p\,\Pr[Y_N\ge \varepsilon]
+(1-p)\,\Pr[Y_N\ge p].
\]
But \(Y_N\to p(1-p+\varepsilon)\) in probability, and for \(\varepsilon<p\),
\[
\varepsilon < p(1-p+\varepsilon) < p.
\]
Hence
\[
\Pr[X\ge p]\to p.
\]
Since
\[
g_4(p):=\Pr[\operatorname{Bin}(4,p)\ge2]>p
\]
throughout \([1/4,1/3]\), taking \(N\) large gives a genuine violation of the proposed lower bound. In fact the same construction works with any fixed pivot \(a\in(0,p)\), not only \(a\approx p\). So the true global near-equality mechanism is pivot+dust, not the four-cap vector.

This does not make the four-cap numerics useless: \((p,p,p,1-3p)\) may well be a local finite-support/coarse-support extremizer. But any cap-vector/majorization statement would need an additional hypothesis excluding a deterministic residual/dust limit, and therefore it cannot be the main global proof of the base interval.

For the remaining interval, I would prioritize the down-set/partition-function or Bellman-induction route, possibly guided by known extremal theorems. In complement form the desired theorem is the very clean statement
\[
q=1-p\ge 2/3,\qquad
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i\eta_i>q\right]\le q,
\quad \eta_i\sim{\rm Bernoulli}(q).
\]
This is the exact phrase I would search for in Kellerer/Samuels/Jogdeo/bold-play literature: “weighted Bernoulli sum exceeds its mean with probability at most \(q\) for \(q\ge2/3\).”

If proving it self-contained, I would build a two-parameter induction, not a diagonal-only one. Define a Bellman quantity such as
\[
B(s,t)=\sup \Pr\!\left[\sum b_i\xi_i<t\right],
\qquad \sum b_i=s,\quad \xi_i\sim{\rm Bernoulli}(p),
\]
and use the recursion obtained by exposing one atom:
\[
B(s,t)\le \sup_a\bigl((1-p)B(s-a,t)+pB(s-a,t-a)\bigr).
\]
The pivot+dust examples tell you what equality should look like: after exposing one atom, the residual is essentially deterministic at its mean. So the induction should be designed to certify that residual variance/coarseness only helps the desired upper tail, with equality approached by infinitesimal splitting.

In short: cap-vector is not globally true; known extremal theorem or a sharp two-parameter down-set/Bellman induction looks much more promising.

For $p\in[1/4,1/3]$, the configurations $(p,p,p,1-3p)$, $(p,p,c,c)$ with $c=(1-2p)/2$, $(1/4,1/4,1/4,1/4)$ at the corner, $(0.28,0.28,0.28,0.16)$, etc., **all** give exactly $\Pr[\mathrm{Bin}(4,p)\ge2]$. **2. A useful local‑exchange derivative.** If you push mass $a\to a+t$, $b\to b-t$ (with $a\ge b$, others fixed), letting $f$ be the density of $S=\sum_{k\ne i,j}w_kv_k$,
$$\tfrac{d}{dt}\Pr[\mathrm{event}]\ =\ p(1-p)\big[f(p-a)-f(p-b)\big].$$
This isn't sign‑definite, but it explains the pattern: mass concentrates onto the cap, and the residual prefers a single atom over many fragments, because fragments populate the conditional region $[p-a-b,p-a]$ where the coefficient is $p^2$ rather than $p$.

**3. Recommended route.** Cap‑vector / weight‑space Schur exchange, *not* down‑set partition function. Yes, your cap-vector heuristic is completely correct, and it is exactly the key to unlocking the remaining base interval. 

For the configuration $w = (p-\epsilon, p-\epsilon, p-\epsilon, 1-3p+3\epsilon)$ with $p \in [1/4, 1/3]$, achieving a sum $\ge p$ requires selecting either at least two of the large weights, or exactly one large weight plus the small weight. This is because $1-3p < p$, and $(p-\epsilon) + (1-3p+3\epsilon) = 1-2p+2\epsilon \ge p$ (since $p \le 1/3$). This selection condition is isomorphic to the event of getting at least 2 successes out of 4 independent trials, giving probability $g_4(p) = \Pr[\operatorname{Bin}(4, p) \ge 2]$. You can algebraically verify that $g_4(p) \ge p$ holds strictly for $p \ge \frac{5-\sqrt{13}}{6} \approx 0.232$, comfortably covering your $[1/4, 1/3]$ interval.

Even better, this mechanism tiles the entire space down to $0$. For any $p \in (\frac{1}{k+1}, \frac{1}{k}]$, the binding hard configuration is the cap-vector $(p, \dots, p, 1-kp)^-$ (with $k$ copies of $p$). The success event logic scales perfectly: it is exactly the event of "selecting at least 2 of the $k+1$ coordinates," yielding $\Pr[\operatorname{Bin}(k+1, p) \ge 2]$. This Binomial tail securely lower-bounds $p$ on every respective interval.

### Recommended Proof Strategy

I strongly advise pursuing a **known extremal theorem route** over down-set partition-function induction. The continuous partition-function invariant $Z_\lambda$ is theoretically elegant but practically brittle; because adding a tiny atom shifts the convex hull of a down-set discontinuously, finding a smooth quantitative induction invariant for this class of inequalities is notoriously frustrating.

Instead, leverage the calculus of the problem. The objective function $F(w) = \Pr[\sum w_i v_i \ge p]$ is **piecewise multi-affine** in the weights. Its regions of linearity are carved by the hyperplanes $\sum_{i \in A} w_i = p$. 

Because it is multi-affine, $F(w)$ cannot have strict local minima in the interior of any region. By iteratively pushing weights along lines until boundaries are hit, the global infimum must be driven to a vertex of this hyperplane arrangement. At such a vertex, the weight vector satisfies a full-rank system of active subset-sum equalities (of the form $w(A) = p$ or $w_i = 0$). Vectors with that many subset-sum equalities are highly degenerate "step vectors" or "cap vectors" taking only a few distinct values.

This is precisely where the **Fokkink–Meester–Pelekis (2023)** reference is most valuable. While their final geometric bounds leave gaps, their methodology relies on this exact structural reduction. They prove that the extrema of weighted Bernoulli combinations are attained at "bold play" stakes (i.e., step vectors). You should not cite FMP as a black box for the final bound, but you can confidently adapt their structural lemma to prove your minimizer must be a cap vector. 

Once you rigorously restrict the infinite-dimensional simplex to step vectors of the form $(x, \dots, x, y)$, the optimization collapses to a finite sequence of 2D calculus problems over $x$ and $y$. Your $g_{k+1}(p)$ polynomials show that you already hold the exact solutions to those problems. 

**Summary:** 
1. Use the piecewise multi-affine property to prove the minimizer is a cap vector.
2. Parameterize the cap vector and map the success event to the Binomial tail $\Pr[\operatorname{Bin}(k+1, p) \ge 2]$.
3. Use elementary calculus to establish $g_{k+1}(p) \ge p$.
