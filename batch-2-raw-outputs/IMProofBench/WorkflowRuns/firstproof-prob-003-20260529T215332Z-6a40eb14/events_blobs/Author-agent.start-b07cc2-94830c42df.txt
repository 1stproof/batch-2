I do not see a counterexample; the pivot inequality looks structurally right. My strongest suggestion is to change the induction target by adding “buffer bands” around the two thresholds. The exact identity below seems important.

Write \(q=1-p\), \(\lambda=p/q\), \(L=p-a\), \(U=p\), and
\[
\Phi(Y):=\Pr[Y\ge U]-\lambda\Pr[Y<L].
\]
If \(Y=R+xv\), \(v\sim{\rm Bernoulli}(p)\), then a direct expansion gives
\[
\Phi(R+xv)
=
\Phi(R)
+p\,\Pr[U-x\le R<U]
+\lambda p\,\Pr[L-x\le R<L].
\tag{*}
\]
So after peeling off a weight \(x\), one should not try to prove the same pivot inequality for \(R\); it is false in the wrong parameter range. The peeled weight creates two compensating strips adjacent to the upper and lower thresholds. This is exactly the “same residual variable at two thresholds” issue.

I would therefore try to prove the stronger buffered invariant: for \(h\ge0\),
\[
\mathbb E F_{a,h}(R)\ge0,\qquad \sum r_i=1-a-h,
\]
where
\[
F_{a,h}(r)
=
{\bf1}_{r\ge p}
-\lambda{\bf1}_{r<p-a}
+p{\bf1}_{p-h\le r<p}
+\lambda p{\bf1}_{p-a-h\le r<p-a}.
\tag{B}
\]
For \(h=x\), \((*)\) says precisely that proving \((B)\) for the residual \(R\) proves the pivot inequality for \(R+xv\). In step-function form, away from overlaps, \(F_{a,h}\) has levels
\[
-\lambda,\ -p,\ 0,\ p,\ 1,
\]
on the successive intervals cut by
\[
p-a-h,\quad p-a,\quad p-h,\quad p.
\]
Those coefficients are not arbitrary: they are exactly the Bernoulli transition coefficients. This is the invariant I would test for pairwise splitting/merging; monotonicity may fail for \(\Phi\), but it may hold for this five-level payoff.

To close an induction, you will probably need one more parameter: allow the top threshold \(t\le p\),
\[
F_{t,a,h}(r)
=
{\bf1}_{r\ge t}
-\lambda{\bf1}_{r<t-a}
+p{\bf1}_{t-h\le r<t}
+\lambda p{\bf1}_{t-a-h\le r<t-a}.
\]
The \(t\)-parameter appears because conditioning on a selected peeled weight shifts both thresholds from \((t,t-a)\) to \((t-x,t-a-x)\). I would try proving \(\mathbb EF_{t,a,h}\ge0\) for \(a\le t\le p\), with overlaps handled literally by the indicator formula.

A second promising recast: by complementing sets, the pivot inequality is equivalent to
\[
\mu_p\{u(A)\ge p\}\ge \lambda\,\mu_q\{u(A)>q\},\qquad q=1-p.
\]
Couple \(B\sim\mu_q\) and thin it with probability \(\lambda=p/q\) to get \(A\sim\mu_p\). A pointwise thinning bound is false, but this formulation may support an averaged threshold-family proof over the weighted upper family \(\{u(A)\ge t\}\). It also connects naturally to Kanter/Kemperman-type comparison results for Bernoulli convolutions, though I’m uncertain which reference gives exactly this sharp two-threshold form.

A few angles I'd push on.

**1. Where the natural induction stalls (precisely).** Conditioning on the largest weight $u_m$, the pivot LHS$-$RHS equals
$$p\bigl[\Pr(Y'\!\ge p{-}u_m) - \lambda\Pr(Y'\!<\!p{-}a{-}u_m)\bigr] + (1{-}p)\bigl[\Pr(Y'\!\ge p) - \lambda\Pr(Y'\!<\!p{-}a)\bigr],$$
with $Y'=\sum_{i<m}u_iv_i$ on weights of total $1{-}a{-}u_m$. The first bracket is *implied* by the natural pivot for $u'$ at $a' = a+u_m$ (combine it with $\Pr[Y'\ge p{-}u_m]\ge\Pr[Y'\ge p]$), so it closes inductively. Bracket two is *stronger* than the natural pivot for $u'$: it demands a bound on $\Pr[Y'<p{-}a]$ where the natural pivot only controls $\Pr[Y'<p{-}a{-}u_m]$, which is smaller. So bracket two is over-strength and can be strictly negative — that is the exact obstruction, and it explains why both plain Bellman and pairwise merging fail. The single structural invariant of both branches is the gap $a = t_2 - t_1$.

**2. The natural 3-parameter invariant to attack.** Define
$$\Psi(s, t_2, a) := \inf_{\sum u_i = s}\bigl[\Pr(Y\!\ge t_2) - \lambda\Pr(Y\!<\!t_2{-}a)\bigr].$$
The pivot is $\Psi(1{-}a, p, a) \ge 0$, and the conditioning identity becomes
$$\Psi(s,t_2,a) \ge p\,\Psi(s{-}u_m, t_2{-}u_m, a) + (1{-}p)\,\Psi(s{-}u_m, t_2, a),$$
with $a$ preserved in both branches; $s - t_2$ is preserved in the first and decreases by $u_m$ in the second. Two easy facts: $\Psi$ is non-decreasing in $s$ (adding weight increases the upper tail and decreases the lower) and in $a$ (lowering $t_1$ shrinks the lower tail). The remaining job is to identify a closed inductive *region* in $(s,t_2,a)$ containing the pivot line and admitting a verifiable lower bound. A pure linear bound on $\Psi$ saturating the pivot line cannot satisfy the $s=0$ base case ($\Psi(0,t_2,a) = -\lambda$ when $0 < a < t_2$) for $a > 0$, so a piecewise or convex-in-$a$ ansatz is required. I'd map $\Psi$ numerically on a small-$m$ grid first: the contours where $\Psi$ vanishes should reveal the right shape, and they likely lie on a curve of the form $s = $ explicit function of $(t_2, a)$ rather than a hyperplane.

**3. Down-set partition / fractional Hall.** Your equivalent
$$\sum_{A: u(A)\ge p}\lambda^{|A|} \;\ge\; \sum_{B: u(B)<p-a}\lambda^{|B|+1}$$
invites a Hall-type single-element injection $B \mapsto B \cup \{\arg\max_{i\notin B} u_i\}$, which works iff $u_{\max} \ge a$. When all $u_i < a$ (uniform-ish weights), it fails, and an $|S| \ge 2$ extension introduces $\lambda^{|S|}$ overcounting. The way out is to drop integrality: set up the bipartite transportation LP between $\mathcal D = \{u(B)<p{-}a\}$ and $\mathcal U = \{u(A)\ge p\}$ with $\lambda$-weighted capacities, and verify Hall/max-flow via the down-set structure (every saturated cut corresponds to a sub–down-set whose convex hull excludes $p\mathbf 1$). The down-set hypothesis should make Hall's condition checkable cell-by-cell. This is the most combinatorial route and the one most likely to yield a clean conceptual proof.

**4. Complement / Pelekis literature.** Equivalent strict bound:
$$\Pr\Bigl[\sum_i u_i \eta_i > q\Bigr] \le q,\qquad q\ge 2/3,\;\eta_i \sim \mathrm{Ber}(q).$$
This is the FMP "hypotenuse" with the *strict* inequality. The strictness eliminates the atomic mass at $q$ and is genuinely easier — Fokkink–Meester–Pelekis prove an adjacent closed rectangle, and an $\epsilon$-perturbation argument off that rectangle may suffice. Pelekis's 2017–2020 series on weighted Bernoulli concentration and the Pelekis–Ramon line on antichains/transport are the natural literature to scan; uncertain whether a directly transferable lemma is in print, but the strict-variant formulation is the right thing to search for.

$$\Lambda(u, a) := (1-p)\Pr[Y \ge p] - p\Pr[Y < p-a].$$
The pivot is $\Lambda \ge 0$. $\Lambda$ has the convenient property that $\partial_a \Lambda \ge 0$ (the second term decreases as $a$ grows). So $\Lambda$ is increasing in $a$ at fixed weight vector $u$. If you can simultaneously prove a *monotonicity in $u$* along a one-parameter deformation from a generic $u$ (with $\sum u_i = 1-a$) toward the extremal one-atom configuration $u = (1{-}a, 0, \ldots)$ — at which $\Lambda = 0$ — you'd be done. The deformation that respects the equality on the pivot line is "concentrate mass onto the heaviest coordinate by $\epsilon$"; track $\Lambda$ along this deformation, allowing for jumps when subset sums cross $p$ or $p{-}a$. Numerically check whether each elementary jump is non-negative; if so, identify the structural reason; if not, the deformation needs to be rerouted around troublesome cells.

(5) is a quick sanity check that may either suggest the extremal mechanism or expose where the inequality is genuinely subtle.

Here is how I would approach the pivot two-tail inequality. You have hit on exactly the right induction target, and there is a profound geometric reason why it works and why adaptive scalar Bellman bounds fail. 

### 1. The "Straddling the Mean" Insight
Let $Y = \sum u_i v_i$ be the residual random variable, with expected value $\mu = p(1-a)$. 
Notice the exact distances from the mean to your two evaluation thresholds:
*   $p - \mu = pa$
*   $\mu - (p-a) = a(1-p)$

The mean $\mu$ divides the interval $[p-a, p]$ in exactly the ratio $p : (1-p)$. Because of this, your target step function 
$$f(y) = (1-p)\mathbf{1}_{y \ge p} + p\mathbf{1}_{y \ge p-a}$$
has the remarkable property that its value at the mean is exactly $p$ (i.e., $f(\mu) = p$, since $\mu$ lies strictly between the two jumps). 

Your pivot inequality is literally $\mathbb{E}[f(Y)] \ge f(\mathbb{E}[Y])$. This perfectly explains why the "pivot plus dust" configuration achieves exactly $p$: the deterministic dust collapses the residual distribution to a Dirac delta at $\mu$, yielding $f(\mu) = p$.

### 2. Splitting is Monotone (Merging is not)
You noted that "naive merging is not monotone." This is technically true, but you should run the heuristic in reverse: look at *splitting*. 

When you split a weight $c$ into $x+y$, the mean $\mu$ is unchanged, but the variance of $Y$ strictly *decreases* (since $x^2 + y^2 < c^2$). Because $f(y)$ acts geometrically like a convex function (it is flat, jumps up, is flat, and jumps up again), reducing the variance pushes probability mass toward the center $\mu$, where $f(\mu)=p$. 

Thus, splitting weights monotonically *decreases* your functional $\Phi_a(w)$. If you can formalize that splitting always drives $\mathbb{E}[f(Y)]$ down, the global minimum is automatically the infinite-split "dust" limit, closing the proof. 

### 3. Crossing Properties and Stochastic Orders
To bypass the lack of a pointwise Bellman bound, you should lift the induction from scalars to the entire shape of the distribution using **stochastic orders** and **Karlin's variation-diminishing properties**.

Let $Y_{merged}$ and $Y_{split}$ be the sum's distribution before and after splitting a weight. Because convolutions of Bernoullis are strongly unimodal/Pólya frequency sequences, the probability mass functions of $Y_{merged}$ and $Y_{split}$ cross exactly twice (the split sum is more sharply peaked). 

Under the $s$-convex stochastic orders (specifically the convex order $\le_{cx}$ adapted for step functions), if the density difference $\Delta(y) = P_{merged}(y) - P_{split}(y)$ crosses twice—positive in the tails, negative in the center—the integral $\int f(y) d\Delta(y)$ takes a predictable positive sign for any function whose "second differences" act convex-like. You do not need to construct a strict pointwise convex minorant $L(y) \le f(y)$; the crossing property of the densities does the heavy lifting for you.

### 4. Concrete Literature
Instead of Fokkink–Meester–Pelekis, look into the exact crossing properties of Bernoulli convolutions. 
*   **Shaked & Shanthikumar, *Stochastic Orders*:** Look at the convex order ($\le_{cx}$) for binomial/Bernoulli sums.
*   **Karlin's Total Positivity:** The Karlin–Novikoff crossing conditions for discrete distributions give the exact exact sign-change theorems you need to prove that $\mathbb{E}[f(Y)]$ drops when a weight is split.
*   Since you already cited **Pinelis (2016)**, review his related papers on "exact Hoeffding bounds" for sums of bounded variables, which frequently utilize these 2-crossing density arguments to bound piecewise step functions.
