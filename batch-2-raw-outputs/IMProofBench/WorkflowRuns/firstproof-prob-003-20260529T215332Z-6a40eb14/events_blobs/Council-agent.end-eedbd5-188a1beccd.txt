A few angles I'd push on.

**1. Where the natural induction stalls (precisely).** Conditioning on the largest weight $u_m$, the pivot LHS$-$RHS equals
$$p\bigl[\Pr(Y'\!\ge p{-}u_m) - \lambda\Pr(Y'\!<\!p{-}a{-}u_m)\bigr] + (1{-}p)\bigl[\Pr(Y'\!\ge p) - \lambda\Pr(Y'\!<\!p{-}a)\bigr],$$
with $Y'=\sum_{i<m}u_iv_i$ on weights of total $1{-}a{-}u_m$. The first bracket is *implied* by the natural pivot for $u'$ at $a' = a+u_m$ (combine it with $\Pr[Y'\ge p{-}u_m]\ge\Pr[Y'\ge p]$), so it closes inductively. Bracket two is *stronger* than the natural pivot for $u'$: it demands a bound on $\Pr[Y'<p{-}a]$ where the natural pivot only controls $\Pr[Y'<p{-}a{-}u_m]$, which is smaller. So bracket two is over-strength and can be strictly negative — that is the exact obstruction, and it explains why both plain Bellman and pairwise merging fail. The single structural invariant of both branches is the gap $a = t_2 - t_1$.

**2. The natural 3-parameter invariant to attack.** Define
$$\Psi(s, t_2, a) := \inf_{\sum u_i = s}\bigl[\Pr(Y\!\ge t_2) - \lambda\Pr(Y\!<\!t_2{-}a)\bigr].$$
The pivot is $\Psi(1{-}a, p, a) \ge 0$, and the conditioning identity becomes
$$\Psi(s,t_2,a) \ge p\,\Psi(s{-}u_m, t_2{-}u_m, a) + (1{-}p)\,\Psi(s{-}u_m, t_2, a),$$
with $a$ preserved in both branches; $s - t_2$ is preserved in the first and decreases by $u_m$ in the second. Two easy facts: $\Psi$ is non-decreasing in $s$ (adding weight increases the upper tail and decreases the lower) and in $a$ (lowering $t_1$ shrinks the lower tail). The remaining job is to identify a closed inductive *region* in $(s,t_2,a)$ containing the pivot line and admitting a verifiable lower bound. A pure linear bound on $\Psi$ saturating the pivot line cannot satisfy the $s=0$ base case ($\Psi(0,t_2,a) = -\lambda$ when $0 < a < t_2$) for $a > 0$, so a piecewise or convex-in-$a$ ansatz is required. I'd map $\Psi$ numerically on a small-$m$ grid first: the contours where $\Psi$ vanishes should reveal the right shape, and they likely lie on a curve of the form $s = $ explicit function of $(t_2, a)$ rather than a hyperplane.

**3. Down-set partition / fractional Hall.** Your equivalent
$$\sum_{A: u(A)\ge p}\lambda^{|A|} \;\ge\; \sum_{B: u(B)<p-a}\lambda^{|B|+1}$$
invites a Hall-type single-element injection $B \mapsto B \cup \{\arg\max_{i\notin B} u_i\}$, which works iff $u_{\max} \ge a$. When all $u_i < a$ (uniform-ish weights), it fails, and an $|S| \ge 2$ extension introduces $\lambda^{|S|}$ overcounting. The way out is to drop integrality: set up the bipartite transportation LP between $\mathcal D = \{u(B)<p{-}a\}$ and $\mathcal U = \{u(A)\ge p\}$ with $\lambda$-weighted capacities, and verify Hall/max-flow via the down-set structure (every saturated cut corresponds to a sub–down-set whose convex hull excludes $p\mathbf 1$). The down-set hypothesis should make Hall's condition checkable cell-by-cell. This is the most combinatorial route and the one most likely to yield a clean conceptual proof.

**4. Complement / Pelekis literature.** Equivalent strict bound:
$$\Pr\Bigl[\sum_i u_i \eta_i > q\Bigr] \le q,\qquad q\ge 2/3,\;\eta_i \sim \mathrm{Ber}(q).$$
This is the FMP "hypotenuse" with the *strict* inequality. The strictness eliminates the atomic mass at $q$ and is genuinely easier — Fokkink–Meester–Pelekis prove an adjacent closed rectangle, and an $\epsilon$-perturbation argument off that rectangle may suffice. Pelekis's 2017–2020 series on weighted Bernoulli concentration and the Pelekis–Ramon line on antichains/transport are the natural literature to scan; uncertain whether a directly transferable lemma is in print, but the strict-variant formulation is the right thing to search for.

**5. One concrete invariant to try before the heavy machinery.** Define
$$\Lambda(u, a) := (1-p)\Pr[Y \ge p] - p\Pr[Y < p-a].$$
The pivot is $\Lambda \ge 0$. $\Lambda$ has the convenient property that $\partial_a \Lambda \ge 0$ (the second term decreases as $a$ grows). So $\Lambda$ is increasing in $a$ at fixed weight vector $u$. If you can simultaneously prove a *monotonicity in $u$* along a one-parameter deformation from a generic $u$ (with $\sum u_i = 1-a$) toward the extremal one-atom configuration $u = (1{-}a, 0, \ldots)$ — at which $\Lambda = 0$ — you'd be done. The deformation that respects the equality on the pivot line is "concentrate mass onto the heaviest coordinate by $\epsilon$"; track $\Lambda$ along this deformation, allowing for jumps when subset sums cross $p$ or $p{-}a$. Numerically check whether each elementary jump is non-negative; if so, identify the structural reason; if not, the deformation needs to be rerouted around troublesome cells.

**My priorities.** (2) numerically first — it's the cleanest from-scratch attack and the $\Psi$ landscape is computable. (3) is the most likely route to a *conceptual* proof if the transportation LP cooperates with the down-set structure. (4) is lowest effort if the right Pelekis lemma exists. (5) is a quick sanity check that may either suggest the extremal mechanism or expose where the inequality is genuinely subtle.
