## Mathematical audit

### Problem statement and interpretation

The universal interpretation is reasonable and faithful: determine those \(p\in[0,1]\) for which the inequality holds for every finite probability vector of nonnegative weights.

However, the introduction still says:

> “I have not closed that lemma…”

This alone prevents the document from being answer-ready.

---

### Coloring criterion and positive families

The coloring criterion remains correct. For rational \(p=a/b\), random coloring into \(b\) colors and selecting a fixed \(a\)-subset of colors correctly produces iid Bernoulli\((a/b)\) selection indicators.

The reciprocal cases \(p=1/k\), \(k\ge3\), are correctly derived.

The pair-counting lemma for \(p=2/b\), \(b\ge6\), still appears valid. The deletion procedure and final count are correct.

The \(p=1/2\) argument is also correct: if
\[
X=\sum_i w_i v_i,
\]
then for \(p=1/2\), \(X\stackrel d=1-X\), so
\[
\Pr[X<1/2]=\Pr[X>1/2],
\]
and hence \(\Pr[X\ge1/2]\ge1/2\).

No fatal issue in these positive partial results.

---

### Multiplicative closure

Proposition `\ref{prop:closure}` is valid. Representing Bernoulli\((pq)\) variables as products of independent Bernoulli\((p)\) and Bernoulli\((q)\) variables works, and the conditional normalization argument is sound.

No issue.

---

### Closedness of \(\mathcal G\)

Proposition `\ref{prop:closed}` is correct.

For fixed finite weights, the set of subset sums is finite, so as \(r\to p\) from either side, the threshold family stabilizes except for the distinction between \(\{w(A)\ge p\}\) and \(\{w(A)>p\}\). The proof handles this correctly.

This proves \(\mathcal G\) is closed, but does not prove that a dense subset of \([0,1/3]\) lies in \(\mathcal G\). Therefore it is only a reduction.

---

### Negative direction

The negative direction remains correct.

For \(1/3<p<1/2\), the equal-weight \(m=3\) example requires at least two successes, giving
\[
3p^2(1-p)+p^3=3p^2-2p^3<p.
\]
For \(1/2<p<1\), the equal-weight \(m=2\) example gives probability \(p^2<p\).

This correctly excludes all \(p\in(1/3,1/2)\cup(1/2,1)\).

### Small-\(p\) lemma

This is the fatal gap. Lemma `\ref{lem:missing}` states:
\[
0\le p\le1/3
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\Pr\!\left[\sum_i w_i v_i\ge p\right]\ge p.
\]
It is not proved, and the paper explicitly says it remains open in the draft.

Thus the full problem is not solved.

### Unit-gap formulation

The unit-gap equivalence appears correct.

Forward direction: applying Lemma `\ref{lem:missing}` to
\[
(1,b_1,\dots,b_n)/(B+1)
\]
and conditioning on the first Bernoulli variable gives the displayed unit-gap inequality.

Converse direction: if all original weights are below \(p\), selecting a maximal pivot \(a\), writing residual weights as \(u_i=ab_i\), and using \(a(B+1)=1\) correctly recovers the pivot inequality, which then gives the original inequality by conditioning on the pivot.

No serious issue here. But it is only an equivalence, not a proof.

\mu_p(\mathcal D)

The \(r\)-wise-intersection paragraph now correctly excludes \(p=0\).

## New coalescence section

The local coalescence formula is essentially correct. For \(q=1-p\), merging two weights \(x\le y\) changes the strict upper-tail probability by
\[
pq\left(
\Pr[q-x-y<Z\le q-y]
-
\Pr[q-x<Z\le q]
\right),
\]
with the displayed endpoint choices matching the strict inequality convention.

However, the proposed coalescence assertion (C) is false.

Take
\[
q=0.7,\qquad p=0.3,\qquad w_1=w_2=w_3=w_4=\frac14.
\]
Here all weights satisfy \(w_i=0.25<p=0.3\), so this is even inside the “nontrivial cap” regime mentioned by the author.

For the original vector,
\[
\Phi_q(w)
=\Pr[\operatorname{Bin}(4,q)\ge3]
=4q^3(1-q)+q^4.
\]
At \(q=0.7\), this equals
\[
4(0.7)^3(0.3)+(0.7)^4=0.6517.
\]

If any pair is coalesced, the new vector is
\[
\left(\frac12,\frac14,\frac14\right).
\]
The event
\[
\frac12\eta_0+\frac14\eta_1+\frac14\eta_2>0.7
\]
occurs exactly when the merged coordinate succeeds and at least one of the two remaining coordinates succeeds. Its probability is
\[
q\bigl(1-(1-q)^2\bigr)
=0.7(1-0.3^2)=0.637.
\]
Thus every possible coalescence strictly decreases \(\Phi_q\):
\[
0.637-0.6517=-0.0147<0.
\]
So assertion (C), as stated, fails.

This does not make the main theorem false; it only invalidates the proposed coalescence route and the claim that computations found no capped counterexample.

There is also a small endpoint inaccuracy: the text says the one-coordinate vector has \(\Phi_q=q\). This is true for \(q<1\), but not for \(q=1\), where the strict event \(\eta>1\) has probability \(0\). Since \(p=0\) is trivial, this is minor, but the statement should be qualified.
