GENERALIZED PERMUTAHEDRA AND
POSITIVE FLAG DRESSIANS
MICHAEL JOSWIG, GEORG LOHO, DANTE LUBER, AND JORGE ALBERTO OLARTE

arXiv:2111.13676v3 [math.CO] 7 Jun 2022

Abstract. We study valuated matroids, their tropical incidence relations, flag matroids and total positivity. This leads to a characterization of permutahedral subdivisions, namely subdivisions of regular permutahedra into generalized permutahedra.
Further, we get a characterization of those subdivisions arising from positive valuated
flag matroids.

1. Introduction
A generalized permutahedron in Rn is a convex polytope such that all its edges are
parallel to ei − ej for i 6= j. Here e1 , e2 , . . . , en denote the standard basis vectors of
Rn . Generalized permutahedra were introduced by Postnikov [28], but they were known
before under different names, most notably in the context of submodular function optimization [13] and discrete convex analysis [23]. Relevant examples of generalized permutahedra include the regular permutahedra, Bruhat interval polytopes, hypersimplices
and more general matroid polytopes. In this way the topic is connected to group and
representation theory, algebraic and tropical geometry, optimization and beyond.
Here we continue the combinatorial study of polyhedral subdivisions of generalized
permutahedra into cells which are again generalized permutahedra [3, 2, 9]. We call
these permutahedral subdivisions. An important special case are the tropical linear spaces,
which amount to regular subdivisions of matroid polytopes with matroidal cells; see [22,
§4.4] and [20, §10.5]. Our first main contribution (Theorem 14) is a description of regular
permutahedral subdivisions of the regular permutahedra in terms of conditions on the
2-skeleton. This result is an adaptation of the characterization of the uniform tropical
linear spaces via the 3-term Plücker relations.
One motivation for research in this direction comes from the wish to understand flags
of tropical linear spaces and flag matroids. As a new tool, we prove a characterization of pairs of valuated matroids in terms of tropical incidence relations (Theorem 10).
Combined with [9], we gain new insights in the flag Dressian, the space of incident valuated matroids. Speyer and Williams [34] and, independently, Arkani-Hamed, Lam, and
Spradlin [4] recently showed that the positive Dressian equals the positive tropical Grassmannian. Our second main result (Theorem 20) is related, as it shows that the valuated
flags which can be realized by a totally positive flag of linear spaces correspond to the
permutahedral subdivisions whose cells are Bruhat interval polytopes.

We denote the symmetric group of degree n as Sym(n). The (regular) permutahedron
Πn ⊆ Rn is defined as the convex hull of the points (σ(1), σ(2), . . . , σ(n)), where σ ranges

by multiplication on the right. We have dim Πn = n−1. Throughout, we identify Sym(n)

5. Explicit computations
The secondary fan of a point configuration stratifies the height functions by the combinatorial type of the regular subdivisions induced. By taking closures and intersecting
with the unit sphere (in the space of height functions) this yields a decomposition of
that sphere into spherical polytopes. Restricting to permutahedral subdivisions yields a
subfan and spherical polytopal subcomplex.
Example 16. We sketch the situation for the regular hexagon Π3 . Its secondary fan, Σ,
is a complete simplicial fan which is 3-dimensional, modulo linealities. In fact, it is the
normal fan of a 3-dimensional associahedron. The 14 maximal cones σ correspond to
the vertices of the associahedron and also to the labeled triangulations of Π3 ; cf. [11,
Figure 1.15]. There are exactly three nontrivial permutahedral subdivisions of Π3 , which
arise from three splits through opposite pairs of vertices of Π3 ; cf. [16]. Each such split
has a 1-dimensional secondary cone, i.e., it forms a ray of Σ. Consequently, the spherical
polytopal complex of permutahedral subdivisions of Π3 comprises three isolated points
in S2 ; see Figure 4.
Tropicalizations of flag varieties for n = 4 and n = 5 have been computed by Bossinger,
Lamboglia, Mincheva and Mohammadi [8]. Here we use the (HEX) and (SQR) conditions
to compute the height vectors in R24 that induce permutahedral subdivisions of the three
dimensional regular permutahedron Π4 , employing polymake [15]. That is, we intersect
the eight tropical hypersurfaces corresponding to hexagonal facets with fourteen linear
subspaces corresponding to hexagons and squares in the two-skeleton of Π4 . We obtain a
six-dimensional polyhedral fan, which we denote Φ4 , and which has a three-dimensional
lineality subspace. The f -vector of the fan Φ4 reads (20, 76, 75). Out of the 75 maximal
cones 72 are simplicial cones, i.e., they are spanned by three rays. The remaining three
cones are spanned by four rays each. In the secondary fan of Π4 , such a cone would

7. Concluding Remarks
In Theorem 14 we showed that the permutahedral subdivisions of a permutahedron
can be recognized by looking at the 2-skeleton. Yet there are distinct permutahedral
subdivisions of Π4 with the same induced subdivision on the 2-skeleton; cf. Figure 3. We
call the latter a permutahedral pattern on the 2-skeleton.
Question 22. For a given permutahedral pattern on the 2-skeleton of Πn , what is the set
of all permutahedral subdivisions of Πn inducing that pattern?
In Section 5 we computed the fans of permutahedral subdivisions of Π3 and Π4 .
Question 23. What can be said in general about the topological type of the spherical
polytopal complex formed by the height functions of permutahedral subdivisions of Πn ?
Note that this complex is essentially the compression of the flag Dressian FlDr(n); see
also [8]. There is reason to believe that the question above may be difficult. First, the
homology of the ordinary Dressians is only known for a few small cases; see [22, §5.4]
Second, already the secondary fan of Π4 is out of reach of current software tools such as
TOPCOM [30], mptopcom [19] or Gfan [18].
While here we analyzed the regular permutahedra and their subpermutahedra, it is
most natural to extend this line of work to generalized permutahedra.
Question 24. How does the characterization in Theorem 14 generalize to permutahedral
subdivisions of arbitrary generalized permutahedra?
