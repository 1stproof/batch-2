# Problem statement

Let $M$ be a matroid, and let $\Trop(M)$ be the corresponding tropical linear space, i.e., the closure of the Bergman fan of $M$ in tropical projective space. The relative Dressian of $M$ is 
\[ \Dr(M) :=\{\text{ tropical linear subspaces of $\Trop M$ }\}, \]
which is partially ordered by inclusion of tropical linear spaces. 

Suppose $M$ admits an order-reversing involution $F\mapsto F^\perp$ on the lattice of flats $\cLL(M)$. Note that there is a natural inclusion $\cLL(M)^{\op} \subset \Dr(M)$ given by $F\mapsto \Trop(M/F\oplus U_{0,F})$.

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{Question.}  Is there an order-reversing involution $\Phi$ on $\Dr(M)$ that extends the involution $F\mapsto F^\perp$?

\section*{0. Conventions and two local facts}

Let $E$ be the ground set, $r=\rk M$, and $\mu_M$ the trivial rank $r$
valuated matroid supported on the bases of $M$.  I use the standard
model of the relative Dressian: a rank $k$ point is a projective
valuated matroid $\nu$ with $\nu\preceq\mu_M$, where $\preceq$ denotes
valuated quotient.  Brandt--Eur--Zhang \cite{BEZ} identify this order with
inclusion of projective tropical linear spaces,
\[
        \Trop(\theta)\subseteq\Trop(\nu)
        \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad \theta\preceq\nu .
\]
For valuated matroids $p,q$ of ranks $a\le b$, $p\preceq q$ is
equivalent to the incidence-Plucker relations
\[
 \min_{i\in J\setminus I}\{p(I\cup i)+q(J\setminus i)\}
 \quad\hbox{attained at least twice}                         \tag{IP}
\]
for all $I\in\binom{E}{a-1}$ and $J\in\binom{E}{b+1}$.  All-$\infty$
minima are regarded as attained at every displayed index.

\begin{criterion}[nested incidence suffices]\label{crit:local}
Let $p,q$ be valuated matroids of ranks $a<b$ whose ordinary supports
are in quotient order.  If {\rm (IP)} holds whenever $I\subseteq J$,
then {\rm (IP)} holds for all $I,J$.
\end{criterion}

\begin{proof}
For a rank $a$ valuated matroid $p$ and an $(a-1)$-set $I$, write
$C^*_p(I)_e=p(I\cup e)$, with value $\infty$ if $e\in I$.  We use the
standard cocircuit-generation theorem of Dress--Wenzel/Speyer \cite{DW,Speyer}: the
tropical linear space of a valuated matroid is the closed tropical convex
hull of its valuated cocircuits.

Fix arbitrary $I\in\binom{E}{a-1}$ and $J\in\binom{E}{b+1}$, and put
$c_j=q(J\setminus j)$ for $j\in J$.  If all $c_j$ are $\infty$ there is
nothing to prove.  Otherwise $J$ contains a $q$-basis.  Since the
ordinary support of $p$ is a quotient of the ordinary support of $q$,
every $q$-basis spans the support matroid of $p$ (indeed,
$a-\rk_p(Q)\le b-\rk_q(Q)=0$ for a $q$-basis $Q$); hence the
restriction $p|J$ has rank $a$.

For every $S\subseteq J$ with $|S|=a-1$, the nested hypothesis with
upper set $J$ says precisely that the cocircuit $C^*_{p|J}(S)$ is
tropically orthogonal to $c=(c_j)_{j\in J}$.  The set of vectors
orthogonal to a fixed $c$ is a tropical hyperplane, hence is tropically
convex; therefore it contains the whole tropical linear space of $p|J$.
It remains only to note that $C^*_p(I)|_J$ is a vector of $p|J$: for
each valuated circuit of $p|J$, indexed by a set
$D\in\binom{J}{a+1}$, the orthogonality relation
\[
 \min_{e\in D}\{p(I\cup e)+p(D\setminus e)\}
\]
is exactly a Plucker relation for $p$.  Hence $C^*_p(I)|_J$ is
orthogonal to $c$, which is the desired arbitrary incidence relation.
\end{proof}

We also use Wang's codimension-one theorem in the following precise
form.  In Wang's terminology the ``three-term incidence relations''
consist of degenerate, parallel, and concurrent hyperplane relations;
the displayed tropical minimum below is only the concurrent relation.

\begin{proposition}[elementary quotient test]\label{prop:elementarytest}
Let $\eta$ be a rank $d$ valuated matroid and let
$\theta:\binom{E}{d-1}\to{\mathbb R}\cup\{\infty\}$ be nonzero.  Suppose
that
\[
        \theta(A)=\infty\quad\hbox{whenever }A
        \hbox{ is dependent in the support matroid of }\eta              \tag{D}
\]
and, when $d\ge2$, for every $S\in\binom{E}{d-2}$ and distinct $x,y,z\notin S$,
\[
\min\{\theta(Sx)+\eta(Syz),\,
      \theta(Sy)+\eta(Sxz),\,
      \theta(Sz)+\eta(Sxy)\}                                      \tag{C}
\]
is attained at least twice.  Then $\theta$ is a valuated matroid and
$\theta\preceq\eta$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For $d=1$ this is the unique rank-zero quotient.  Let $d\ge2$.  By
Wang's Theorem 4.3 \cite{Wang}, $\Dr^1(\eta)$ is cut out by the
degenerate, parallel, and concurrent three-term incidence relations.
Condition (D) is exactly the degenerate part, and (C) contains the
concurrent part.  It remains only to see that the parallel part follows
from (C).  Write $A=Di$ and $B=Dj$ for independent $(d-1)$-sets in the
support matroid of $\eta$ with the same closure, and choose $k$ outside
that closure, so $Dik$ and $Djk$ are bases.  Applying (C) to
$D,i,j,k$, the third upper coordinate $\eta(Dij)$ is $\infty$, while
$\eta(Dik)$ and $\eta(Djk)$ are finite.  Hence
\[
        \theta(Di)+\eta(Djk)=\theta(Dj)+\eta(Dik),
\]
which is Wang's parallel hyperplane relation.  Thus all of Wang's
defining relations hold, so $\theta$ represents a codimension-one
tropical linear subspace of $\Trop(\eta)$.
\end{proof}

Finally, if $\nu\preceq\mu_M$ has rank $k$, we use its valuated Higgs
completion
\[
 h_j(B)=
 \begin{cases}
 \min\{\nu(C): B\subseteq C,\ |C|=k\}, & j\le k,\ |B|=j,\\
 \min\{\nu(C): C\subseteq B,\ |C|=k\}, & j\ge k,\ B\in\mathcal I_j(M),\\
 \infty, & j\ge k,\ B\notin\mathcal I_j(M).
 \end{cases}                                                \tag{1}
\]
Murota's truncation and relative elongation theorems \cite[Theorems 2.1 and 4.1]{Murota}, equivalently the
Dress--Terhalle well-layering construction \cite{DT}, give valuated matroids
$h_j$ and adjacent quotients $h_j\preceq h_{j+1}$.  The top layer is
projectively $\mu_M$: if $B'=B-e+f$ are adjacent $M$-bases and
$C\subseteq B$ minimizes (1), then either $e\notin C$ and $C\subseteq B'$,
or the incidence relation for $\nu\preceq\mu_M$ with lower set $C-e$ and
upper set $B\cup f$ gives a $k$-subset of $B'$ of value at most
$\nu(C)$; by symmetry the two top-layer values are equal.

\section*{1. Flat-lattice duality and flat constancy}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:modular}
If $\cL(M)$ admits an order-reversing involution $X\mapsto X^\perp$,
then $\cL(M)$ is modular and $\rk(X^\perp)=r-\rk(X)$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The anti-automorphism reverses maximal chains, so it sends rank $i$ to
rank $r-i$.  Applying it to the semimodular inequality for the geometric
lattice $\cL(M)$ gives the reverse inequality, hence equality.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[flat constancy]\label{lem:flatconst}
Let $\nu\preceq\mu_M$ have rank $k$.  If $B,B'$ are independent
$k$-subsets of $M$ with $\cl(B)=\cl(B')$, then $\nu(B)=\nu(B')$; and
$\nu(A)=\infty$ whenever $A$ is dependent in $M$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The support matroid of $\nu$ is an ordinary quotient of $M$, so its
bases are independent in $M$.  For $k>0$ it suffices to compare
$B=A\cup b$ and $B'=A\cup b'$ spanning the same rank $k$ flat.  Choose
one complement $C$ so that both $AbC$ and $Ab'C$ are $M$-bases.  In the
incidence relation for $\nu\preceq\mu_M$ with lower set $A$ and upper
set $A\cup\{b,b'\}\cup C$, the only finite $\mu_M$-terms are the
$b$- and $b'$-terms.  Thus $\nu(Ab)=\nu(Ab')$.
\end{proof}

Write $\bar\nu(X)$ for the common value on independent $k$-sets spanning
the rank $k$ flat $X$, and put $\bar\nu(X)=\infty$ if no basis of $\nu$
spans $X$.

\section*{2. The polar transform}

For a rank $k$ quotient $\nu\preceq\mu_M$ define
\[
 \nu^{\perp_M}(A)=
 \begin{cases}
   \bar\nu\bigl((\cl A)^\perp\bigr),
      & A\text{ independent in }M,\ |A|=r-k,\\
   \infty,&\text{otherwise.}
 \end{cases}                                                 \tag{2}
\]
This is projectively well-defined, and applying it twice recovers the
original flat coordinates.

\begin{lemma}[ordinary polar rank]\label{lem:ordinary}
Let $N$ be an ordinary quotient of $M$ of rank $k$, with rank function
$\rho_N$.  Then
\[
        \rho^\perp(A)=\rk_M(A)-k+\rho_N((\cl A)^\perp)          \tag{3}
\]
is a matroid rank function.  Its bases are precisely the finite support
of (2).  If $N_p$ is a quotient of $N_q$, then
$N_q^{\perp_M}$ is a quotient of $N_p^{\perp_M}$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since $N$ is a quotient of $M$, $\rho_N$ is constant on $M$-closures:
if $e\in\cl_M(A)$, then the $N$-rank increment from $A$ to $Ae$ is at
most the corresponding $M$-increment, namely $0$.  First restrict (3) to
flats.  For flats $X\le Y$,
\[
\rho^\perp(Y)-\rho^\perp(X)
 =\rk(Y)-\rk(X)-\bigl(\rho_N(X^\perp)-\rho_N(Y^\perp)\bigr)
\]
is between $0$ and $\rk(Y)-\rk(X)$ by the strong-map increment
inequality, so $\rho^\perp$ is monotone with unit increments on flats.
For flats $X,Y$, modularity of $\rk_M$ and the identities
$(X\wedge Y)^\perp=X^\perp\vee Y^\perp$ and
$(X\vee Y)^\perp=X^\perp\wedge Y^\perp$ reduce submodularity of
$\rho^\perp$ to submodularity of $\rho_N$.

For arbitrary subsets, $\rho^\perp(A)=\rho^\perp(\cl_M A)$.  If
$X=\cl A$ and $Y=\cl B$, then
$\cl(A\cap B)\le X\wedge Y$ and $\cl(A\cup B)=X\vee Y$; by monotonicity
on flats, submodularity for $X,Y$ implies submodularity for $A,B$.
The unit-increment property also passes from flats to subsets.  Thus
(3) is a matroid rank function.  An $M$-independent set $A$ of size
$r-k$ has full $\rho^\perp$-rank exactly when
$\rho_N((\cl A)^\perp)=k$, and $M$-dependent sets cannot be bases; this
is precisely the finite support of (2).

If $N_p$ is a quotient of $N_q$, then on every interval the rank
increment of $N_p$ is at most that of $N_q$.  The displayed formula for
increments therefore reverses the inequality for the polar ranks, which
is exactly the strong-map criterion saying that $N_q^{\perp_M}$ is a
quotient of $N_p^{\perp_M}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[elementary opposition]\label{lem:elementary}
Let $p\preceq q\preceq\mu_M$, with $\rk p=d$ and $\rk q=d+1$.  Then
$q^{\perp_M}$ and $p^{\perp_M}$ satisfy all adjacent three-term
relations of ranks $r-d-1$ and $r-d$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Fix $A$ of size $r-d-2$ and distinct $x,y,z\notin A$.  Put
$U=\cl(A)$, $W=\cl(Axyz)$, and $\delta=\rk W-\rk U$.  If $A$ is
dependent, or if $\delta\le1$, each term contains a dependent set of the
required size and hence is $\infty$.

If $\delta=2$, let $G=W^\perp$ and $H=U^\perp$, of ranks $d$ and
$d+2$.  Whenever the term indexed by $x$ is finite, $Ayz$ spans $W$,
so its $p$-factor is the common value $\bar p(G)$; its $q$-factor is
$\bar q((\cl(Ax))^\perp)$, and similarly for $y,z$.  In the rank-two
interval $[U,W]$, a loop over $U$ makes all terms involving it
infinite, and two parallel elements give equal corresponding
$q$-coordinates.  Thus only the case of three non-parallel points over
$U$ needs proof.  Choose a basis $S$ of $G$, representatives
$a_x,a_y,a_z$ for the three dual rank-one flats over $G$ in $[G,H]$,
and a complement $C$ of $H$ to a basis of $M$.  In the incidence
relation for $q\preceq\mu_M$ with lower set $S$ and upper set
$S\cup\{a_x,a_y,a_z\}\cup C$, the only finite $\mu_M$-terms are those
obtained by deleting one of $a_x,a_y,a_z$.  Hence the three displayed
$q$-coordinates have a non-unique minimum; adding the common
$\bar p(G)$ proves the transformed relation.

If $\delta=3$, then $x,y,z$ form a Boolean frame over $U$.  Let
$G=W^\perp$ and
\[
 P_x=(\cl(Ayz))^\perp,\quad P_y=(\cl(Axz))^\perp,\quad
 P_z=(\cl(Axy))^\perp .
\]
These are the three atoms over $G$ in the dual frame, and
$(\cl(Ax))^\perp=P_y\vee P_z$, with the analogous identities for
$y,z$.  Choose a basis $S$ of $G$ and representatives
$a_e\in P_e\setminus G$.  The adjacent incidence relation for
$p\preceq q$ applied to the lower set $S$ and to
$S\cup\{a_x,a_y,a_z\}$ is exactly the transformed three-term relation,
using flat constancy to identify the displayed coordinates.
\end{proof}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:set}
The formula $\nu\mapsto\nu^{\perp_M}$ defines an involution of the set
underlying $\Dr(M)$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Insert $\nu$ in the complete flag (1).  We prove inductively that
\[
        h_r^{\perp_M}\preceq h_{r-1}^{\perp_M}\preceq\cdots
        \preceq h_0^{\perp_M}=\mu_M .
\]
After harmless scalar normalization, the right endpoint
$h_0^{\perp_M}=\mu_M$ is clear from (2).  If $r\le1$, there is
nothing more to check beyond the automatic rank-zero quotient.  Now assume $r\ge2$.
Suppose $0\le j\le r-2$ and $h_j^{\perp_M}$ has
already been proved valuated.  Since $h_j\preceq h_{j+1}$, Lemma
\ref{lem:ordinary} says that the ordinary support of
$h_{j+1}^{\perp_M}$ is an elementary quotient of the ordinary support of
$h_j^{\perp_M}$; in particular it satisfies the degenerate support
condition (D) relative to $h_j^{\perp_M}$.  Lemma
\ref{lem:elementary} gives the displayed concurrent relations (C) for
the pair $h_{j+1}^{\perp_M},h_j^{\perp_M}$.  Proposition
\ref{prop:elementarytest} then implies
$h_{j+1}^{\perp_M}\preceq h_j^{\perp_M}$.  This proves the induction
down to $h_{r-1}^{\perp_M}$; the last rank-zero quotient
$h_r^{\perp_M}\preceq h_{r-1}^{\perp_M}$ is automatic.  Hence $\nu^{\perp_M}$, one layer of the flag, lies in
$\Dr(M)$.  Involutivity follows from (2) and $(X^\perp)^\perp=X$.
\end{proof}

\section*{3. Order reversal}

\begin{lemma}[circuit minima]\label{lem:circuit}
Let $w\preceq\mu_M$ have rank $k\ge1$, let $A$ be an independent
$(k-1)$-set of $M$, and let $C$ be a circuit of $M/A$ with $|C|\ge2$.
Then $\min_{i\in C}w(A\cup i)$ is attained at least twice.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let $F=\cl(A\cup C)$.  For every $i\in C$, $A\cup(C-i)$ is independent
and spans $F$.  Choose one complement $D$ of $F$ to an $M$-basis.  In
the incidence relation for $w\preceq\mu_M$ with lower set $A$ and upper
set $A\cup C\cup D$, the finite $\mu_M$-terms are exactly those indexed
by $i\in C$.
\end{proof}

\begin{theorem}\label{thm:order}
If $p\preceq q\preceq\mu_M$, then
\[
        q^{\perp_M}\preceq p^{\perp_M}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Let $\rk p=a$ and $\rk q=b$.  If $a=b$, the strong-map inequalities applied to
$\emptyset\subseteq A$ and to $A\subseteq E$ force the two ordinary
support rank functions to be equal.  Then $p$ and $q$ are projectively
equal: the equal-rank incidence relation on adjacent bases shows
$p(B)-q(B)$ is constant on the common basis graph.  If $a=0$, then $p^{\perp_M}=\mu_M$, so the
assertion is $q^{\perp_M}\preceq\mu_M$; if $b=r$, it is the vacuous
rank-zero lower quotient.  Hence assume $0<a<b<r$.

By Proposition \ref{prop:set} the polars are valuated matroids, and by
Lemma \ref{lem:ordinary} their ordinary supports are in quotient order.
By Criterion \ref{crit:local} it remains to prove nested incidence.
Fix $A\subseteq T$ with
$|A|=r-b-1$, $|T|=r-a+1$, set $B=T\setminus A$, and
$m=|B|=b-a+2$.  If $A$ is dependent all terms are $\infty$.  Otherwise
put $U=\cl(A)$, $W=\cl(T)$, and $\delta=\rk W-\rk U$.  A finite term
forces $T-i$ to be independent, hence $\delta\ge m-1$; since
$\delta\le m$, only two cases remain.

If $\delta=m$, then $T$ is independent over $U$.  Let $G=W^\perp$ and
$H=U^\perp$, of ranks $a-1$ and $b+1$.  The interval generated by $B$
is Boolean; after applying $^\perp$, the flats
$X_i=(\cl(T-i))^\perp$ are the atoms over $G$, and
$Y_i=(\cl(Ai))^\perp$ is the join of all $X_j$ with $j\ne i$.  Choose a
basis $S$ of $G$ and representatives $x_i\in X_i\setminus G$.  The
nested incidence relation for $p\preceq q$ with lower set $S$ and upper
set $S\cup\{x_i:i\in B\}$ is exactly the desired transformed relation.

If $\delta=m-1$, then $B$ has nullity one in $M/A$, hence a unique
circuit $C$.  A loop circuit gives only $\infty$ terms.  Otherwise, for
$i\notin C$ the set $T-i$ is dependent; for $i\in C$ it is independent
and spans $W$, so the $p^{\perp_M}(T-i)$ factor is the common value
$\bar p(W^\perp)$.  Lemma \ref{lem:circuit}, applied to
$w=q^{\perp_M}$ and the circuit $C$ of $M/A$, gives two minima among
the remaining $q^{\perp_M}(Ai)$ terms.  This proves nested incidence.
\end{proof}

\section*{4. Agreement with flats}

Let $F$ be a flat of rank $f$, and let $q_F$ be the trivial rank
$r-f$ valuated matroid of $M/F\oplus U_{0,F}$.  For an independent
$(r-f)$-set $B$ with $Z=\cl(B)$,
\[
        q_F(B)<\infty\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad Z\vee F=\hat1.
\]
For an independent $f$-set $A$ with $Y=\cl(A)$, formula (2) gives
\[
 q_F^{\perp_M}(A)<\infty
 \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad Y^\perp\vee F=\hat1 .
\]
By modularity, since $\rk(Y^\perp)=r-f$ and $\rk F=f$, this is
equivalent to $Y^\perp\wedge F=\hat0$.  Applying the anti-automorphism
gives $Y\vee F^\perp=\hat1$, the basis condition for
$M/F^\perp\oplus U_{0,F^\perp}$.  The finite values are zero on both
sides.  Thus the polar transform sends the flat $F$ to $F^\perp$.

Combining Proposition \ref{prop:set}, Theorem \ref{thm:order}, and this
flat computation, the map
\[
        \Phi([\nu])=[\nu^{\perp_M}]
\]
is an order-reversing involution of $\Dr(M)$ extending the given
order-reversing involution of $\cL(M)$.

\section*{Research notes}

Problem: Given a matroid $M$ and the relative Dressian $\Dr(M)$ of
tropical linear subspaces contained in $\Trop(M)$, suppose
$\cL(M)$ has an order-reversing involution $F\mapsto F^\perp$.
The embedded copy of $\cL(M)^{\rm op}$ sends a flat $F$ to the quotient
matroid $M/F\oplus U_{0,F}$.  We seek an order-reversing involution
of $\Dr(M)$ extending this flat opposition.

\subsection*{Conventions and quotient criterion}

Let $\mu_M$ be the trivial rank $r=\rk M$ valuated matroid supported
on the bases of $M$.  A rank $k$ point of $\Dr(M)$ is represented by
a projective rank $k$ valuated matroid $\nu$ with
$\nu\preceq\mu_M$, where $p\preceq q$ means that $p$ is a valuated
quotient of $q$.  Inclusion of projective tropical linear spaces is
the quotient order:
\[
\Trop(\theta)\subseteq\Trop(\nu)\Longleftrightarrow \theta\preceq\nu.
\]
Valuated matroids are considered up to adding a common scalar to all
finite Plucker coordinates.

For valuated matroids $p,q$ of ranks $a\le b$, the nested incidence
criterion is
\[
\mathcal I_{a,b}(p,q;S,T):\quad
\min_{i\in T\setminus S}\{p(S\cup i)+q(T\setminus i)\}
\text{ is attained at least twice},
\]
for $|S|=a-1$, $|T|=b+1$, $S\subseteq T$.  Together with the
valuated matroid axioms and the underlying ordinary quotient condition,
this standard nested cryptomorphic form is the quotient relation; it is
equivalent to the arbitrary-index incidence-Plucker relations.
For consecutive ranks it is the three-term relation
\[
 \min\{p(Sx)+q(Syz),p(Sy)+q(Sxz),p(Sz)+q(Sxy)\}.
\]
The convention is that, if all terms are $\infty$, the minimum is
attained at every index.

Elementary codimension-one test: Wang's ``three-term incidence
relations'' for \(\Dr^1(\eta)\) are a package consisting of
degenerate, parallel, and concurrent hyperplane relations.  The
concurrent tropical minimum alone is not sufficient for arbitrary
functions.  A useful sufficient form is: if \(\eta\) is valuated of
rank \(d\), \(\theta\) is a nonzero function on \((d-1)\)-sets,
\(\theta(A)=\infty\) for every \(A\) dependent in the support matroid
of \(\eta\), and all concurrent minima
\[
\min\{\theta(Sx)+\eta(Syz),\theta(Sy)+\eta(Sxz),
       \theta(Sz)+\eta(Sxy)\}
\]
vanish tropically, then \(\theta\) is a valuated elementary quotient
of \(\eta\).  The parallel relations follow by taking
\(A=Di,B=Dj\) with the same \(\eta\)-closure and choosing
\(k\) outside that closure; the term \(\eta(Dij)\) is then
\(\infty\), so the concurrent relation gives the required equality.

\subsection*{Modularity forced by the involution}

A finite flat lattice is ranked and semimodular.  If it has an
anti-automorphism $x\mapsto x^\perp$, ranks are reversed:
$\rk(x^\perp)=r-\rk(x)$.  Applying the anti-automorphism to
semimodularity gives the reverse inequality, hence
\[
\rk(x)+\rk(y)=\rk(x\wedge y)+\rk(x\vee y)
\]
for all $x,y$.  Therefore $\cL(M)$ is modular and
\[
(x\vee y)^\perp=x^\perp\wedge y^\perp,\qquad
(x\wedge y)^\perp=x^\perp\vee y^\perp.
\]
No simplification reduction is necessary: loops lie in
$\cl(\emptyset)$ and parallel representatives receive equal values by
flat constancy.

\subsection*{Flat constancy}

If $\nu\preceq\mu_M$ has rank $k$, then finite $\nu$-bases are
independent in $M$ because the underlying matroid of $\nu$ is a
quotient of $M$.  Also $\nu(B)$ depends only on $\cl_M(B)$ for
independent $k$-sets $B$.

Adjacent-basis proof for $0<k\le r$: compare $B=A b$ and
$B'=A b'$ with $\cl(A b)=\cl(A b')$.  Choose $C$ of size $r-k$ such
that $A b C$ is a basis of $M$; then $A b' C$ is also a basis.  In
$\mathcal I_{k,r}(\nu,\mu_M;A,A b b' C)$ only the $b,b'$ terms have
finite $\mu_M$ factor, since deleting an element of $C$ leaves the
dependent subset $A b b'$.  Thus $\nu(A b)=\nu(A b')$.  The case
$k=0$ is trivial.

Thus a rank $k$ quotient gives flat coordinates
\[
\bar\nu(X)=\nu(B)\quad\text{for }\rk X=k,\ \cl(B)=X,
\]
and $\nu(A)=\bar\nu(\cl A)$ for independent $A$, while dependent
$A$ has value $\infty$.

\subsection*{One-point completion through a given quotient}

For a single quotient $\nu\preceq\mu_M$ of rank $k$, standard
valuated truncation and relative Higgs elongation insert $\nu$ into a
complete flag:
\[
 h_j(B)=
 \begin{cases}
 \min\{\nu(C): B\subseteq C,\ |C|=k\}, & j\le k,\ |B|=j,\\
 \min\{\nu(C): C\subseteq B,\ |C|=k\}, & j\ge k,\ B\in\mathcal I_j(M),\\
 \infty, & j\ge k,\ B\notin\mathcal I_j(M).
 \end{cases}
\]
This is Murota's valuated truncation/elongation or
$M^\natural$-convex convolution with the indicator of $\mathcal I(M)$.
It gives $h_0\preceq\cdots\preceq h_r=\mu_M$ up to scalar and
$h_k=\nu$.

Endpoint check for $h_r$: for an $M$-basis $B$, let
$m(B)=\min\{\nu(C):C\subseteq B,\ |C|=k\}$.  If
$B'=B-e+f$ and $C\subseteq B$ minimizes $m(B)$, then if $e\notin C$
we have $C\subseteq B'$.  If $e\in C$, apply the incidence relation
for $\nu\preceq\mu_M$ with lower set $C-e$ and upper set $B\cup f$.
The $e$-term has value $\nu(C)$, so either it is not minimal or
there is another finite term of value at most $\nu(C)$; that term
gives a $k$-subset of $B'$.  Hence $m(B')\le m(B)$, and by symmetry
$m$ is constant on the basis graph.  After scalar normalization
$h_r=\mu_M$.

Important distinction: this one-point completion is used only to prove
that the opposition transform maps the underlying set of $\Dr(M)$ to
itself.  It is not a general partial-flag completion theorem for an
arbitrary pair $\theta\preceq\nu$; full order reversal is proved
directly by incidence duality.

\subsection*{Candidate transform and elementary opposition}

For a rank $k$ quotient $\nu$ define
\[
\nu^{\perp_M}(A)=
\begin{cases}
\bar\nu((\cl A)^\perp),& A\text{ independent in }M,\ |A|=r-k,\\
\infty,&\text{otherwise}.
\end{cases}
\]
Adding a scalar to $\nu$ adds the same scalar to $\nu^{\perp_M}$.
The formula is involutive on flat coordinates.

Local elementary opposition: Let $p\preceq q\preceq\mu_M$ with
$\rk p=d$, $\rk q=d+1$.  To check the elementary three-term relation
for $q^{\perp_M},p^{\perp_M}$, fix $A$ of size $r-d-2$ and
$x,y,z\notin A$.  Let $U=\cl(A)$ and $W=\cl(Axyz)$, with
$\delta=\rk W-\rk U$.

If $A$ is dependent or $\delta\le1$, all relevant transformed terms
are $\infty$.

If $\delta=2$, set $G=W^\perp$ (rank $d$) and $H=U^\perp$ (rank
$d+2$).  All non-forced transformed terms share the same $p$-coordinate
$\bar p(G)$.  A term indexed by $e$ has $q$-coordinate
$\bar q(L_e)$ for $L_e=(\cl(Ae))^\perp$, a rank $d+1$ flat in
$[G,H]$.  In a rank-two interval, either two non-forced terms have the
same $L_e$ (hence are equal and all other terms are $\infty$), or
there are three distinct points.  In the three-distinct-points case,
use $q\preceq\mu_M$: choose a basis $S$ of $G$, representatives
$\ell_x,\ell_y,\ell_z$ in the three points, and a complement $C$
outside $H$.  The incidence relation for $q\preceq\mu_M$ with lower
set $S$ and upper set $S\ell_x\ell_y\ell_z C$ has precisely the
three finite $\mu_M$-terms $\bar q(L_x),\bar q(L_y),\bar q(L_z)$.

If $\delta=3$, set $G=W^\perp$ (rank $d-1$) and $H=U^\perp$ (rank
$d+2$).  Define
\[
P_x=(\cl(Ayz))^\perp,\quad P_y=(\cl(Axz))^\perp,\quad
P_z=(\cl(Axy))^\perp
\]
and
\[
Q_x=(\cl(Ax))^\perp,\quad Q_y=(\cl(Ay))^\perp,\quad
Q_z=(\cl(Az))^\perp.
\]
Modularity in the rank-three interval gives that $P_x,P_y,P_z$ are
distinct atoms over $G$ and
\[
Q_x=P_y\vee P_z,\quad Q_y=P_x\vee P_z,\quad Q_z=P_x\vee P_y.
\]
Choosing representatives $a_x,a_y,a_z$ over a basis $S$ of $G$, the
original three-term relation for $p\preceq q$ is exactly the
transformed relation.

Applying this elementary lemma along a one-point completion flag
through $\nu$ shows $\nu^{\perp_M}\preceq\mu_M$ for every
$\nu\preceq\mu_M$.  Thus the formula gives an involution on the
underlying projective valuated quotients representing $\Dr(M)$.

\subsection*{Underlying ordinary polar quotient}

For the nested incidence cryptomorphism one also has to track the
underlying ordinary quotient.  If $N$ is an ordinary quotient of $M$ of
rank $k$, the support defined by the polar construction has the standard
rank formula on flats $X$ of $M$:
\[
\rho_{N^{\perp_M}}(X)=\rk_M(X)-k+\rho_N(X^\perp).
\]
This is the usual dual rank formula for matroids on a modular
geometric lattice (the $q$-matroid/polarity formula).  Its bases are
exactly independent $(r-k)$-sets whose polar rank-$k$ flat spans $N$,
matching the support of $\nu^{\perp_M}$.

If $N_p$ is a quotient of $N_q$, then for flats $Y^\perp\subseteq
X^\perp$ the rank increments satisfy
\[
\rho_{N_p}(X^\perp)-\rho_{N_p}(Y^\perp)
\le
\rho_{N_q}(X^\perp)-\rho_{N_q}(Y^\perp).
\]
Substituting in the displayed polar rank formula gives
\[
\rho_{N_q^{\perp_M}}(Y)-\rho_{N_q^{\perp_M}}(X)
\le
\rho_{N_p^{\perp_M}}(Y)-\rho_{N_p^{\perp_M}}(X),
\]
which is the ordinary strong-map criterion for
$N_q^{\perp_M}$ to be a quotient of $N_p^{\perp_M}$.

\subsection*{Circuit-minimum lemma for relative quotients}

If $w\preceq\mu_M$ has rank $k$, $A$ is independent of size $k-1$,
and $C$ is a circuit of the contraction $M/A$ with $|C|\ge2$, then
\[
\min_{i\in C} w(Ai)
\]
is attained at least twice.  Proof: for each $i\in C$,
$A\cup(C-i)$ is independent and spans the same flat.  Choose a
common complement $D$ such that $A\cup(C-i)\cup D$ is an $M$-basis.
Apply $\mathcal I_{k,r}(w,\mu_M;A,A\cup C\cup D)$.  The finite
$\mu_M$-terms are exactly those indexed by $C$; deleting an element
of $D$ leaves the circuit $C$ over $A$.

\subsection*{Direct full-incidence duality}

Let $p\preceq q\preceq\mu_M$ with ranks $a\le b$.  Having already
proved that transforms are quotients of $\mu_M$, and using the ordinary
polar quotient formula for the underlying matroids, it remains to prove
the nested incidence relations for $q^{\perp_M}\preceq p^{\perp_M}$.
If $b=r$, the lower
transformed rank is zero and there are no incidence relations.  Assume
$b<r$.

Fix $A\subseteq T$ with
\[
|A|=r-b-1,\qquad |T|=r-a+1,
\]
and put $B=T\setminus A$, $m=|B|=b-a+2$, $U=\cl(A)$, $W=\cl(T)$.
If $A$ is dependent all transformed terms are $\infty$.  Otherwise
$\rk U=r-b-1$.  A transformed term can be finite only if $T-i$ is
independent, which requires
$\rk W-\rk U\ge b-a+1=m-1$.  Thus the only nontrivial cases are
$\delta=\rk W-\rk U=m$ and $\delta=m-1$.

Nondegenerate case $\delta=m$: $T$ is independent (this case is empty
if $a=0$).  Let $G=W^\perp$, $H=U^\perp$, of ranks $a-1$ and
$b+1$.  For $i\in B$ set
\[
X_i=(\cl(T-i))^\perp,\qquad Y_i=(\cl(Ai))^\perp.
\]
For $J\subseteq B$, the flats $\cl(A\cup J)$ have ranks
$\rk U+|J|$ and form the Boolean sublattice generated by the frame.
After anti-isomorphism, the $X_i$ are atoms over $G$ and
\[
Y_i=\bigvee_{j\ne i}X_j .
\]
Choose a basis $S$ of $G$ and representatives $x_i\in X_i\setminus G$.
Then $S\cup\{x_i:i\in B\}$ has size $b+1$ and the original incidence
relation $\mathcal I_{a,b}(p,q;S,S\cup\{x_i\})$ has terms
\[
p(Sx_i)+q(S\cup\{x_j:j\ne i\})
=\bar p(X_i)+\bar q(Y_i)
=p^{\perp_M}(T-i)+q^{\perp_M}(Ai).
\]
This is exactly the transformed relation.

Degenerate case $\delta=m-1$: in $M/A$, the set $B$ has nullity one
and hence a unique circuit $C$.  If $C$ is a singleton loop, then all
transformed terms are $\infty$.  If $|C|\ge2$, the indices outside
$C$ leave $T-i$ dependent.  For $i\in C$, $T-i$ is independent with
closure $W$, so the $p^{\perp_M}$-coordinate is the common value
$\bar p(W^\perp)$.  The circuit-minimum lemma applied to the quotient
$q^{\perp_M}\preceq\mu_M$ and to the circuit $C$ of $M/A$ gives that
\[
\min_{i\in C} q^{\perp_M}(Ai)
\]
is attained at least twice.  Adding the common $p$-coordinate proves
the transformed incidence relation.

Thus $p\preceq q$ implies $q^{\perp_M}\preceq p^{\perp_M}$ for all
rank gaps.  This is the full order reversal.

\subsection*{Embedded flats}

For an embedded flat $F$ of rank $f$, $q_F=M/F\oplus U_{0,F}$ has
finite rank $r-f$ flat coordinate at $Z$ iff $Z\vee F=\hat1$.  For an
independent $f$-set $A$ with $Y=\cl(A)$,
\[
q_F^{\perp_M}(A)<\infty
\Longleftrightarrow Y^\perp\vee F=\hat1
\Longleftrightarrow Y^\perp\wedge F=\hat0
\Longleftrightarrow Y\vee F^\perp=\hat1,
\]
using modularity and complementary ranks.  This is exactly the basis
condition for $q_{F^\perp}=M/F^\perp\oplus U_{0,F^\perp}$; all finite
values are zero.  Hence the opposition transform extends the given
flat involution.


\subsection*{Local-to-global flag criterion (used to bridge nested and arbitrary incidence)}

The full BEZ incidence relation for a valuated quotient $p\preceq q$ of
ranks $a\le b$ is indexed by arbitrary
$I\in\binom E{a-1}$ and $J\in\binom E{b+1}$:
\[
\min_{i\in J\setminus I}\{p(I\cup i)+q(J-i)\}\quad\text{attained twice.}
\]
In the written proof the direct modular-lattice computation checks the
nested subfamily $I\subseteq J$.  The bridge is Murota's local exchange
theorem for $M^\natural$-concave functions / valuated generalized
matroids: for a two-step flag with ordinary supports in quotient order,
the local exchange tests are precisely the valuated matroid axioms for
the two homogeneous layers plus the nested incidence relations; the
global exchange inequalities are the arbitrary incidence-Plucker
relations.  Thus nested incidence plus valuatedness and ordinary support
quotient is equivalent to the full quotient relation.  For complete
flags, the same theorem says that adjacent three-term nested relations
are equivalent to a complete valuated flag.  Formula
\[
 h_j(B)=
 \begin{cases}
 \min\{\nu(C):B\subseteq C,\ |C|=k\},&j\le k,\\
 \min\{\nu(C):C\subseteq B,\ |C|=k\},&j\ge k,\ B\in\mathcal I_j(M),\\
 \infty,&j\ge k,\ B\notin\mathcal I_j(M)
 \end{cases}
\]
is the Dress--Terhalle/Murota truncation and Higgs elongation of the
independent-set valuation associated with $\nu$.

\subsection*{Ordinary polar support rank proof}

For an ordinary quotient $N$ of $M$ of rank $k$, the polar support has
bases
\[
\{A:\ |A|=r-k,\ A\in\mathcal I(M),\ \rho_N((\cl A)^\perp)=k\}.
\]
A subset $A$ with $Y=\cl_M(A)$ is independent in this polar matroid iff
$A$ is $M$-independent and $\rho_N(Y^\perp)=k$.  Necessity is clear
from containment in a polar basis.  Conversely choose an $N$-basis
inside $Y^\perp$, let $W$ be its $M$-closure, and extend $A$ inside
$W^\perp$ to an $M$-basis of $W^\perp$.

Therefore for a flat $X$,
\[
\rho_{N^{\perp_M}}(X)=
\max\{\rk Y: Y\le X,\ \rho_N(Y^\perp)=k\}
=r-\min\{\rk W:W\ge X^\perp,\ \rho_N(W)=k\}.
\]
If $G=X^\perp$ and $\rho_N(G)=s$, the minimum is
$\rk G+k-s$: the lower bound follows because quotient rank increases
by at most one along a cover; equality is obtained by repeatedly adding
an element outside the current $N$-closure, which is also outside the
current $M$-flat since $N$-flats are $M$-flats.  Hence
\[
\rho_{N^{\perp_M}}(X)=\rk_M(X)-k+\rho_N(X^\perp).
\]
If $N_p$ is a quotient of $N_q$, then rank increments in $N_p$ are at
most those in $N_q$; applying this formula to $Y^\perp\le X^\perp$
gives that $N_q^{\perp_M}$ is a quotient of $N_p^{\perp_M}$.

\subsection*{Round 6 local-criterion sharpening}

A useful way to formulate the local flag input is via the following
standard cryptomorphisms.  For a two-step ordinary flag support, i.e.
two matroids \(N_a,N_b\) in quotient order, the union of the two basis
layers is a \(0\)-\(1\) generalized polymatroid.  Murota--Shioura's
local exchange theorem for \(M^\natural\)-concave functions on
generalized polymatroids (Theorem 4.2 in the 2018 simpler-exchange
paper; equivalently the local-exchange theorem in Murota's book) says
that local exchange on this effective domain promotes to global
exchange.  In the two homogeneous layers, local exchange is exactly the
nested incidence relation
\[
\min_{i\in T\setminus S}\{p(Si)+q(T-i)\}\quad(S\subseteq T),
\]
while global exchange is Brandt--Eur--Zhang's valuated quotient
condition, equivalently the arbitrary incidence-Plucker relations.  This
is the intended rigorous bridge from nested to arbitrary incidence,
including \(\infty\)-valued nonuniform supports, since the domain is
part of the hypotheses and \(\infty\) just means outside the effective
domain.

For full flags one can alternatively invoke Jarra--Lorscheid: over the
perfect tropical tract, the adjacent Plucker flag relations define a
flag \(\mathbb T\)-matroid, and their Proposition 2.21 identifies these
with BEZ valuated flag matroids/chains of tropical linear spaces.  This
is the cleanest reference for the statement that a complete sequence
with ordinary complete-flag support and adjacent local three-term
relations is a complete valuated flag.

The support side can be handled independently of valuations.  If
\(N\) is an ordinary quotient of \(M\) of rank \(k\), define
\[
\rho^\perp(X)=\rk_M(X)-k+\rho_N(X^\perp)
\]
on \(M\)-flats \(X\), extended to subsets by \(M\)-closure.  Modularity
of \(\cL(M)\), submodularity of \(\rho_N\), and the strong-map
increment inequalities show that \(\rho^\perp\) is a matroid rank
function.  Its independent sets are exactly the \(M\)-independent sets
\(A\) with \(\rho_N((\cl A)^\perp)=k\).  Thus the polar support in the
coordinate formula is an ordinary matroid before any valuated assertion
is made, and quotient order is reversed by comparing rank increments in
the displayed formula.

Formula (1) for one-point completion should be understood as
Dress--Terhalle/Murota well-layering of the independent-set valuation
attached to \(\nu\).  The ordinary supports are: for \(j\le k\), the
rank-\(j\) independent sets of the support \(N\) of \(\nu\); for
\(j\ge k\), the \(j\)-sets independent in \(M\) that contain an
\(N\)-basis.  These are the ordinary truncations and Higgs lifts between
\(N\) and \(M\).  The top layer is projectively \(\mu_M\): adjacent
\(M\)-bases \(B,B'=B-e+f\) have equal values
\(\min\{\nu(C):C\subseteq B, |C|=k\}\) by the incidence relation for
\(\nu\preceq\mu_M\), as detailed above.

Edge cases in the order-reversal incidence check: if \(a=0\), then
\(p^{\perp_M}=\mu_M\) and \(q^{\perp_M}\preceq\mu_M\) is already the
set-preservation proposition; if \(b=r\), the transformed lower rank is
zero and no incidence relations are present.  In the nondegenerate
case \(0<a\le b<r\), the Boolean-frame assertion follows because
\(\delta=|T\setminus A|\) means \(T\setminus A\) is independent over
\(\cl(A)\), so all subjoins have the expected ranks and form the
Boolean sublattice generated by that frame in the modular interval.



\subsection*{Resolved local-to-global criteria}

Two-rank endpoint criterion.  Let \(p,q\) be valuated matroids of ranks
\(a<b\), with ordinary supports in quotient order.  The nested
incidence relations
\[
\mathcal I_{a,b}(p,q;S,T)\quad(S\subseteq T,\ |S|=a-1,\ |T|=b+1)
\]
imply the full arbitrary BEZ incidence relations.  The clean proof uses
cocircuit generation, not \(M^\natural\)-convexity of the two
non-adjacent layers.  For \(I\in\binom E{a-1}\) write the valuated
cocircuit \(C_p^*(I)_e=p(Ie)\).  Fix \(J\in\binom E{b+1}\) and set
\(c_j=q(J-j)\) for \(j\in J\).  If all \(c_j=\infty\), the incidence
relation is trivial.  Otherwise \(J\) contains a \(q\)-basis \(Q\), and
the ordinary quotient rank-increment inequality gives
\[
a-\rk_p(Q)\le b-\rk_q(Q)=0,
\]
so \(J\) spans \(p\) and \(p|J\) has rank \(a\).  The nested relations
with upper set \(J\) say that \(c\) is orthogonal to every cocircuit of
\(p|J\).  By the Dress--Wenzel/Speyer cocircuit-generation theorem, the
tropical linear space of \(p|J\) is the closed tropical convex hull of its
cocircuits; the hyperplane of vectors orthogonal to fixed \(c\) is
tropically convex, so it contains all vectors of \(p|J\).  Finally
\(C_p^*(I)|J\) is a vector of \(p|J\), since orthogonality to each
circuit \(D\in\binom J{a+1}\) is exactly the Pluecker relation
\[
\min_{e\in D}\{p(Ie)+p(D-e)\}\ \text{twice}.
\]
Thus \(C_p^*(I)|J\perp c\), which is the full incidence relation.

Elementary adjacent criterion used in the polar induction.  Jidong
Wang's theorem (arXiv:2412.12059v2, Theorem 4.3) states that for any
valuated matroid \(\mu\), the codimension-one relative Dressian
\(\Dr^1(\mu)\) is cut out by the three-term incidence relations, where
``three-term'' includes degenerate hyperplanes, parallel hyperplanes,
and concurrent triples.  Hence the safe induction step is not
``concurrent minima alone'', but rather: an already-valuated upper layer
\(\eta\), a lower function \(\theta\) whose finite support is
independent in the support matroid of \(\eta\), and all concurrent
minima imply \(\theta\preceq\eta\).  Degenerate relations are the
support condition; parallel relations are forced from a concurrent
relation with one upper coordinate \(\infty\).  In the polar flag,
the ordinary polar-support lemma supplies exactly this support condition,
and the elementary opposition lemma supplies the concurrent minima.

The earlier attempted shortcut through Murota--Shioura's local
\(M^\natural\)-exchange theorem is not appropriate for the two-rank
endpoint criterion: the union of two non-adjacent basis layers need not
be an \(M^\natural\)-convex domain.  Murota's truncation/relative
elongation theorems are still used separately for the valuated Higgs
completion between \(\nu\) and \(\mu_M\).

\subsection*{Safer established pieces}

The flat-lattice anti-automorphism forces modularity.  Flat constancy of
\(\nu\preceq\mu_M\) is solid: adjacent \(k\)-bases in the same \(M\)-flat
are compared by an incidence relation with \(\mu_M\), and a common
complement to the rank-\(k\) flat works for both bases.

The ordinary polar support is controlled by
\[
\rho^\perp(A)=\rk_M(A)-k+\rho_N((\cl A)^\perp).
\]
For flats this is monotone, submodular, and has unit increments because
rank increments in \(N\) are bounded by those in \(M\); extension by
\(M\)-closure gives a matroid rank function.  If \(N_p\) is a quotient of
\(N_q\), then the increment inequality reverses after applying the
lattice anti-automorphism, so \(N_q^{\perp_M}\) is a quotient of
\(N_p^{\perp_M}\).

The elementary opposition lemma for adjacent ranks is reliable.  In the
rank-two interval case, finite terms are either equal by parallelism or
come from the circuit-minimum property of \(q\preceq\mu_M\).  In the
rank-three interval case, the dual frame converts the transformed
three-term relation exactly into the adjacent incidence relation for
\(p\preceq q\).

The order-reversal proof for arbitrary \(p\preceq q\) verifies the
nested transformed incidence relations.  For \(A\subseteq T\), set
\(B=T\setminus A\) and \(m=|B|=b-a+2\).  If
\(\rk\cl(T)-\rk\cl(A)=m\), the interval generated by \(B\) is Boolean and
the relation is exactly the original nested incidence for \(p\preceq q\)
after dualizing the frame.  If the rank difference is \(m-1\), \(B\) has
a unique circuit in \(M/A\); all finite terms share the \(p^\perp\)
coordinate and the circuit-minimum lemma for \(q^\perp\preceq\mu_M\)
gives two minima.  If the rank difference is at most \(m-2\), all terms
are infinite.

Potential direct single-layer route: prove that for every
\(\nu\preceq\mu_M\), the polar \(\nu^{\perp_M}\) satisfies Pluecker
relations.  The Boolean rank-four case dualizes directly to a Pluecker
relation for \(\nu\).  The rank-two degeneration is elementary.  The
hard degeneration is a rank-three interval: it asks for the polar of a
rank-one quotient of a rank-three modular geometry to satisfy rank-two
Pluecker.  Full incidence of \(\nu\preceq\mu_M\), not just circuit
minima, appears necessary here; PG\((2,3)\) tests show that Bergman
circuit-minimum weights alone are insufficient, while full quotient
weights passed all small checks.
