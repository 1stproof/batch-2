## Referee report on `answer.tex`

### Overall verdict

I **cannot certify** `answer.tex` as a complete rigorous solution. The proposed construction is plausible and several parts check out, but the proof has an **essential gap** in the “local flag criterion,” which is then used critically in Proposition 2.4 and Theorem 3.2. In particular, the cited Murota–Shioura local exchange theorem does **not**, as written, justify the claimed equivalence between full incidence-Plücker relations and the nested subfamily for arbitrary rank gaps. The proof also contains several smaller but nontrivial missing justifications.

The LaTeX contract itself appears satisfied: I compiled `answer.tex` twice with `pdflatex`; compilation succeeded, the PDF has 6 pages, the document class is exactly `\documentclass[12pt]{article}`, and I found no prohibited margin, spacing, or in-document font-size commands.

## Literature validation

Brandt–Eur–Zhang do define tropical incidence-Plücker relations for arbitrary index sets \(I'\in\binom{[n]}{r-1}\), \(J'\in\binom{[n]}{s+1}\), not only nested ones, and Proposition 4.2.3 identifies points of the flag Dressian with valuated matroid quotients. ([par.nsf.gov](https://par.nsf.gov/servlets/purl/10391296)) Their Theorem 4.3.1 also supports the convention that valuated quotient order corresponds to inclusion of projective tropical linear spaces. ([par.nsf.gov](https://par.nsf.gov/servlets/purl/10391296))

Murota–Shioura’s Theorem 4.2 does give a local-exchange characterization of \(M^\natural\)-concavity, but the theorem requires an \(M^\natural\)-convex effective domain and uses specific local conditions \(L1,L2,L3\), not the arbitrary-rank nested incidence relations stated as (N). ([educ.titech.ac.jp](https://educ.titech.ac.jp/iee/publications/file/pub_15646.pdf)) This is the main point where `answer.tex` overclaims what the cited theorem provides.

The Jarra–Lorscheid citation is broadly relevant: their work identifies tropical flag matroids / valuated flag matroids with flag matroids over the tropical tract, and the cited Proposition 2.21 is indeed about the equivalence with Brandt–Eur–Zhang valuated flag matroids. ([sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S000187082300539X?utm_source=openai)) But `answer.tex` still needs to spell out precisely why the displayed adjacent three-term relations alone imply the full complete valuated flag structure in the nonuniform \(\infty\)-valued setting.

## Detailed audit

### Opening conventions

The valuated-matroid interpretation of \(\Dr(M)\) is reasonable and supported by the Brandt–Eur–Zhang quotient/inclusion theorem. However, the problem statement defines \(\Dr(M)\) as a poset of tropical linear subspaces, while the answer works with projective valuated matroids. The answer should explicitly state that it is using the standard identification between projective valuated matroids modulo scalar and their projective tropical linear spaces. As written, this is mostly implicit.

The full incidence-Plücker condition (A) is correctly stated in spirit. It matches the literature’s arbitrary \(I,J\) incidence relations, not just nested ones.

### The “local flag criterion” lemma

This is the most serious problem.

The first assertion says full incidence (A) is equivalent to nested incidence (N), assuming the ordinary supports are in quotient order. This may be true, but `answer.tex` does **not** prove it. The given proof says:

> “For two ranks, the support condition says exactly that the union of the two basis layers is an ordinary flag matroid, i.e. a \(0\)-\(1\) generalized polymatroid.”

This statement is false for non-adjacent ranks. For example, take supports \(U_{2,6}\) and \(U_{4,6}\), which are in ordinary quotient order. The union of the 2-subsets and 4-subsets is not an \(M^\natural\)-convex set / generalized polymatroid: with \(X=\{1,2,3,4\}\), \(Y=\{1,2\}\), and \(i=3\in X\setminus Y\), the \(B^\natural\)-exchange axiom fails because the intermediate 3-subsets are not in the domain and \(Y\setminus X=\varnothing\). Thus Murota–Shioura’s local theorem cannot be applied to the two-layer domain in the way claimed.

Moreover, Murota–Shioura’s local theorem concerns local exchange conditions \(L1,L2,L3\), whereas relation (N) is a nested incidence relation with \(|T\setminus S|=b-a+2\), which is not local when \(b-a>1\). The proof does not explain how to pass from Murota–Shioura’s theorem to exactly (N), nor how (N) implies all arbitrary incidence-Plücker relations (A).

This gap is essential because Theorem 3.2 later proves only nested incidence for \(q^{\perp_M}\preceq p^{\perp_M}\) and then invokes this lemma to conclude the full quotient relation.

The complete-flag part of the lemma is also under-justified. It may be recoverable from \(M^\natural\)-concavity on all ranks or from Jarra–Lorscheid, but the answer does not precisely state which theorem says that the displayed adjacent three-term relations alone imply all Plücker and flag-Plücker relations for every layer, especially with nonuniform supports and \(\infty\)-coordinates.

The insertion formula (1) is likewise cited as Dress–Terhalle–Murota well-layering, but the proof only checks the top layer. It does not prove or cite a precise theorem showing that all \(h_j\) are valuated matroids and form a complete valuated flag.

### Lemma 1.1: modularity of the flat lattice

This argument is essentially correct. A lattice anti-automorphism reverses ranks, and applying it to semimodularity gives the reverse inequality, hence modularity. No serious issue here.

### Lemma 1.2: flat constancy

The proof is mostly sound. The use of the incidence relation with lower set \(A\) and upper set \(A\cup\{b,b'\}\cup C\) correctly forces \(\nu(Ab)=\nu(Ab')\), including the case where both are \(\infty\). Minor point: the proof should explicitly say that \(C\) can be chosen so both \(AbC\) and \(Ab'C\) are bases because \(Ab\) and \(Ab'\) span the same rank-\(k\) flat.

### Definition of \(\nu^{\perp_M}\)

The formula is well-defined if flat constancy holds. The involutivity on flat coordinates is plausible. No major issue here.

### Lemma 2.1: ordinary polar rank

The lemma is important, but the proof is too compressed.

The claim that
\[
\rho^\perp(X)=\rk_M(X)-k+\rho_N(X^\perp)
\]
extends to a matroid rank function requires more verification than given. One must check:

1. \(\rho^\perp\) is constant on \(M\)-closures.
2. \(0\le \rho^\perp(A)\le |A|\) for arbitrary subsets.
3. \(\rho^\perp\) has unit increments.
4. Submodularity holds for arbitrary subsets, not only \(M\)-flats.
5. The use of \(\rho_N(X^\perp\vee Y^\perp)\) is legitimate, since \(\rho_N\) is a set rank function and the join is an \(M\)-closure.

These facts are likely true using the quotient rank-increment inequality, but they are not proved. The final assertion that arbitrary subsets reduce to \(M\)-closures is also not justified. This is a fixable but real gap.

### Lemma 2.2: elementary opposition

The case analysis appears mostly correct, but several details are left implicit:

- In the \(\delta=2\) case, the proof should explicitly identify the transformed three terms
  \[
  q^{\perp_M}(Ax)+p^{\perp_M}(Ayz),\quad
  q^{\perp_M}(Ay)+p^{\perp_M}(Axz),\quad
  q^{\perp_M}(Az)+p^{\perp_M}(Axy).
  \]
- The claims about loops and coincident points in the rank-two interval should be spelled out carefully.
- In the \(\delta=3\) case, the proof should state that \(G=W^\perp\) has rank \(d-1\), so that a basis \(S\) of \(G\) has the right size for the adjacent incidence relation of \(p\preceq q\).

These are mostly clarity issues, not necessarily fatal.

### Proposition 2.3: set-level involution on \(\Dr(M)\)

This proposition depends directly on the local flag criterion and on the unproved completeness of the flag constructed by (1). Since those inputs are not rigorously established, this proposition is not fully proved.

If Lemma 2.2 and a correct complete-flag local-to-global theorem were supplied, the argument would be plausible.

### Lemma 3.1: circuit minima

The idea is correct, but the proof should specify that \(D\) is chosen as a common complement to the flat spanned by \(A\cup C\). As written, “Choose \(D\)” after “For each \(i\in C\)” is ambiguous: the same \(D\) must work for all \(i\in C\) in the single incidence relation.

This is fixable.

### Theorem 3.2: full order reversal

The internal case analysis for nested incidence is strong and appears essentially correct. The \(\delta=m\) Boolean-frame argument and the \(\delta=m-1\) unique-circuit argument are plausible.

However, the theorem only checks nested incidence. The conclusion that this gives the full valuated quotient relation relies entirely on the first part of Lemma 1, whose proof is invalid for non-adjacent ranks. Therefore Theorem 3.2 is not rigorously established.

The edge cases \(a=0\) and \(b=r\) are handled adequately, though the \(b=r\) case should explicitly note that the rank-zero lower transformed valuated matroid is automatically a quotient of the upper one.

### Agreement with the flat involution

This section is essentially correct. The equivalence
\[
Y^\perp\vee F=\hat 1
\iff
Y^\perp\wedge F=\hat 0
\iff
Y\vee F^\perp=\hat 1
\]
uses modularity and complementary ranks correctly. The basis condition for \(M/F^\perp\oplus U_{0,F^\perp}\) is correctly identified.

This part would be acceptable once the global construction of \(\Phi\) is made rigorous.
