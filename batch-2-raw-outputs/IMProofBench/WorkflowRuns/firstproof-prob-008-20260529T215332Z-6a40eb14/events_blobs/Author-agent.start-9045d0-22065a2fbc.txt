**Plausibility.** I believe the lemma is true but is genuinely non-trivial — I do not know an off-the-shelf theorem that closes all cases. In flat coordinates the tropical Plücker + incidence relations become lattice-theoretic identities on $\bar\nu$, so the question is precisely whether this defining set is self-dual under $\perp$.

**Strategy I would try.**

(i) *Reduce to irreducible modular geometric lattices.* By Birkhoff, a finite modular geometric lattice is a product of irreducibles: rank-2 lines $U_{2,n}$, projective planes (possibly non-Desarguesian), and Desarguesian $PG(d-1,K)$ for $d\ge3$ (Veblen–Young). A polarity either restricts to one irreducible or swaps two isomorphic factors; the swap is formal. Rank-$\le2$ is immediate.

(ii) *Realizable case ($d\ge3$).* Every polarity of $PG(d-1,K)$ comes from a non-degenerate sesquilinear form on $K^d$. The algebraic $W\mapsto W^\perp$ on Grassmannians of $K^d$ tropicalizes to the desired involution on the *realizable* part of $\Dr$, with all flag/incidence relations preserved automatically.

(iii) *Combinatorial extension to all of $\Dr$.* Since $\Dr$ strictly contains the tropical Grassmannian, this is the actual content. Using $(X\vee Y)^\perp=X^\perp\wedge Y^\perp$, a 3-term Plücker for $\theta=\nu^\perp$ at $(S',\{a,b,c,d\})$ reads, in flat coordinates of $\bar\nu$, as a 3-term identity at the pairwise meets of $4$ hyperplanes $h_i=F\wedge \cl(i)^\perp$ inside the rank-$(k+2)$ flat $F=\cl(S')^\perp$. Independence of $S'ij$ makes the $h_i$ pairwise distinct hyperplanes of $F$ and the meets $h_i\wedge h_j$ rank-$k$ flats of $F$. The subset-indexed incidence $\mathcal I_{a,b}$ translates the same way, with more hyperplanes. So Lemma~1 reduces to a *local dual Plücker–incidence statement*:

> *For any rank-$k$ valuated quotient $\nu$ of a rank-$(k+\ell)$ modular geometric matroid, the dual 3-term/incidence relations at meets of $\le \ell{+}2$ hyperplanes follow from the standard $\nu$-Plücker–incidence relations.*

For the base case ($\ell=2$, 3-term) this is the standard rank-1 / rank-$(k+1)$ duality on a single matroid, cleanest via the Dress–Wenzel vector–cocircuit cryptomorphism: polarity exchanges valuated vectors of $\nu$ with valuated cocircuits. For larger $\ell$, project modulo a hyperplane and recurse on rank.

**Counterexample worries.**

- *Non-Desarguesian projective planes with polarities* (Hughes, certain translation planes): not coordinatized over a division ring, so (ii) does not apply. I would explicitly test a small example with strictly tropical (non-realizable) rank-1 values before trusting the general lemma. Rank 3 is where I'd worry most.
- *Non-realizable points of $\Dr$ even for Desarguesian $M$.* Step (ii) only covers the realizable locus; step (iii) carries the real load. Verify at least one non-realizable Plücker vector numerically.
- *The lemma is about flags.* The incidence $\mathcal I_{a,b}(p_i,p_j)$ between two non-trivial quotients also has to dualize correctly; the translation in (iii) handles it, but the local lemma must include this case, not only incidence with $\mu_M$.

**References to consult carefully.**

- Brandt–Eur–Zhang §3 for the precise flag relations and the restriction-to-a-flat operation.
- Dress–Wenzel; Murota, *Matroids and Matrices* / *Discrete Convex Analysis*, for the vector/cocircuit cryptomorphism (natural language for (iii)).
- Hirai 2019 — check whether his lattice cryptomorphism for valuated matroids is *symmetric under lattice anti-automorphism*. If yes, this could close all modular cases uniformly and bypass the case analysis above.
- Borovik–Gelfand–White, *Coxeter Matroids* (type $BCD$ = polar-space matroids); recent papers on tropical orthogonal/symplectic Grassmannians (Rincón; Joswig and collaborators) may contain a directly usable statement.

The Flat-coordinate duality lemma is **true**, and your candidate opposition works exactly as defined. 

To rigorously prove it without getting bogged down in horrific relation-by-relation subset combinatorics, I recommend a hybrid algebraic-combinatorial strategy based on the classification of modular geometric lattices. This directly gives you the "precise known theorem" approach you are looking for, while cleanly handling non-realizable quotients.

Here is the decomposition of the proof:

**1. Reduction via Classification**
By the Maeda–Birkhoff classification, every finite modular geometric lattice is a direct product of irreducible components: Boolean lines $U_{1,2}$, uniform rank-2 matroids $U_{2,n}$, projective planes (rank 3), and higher projective geometries $PG(d, K)$ for finite fields $K$ ($d \ge 3$). 
Because the relative Dressian of a direct sum of matroids canonically decouples into the product of their Dressians, and any orthocomplement $X \mapsto X^\perp$ must permute direct summands of the same type, you only need to prove the lemma for the individual irreducible components.

**2. The Representable Cases (Desarguesian components)**
This handles all components of rank $\ge 4$, as well as Desarguesian projective planes. Here, the flat lattice is representable over a field $K$. The involution $X \mapsto X^\perp$ is a geometric polarity arising from a non-degenerate reflexive bilinear/sesquilinear form on the underlying vector space $V$. 
Algebraically, this polarity induces an isomorphism $W \mapsto W^\perp$ on the full flag variety. In Plücker coordinates, evaluating the orthogonal subspace on a tuple of vectors $A$ gives $P_{W^\perp}(A) = \pm P_W(B)$, where $B$ is any basis of the polar flat $(\cl A)^\perp$. 

Taking the tropical valuation of this algebraic identity yields exactly your candidate formula: $\operatorname{val}(P_{W^\perp}(A)) = \bar{p}((\cl A)^\perp)$. Because the algebraic Plücker and incidence ideals are mapped to themselves under this monomial substitution, the tropical prevarieties they define (the Dressians and tropical flag varieties) are unconditionally invariant under your candidate map. Since a tropical prevariety's defining relations are preserved regardless of whether individual points are algebraically realizable, this perfectly handles non-realizable valuated quotients.

**3. The Low-Rank and Non-Representable Cases**
You can handle the remaining isolated cases combinatorially:
*   **$U_{2,n}$:** As you noted, the orthocomplement is simply a permutation $\sigma$ of the ground set. The subset-indexed relation for rank-1 quotients (minimum attained twice on every 3-element subset) is invariant under the full symmetric group $S_n$, so substituting $p^\perp(i) = p(\sigma(i))$ trivially preserves the relations.
*   **Non-Desarguesian Projective Planes:** These have rank 3, so proper quotients only have ranks 1 and 2. The relation $\mathcal{I}_{1,3}$ relies on sets of size 4. By the geometric point-line duality of the plane, a configuration of 4 points and their collinearities perfectly dualizes to a configuration of 4 lines and their intersections. This directly bijects the rank-1 subset relations to the dual rank-2 subset relations without needing algebraic representability.

By framing the proof this way, you use the fundamental theorem of tropical geometry (invariance of tropical prevarieties under ideal-preserving monomial maps) to do the heavy lifting for the general subset-indexed relations, isolating the combinatorial checks to trivial low-rank exceptions.
