\begin{center}
{\bf Uniqueness of the invariant measure}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Let \(E=C_0([0,1])\), \(H=L^2(0,1)\), and let \(S_t\) be the Dirichlet heat
semigroup generated by \(\frac12\Delta_D\).  I use the Dirichlet version of
the Athreya--Butkovsky--L\^e--Mytnik construction stipulated in the problem.
In particular, when \(b_n=G_{1/n}\), the assumed convergence of the
regularizations is used in the pointwise sense
\[
        P_t^n\Phi(x)\longrightarrow P_t\Phi(x),
        \qquad x\in E,\quad t>0,\quad \Phi\in C_b(E).        \tag{A}
\]
The proof uses the local-time identification of the ABLM solution for
non-negative measure drifts recalled in Proposition \ref{prop:lt} below.  This
is a consequence of the same random-control estimates by which ABLM treat
finite non-negative Radon measure drifts, transferred to the assumed Dirichlet
setting.

The statement is true: the semigroup has exactly one invariant probability
measure.

\section*{The reversible Gibbs measure}

Let \(\gamma\) be Brownian bridge measure on \(E\), i.e. the centered Gaussian
measure with covariance \((-\Delta_D)^{-1}\).  Put
\[
  \Lambda(v)=\int_0^1\1_{\{v(r)>0\}}\,dr,\qquad
  \pi(dv)=Z^{-1}e^{2\Lambda(v)}\,\gamma(dv).              \tag{1}
\]
Since \(0\le \Lambda\le1\), \(\pi\sim\gamma\), and \(\pi\) has full support.
The value of the indicator at zero is irrelevant because Brownian bridge
paths spend zero Lebesgue time at zero.

For \(b_n=G_{1/n}\) set
\[
   B_n(a)=\int_{-\infty}^aG_{1/n}(r)\,dr,\qquad
   \pi_n(dv)=Z_n^{-1}\exp\left(2\int_0^1B_n(v(r))\,dr\right)\gamma(dv).
\]
Then \(0\le B_n\le1\), \(B_n(a)\to \1_{\{a>0\}}\) for \(a\ne0\), and dominated
convergence gives
\[
        \|\pi_n-\pi\|_{\TV}\longrightarrow0 .              \tag{2}
\]
For fixed \(n\), \(b_n\) is bounded and smooth.  Gaussian integration by parts
for \(\gamma\) gives, on smooth cylinder functions,
\[
 L_nF(v)=\frac12{\rm Tr}\,D^2F(v)
       +\left\langle \frac12\Delta_Dv+b_n(v),DF(v)\right\rangle_H
\]
and
\[
 \int_E G\,L_nF\,d\pi_n
 =-\frac12\int_E\langle DF,DG\rangle_H\,d\pi_n
 =\int_E F\,L_nG\,d\pi_n .                                \tag{3}
\]
The standard variational/mild theory for bounded Lipschitz reaction terms
identifies this generator with the regularized SPDE.  Hence \(P_t^n\) is
\(\pi_n\)-invariant and reversible.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:gap}
The measure \(\pi\) is \(P_t\)-invariant.  Moreover \(P_t\) extends to a
symmetric Markov contraction on \(L^2(\pi)\), and there is \(c>0\) such that
\[
       \Var_\pi(P_t f)\le e^{-2ct}\Var_\pi(f),
       \qquad f\in L^2(\pi),\ t\ge0 .                      \tag{4}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For \(f,g\in C_b(E)\), (A), (2), and the invariance and symmetry of
\((P_t^n,\pi_n)\) imply
\[
 \int P_tf\,d\pi=\lim_n\int P_t^nf\,d\pi_n=\lim_n\int f\,d\pi_n=\int f\,d\pi
\]
and
\[
 \int gP_tf\,d\pi=\lim_n\int gP_t^nf\,d\pi_n
       =\lim_n\int fP_t^ng\,d\pi_n=\int fP_tg\,d\pi .
\]
Jensen's inequality gives the \(L^2(\pi)\)-contraction.

The Brownian bridge measure satisfies a Gaussian Poincare inequality.  Since
\(d\pi_n/d\gamma\) is bounded above and below uniformly in \(n\), the
Holley--Stroock perturbation argument gives
\[
      \Var_{\pi_n}(F)\le C\,\frac12\int |DF|_H^2\,d\pi_n
\]
with \(C\) independent of \(n\).  By reversibility,
\[
      \Var_{\pi_n}(P_t^n f)\le e^{-2ct}\Var_{\pi_n}(f),
      \qquad f\in C_b(E),
\]
with \(c=C^{-1}\).  Passing to the limit with (A) and (2), then using density
of \(C_b(E)\) in \(L^2(\pi)\), yields (4).
\end{proof}

\section*{Fixed-time absolute continuity}

The only point not supplied by the \(L^2(\pi)\) argument is an
all-starting-points smoothing statement.  It follows from the local-time form
of the ABLM drift and a time-shifted Girsanov argument.

\begin{proposition}[local-time form of the drift]\label{prop:lt}
Let \(u\) be the ABLM solution started from deterministic \(x\in E\), and
write
\[
        u_t=S_tx+V_t+K_t,\qquad
        V_t=\int_0^tS_{t-s}\,dW_s .
\]
There is a predictable family \((\mu_s)_{s>0}\) of finite positive measures on
\([0,1]\) such that
\[
        K_t=\int_0^t S_{t-s}\mu_s\,ds,\qquad t>0,          \tag{5}
\]
and, for every \(t>0\),
\[
        \int_0^t \|S_{(t-s)/2}\mu_s\|_H^2\,ds<\infty
        \quad\hbox{a.s.}                                  \tag{6}
\]
Here \(\mu_s\) is the spatial local-time measure at level \(0\) of
\(r\mapsto u_s(r)\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
This is the standard measure-drift identification in the ABLM construction,
so we recall the argument.  For each \(s>0\), the stochastic convolution
\(V_s(\cdot)\) is a Brownian bridge plus a \(C^1\) Gaussian function: in the
Dirichlet sine basis its covariance is
\((-\Delta_D)^{-1}(I-e^{s\Delta_D})\), whose high-frequency asymptotics agree
with Brownian bridge covariance.  The ABLM random-control estimates for
non-negative measure drifts imply that \(S_sx+K_s\) is spatially
\(C^\alpha\), for every \(\alpha<1\), locally uniformly for \(s>0\).  Thus
\(r\mapsto u_s(r)\) is a Brownian semimartingale plus a zero-quadratic-
variation \(C^\alpha\) perturbation with \(\alpha>1/2\).  Its spatial local
time \(L_s^a(r)\) exists and satisfies the occupation formula
\[
   \int_0^1\varphi(r)g(u_s(r))\,dr
   =\int_\R g(a)\left(\int_0^1\varphi(r)\,d_rL_s^a(r)\right)da
\]
for bounded Borel \(g\) and continuous \(\varphi\).  Hence
\(G_{1/n}(u_s(r))\,dr\) converges weakly to
\(\mu_s(dr):=d_rL_s^0(r)\).

The defining ABLM approximation of the drift gives, uniformly on compact
time intervals and in \(x\),
\[
 \int_0^tS_{t-s}\big(G_{1/n}(u_s)\big)\,ds\longrightarrow K_t
\]
in probability.  Passing to the above occupation-density limit identifies
\(K\) with (5).

Finally, the same random-control estimate gives, for every finite \(T\) and
\(m\ge2\),
\[
        \E\big[\mu_s([0,1])^m\big]\le C_{m,T}\,(1+s^{-m/4}),
        \qquad 0<s\le T .                                \tag{7}
\]
Concretely, this is first proved uniformly for
\(\mu_s^n(dr)=G_{1/n}(u_s(r))dr\) in the ABLM random-control argument for
finite positive measure drifts; the weak convergence
\(\mu_s^n\Rightarrow\mu_s\) and Fatou's lemma give (7).
For a finite measure \(\nu\), the one-dimensional Dirichlet heat kernel
satisfies
\[
        \|S_r\nu\|_H^2
        =\iint p_{2r}(a,b)\,\nu(da)\nu(db)
        \le C r^{-1/2}\|\nu\|_{\TV}^2 .                   \tag{8}
\]
Combining (7) with (8),
\[
 \E\int_0^t\|S_{(t-s)/2}\mu_s\|_H^2ds
 \le C\int_0^t (t-s)^{-1/2}(1+s^{-1/2})\,ds<\infty,
\]
which implies (6).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[time-shifted Girsanov]\label{lem:girs}
For every \(x\in E\) and \(t>0\),
\[
        P_t(x,\cdot)\ll \gamma .
\]
Consequently \(P_t(x,\cdot)\ll\pi\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(u\) start from \(x\) and let \(\mu\) be as in Proposition
\ref{prop:lt}.  Define the \(H\)-valued adapted process
\[
        \widehat F_s
        =2\1_{[t/2,t]}(s)\,S_{t-s}\mu_{2s-t}.
\]
By (6),
\[
        \int_0^t\|\widehat F_s\|_H^2\,ds
        =2\int_0^t\|S_{(t-r)/2}\mu_r\|_H^2\,dr<\infty
        \quad\hbox{a.s.}                                  \tag{9}
\]
Let
\[
 \widehat u_r=S_rx+V_r+\int_0^r S_{r-s}\widehat F_s\,ds,\qquad 0\le r\le t .
\]
After localization by the stopping times on which the integral in (9) is
bounded, the classical Hilbert-space Girsanov theorem shows that the law of
\((\widehat u_r)_{0\le r\le t}\) is absolutely continuous with respect to the
Ornstein--Uhlenbeck path law
\((S_rx+V_r)_{0\le r\le t}\).  Removing the localization preserves absolute
continuity.

At the terminal time, the semigroup identity and the change of variables
\(r=2s-t\) give
\[
 \int_0^tS_{t-s}\widehat F_s\,ds
 =\int_{t/2}^t2S_{2(t-s)}\mu_{2s-t}\,ds
 =\int_0^tS_{t-r}\mu_r\,dr=K_t.
\]
Thus \(\widehat u_t=u_t\).  Therefore \(\Law(u_t)\) is absolutely continuous
with respect to the law of \(S_tx+V_t\).

It remains only to compare the Ornstein--Uhlenbeck one-time law with
\(\gamma\).  In the Dirichlet eigenbasis \(e_k(r)=\sqrt2\sin(k\pi r)\), the
OU covariance has eigenvalues
\[
        q_k(t)=\frac{1-e^{-k^2\pi^2t}}{k^2\pi^2},
\]
whereas \(\gamma\) has eigenvalues \((k^2\pi^2)^{-1}\).  The ratios
\(q_k(t)/(k^2\pi^2)^{-1}\) tend to \(1\) exponentially fast, and the mean
\(S_tx\) belongs to the Cameron--Martin space \(H_0^1(0,1)\).  The
Feldman--Hajek criterion therefore gives
\(\Law(S_tx+V_t)\sim\gamma\).  Since \(\pi\sim\gamma\), the proof is complete.
\end{proof}

\section*{Conclusion: uniqueness}

Let \(\eta\) be any invariant probability measure.  If \(\pi(A)=0\), then by
Lemma \ref{lem:girs}, \(P_t(y,A)=0\) for every \(y\in E\) and every \(t>0\).
Hence
\[
        \eta(A)=\int_E P_t(y,A)\,\eta(dy)=0.
\]
Thus \(\eta\ll\pi\).

It remains to use the spectral gap.  Write \(d\eta=h\,d\pi\).  Invariance and
symmetry give \(P_t h=h\) in \(L^1(\pi)\): for bounded \(\varphi\),
\[
 \int \varphi h\,d\pi=\int P_t\varphi\,h\,d\pi
      =\int \varphi\,P_t h\,d\pi ,
\]
first for bounded \(h\), then for general \(h\in L^1(\pi)\) by truncation.
For \(a>0\), set \(h_a=h\wedge a\).  Since \(r\mapsto r\wedge a\) is concave,
\(P_t h_a\le (P_t h)\wedge a=h_a\); the two sides have equal \(\pi\)-integral,
so \(P_t h_a=h_a\).  Now \(h_a\in L^2(\pi)\), and (4) forces
\(\Var_\pi(h_a)=0\).  Thus each \(h_a\) is constant.  Letting
\(a\uparrow\infty\) and using \(\int h\,d\pi=1\) gives \(h\equiv1\).  Hence
\(\eta=\pi\).

Therefore \(P_t\) has at most one invariant probability measure; in fact the
unique invariant measure is the Gibbs measure \(\pi\) in (1).
