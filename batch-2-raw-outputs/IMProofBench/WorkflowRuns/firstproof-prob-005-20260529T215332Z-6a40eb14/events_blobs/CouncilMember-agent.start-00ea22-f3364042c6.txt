\begin{center}
{\bf Invariant measures for the Dirichlet skew stochastic heat equation}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Let \(E=C_0([0,1])\) and let \(S_t\) be the Dirichlet heat semigroup.
I use the ABLM regularized/mild interpretation specified in the problem,
including the assumed Dirichlet-boundary extension.  I also use the following
pointwise interpretation of the stated weak convergence of regularizations:
for every bounded continuous \(\Phi:E\to\R\), deterministic \(x\in E\), and
\(t>0\),
\[
        P_t^n\Phi(x)\longrightarrow P_t\Phi(x).        \tag{A}
\]
The drift is formally a gradient drift:
\[
        \delta_0=-\frac12\frac{d}{dr}(-2H_+),\qquad
        H_+(r)=\1_{\{r>0\}} .
\]
If \(\gamma\) denotes Brownian bridge measure on \(E\), the corresponding
Gibbs probability is
\[
   \Lambda(v)=\int_0^1\1_{\{v(x)>0\}}\,dx,\qquad
   \pi(dv)=Z^{-1}e^{2\Lambda(v)}\gamma(dv).       \tag{1}
\]
The density is bounded above and below because \(0\le\Lambda\le1\), hence
\(\pi\sim\gamma\), and \(\gamma\) has full support on \(E\).  The convention
at zero is immaterial since a Brownian bridge spends zero Lebesgue time at
zero:
\[
   \E_\gamma\int_0^1\1_{\{\beta(x)=0\}}\,dx
    =\int_0^1\mathbb P(\beta(x)=0)\,dx=0 .
\]

\section*{What the approximation proves}

Set
\[
   B_n(r)=\int_{-\infty}^rG_{1/n}(s)\,ds,\qquad
   \pi_n(dv)=Z_n^{-1}\exp\!\left(2\int_0^1B_n(v(x))\,dx\right)\gamma(dv).
\]
Then \(0\le B_n\le1\), \(B_n(r)\to H_+(r)\) for \(r\ne0\), and dominated
convergence gives
\[
       \|\pi_n-\pi\|_{\TV}\longrightarrow0 .          \tag{2}
\]
For fixed \(n\), \(v\mapsto G_{1/n}(v)\) is a bounded Lipschitz
\(L^2(0,1)\)-valued Nemytskii map.  The usual Galerkin/Dirichlet-form
identification for semilinear stochastic heat equations gives the
reversible form
\[
   \cE_n(F,G)=\frac12\int_E\langle DF,DG\rangle_{L^2(0,1)}\,d\pi_n
\]
on smooth cylinder functions and its closure in \(L^2(\pi_n)\).  Equivalently,
for cylinder \(F,G\),
\[
 \int G\,L_nF\,d\pi_n
   =-\frac12\int\langle DF,DG\rangle_{L^2(0,1)}\,d\pi_n
   =\int F\,L_nG\,d\pi_n,                         \tag{3}
\]
where
\[
 L_nF(v)=\frac12{\rm Tr}\,D^2F(v)
       +\left\langle \frac12\Delta_Dv+G_{1/n}(v),DF(v)\right\rangle .
\]
Thus \(P_t^n\) is reversible and invariant with respect to \(\pi_n\).

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:limit}
Under (A), \(\pi\) is invariant for \(P_t\).  Moreover \(P_t\) has a
symmetric Markov contraction extension to \(L^2(\pi)\), and there is
\(c>0\) such that
\[
     \Var_{\pi}(P_t f)\le e^{-2ct}\Var_\pi(f),
     \qquad f\in L^2(\pi),\ t\ge0.                 \tag{4}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For \(f,g\in C_b(E)\), (A), dominated convergence, (2), and invariance of
\(\pi_n\) yield
\[
 \int P_tf\,d\pi
 =\lim_n\int P_t^nf\,d\pi
  =\lim_n\int P_t^nf\,d\pi_n
  =\lim_n\int f\,d\pi_n
  =\int f\,d\pi .
\]
The same argument with (3) gives
\[
 \int g\,P_tf\,d\pi=\int f\,P_tg\,d\pi,\qquad f,g\in C_b(E).
\]
Since \(E\) is Polish, equality against bounded continuous functions
determines the relevant finite measures; invariance also gives the Jensen
bound \(\|P_t f\|_{L^2(\pi)}\le\|f\|_{L^2(\pi)}\).  Hence \(P_t\) extends
uniquely from \(C_b(E)\) to a symmetric Markov contraction on \(L^2(\pi)\).

Brownian bridge measure satisfies a Gaussian Poincare inequality, and the
densities \(d\pi_n/d\gamma\) are bounded above and below uniformly in \(n\).
Consequently,
\[
     \Var_{\pi_n}(F)\le C\,\cE_n(F,F)                 \tag{5}
\]
with \(C\) independent of \(n\).  By the spectral theorem for the reversible
semigroups \(P_t^n\),
\[
     \Var_{\pi_n}(P_t^n f)\le e^{-2ct}\Var_{\pi_n}(f) \tag{6}
\]
for bounded continuous \(f\), with \(c=C^{-1}\) after changing constants.
Letting \(n\to\infty\) in (6), using (A) and (2), gives (4) for bounded
continuous \(f\); density of \(C_b(E)\) in \(L^2(\pi)\) gives the general
case.
\end{proof}

The spectral gap already settles all invariant probabilities which are
absolutely continuous with respect to \(\pi\).

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:ac}
If \(\eta\) is \(P_t\)-invariant and \(\eta\ll\pi\), then \(\eta=\pi\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Write \(\eta=h\pi\), \(h\ge0\), \(\int h\,d\pi=1\).  For bounded
\(\varphi\),
\[
 \int\varphi h\,d\pi=\int P_t\varphi\,h\,d\pi
     =\int\varphi\,P_t h\,d\pi ,
\]
where the last identity follows first for bounded \(h\) by symmetry and
then for \(h\in L^1(\pi)\) by truncation and the \(L^1\)-contraction of the
Markov operator.  Thus \(P_t h=h\) in \(L^1(\pi)\).  For \(a>0\), put
\(h_a=h\wedge a\).  Since \(r\mapsto r\wedge a\) is concave,
\[
       P_t h_a\le (P_t h)\wedge a=h_a .
\]
The two sides have the same \(\pi\)-integral, hence \(P_t h_a=h_a\).
Now \(h_a\in L^2(\pi)\), so (4) implies \(\Var_\pi(h_a)=0\).  Thus each
\(h_a\) is constant, and letting \(a\uparrow\infty\) gives \(h\equiv1\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Exact criteria which would imply full uniqueness}

The unresolved point is whether every invariant probability is absolutely
continuous with respect to \(\pi\).  The following criteria isolate the
needed all-starting-points input.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:resolvent}
If, for some \(\lambda>0\),
\[
     R_\lambda^P(x,\cdot)\ll \pi,\qquad
     R_\lambda^P(x,A):=\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t}P_t(x,A)\,dt,
     \quad x\in E,                                  \tag{7}
\]
then \(\pi\) is the only invariant probability measure.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\eta\) be invariant.  If \(\pi(A)=0\), then
\[
   \eta(A)=\lambda\int_E R_\lambda^P(x,A)\,\eta(dx)=0.
\]
Hence \(\eta\ll\pi\), and Lemma \ref{lem:ac} applies.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:sf}
Either of the following implies (7): \(P_t\) is strong Feller for every
\(t>0\), or, for one \(\lambda>0\), \(R_\lambda^P\) maps bounded Borel
functions to continuous functions.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
If \(\pi(A)=0\), invariance gives \(\int P_t1_A\,d\pi=0\), respectively
\(\int R_\lambda^P1_A\,d\pi=0\).  The relevant function is nonnegative and
continuous.  Since \(\pi\) has full support, it must vanish identically.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[eventual equicontinuity criterion]\label{lem:eprop}
Assume that for every bounded Lipschitz \(f:E\to\R\), every \(x\in E\), and
every \(\varepsilon>0\), there are a neighbourhood \(U\) of \(x\) and
\(t_0>0\) such that
\[
        |P_t f(y)-P_t f(x)|\le\varepsilon,
        \qquad y\in U,\ t\ge t_0 .                  \tag{8}
\]
Then \(\pi\) is the unique invariant probability measure.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By (4), \(P_t f\to\pi(f)\) in \(L^2(\pi)\).  Fix \(x\) and choose \(U\) as
in (8).  Since \(\pi\) has full support, \(\pi(U)>0\), and for \(t\ge t_0\),
\[
 |P_t f(x)-\pi(f)|
 \le \varepsilon+
 \left(\pi(U)^{-1}\int_U |P_t f(y)-\pi(f)|^2\,\pi(dy)\right)^{1/2}.
\]
Letting \(t\to\infty\), then \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\), gives
\(P_t f(x)\to\pi(f)\) for every \(x\).  Invariance of any \(\eta\) and
dominated convergence give \(\eta(f)=\pi(f)\).  Bounded Lipschitz functions
determine probability measures on \(E\).
\end{proof}

\section*{The Bounebache--Zambotti exceptional set}

For the same bounded potential, Bounebache--Zambotti construct the
Dirichlet form
\[
        \cE(F,G)=\frac12\int_E\langle DF,DG\rangle_{L^2(0,1)}\,d\pi .
\]
Their absolute-continuity theorem gives a resolvent density only outside a
properly exceptional set \(N\) (the displayed kernel is extended arbitrarily
on \(N\)).  Thus it proves \(R_\lambda(y,\cdot)\ll\pi\) for quasi-every
starting point \(y\), not for every deterministic \(y\in E\).  If the ABLM
and BZ solutions are identified, uniqueness follows as soon as invariant
laws are known not to charge \(N\).

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:entrance}
Let \(N\) be a BZ exceptional set such that \(R_\lambda^P(y,\cdot)\ll\pi\)
for \(y\notin N\).  If
\[
          P_s(x,N)=0,\qquad x\in E,\ s>0,              \tag{9}
\]
then (7) holds for every \(x\in E\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\pi(A)=0\).  For \(y\notin N\), \(R_\lambda^P(y,A)=0\).  For
\(\varepsilon>0\), the resolvent identity gives
\[
 R_\lambda^P(x,A)
 \le \varepsilon
  +e^{-\lambda\varepsilon}\int_E R_\lambda^P(y,A)P_\varepsilon(x,dy)
 \le \varepsilon+\lambda^{-1}P_\varepsilon(x,N).
\]
Use (9) and let \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\).
\end{proof}

A non-pointwise way to get the same conclusion is a capacity-Krylov
estimate.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:krylov}
Suppose \(R_\lambda^P(y,\cdot)\ll\pi\) for \(y\notin N\), where \(N\) is a
BZ exceptional set.  Assume also that for every compact \(K\subset E\) and
\(0<\varepsilon<T\), there are \(C,\alpha>0\) such that for every open
\(O\subset E\),
\[
 \sup_{x\in K}\E_x\int_\varepsilon^T\1_O(u_s)\,ds
      \le C\,\capacity(O)^\alpha .                       \tag{10}
\]
Then every invariant probability charges no BZ exceptional set.  Hence
\(\eta\ll\pi\) for every invariant \(\eta\), and Lemma \ref{lem:ac} gives
uniqueness.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(\eta\) be invariant and \(O\) open.  By tightness choose compact
\(K\) with \(\eta(K^c)<\delta\).  Stationarity and (10) imply
\[
 (T-\varepsilon)\eta(O)
 =\int_E\E_x\int_\varepsilon^T\1_O(u_s)\,ds\,\eta(dx)
 \le C\capacity(O)^\alpha+(T-\varepsilon)\delta .
\]
If \(N_0\) has zero capacity, outer regularity gives open \(O\supset N_0\)
with \(C\capacity(O)^\alpha<(T-\varepsilon)\delta\).  Hence
\(\eta(N_0)\le2\delta\), and then \(\delta\downarrow0\) gives
\(\eta(N_0)=0\).  In particular \(\eta(N)=0\).  If \(\pi(A)=0\), invariance
and the quasi-everywhere resolvent absolute continuity on \(N^c\) give
\[
   \eta(A)=\lambda\int_{N^c}R_\lambda^P(y,A)\,\eta(dy)=0 .
\]
Thus \(\eta\ll\pi\), and Lemma \ref{lem:ac} applies.
\end{proof}

\section*{Remaining open issues}

The preceding argument proves uniqueness of all invariant laws once one
can rule out invariant mass on the BZ exceptional set.  What remains
unproved from the hypotheses stated in the problem is one of the following
all-starting-points upgrades:
\[
\begin{array}{ll}
\text{(i)}& \text{resolvent absolute continuity (7),}\\
\text{(ii)}& \text{strong/resolvent Feller regularity,}\\
\text{(iii)}& \text{eventual equicontinuity (8),}\\
\text{(iv)}& \text{entrance into }N^c\text{ as in (9),}\\
\text{(v)}& \text{the capacity-Krylov estimate (10).}
\end{array}
\]
The regularized Gibbs measures and semigroups give the invariant reversible
law \(\pi\) and the \(L^2(\pi)\) spectral gap, but these are insensitive to
changes of a Markov process on a \(\pi\)-null exceptional set.  Therefore the
present proof establishes uniqueness among invariant laws absolutely
continuous with respect to \(\pi\) and gives precise sufficient criteria for
full uniqueness, but it does not yet prove or disprove the original
all-starting-points statement without an additional estimate excluding
exceptional invariant measures.
