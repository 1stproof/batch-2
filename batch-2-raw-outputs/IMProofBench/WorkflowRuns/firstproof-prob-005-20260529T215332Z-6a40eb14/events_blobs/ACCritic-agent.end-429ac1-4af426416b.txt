## Literature validation

The cited literature supports the general background but does **not** close the missing gap.

- ABLM 2024 indeed treats stochastic heat equations with distributional drift and includes the skew case \(b=\kappa\delta_0\), with strong existence/uniqueness and approximation results. However, ABLM 2024 explicitly says it does **not** analyze Dirichlet boundary conditions, although the authors expect their approach to adapt. The problem statement separately assumes this Dirichlet extension. ([eprints.whiterose.ac.uk](https://eprints.whiterose.ac.uk/206080/7/Comm%20Pure%20Appl%20Math%20-%202023%20-%20Athreya%20-%20Well%E2%80%90posedness%20of%20stochastic%20heat%20equation%20with%20distributional%20drift%20and%20skew.pdf))

- Bounebache–Zambotti construct a Dirichlet-form Markov solution with Gibbs invariant measure and prove an absolute-continuity property for a resolvent kernel. But their Proposition 2.5 represents the \(L^2(\nu)\)-resolvent only \(\nu\)-a.e.; in the proof, the kernel is set to zero on an exceptional set. Their process solves the SPDE for quasi-every initial point, not automatically for every deterministic point in \(C_0([0,1])\). This is exactly the exceptional-set issue the revised proof now identifies. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1105.2779))

- The 2025 ABLM weak/mild-equivalence paper does prove equivalence of weak-regularized and mild-regularized notions under its hypotheses, but its stated settings are \(\mathbb R\), periodic boundary conditions, and Neumann boundary conditions; it does not by itself provide the all-starting-points Dirichlet identification needed here. ([link.springer.com](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s40072-025-00392-x))

## Detailed mathematical audit

### 1. Problem interpretation and candidate measure

This section is largely correct. The identity
\[
\delta_0=-\frac12\frac{d}{dr}(-2H_+)
\]
has the correct sign, and the Gibbs density \(e^{2\Lambda}\) is consistent with the regularized drift \(G_{1/n}=B_n'\).

The proof that \(\Lambda\) is Borel is adequate. The Brownian-bridge zero-level occupation argument is also correct:
\[
\E_\gamma\int_0^1\mathbf 1_{\{\beta(x)=0\}}\,dx=0.
\]

No serious issue here.

---

### 2. Total-variation convergence \(\pi_n\to\pi\)

The argument is valid. Since \(0\le B_n\le1\), \(B_n(r)\to H_+(r)\) for \(r\ne0\), and Brownian-bridge paths spend zero Lebesgue time at zero, one gets
\[
\int_0^1 B_n(v(x))\,dx\to \int_0^1H_+(v(x))\,dx
\]
for \(\gamma\)-a.e. \(v\). Dominated convergence then gives \(L^1(\gamma)\) convergence of the normalized densities, hence total-variation convergence.

This fixes a minor omission from the earlier draft.

---

### 3. Reversibility of the regularized equations

The formal generator calculation is correct:
\[
L_nF(v)=\frac12\operatorname{Tr}D^2F(v)
+\left\langle \frac12\Delta_Dv+G_{1/n}(v),DF(v)\right\rangle.
\]
The Gibbs density
\[
\pi_n(dv)\propto \exp\!\left(2\int_0^1B_n(v(x))\,dx\right)\gamma(dv)
\]
has the correct sign.

However, the step
> “Consequently \(P_t^n\) is reversible with invariant law \(\pi_n\)”  
is still somewhat compressed. A formal integration-by-parts identity on smooth cylinder functions does not by itself prove reversibility of the full SPDE semigroup. For smooth globally Lipschitz \(G_{1/n}\), this is standard and repairable, but a fully rigorous proof should either cite a standard theorem or briefly explain that smooth cylinders are a core for the associated gradient Dirichlet form and that the mild SPDE semigroup coincides with the associated symmetric Markov semigroup.

This is a repairable gap, not the main fatal issue.

---

### 4. Proposition: passing invariance and symmetry to the limit

The proof is mostly sound under the strengthened convergence assumption (4):
\[
P_t^n\Phi(x)\to P_t\Phi(x),\qquad x\in E.
\]

The invariance proof is correct: total-variation convergence of \(\pi_n\to\pi\), boundedness of \(P_t^nf\), and pointwise convergence give
\[
\int P_tf\,d\pi=\int f\,d\pi.
\]

The symmetry proof is also correct for bounded continuous \(f,g\), and the extension to \(L^2(\pi)\) using density of \(C_b(E)\) and \(L^2\)-contractivity is standard.

One caveat: the problem statement’s approximation assumption is slightly informal. The draft upgrades it to convergence for every deterministic initial condition \(x\in E\) and every bounded continuous observable. If the original assumption was meant only for the originally fixed initial condition \(u_0\), then Proposition 1 assumes more than was given. Because \(P_t\) is defined for all \(u_0\), this strengthened interpretation is probably intended, but the proof depends on it.

---

### 5. Spectral gap passage

The strategy is valid in principle.

The Brownian bridge Gaussian Poincaré inequality plus uniformly bounded densities \(d\pi_n/d\gamma\) gives a uniform Poincaré inequality for \(\pi_n\). Then reversible semigroup theory gives
\[
\operatorname{Var}_{\pi_n}(P_t^nf)
\le e^{-2ct}\operatorname{Var}_{\pi_n}(f)
\]
with \(c\) independent of \(n\). Passing to the limit for bounded continuous \(f\) is justified by pointwise convergence and total-variation convergence, and the extension to all \(L^2(\pi)\) functions is fine.

Again, a completely rigorous write-up should cite the spectral theorem/Dirichlet-form result connecting the Poincaré inequality for \(\mathcal E_n\) to the \(L^2(\pi_n)\) decay of the actual semigroup \(P_t^n\). But this is not the fatal obstruction.

---

### 6. Abstract uniqueness criterion

The lemma is essentially correct.

If \(\eta\) is invariant and (8) holds, then
\[
\eta(A)=\lambda\int R_\lambda(x,A)\,\eta(dx),
\]
so \(\eta\ll\pi\). Writing \(h=d\eta/d\pi\), symmetry gives \(P_th=h\) in \(L^1(\pi)\). The truncation argument with \(h_a=h\wedge a\), concavity, and the spectral gap then forces \(h\) to be constant.

Minor notation issue: the displayed line
\[
R_\lambda(x,A)\ll \pi(A)
\]
is not standard. It should be written
\[
R_\lambda(x,\cdot)\ll\pi.
\]
But the intended meaning is clear.

---

### 7. Relation with Bounebache–Zambotti

This section is now accurate in spirit but still not a proof of the needed property.

The revised draft correctly observes that BZ Proposition 2.5 gives an \(L^2(\pi)\)-resolvent kernel with absolute continuity, but that this does not automatically prove
\[
R_\lambda^P(x,\cdot)\ll\pi
\]
for the ABLM semigroup at every deterministic \(x\in E\). BZ’s construction is quasi-regular Dirichlet-form theory; statements are naturally quasi-everywhere or \(L^2(\pi)\)-level, whereas the problem requires a statement about the particular probabilistic semigroup \(P_t\) for all deterministic initial conditions.

This is the same essential gap as before, now explicitly acknowledged.
