\begin{center}
{\bf Uniqueness of the invariant measure}
\end{center}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

Let \(E=C_0([0,1])\), \(H=L^2(0,1)\), and let \(S_t\) be the Dirichlet heat
semigroup generated by \(\frac12\Delta_D\).  I use the Dirichlet extension of
the Athreya--Butkovsky--L\^e--Mytnik (ABLM) theory stipulated in the problem.
Thus, for \(b_n=G_{1/n}\), the regularized semigroups satisfy
\[
        P_t^n\Phi(x)\longrightarrow P_t\Phi(x),
        \qquad x\in E,\quad t>0,\quad \Phi\in C_b(E).        \tag{A}
\]
I shall also use the drift regularity which is part of the ABLM solution class
for finite non-negative measure drifts.  Namely, if
\[
        u_t=S_tx+V_t+K_t,\qquad
        V_t=\int_0^t S_{t-s}\,dW_s,\qquad K_0=0,
\]
then for every \(T<\infty\) and \(m>2\)
\[
 \sup_{0<s<t\le T}\sup_{r\in[0,1]}
 \frac{\|K_t(r)-S_{t-s}K_s(r)\|_{L^m(\Omega)}}{|t-s|^{3/4}}<\infty .
                                                            \tag{B}
\]
In ABLM notation this is \(K\in V(3/4)\); for measure drifts it is obtained
in their Proposition 3.8.  No spatial local-time representation is used below.

The statement is true: the semigroup has exactly one invariant probability
measure.

\section*{The reversible Gibbs measure}

Let \(\gamma\) be Brownian bridge measure on \(E\), the centered Gaussian
measure with covariance \((-\Delta_D)^{-1}\).  Put
\[
  \Lambda(v)=\int_0^1\1_{\{v(r)>0\}}\,dr,\qquad
  \pi(dv)=Z^{-1}e^{2\Lambda(v)}\,\gamma(dv).              \tag{1}
\]
Since \(0\le\Lambda\le1\), \(\pi\sim\gamma\) and \(\pi\) has full support.

For \(b_n=G_{1/n}\) set
\[
   B_n(a)=\int_{-\infty}^aG_{1/n}(r)\,dr,\qquad
   \pi_n(dv)=Z_n^{-1}\exp\left(2\int_0^1B_n(v(r))\,dr\right)\gamma(dv).
\]
Then \(0\le B_n\le1\), \(B_n(a)\to \1_{\{a>0\}}\) for \(a\ne0\), and Brownian
bridge paths spend zero Lebesgue time at zero.  Dominated convergence gives
\[
        \|\pi_n-\pi\|_{\TV}\longrightarrow0 .              \tag{2}
\]
For fixed \(n\), \(b_n\) is bounded and smooth.  Gaussian integration by parts
shows that the generator
\[
 L_nF(v)=\frac12{\rm Tr}\,D^2F(v)
       +\left\langle \frac12\Delta_Dv+b_n(v),DF(v)\right\rangle_H
\]
is symmetric in \(L^2(\pi_n)\) on smooth cylinder functions:
\[
 \int_E G\,L_nF\,d\pi_n
 =-\frac12\int_E\langle DF,DG\rangle_H\,d\pi_n
 =\int_E F\,L_nG\,d\pi_n .                                \tag{3}
\]
The standard variational/mild theory for bounded Lipschitz reaction terms
therefore gives that \(P_t^n\) is \(\pi_n\)-invariant and reversible.

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:gap}
The measure \(\pi\) is \(P_t\)-invariant.  Moreover \(P_t\) extends to a
symmetric Markov contraction on \(L^2(\pi)\), and there is \(c>0\) such that
\[
       \Var_\pi(P_t f)\le e^{-2ct}\Var_\pi(f),
       \qquad f\in L^2(\pi),\ t\ge0 .                      \tag{4}
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For \(f,g\in C_b(E)\), (A), (2), and the invariance and symmetry of
\((P_t^n,\pi_n)\) imply, by bounded convergence,
\[
 \int P_tf\,d\pi=\lim_n\int P_t^nf\,d\pi_n=\lim_n\int f\,d\pi_n=\int f\,d\pi
\]
and
\[
 \int gP_tf\,d\pi=\lim_n\int gP_t^nf\,d\pi_n
       =\lim_n\int fP_t^ng\,d\pi_n=\int fP_tg\,d\pi .
\]
Jensen's inequality gives the \(L^2(\pi)\)-contraction.

The Brownian bridge measure satisfies a Gaussian Poincare inequality.  Since
\(d\pi_n/d\gamma\) is bounded above and below uniformly in \(n\), the
Holley--Stroock perturbation argument gives
\[
      \Var_{\pi_n}(F)\le C\,\frac12\int |DF|_H^2\,d\pi_n
\]
with \(C\) independent of \(n\).  Reversibility gives
\[
      \Var_{\pi_n}(P_t^n f)\le e^{-2ct}\Var_{\pi_n}(f),
      \qquad f\in C_b(E),
\]
with \(c=C^{-1}\).  Passing to the limit with (A) and (2), then using density
of \(C_b(E)\) in \(L^2(\pi)\), yields (4).
\end{proof}

\section*{Fixed-time absolute continuity}

The remaining point is that the process enters the \(\pi\)-class from every
deterministic initial condition.  This follows from (B) by an adapted
dyadic-control construction.

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:control}
For every deterministic \(x\in E\) and \(t>0\),
\[
        P_t(x,\cdot)\ll \gamma .
\]
Consequently \(P_t(x,\cdot)\ll\pi\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Fix \(t>0\) and write \(u_t=S_tx+V_t+K_t\).  Let
\(r_k=t(1-2^{-k})\), \(k\ge0\), and \(\Delta_k=r_{k+1}-r_k\).  Set
\[
        D_k=K_{r_{k+1}}-S_{\Delta_k}K_{r_k}.
\]
The first \(D_0=K_{t/2}\) is \(H\)-valued with finite second moment, and by
(B), for \(k\ge1\),
\[
 \E\|D_k\|_H^2\le C_t\,\Delta_k^{3/2}.
\]
Define an adapted \(H\)-valued process on \([0,t]\) by
\[
 F_s=\sum_{k\ge0}\frac{\1_{(r_{k+1},r_{k+2}]}(s)}{\Delta_{k+1}}\,
        S_{s-r_{k+1}}D_k .                                \tag{5}
\]
Here \(D_k\) is \(\mathcal F_{r_{k+1}}\)-measurable, while
\(s>r_{k+1}\) on the corresponding interval.  Moreover
\[
 \E\int_0^t\|F_s\|_H^2\,ds
 \le \frac{\E\|D_0\|_H^2}{\Delta_1}
      +\sum_{k\ge1}\frac{\E\|D_k\|_H^2}{\Delta_{k+1}}
 \le C_t\left(1+\sum_{k\ge1}\Delta_k^{1/2}\right)<\infty . \tag{6}
\]
Thus \(\int_0^t\|F_s\|_H^2ds<\infty\) a.s.  The terminal effect of this
control is exactly \(K_t\): for partial sums,
\[
\begin{aligned}
 \int_0^t S_{t-s}F_s\,ds
 &=\lim_{N\to\infty}\sum_{k=0}^N
     S_{t-r_{k+1}}D_k                                      \\
 &=\lim_{N\to\infty}S_{t-r_{N+1}}K_{r_{N+1}}=K_t
\end{aligned}                                             \tag{7}
\]
in \(H\).  The middle equality is telescoping, and the last follows from
the continuity of \(K\) and the strong continuity of \(S\).

Let
\[
        \widehat u_s=S_sx+V_s+\int_0^sS_{s-r}F_r\,dr,\qquad 0\le s\le t .
\]
By (7), \(\widehat u_t=u_t\).  Localize with
\(\tau_N=\inf\{s:\int_0^s\|F_r\|_H^2dr>N\}\).  For
\(F^N=F\1_{[0,\tau_N]}\), Novikov's condition is automatic, and the
Hilbert-space Girsanov theorem implies that the law of
\(S_\cdot x+V_\cdot+\int_0^\cdot S_{\cdot-r}F^N_r\,dr\) on \([0,t]\) is
absolutely continuous with respect to the Ornstein--Uhlenbeck law.  On
\(\{\tau_N>t\}\) this stopped process equals \(\widehat u\); since
\(\tau_N\uparrow\infty\) a.s., \(\Law(u_t)\) is absolutely continuous with
respect to the one-time law of \(S_tx+V_t\).

Finally, the latter law is equivalent to \(\gamma\).  In the Dirichlet
eigenbasis \(e_k(r)=\sqrt2\sin(k\pi r)\), its covariance eigenvalues are
\[
        q_k(t)=\frac{1-e^{-k^2\pi^2t}}{k^2\pi^2},
\]
whereas \(\gamma\) has eigenvalues \((k^2\pi^2)^{-1}\).  The covariance
ratios tend to \(1\) exponentially fast, and \(S_tx\in H_0^1(0,1)\), the
Cameron--Martin space of \(\gamma\).  The Feldman--Hajek criterion gives
\(\Law(S_tx+V_t)\sim\gamma\).  Since \(\pi\sim\gamma\), the lemma follows.
\end{proof}

\section*{Conclusion: uniqueness}

Let \(\eta\) be any invariant probability measure.  If \(\pi(A)=0\), then by
Lemma \ref{lem:control}, \(P_t(y,A)=0\) for every \(y\in E\) and every
\(t>0\).  Hence
\[
        \eta(A)=\int_E P_t(y,A)\,\eta(dy)=0,
\]
so \(\eta\ll\pi\).

Write \(d\eta=h\,d\pi\).  Invariance of \(\eta\) and symmetry of \(P_t\) in
\(L^2(\pi)\) give \(P_t h=h\) in \(L^1(\pi)\).  For \(a>0\), let
\(h_a=h\wedge a\).  Since \(r\mapsto r\wedge a\) is concave,
\[
        P_t h_a\le (P_t h)\wedge a=h_a .
\]
The two sides have the same \(\pi\)-integral, so \(P_t h_a=h_a\).  Now
\(h_a\in L^2(\pi)\), and the spectral gap (4) forces
\(\Var_\pi(h_a)=0\).  Thus each \(h_a\) is constant.  Letting
\(a\uparrow\infty\) and using \(\int h\,d\pi=1\) gives \(h\equiv1\).  Hence
\(\eta=\pi\).

Therefore \(P_t\) has at most one invariant probability measure; in fact the
unique invariant measure is the Gibbs measure \(\pi\) in (1).
