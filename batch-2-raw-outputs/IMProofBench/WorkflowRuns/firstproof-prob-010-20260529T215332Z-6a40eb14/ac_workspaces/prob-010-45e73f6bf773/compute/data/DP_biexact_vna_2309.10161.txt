                                                                   BIEXACT VON NEUMANN ALGEBRAS

                                                                         CHANGYING DING AND JESSE PETERSON
arXiv:2309.10161v1 [math.OA] 18 Sep 2023




                                                  Abstract. We introduce the notion of biexactness for general von Neumann algebras, naturally
                                                  extending the notion from group theory. We show that biexactness implies solidity for von
                                                  Neumann algebras, and that many of the examples of solid von Neumann algebras contained in
                                                  the literature are, in fact, biexact. We also give examples of certain crossed products arising
                                                  from Gaussian actions that are solid but not biexact, and we give examples of certain q-Gaussian
                                                  von Neumann algebras that are strongly solid but not biexact. The techniques developed involve
                                                  studying a certain weak form of nuclear embeddings, and we use this setting to give a new
                                                  description of weak exactness for von Neumann algebras, which allows us to answer several open
                                                  problems in the literature about weakly exact von Neumann algebras.


                                                                                       1. Introduction

                                           Recall that a finite von Neumann algebra M is solid if, for any diffuse von Neumann subalgebra
                                           A ⊂ M , the relative commutant A′ ∩M is amenable. A seminal result of Ozawa [Oza04] is that free
                                           group factors are solid and, as a consequence, every nonamenable subfactor of a free group factor
                                           is prime. Ozawa’s proof, which is based on C∗ -algebra theory, holds more generally for the class
                                           of biexact groups [BO08, Definition 15.1.2], which are groups that admit a topologically amenable
                                           action on a certain small-at-infinity boundary. Biexact groups have since been shown to have
                                           many applications to the theory of von Neumann algebras [Oza04, Oza06b, Oza06a]. Combining
                                           the techniques of biexact groups with Popa’s deformation/rigidity theory has produced a number
                                           of striking results where certain structural properties of a von Neumann algebra can be recovered
                                           as in [OP04, CS13, PV14a, CdSS16, CIW21, HIK22, Mar17].
                                           In this article we introduce the notion of biexactness in the setting of von Neumann algebras,
                                           thereby allowing for the previous results to be put in a systematic framework. This allows
                                           for a more integrated use of C∗ and von Neumann algebraic techniques, and allows us to find
                                           a common von Neumann algebraic setting for many solidity results such as those obtained in
                                           [Oza04, Pop07, Pet09, CI18, Bou13, HR15, Iso15b, Shl04]. This then leads to natural extensions
                                           of these results to a larger class of von Neumann algebras.
                                           To describe biexactness for von Neumann algebras, we first recall in greater detail the corre-
                                           sponding property for groups (see [BO08, Chapter 15] for full details). Given a countable group
                                           Γ, we have commuting actions of Γ on ℓ∞ Γ given by left and right multiplication, respectively,
                                           i.e., Lt (f )(x) = f (t−1 x) and Rt (f )(x) = f (xt) for f ∈ ℓ∞ Γ and x, t ∈ Γ. We denote
                                                                      S(Γ) = {f ∈ ℓ∞ Γ | f − Rt (f ) ∈ c0 Γ, ∀ t ∈ Γ},
                                           which is a left-invariant C∗ -subalgebra of ℓ∞ Γ. By Gelfand duality, we may identify S(Γ) with
                                           continuous functions on its spectrum Γ, and we then have an action of Γ on Γ by homeomorphisms.
                                           The space Γ is called the small-at-infinity compactification of Γ. The group Γ is biexact if the
                                           action Γ y Γ is topologically amenable in the sense of [AD87], which is equivalent to the inclusion
                                           Cλ∗ Γ ⊂ C(Γ) ⋊r Γ being nuclear. Topological amenability is inherited through factor maps, and
                                           so in practice one usually checks biexactness of a group via natural boundary type actions, e.g.,
                                           hyperbolic groups acting on their Gromov boundary.

Let now M be a von Neumann algebra and X ⊂ B(H) an operator M -bimodule. Magajna

denote the closure of K(L2 M ) under both the M -topology and the M ′ -topology. In the case
when M has a normal faithful trace τ , we may view M ⊂ L2 (M, τ ) ⊂ L1 (M, τ ), and then by

In [DKEP22] we introduced a von Neumann analog of the small-at-infinity compactification as
                      S(M ) = {T ∈ B(L2 M ) | [T, a] ∈ K∞,1 (M ), ∀ a ∈ M ′ }.
The advantage of working with the generalized compact operators from K∞,1 (M ) is that to check
that an operator T is contained in S(M ), it suffices to check that [T, a] ∈ K∞,1 (M ) for a in some
generating set of M (generated as a von Neumann algebra). In particular, if [T, a] is compact for
each element a in some generating set, then T ∈ S(M ). This shows that the space S(M ) is robust
enough so that the inclusion M ⊂ S(M ) may have interesting properties. For example, if M = LΓ
for a group Γ, then we have a natural inclusion S(Γ) ⋊r Γ ⊂ S(LΓ). Also, the space S(M ) is large
enough so that it admits non-trivial 1-cohomology [HPP23, Theorem 7.3]. A consequence of this
robustness, though, is that the the space S(M ) is not a C∗ -algebra, but is rather an M -system,
i.e., an operator system that is also an operator M -bimodule.
Since S(M ) is an operator M -bimodule, we may again consider the M -topology on S(M ). We
say that the von Neumann algebra M is biexact if the inclusion M ⊂ S(M ) is M -nuclear in the
sense that there exists nets of u.c.p. maps φi : M → Mn(i) (C) and ψi : Mn(i) (C) → S(M ) so that
for each x ∈ M we have ψi ◦ φi (x) → x in the M -topology.
This notion is flexible enough to show that many von Neumann algebras already studied in the
literature are biexact. In particular, we show in Theorem 7.17 that all von Neumann algebras
satisfying condition (AO)+ or strong property (AO) are biexact. We therefore have that each of
the following classes of von Neumann algebras is contained in the class of biexact von Neumann
algebras:
    (1) Amenable von Neumann algebras.
    (2) Group von Neumann algebras associated to locally compact second countable biexact
        groups (Proposition 6.2 in [Dep20]).
    (3) Von Neumann algebras of universal orthogonal and unitary discrete quantum groups
        (Proposition 3.1.2 in [Iso15a]).
    (4) Free Araki-Woods von Neumann algebras (The proof of Theorem 4.4 in [Hou07]).
    (5) q-Gaussian von Neumann algebras associated to finite-dimensional real Hilbert space
        (Section 4 in [Shl04] for some q, and [Kuz23] in general).
    (6) Free products of biexact von Neumann algebras with respect to normal faithful states
        (Proposition 6.16).
    (7) Von Neumann subalgebras with expectation of biexact von Neumann algebras (Proposi-
        tion 6.10).
    (8) Amplifications and commutants of biexact von Neumann algebras (Proposition 6.5).
In the case of a group von Neumann algebra associated to a discrete group M = LΓ, we have a
canonical diagonal embedding of ℓ∞ Γ into B(ℓ2 Γ), as well as a normal conditional expectation

considering the positive type functions Γ ∋ t 7→ Eℓ∞ (ψi ◦ φi (λt )λ∗t ) ∈ S(Γ) we deduce that the
action Γ y Γ is amenable. The converse implication also holds by using local reflexivity of Cλ∗ Γ

One of the technical achievements of our approach (carried out in Section 4) is that, in fact,
we do not need to use local reflexivity of Cλ∗ Γ directly. If we have a von Neumann algebra M
and an ultraweakly dense C∗ -subalgebra M0 , we consider a version of the M -topology called the
(M0 ⊂ M )-topology that takes the dense C∗ -algebra M0 into account. If the inclusion M0 ⊂ S(M )
is (M0 ⊂ M )-nuclear, we then have a general upgrading result showing that M is biexact. While
this general result is not needed to show that biexactness is stable under W ∗ -equivalence, it is
crucial in showing how biexactness interacts with general constructions. For instance, this allows
us to show that the free product of biexact von Neumann algebras (with respect to arbitrary
normal faithful states) is again biexact. This also allows us to show that tensor products of
biexact von Neumann algebras satisfy a relative biexactness property from which one deduces
the unique prime decomposition results of Ozawa and Popa [OP04]. This holds, in fact, for
general biexact von Neumann algebras, and puts this into the framework of Houdayer and Isono
[HI17] so that unique prime decomposition results can be deduced also in the type III setting as
well (see Corollary 6.15).
If we consider the larger M -bimodule B(L2 M ) instead of S(M ), then (M0 ⊂ M )-nuclearity for
the inclusion M ⊂ B(L2 M ) is related to Isono’s notion of M0 being weakly exact in M [Iso13]. In
Section 5 we make this connection explicit, and as an application we show in Corollary 5.11 that
the free product (with respect to arbitrary normal faithful states) of weakly exact von Neumann
algebras is again weakly exact, which was previously shown by Isono to hold in many cases. We
also use this perspective to provide a new characterization of weak exactness for von Neumann
algebras, answering Problem 10.4.3 from [BO08] (see Theorem 5.8).
Biexact von Neumann algebras in general also have many of the indecomposability type properties
that are known to hold for the group von Neumann algebras associated to biexact groups. We
show a biexact von Neumann algebra M is always full, solid, and properly proximal. Moreover,
if M is finite and weakly amenable, then M is also strongly solid in the sense of Ozawa and Popa
[OP10b].
It was shown in [DKEP22] that if Γ is a nonamenable group and we have an embedding LΓ ⊂ LF2 ,
then it follows that Γ is properly proximal and consequently is not inner amenable (an alternate
proof of this fact was also found recently in [Dri23]). Since biexactness is inherited to subalgebras
it follows that Γ is even biexact. In fact, what we establish is a general principle that higher-
rank lattices cannot “W ∗ -embed” into rank one lattices. Specifically, if G is a connected real
semisimple Lie group with finite center and R−rank(G) ≥ 2, and if Γ < G is a lattice, then by
[Mor15, Exercise 8.2.7], together with [Sak09, Dep20], it follows that Γ is not biexact and hence
LΓ cannot embed into LΛ for any lattice Λ in a rank one connected real simple Lie group with
finite center.
It was shown recently by Caspers [Cas22] that if H is an infinite dimensional real Hilbert space
and −1 < q < 1 with q 6= 0, then the q-Gaussian von Neumann algebra Mq (H) is not isomorphic
to a free group factor. Casper’s result shows, in fact, that Mq (H) is not biexact, and hence it
then follows that Mq (H) cannot even embed into a free group factor. This last fact can also be
deduced directly from [Oza10], together with Theorem 7.20 below, which is independent of the
results in the earlier sections. We remark that this can also be deduced by combining Caspers
work with Corollary 3.5 in [Mar23].

Despite the fact that Mq (H) is not biexact when −1 < q < 1, q 6= 0, and H is infinite dimensional,
we show in Section 8, that Mq (H) shares many of the same indecomposability results that biexact
von Neumann algebras have. In particular, it is strongly solid and every von Neumann subalgebra
not having an amenable summand is properly proximal. Strong solidity for Mq (H) was shown
previously by Avsec [Avs11] in the case when H is finite-dimensional. Our proof in the infinite
dimensional case uses deformation/rigidity techniques, together with techniques developed in
[DKEP22], to reduce the problem to the finite-dimensional case where Avsec’s results may then
be applied.
In Section 8 we also give another source of solid von Neumann algebras that are not biexact
through the use of Gaussian actions. Specifically, we show that if Γ is a biexact group and
π : Γ → O(H) is an orthogonal representation satisfying π 6≺ λ, but π ⊗k ≺ λ for some k > 1,
then the crossed product of the Gaussian action AH ⊗ ℓ2 N ⋊σπ⊗1 Γ is solid, but is not biexact.
Solidity of this von Neumann algebra is a consequence of Boutonnet’s solid ergodicity result from
[Bou12], and in Section 8 we also give a relatively direct proof of this result.

                                2. Preliminaries and notation

For C∗ -algebras or operator systems A and B, we denote the algebraic tensor product by A ⊙ B
and the minimal tensor product by A ⊗ B. We will use the abbreviation c.c. for completely
contractive, c.p. for completely positive, c.c.p. for contractive completely positive, and u.c.p. for
unital completely positive.
For a von Neumann algebra M , we denote by (M, L2 M, J, B) its standard form [Haa75]. If M
is σ-finite and we have a normal faithful state µ on M , then the standard form may be realized
as (M, L2 (M, µ), Jµ , Bµ ), where the representation of M on L2 (M, µ) is given by the GNS-
construction, Jµ is modular conjugation on L2 (M, µ), and Bµ is the positive part of L2 (M, µ).
We denote by µ1/2 ∈ L2 (M, µ) the canonical cyclic vector so that µ(x) = hxµ1/2 , µ1/2 i for x ∈ M .
The centralizer algebra with respect to µ is denoted by M µ .
For von Neumann algebras M and N , a normal Hilbert M -N bimodule is a Hilbert space H,
together with a unital ∗-representation πH of the algebraic tensor product M ⊙ N op such that
πH is normal and faithful when restricted to M and N op separately. We will write simply
xξy = πH (x ⊗ y op )ξ, for x ∈ M , y ∈ N , and ξ ∈ H. A normal Hilbert M -N bimodule H is
weakly contained in another normal Hilbert M -N bimodule K (written H ≺ K) if the identity
map on M ⊙ N op extends to a ∗-homomorphism from C ∗ (πK (M ⊙ N op )) to C ∗ (πH (M ⊙ N op )).
The trivial bimodule for M is given by L2 M with the bimodule structure xξy = xJy ∗ Jξ, and
the coarse M -N bimodule is given by L2 M ⊗ L2 N with the bimodule structure given by the
canonical representation of M ⊗ N op in B(L2 M ⊗ L2 N ).
We will say that a von Neumann subalgebra N ⊂ M is with expectation if there exists a normal
faithful conditional expectation E : M → N . Given a normal faithful conditional expectation E :
M → N , and a normal faithful semifinite weight ψN on N , we obtain a normal faithful semifinite
weight on M by ψ = ψN ◦ E, and this gives rise to an inclusion L2 (N, ψN ) ⊂ L2 (M, ψ). The
Jones projection eN : L2 (M, ψ) → L2 (N, ψN ) is the corresponding orthogonal projection. The
Jones projection does not depend on the normal faithful semifinite weight ψN [HI17, Appendix
A] and so we may consider it abstractly as a coisometry eN : L2 M → L2 N . The Jones projection
eN satisfies
    (1) e∗N xeN = E(x), for x ∈ M .

                              3. A relative topology on C∗ -bimodules

In this section we collect some needed background information, and we introduce a generaliza-
tion of Magajna’s M -N -topology on operator bimodules [Mag00]. Many of the results here are
adaptations of current techniques in the literature, although a few results (e.g., Lemma 3.7) use
new techniques.
Let M0 and N0 be C∗ -algebras. An (concrete) operator M0 -N0 -bimodule consists of a concrete
operator space X ⊂ B(H), together with two faithful nondegenerate representations π : M0 →
B(H) and ρ : N0 → B(H) so that X is an π(M0 )-ρ(N0 )-bimodule, whose bimodule structure
is given by composition of operators. An (concrete) operator M0 -system consists of concrete
operator system X ⊂ B(H), together with a faithful nondegenerate representation π : M0 → B(H)
6                               CHANGYING DING AND JESSE PETERSON




so that X is also an π(M0 )-bimodule. Note that we then have M0 ∼ = π(M0 ) ⊂ X. We say that X
is a dual operator M0 -N0 -bimodule (resp. dual operator M0 -system) if X may be taken as above
to be ultraweakly closed.
If M0 = M and N0 = N are von Neumann algebras, then the operator M -N -bimodule (resp.
operator M -system) is normal if this concrete realization can be made so that π and ρ (resp.
π) is a normal representation. More generally, we will say that an operator M0 -N0 -bimodule
(resp. operator M0 -system) is (M0 ⊂ M )-(N0 ⊂ N )-normal (resp. (M0 ⊂ M )-normal) if there
the concrete realization can be made so that π and ρ (resp. π) extend to normal representations
of M and N (resp. M ).

                                         4. M -nuclearity

Let M be a von Neumann algebra, F an operator system or C∗ -algebra, and E an operator
M -system or an M -C∗ -algebra. We say a c.c.p. map φ : F → E is M -nuclear if there exist nets
of c.c.p. maps φi : F → Mn(i) (C) and ψi : Mn(i) (C) → E such that ψi ◦ φi (x) converges to φ(x)
in the M -topology for any x ∈ F . By [Mag00, Theorem 3.7] and a standard convexity argument,
this is equivalent to the existence of such maps φi and ψi so that ψi ◦ φi (x) converges to φ(x) in
the weak M -topology for any x ∈ F .
More generally, if M0 ⊂ M is an ultraweakly dense C∗ -subalgebra and E is an M0 -system or
M0 -C∗ -algebra, we then say a c.c.p. map φ : F → E is (M0 ⊂ M )-nuclear if there exist nets of
c.c.p. maps φi : F → Mn(i) (C) and ψi : Mn(i) (C) → E such that ψi ◦ φi (x) converges to φ(x)
in the (M0 ⊂ M )-topology for any x ∈ F . Equivalently, by Proposition 3.3, there exist a net of
c.c.p. maps φi : F → Mn(i) (C) and ψi : Mn(i) (C) → E such that ψi ◦ φi (x) converges to φ(x) in
the weak (M0 ⊂ M )-topology.

                           5. Weakly exact von Neumann algebras

Recall from [Iso13, Definition 3.1.1] that for a von Neumann algebra M and an ultraweakly
dense C∗ -algebra M0 ⊂ M , the C∗ -algebra M0 is said to be weakly exact in M if for any unital
C∗ -algebra B with a closed two-sided ideal J and any representation π : M0 ⊗ B → B(K)
with M0 ⊗ J ⊂ ker π that is ultraweakly continuous on M ⊗ C, the induced representation
π̃ : M0 ⊙ B/J → B(H) is min-continuous. This notion generalizes weak exactness for von

Neumann algebras and exactness for C∗ -algebras simultaneously: if M0 = M is a von Neumann
algebra, then M is weakly exact in M if and only if M is weakly exact in the sense of Kirchberg

exact in (M0 )∗∗ if and only if M0 is exact.
In this section, we characterize weak exactness via (M0 ⊂ M )-nuclearity, analogous to the case
of exactness for C∗ -algebras.

The principal example of a property (AO) von Neumann algebra is the group von Neumann

ν : Cλ∗ Γ ⊙ Cρ∗ Γ → B(ℓ2 Γ)/K(ℓ2 Γ) extends continuously to Cλ∗ Γ ⊗ Cρ∗ Γ and has a u.c.p. lift into

Several variations on this definition have since appeared in the literature in connection with

the connection between these properties and biexactness. As there is extensive literature devoted
toward von Neumann algebras with (AO)-type properties [Oza04, Shl04, Oza06b, VV07, GJ07,

a rich source of examples of biexact von Neumann algebras. We begin by recalling some of these
definitions.
