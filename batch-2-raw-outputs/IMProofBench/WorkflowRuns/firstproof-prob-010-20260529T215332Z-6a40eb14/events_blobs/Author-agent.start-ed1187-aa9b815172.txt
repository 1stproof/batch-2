\author{}

\section*{Problem statement and interpretation}

I use the standard graph-product meaning of irreducible: the finite graph
\(\Gamma=(V,E)\) is not a non-trivial join, equivalently the complement
\(\Gamma^c\) is connected.  The traces are faithful normal traces.  For
\(U\subset V\), \(M_U\) denotes the graph product over the induced subgraph and
\(E_U:M\to M_U\) its canonical expectation.

Put \(A_N=\mathbb B(L^2N)\) and
\[
        \cA_N=(A_N^{\sharp_J})^*
\]
for the simultaneous \(N\)- and \(JNJ\)-normal bidual of
Ding--Kunnawalkam Elayavalli--Peterson (DKEP) \cite{DKEP}.  If \(X\) is an
\(N\)-boundary piece, \(q_X\in\cA_N\) denotes the support projection of the
corner \((K_X(N)^{\sharp_J})^*\).  Thus
\[
 \widetilde S_X(N)=
 \{T\in\cA_N:[T,a]\in q_X\cA_Nq_X\hbox{ for all }a\in JNJ\}.
\]
For \(X=\mathbb K(L^2N)\) write \(q_0\) and \(\widetilde S(N)\).  We use DKEP
Lemma 8.5 for the trivial group action: if \(N\) is a separable finite factor,
then proper proximality relative to \(X\) is equivalent to the absence of an
\(N\)-central state on \(\widetilde S_X(N)\) restricting to the trace.

\section*{Normal supports and graph-product boundary corners}

Let \(A=\mathbb B(L^2N)\).  If \(X\subset A\) is a DKEP boundary piece with
\(N,JNJ\subset M(X)\), let \(q_X\) be the unit of the weak-* closure of \(X\)
in \(\cA_N\).  This is the same projection as the support of
\((K_X(N)^{\sharp_J})^*\) used in DKEP Lemma 8.5.  Indeed \(X\subset K_X(N)\),
and DKEP Theorem 5.6 implies that every functional in \(A^{\sharp_J}\) which
vanishes on \(X\) vanishes on \(K_X(N)\): since \(X\) is hereditary,
\(XAX\subset X\), so Theorem 5.6(4) applies to all elements of \(K_X(N)\subset
K_X^{\infty,1}(N)\).  Thus \(X\) and \(K_X(N)\) have the same
\(A^{\sharp_J}\)-annihilator.  Consequently, for \(T\in A\),
\[
 \phi(T)=0\hbox{ for all }\phi\in A^{\sharp_J}\hbox{ with }\phi|_X=0
 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad T\in q_X\cA_Nq_X .                 \tag{1}
\]
In the von Neumann algebra \(\cA_N\) we shall use the elementary fact
\[
R^*R\in r\cA_Nr,\quad S^*S\in s\cA_Ns
       \quad\Longrightarrow\quad R^*S\in r\cA_Ns,              \tag{2}
\]
for projections \(r,s\in\cA_N\) and \(R,S\in A\).  Indeed
\(R(1-r)=0\) and \(S(1-s)=0\).

For a von Neumann subalgebra \(B\subset N\), set
\[
        \cH_B={}_NL^2N_B\otimes_B{}_BL^2N_N .
\]

\begin{lemma}[induced subalgebra boundary]\label{lem:coeff}
For every expected subalgebra \(B\subset N\), \(X_{\cH_B}=X_B\), where the
left hand side is DKEP's coefficient boundary of the \(N\)-\(N\)
correspondence \(\cH_B\), and \(X_B\) is the subalgebra boundary of DKEP
Example 3.4.  In the tracial case
\(\overline{\cH_B}\cong\cH_B\) as \(N\)-\(N\) correspondences.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Let \(H={}_NL^2N_B\) and \(K={}_BL^2N_N\).  DKEP Example 5.11 gives
\(X_H=X_B\).  For the vector \(\widehat1\in H\), the operator
\(T_{\widehat1}:L^2N\to L^2B\) is \(E_B\), so
\(T_{\widehat1}^{\,*}T_{\widehat1}=e_B\) and
\(T_{\widehat1}T_{\widehat1}^{\,*}=1_{L^2B}\).  Thus \(H\) is mixing relative
to \(X_B\times \mathbb B(L^2B)\).  For \(\widehat1\in K\),
\(T_{\widehat1}:L^2B\to L^2N\) is the inclusion, so
\(T_{\widehat1}^{\,*}T_{\widehat1}=1_{L^2B}\); hence \(K\) is mixing relative
to \(\mathbb B(L^2B)\times Y_K\), where \(Y_K\) is its right coefficient
boundary.  DKEP Theorem 5.10 and the fusion identity
\(T_{\xi\otimes\eta}=T_\eta T_\xi\) give that
\(H\otimes_BK=\cH_B\) is mixing relative to \(X_B\times Y_K\).  By the
minimality in DKEP Example 5.11, \(X_{\cH_B}\subset X_B\).

Conversely, if \(\xi,\eta\) are bounded in \(H\), then
\(\xi\otimes\widehat1\) and \(\eta\otimes\widehat1\) are bounded in
\(\cH_B\), and, since \(T_{\widehat1}^{\,*}T_{\widehat1}=1_{L^2B}\) for
\(\widehat1\in K\),
\[
 T_{\xi\otimes\widehat1}^{\,*}T_{\eta\otimes\widehat1}
 =T_\xi^{\,*}T_\eta .
\]
Thus every coefficient generator of \(X_H=X_B\) belongs to \(X_{\cH_B}\).
The map \(\overline{\xi\otimes\eta}\mapsto J\eta\otimes J\xi\) gives the
stated self-conjugacy.
\end{proof}

If \(Z\subset U\subset V\), then \(e_{M_Z}\le e_{M_U}\), hence
\(X_{M_Z}\subset X_{M_U}\).

\begin{lemma}[direct sums and finite coefficient algebras]\label{lem:dsupport}
Let \(X\subset\mathbb B(L^2N)\) be a boundary piece with
\(N,JNJ\subset M(X)\).  If
\(\cK=\bigoplus_{j\in J}\cK_j\) is an \(N\)-\(N\) correspondence and
\(X_{\cK_j}\subset X\) for every \(j\), then every coefficient of \(\cK\)
belongs, after the canonical embedding
\(\mathbb B(L^2N)\subset\cA_N\), to \(q_X\cA_Nq_X\).

For \(U\subset V\), let \(\cF_U\) be the class of non-empty finite Connes
fusions of copies of
\(\cH_U=L^2M\otimes_{M_U}L^2M\) and \(\overline{\cH_U}\).  Define
\[
 \mathcal C_U=\operatorname{span}\{\,T_\xi^*T_\eta:
       \xi\in\cK,\ \eta\in\cL \hbox{ bounded},\ \cK,\cL\in\cF_U\,\}.
\]
Then \(\mathcal C_U\) is a self-adjoint algebra, and \(X_{M_U}\) has a
contractive approximate unit contained in the norm closure of \(\mathcal C_U\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Since \(X_{\cK_j}\subset X\), DKEP Example 5.11 and monotonicity in the
boundary pieces imply that each \(\cK_j\) is mixing relative to
\(X\times\mathbb B(L^2N)\).  Let \(H_0\) be the set of bounded vectors
\(\xi\in\cK\) for which \(L_\xi\in B_N(L^2N,\cK)\) is
\(X\)-mixing in the sense of DKEP Section 5.2.  Finite sums of vectors from the
summands lie in \(H_0\).  If \(\xi_n\in H_0\) and \(\xi_n\to\xi\) in Hilbert
norm, with \(\xi\) bounded, then the closure argument in the proof of DKEP
Theorem 5.10 applies: for the functionals
\(\rho_n(a)=\langle(\xi-\xi_n)a,(\xi-\xi_n)\rangle\) on \(N\), choose
projections \(p_k\uparrow1\) so that, for each fixed \(k\),
\(\|L_{\xi-\xi_n}p_k\|\to0\); DKEP Proposition 5.1 says the mixing operators
form a strong right \(N\)-module, whence \(\xi p_k\in H_0\) for all \(k\), and
again strong closedness gives \(\xi\in H_0\).  Taking \(\xi_n\) to be finite
coordinate cutdowns of an arbitrary bounded \(\xi\in\cK\), we obtain
\(\cK\) mixing relative to \(X\times\mathbb B(L^2N)\).  DKEP Theorem 5.10 and
Proposition 5.9 then give \(T_\xi^*T_\xi\in K_X(N)\) for every bounded \(\xi\);
polarization and (1) give the first assertion.

For the algebra assertion, if
\(a=T_{\xi_1}^*T_{\xi_2}\) and \(b=T_{\eta_1}^*T_{\eta_2}\) are elementary
rectangular coefficients from \(\cF_U\), then
\[
 ab=T_{\xi_1\otimes\overline{\xi_2}\otimes\eta_1}^{\,*}T_{\eta_2},
\]
using \(T_{\overline{\xi_2}}=T_{\xi_2}^*\) and the fusion identity; adjoints
are immediate.  By Lemma \ref{lem:coeff}, \(X_{M_U}=X_{\cH_U}\), and this
hereditary algebra is generated by the positive coefficients
\(T_\xi^*T_\xi\) of \(\cH_U\).  The usual net
\(f_\varepsilon(\sum_{j=1}^nT_{\xi_j}^*T_{\xi_j})\), with
\(0\le f_\varepsilon\le1\), \(f_\varepsilon(0)=0\), over finite positive sums
and \(\varepsilon\downarrow0\), is a contractive approximate unit for this
hereditary algebra, and each term is a norm limit of polynomials with zero
constant term in elements of \(\mathcal C_U\).
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[boundary-corner intersection]\label{lem:intersection}
Let \(q_U=q_{X_{M_U}}\).  For all \(U,W\subset V\),
\[
        q_W(q_U\cA_Mq_U)q_W\subset q_{U\cap W}\cA_Mq_{U\cap W}.   \tag{3}
\]
Consequently, if \(U_1,\ldots,U_m\subset V\) and \(I=\cap_iU_i\), then
\[
q_{U_m}q_{U_{m-1}}\cdots q_{U_1}q_{U_2}\cdots q_{U_{m-1}}q_{U_m}
\in q_I\cA_Mq_I .
\]
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Put \(I=U\cap W\).  Choose contractive approximate units
\(h_\alpha^W\in\overline{\mathcal C_W}\) and
\(h_\beta^U\in\overline{\mathcal C_U}\) as in Lemma \ref{lem:dsupport}; they
converge weak-* in \(\cA_M\) to \(q_W\) and \(q_U\).  We first show
\[
        h_\alpha^Wh_\beta^U\in q_I\cA_Mq_U,\qquad
        h_\beta^Uh_\alpha^W\in q_U\cA_Mq_I .                    \tag{4}
\]
By norm closure it is enough to take
\(a=T_{\xi_1}^*T_{\xi_2}\in\mathcal C_W\) and
\(b=T_{\eta_1}^*T_{\eta_2}\in\mathcal C_U\).  Then
\[
        ab=T_{\xi_1\otimes\overline{\xi_2}\otimes\eta_1}^{\,*}T_{\eta_2}.
                                                                        \tag{5}
\]
CDHJKN identify \(\cH_Y\) with their graph-product correspondence \(H_Y(V,V)\),
and their fusion rule \cite[Theorem 5.4 and Proposition 5.5]{CDHJKN} says that
\(\cH_Y\otimes_M\cH_Z\) decomposes as a direct sum of copies of \(\cH_R\) with
\(R\subset Y\cap Z\).  Hence any finite \(U\)-fusion decomposes over subsets of
\(U\), while the correspondence containing the first vector in (5), which
contains both \(W\)- and \(U\)-blocks, decomposes over subsets of \(I\).  Lemma
\ref{lem:dsupport}, together with \(X_{M_R}\subset X_{M_I}\) or
\(X_{M_U}\), gives
\[
T_{\xi_1\otimes\overline{\xi_2}\otimes\eta_1}^{\,*}
T_{\xi_1\otimes\overline{\xi_2}\otimes\eta_1}\in q_I\cA_Mq_I,\quad
T_{\eta_2}^{\,*}T_{\eta_2}\in q_U\cA_Mq_U .
\]
The rectangular fact (2) gives \(ab\in q_I\cA_Mq_U\); the second inclusion in
(4) follows by adjoints.

Now take \(x\in q_U\cA_Mq_U\).  By (4),
\[
h_\alpha^Wh_\beta^U\,x\,h_\gamma^Uh_\delta^W\in q_I\cA_Mq_I .
\]
Separate weak-* continuity of multiplication in \(\cA_M\), followed
successively in the four indices, gives
\(q_Wxq_W\in q_I\cA_Mq_I\).  This proves (3).  The palindromic assertion is an
induction, applying (3) at the last step to the subset already obtained.
\end{proof}

\section*{A finite boundary criterion}

\begin{lemma}\label{lem:finite}
Let \(N\) be a separable non-amenable finite factor.  Let \(X_1,\ldots,X_m\) be
boundary pieces and put \(q_i=q_{X_i}\).  Assume
\[
        q_mq_{m-1}\cdots q_1q_2\cdots q_{m-1}q_m\in q_0\cA_Nq_0.  \tag{6}
\]
If \(N\) is properly proximal relative to each \(X_i\), then \(N\) is properly
proximal.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Assume \(N\) is not properly proximal.  DKEP Lemma 8.5 gives an \(N\)-central
state \(\omega\) on \(\widetilde S(N)\) with \(\omega|_N=\tau\).  Fix \(i\) and
write \(q=q_i\).  Since \(q\) commutes with \(N\) and \(JNJ\),
\(q^\perp\widetilde S_{X_i}(N)q^\perp\subset\widetilde S(N)\).  If
\(\omega(q^\perp)>0\), then
\[
 y\mapsto \omega(q^\perp yq^\perp)/\omega(q^\perp)
\]
is an \(N\)-central state on \(\widetilde S_{X_i}(N)\).  Its restriction to
\(N\) is dominated by \(\tau\) on \(N_+\), hence is normal; centrality and the
factor assumption make it a scalar multiple of \(\tau\), and normalization
makes it equal to \(\tau\).  This contradicts relative proper proximality, so
\(\omega(q_i^\perp)=0\) for all \(i\).

Extend \(\omega\) to a state on the von Neumann algebra \(\cA_N\).  In its GNS
space each \(q_i\) fixes the cyclic vector, and therefore the positive
contraction in (6) has value \(1\).  Since it is supported by \(q_0\), we have
\(\omega(q_0)=1\) (here \(q_0\in\widetilde S(N)\), so this value is independent
of the extension).  For \(T\in\mathbb B(L^2N)\) put
\[
        \Theta(T)=q_0Tq_0 .
\]
This is the \(N\)- and \(JNJ\)-bimodular u.c.p. compression used in the proof of
DKEP Lemma 8.5; moreover \(\Theta(T)\in\widetilde S(N)\), since its
\(JNJ\)-commutators are supported by \(q_0\).  Hence
\(\psi(T)=\omega(\Theta(T))\) is defined using the original central state, is
\(N\)-central, and satisfies
\(\psi(x)=\omega(xq_0)=\omega(x)=\tau(x)\) for \(x\in N\).  Thus \(\psi\) is a
Connes hypertrace, contradicting non-amenability of the finite factor \(N\).
\end{proof}

\section*{A relative Bass--Serre paradox}

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:BS}
Let \(N=P*_{B}Q\) with trace-preserving expectations and suppose
\(Q=B\bbarotimes A\).  Assume that \(A\) contains a trace-zero unitary \(a\) and
that \(P\) contains unitaries \(w_1,w_2\) satisfying
\[
        E_B(w_i)=0,\qquad E_B(w_1^*w_2)=0 .
\]
Then \(N\) is properly proximal relative to \(X_B\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Let \(H_P=L^2P\ominus L^2B\) and \(H_Q=L^2Q\ominus L^2B\).  In the AFP
decomposition of \(L^2N\), let \(p\) and \(q\) be the projections onto reduced
words whose first letter lies in \(H_P\) and \(H_Q\), respectively; then
\(1=e_B+p+q\).  For \(R_x=Jx^*J\), reduced-word inspection gives
\[
[p,R_x]=R_xe_B-e_BR_x,\ [q,R_x]=0\quad(x\in P\ominus B),
\]
and the same formula with \(p,q\) interchanged for \(x\in Q\ominus B\); elements
of \(B\) commute with \(p,q\).  These commutators are \(N\)- and \(JNJ\)-
translates of \(e_B\), so DKEP Lemma 6.1 implies \(p,q\in S_{X_B}(N)\).

Let \(\varphi\) be an \(N\)-central state on \(S_{X_B}(N)\) normal on \(N\).
Left multiplication by a centered \(P\)-letter sends \(qL^2N\) into \(pL^2N\),
and \(q w_1^*w_2q=0\) because \(E_B(w_1^*w_2)=0\).  Hence the two ranges are
orthogonal and
\(w_1qw_1^*+w_2qw_2^*\le p\).  Similarly, since \(a\in Q\ominus B\),
\(apa^*\le q\).  Centrality gives
\[
        2\varphi(q)\le\varphi(p)\le\varphi(q),
\]
so \(\varphi(p)=\varphi(q)=0\).  The unitary \(h=w_1a\) is \(B\)-Haar: every
non-zero power is a reduced alternating word and has \(B\)-expectation zero.
Thus the projections \(h^ne_Bh^{-n}\) are pairwise orthogonal and have the same
\(\varphi\)-value; hence \(\varphi(e_B)=0\).  This contradicts
\(1=e_B+p+q\).  DKEP Theorem 6.2 gives proper proximality relative to \(X_B\).
\end{proof}

\section*{Application to graph products}

Fix \(v\in V\) and let \(L_v=\Lk_\Gamma(v)\).  The graph product splits as
\[
        M\cong M_{V\setminus\{v\}}*_{M_{L_v}}
        (M_{L_v}\bbarotimes M_v).                                \tag{10}
\]
Let \(C(v)=\{r\ne v:r\not\sim_\Gamma v\}\).  Since \(\Gamma^c\) is connected,
\(C(v)\ne\emptyset\).  If \(C(v)\) contains distinct \(r,t\), choose trace-zero
unitaries \(u_r\in M_r,u_t\in M_t\) and put \(w_1=u_r,w_2=u_t\).  If
\(C(v)=\{r\}\), connectedness and \(|V|\ge3\) give
\(s\in L_v\) with \(s\not\sim_\Gamma r\); put \(w_1=u_r,w_2=u_su_r\).  In both
cases reduced-word calculus gives
\[
        E_{M_{L_v}}(w_i)=0,\qquad E_{M_{L_v}}(w_1^*w_2)=0 .
\]
The trace-zero unitary in \(M_v\) is the unitary \(a\) in (10), so
Proposition \ref{prop:BS} shows that \(M\) is properly proximal relative to
\(X_{M_{L_v}}\) for every \(v\in V\).

Enumerate \(V=\{v_1,\ldots,v_m\}\).  Since no vertex belongs to its own link,
\(\cap_iL_{v_i}=\emptyset\).  Lemma \ref{lem:intersection} gives
\[
 q_{L_{v_m}}\cdots q_{L_{v_1}}q_{L_{v_2}}\cdots q_{L_{v_m}}
 \in q_{\emptyset}\cA_Mq_{\emptyset}.
\]
Here \(M_\emptyset=\C\), and \(X_{M_\emptyset}=\mathbb K(L^2M)\).

Finally, CDHJKN Main Theorem 0.5 implies that \(M\) is a factor: the possible
obstructions would give either a universal vertex of \(\Gamma\) or an isolated
exceptional non-edge component, both impossible because \(\Gamma^c\) is
connected with at least three vertices.  CDHJKN Proposition 6.3 similarly
excludes amenability: its amenable non-edge components are single vertices or
isolated exceptional edges, whereas \(\Gamma^c\) is connected with at least
three vertices.  Thus \(M\) is a separable non-amenable finite factor.
Lemma \ref{lem:finite} applies and proves that \(M\) is properly proximal.
