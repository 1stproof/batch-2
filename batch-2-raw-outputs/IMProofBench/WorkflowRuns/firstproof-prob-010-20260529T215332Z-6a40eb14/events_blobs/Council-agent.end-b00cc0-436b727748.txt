The overarching strategy you have proposed—bypassing the need for the projections to commute by using a palindromic product and checking the algebraic fusion rules—is highly elegant and structurally correct. 

To answer your question directly: your intuition is perfectly on target, but the transition labeled "Passing to hereditary closures and then to the normal bidual" currently skips a couple of functional analytic steps. Because the weak-* product of weakly dense sets is not necessarily weakly dense, you cannot map the inclusion directly to the corners $q_U \mathcal{A}_M q_U$. 

Fortunately, the workaround is purely algebraic and very clean. Here is how you can rewrite the middle of the proof of Lemma 1 to make it completely rigorous.

### 1. Upgrade from sets to C*-algebras
First, clarify that the coefficient boundaries are not just Banach spaces, but C*-algebras. Because $X_W$ is generated by $M$-translates of $e_{M_W}$, it is the norm closure of the dense *-algebra $X_W^0 = M e_{M_W} M$.
Your observation about the coefficients of $\mathcal{L}$ tells us exactly that the dense algebraic span of the coefficients of $\mathcal{L}$ is $X_W^0 X_U^0 X_W^0$. Taking norm closures immediately yields the C*-algebra inclusion:
\[
    X_W X_U X_W \subset X_{\mathcal{L}}.
\]

### 2. Passing to the bidual via projection inequalities
Because $X_W, X_U,$ and $X_{\mathcal{L}}$ are C*-algebras, you can pass to their biduals. Multiplication in a von Neumann algebra is separately weak-* continuous, meaning $a \mapsto x a y$ is continuous. This guarantees that the bidual von Neumann algebras satisfy:
\[
    X_W^{**} X_U^{**} X_W^{**} \subset X_{\mathcal{L}}^{**}.
\]
The support projections $q_W, q_U, q_{\mathcal{L}}$ in $\mathcal{A}_M$ are exactly the identity elements of these respective bidual algebras. Evaluating the above inclusion at the identities gives the projection inequality:
\[
    q_W q_U q_W \le q_{\mathcal{L}}.
\]

### 3. Recovering the corner inclusion
With the operator inequality in hand, the corner inclusion follows by standard von Neumann algebra mechanics. Since $q_W q_U q_W \le q_{\mathcal{L}}$, sandwiching by $q_{\mathcal{L}}^\perp$ yields:
\[
    q_{\mathcal{L}}^\perp q_W q_U q_W q_{\mathcal{L}}^\perp = 0 \implies q_{\mathcal{L}}^\perp q_W q_U = 0.
\]
Thus $q_W q_U = q_{\mathcal{L}} q_W q_U$. For any $T \in \mathcal{A}_M$, we then have:
\[
    q_W q_U T q_U q_W = (q_{\mathcal{L}} q_W q_U) T (q_U q_W q_{\mathcal{L}}) \in q_{\mathcal{L}} \mathcal{A}_M q_{\mathcal{L}}.
\]
This proves (3) rigorously without ever needing $q_W$ and $q_U$ to commute or worrying about weak density of products.

### 4. A minor detail on the CDHJKN direct sums
When applying the fusion rules from [CDHJKN], it is worth adding one sentence to justify why $q_{\mathcal{L}} \le q_{U \cap W}$. If $\mathcal{L} = \bigoplus_i \mathcal{H}_{Z_i}$, the cross-terms in the coefficient formula $T_{\xi_i}^* T_{\eta_j}$ vanish precisely because the sub-bimodules are orthogonal (i.e., $\langle \xi_i, \eta_j \rangle_M = 0$). Thus, the coefficient boundary of a direct sum is exactly the norm-closed sum of the individual coefficient boundaries. Since $Z_i \subset U \cap W \implies X_{M_{Z_i}} \subset X_{M_{U \cap W}}$, this rigorously forces $X_{\mathcal{L}} \subset X_{M_{U \cap W}}$, and hence $q_{\mathcal{L}} \le q_{U \cap W}$.
