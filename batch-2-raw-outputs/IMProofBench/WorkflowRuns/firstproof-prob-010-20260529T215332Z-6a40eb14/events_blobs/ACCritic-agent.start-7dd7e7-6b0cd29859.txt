## Referee report

However, the proof relies crucially on a “DKEP permanence” theorem whose two parts are neither proved nor correctly validated from the cited literature. This is a fatal mathematical gap.

### Literature validation

The quoted relative amenability theorem is essentially consistent with Charlesworth–de Santiago–Hayes–Jekel–Kunnawalkam Elayavalli–Nelson: their Main Theorem 0.1 states the tracial graph-product relative amenability criterion under the trace-zero unitary hypothesis, with exactly the amenability condition and the exceptional two-dimensional adjacency alternative used in the proof. ([ems.press](https://ems.press/content/serial-article-files/51743))

The serious issue is the claimed **DKEP permanence theorem**. The cited DKEP paper does discuss examples including free products, but the located free-product result in the paper is Proposition 6.7, which states only that the free product of **two diffuse tracial von Neumann algebras** is properly proximal. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2204.00517)) This does **not** cover the answer’s stated free-product permanence for arbitrary tracial algebras with trace-zero unitaries, nor the finite-dimensional cases needed in the base step.

Even more seriously, the cited DKEP paper does not appear to contain the claimed amalgamated-free-product permanence theorem; a text search of the DKEP PDF for “amalgam” returned no match. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2204.00517)) The closest “upgrading” technology I found is in a separate Ding–Kunnawalkam Elayavalli preprint, but its Theorem 1.1 has substantial extra hypotheses — diffuse ambient algebra, diffuse subalgebras, commuting Jones projections, bounded Pimsner–Popa bases, mixing — and a central-projection decomposition conclusion, not the simple permanence statement asserted in `answer.tex`. ([arxiv.org](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2211.05298))

### Paragraph-by-paragraph mathematical audit

1. **Problem statement and interpretation.**  
   The adopted interpretation of irreducibility as “not a non-trivial join,” equivalently connected complement graph, is standard and faithful to the likely intended meaning. The original problem statement is somewhat ambiguous because it does not explicitly say the join pieces are nonempty and disjoint, but `answer.tex` does record its interpretation. This part is acceptable.

2. **Notation \(M_U\).**  
   Standard and acceptable. The proof implicitly uses the canonical trace-preserving conditional expectations \(M \to M_U\); this is standard for graph products, though it would be better to state explicitly.

3. **“Standard inputs” paragraph.**  
   This is the main fatal gap. The proof says it uses free-product and amalgamated-free-product permanence results “proved by Ding–Kunnawalkam Elayavalli–Peterson,” but no theorem numbers are given, and the web/literature check does not validate the stated results in the cited DKEP paper. Since these permanence results are the engine of both the base case and induction, they cannot be left as a vague “standard fact.”

4. **Theorem `DKEP permanence`, part (1).**  
   This is essential and unvalidated:
   \[
   P\text{ properly proximal and not amenable relative to }B
   \implies P *_B (B\bar\otimes A)\text{ properly proximal}.
   \]
   The proof gives no argument for this and the cited DKEP source does not visibly contain it. The actual known upgrading theorem I found has much stronger hypotheses and a different conclusion. This is a fatal gap.

5. **Theorem `DKEP permanence`, part (2).**  
   This is also essential and unvalidated. DKEP’s located free-product proposition covers diffuse tracial algebras only, while the answer needs finite-dimensional cases. For example, the hypotheses of the problem allow all vertex algebras to be \(\mathbb C^2\) with the equal trace, and then the base case may involve finite-dimensional free-product factors such as \(\mathbb C^4 * \mathbb C^2\). The cited diffuse free-product result cannot be applied there.

6. **Relative amenability theorem.**  
   This input is correctly aligned with the cited CDHJKN theorem. The answer’s specialization to \(X=V_{k-1}\), \(Y=L\) is legitimate.

7. **Graph-product AFP decomposition.**  
   The decomposition
   \[
   M_{W\cup\{v\}}\cong M_W*_{M_L}(M_L\bar\otimes M_v)
   \]
   is standard and the one-sentence reduced-word justification is acceptable for an expert proof, though it should ideally specify the expectations and trace-preserving identification.

8. **Ordering of vertices.**  
   The construction is basically correct: since \(\Gamma^c\) is connected and \(|V|\ge3\), one can choose a length-two complement path \(v_1-v_2-v_3\), then add vertices so each new vertex attaches by a complement edge to the previous set. The sentence about “listing vertices by nondecreasing distance from the root” is slightly imprecise because the displayed initial order \(v_1,v_2,v_3\) is not itself nondecreasing in distance from root \(v_2\), but this is easily repaired and not fatal.

9. **Base case.**  
   The graph-product identification
   \[
   M_{V_3}\cong M_{\{v_1,v_3\}}*M_{v_2}
   \]
   is correct. The assertion that \(M_{\{v_1,v_3\}}\) has a trace-zero unitary and is not two-dimensional is also correct. However, the conclusion that this free product is properly proximal depends entirely on the unvalidated Theorem `DKEP permanence`(2). This is a fatal gap in the base case.

10. **Inductive AFP step.**  
   The decomposition into
   \[
   M_{V_k}\cong P*_{M_L}(M_L\bar\otimes M_{v_k})
   \]
   is correct. But the conclusion that proper proximality passes to this AFP is exactly the unvalidated Theorem `DKEP permanence`(1). This is the second fatal use of the unsupported input.

11. **Proof that \(P\) is not amenable relative to \(M_L\).**  
   This part is sound. Since \(r_k\notin L\) and \(r_k\) is nonadjacent to \(s_k\) in \(\Gamma\), the relative amenability criterion forces the exceptional alternative, making \(\{r_k,s_k\}\) an isolated connected component of the induced complement graph on \(V_{k-1}\), contradicting connectedness and \(|V_{k-1}|\ge3\).

12. **Conclusion.**  
   The induction conclusion is valid only conditionally on the unproved permanence theorem. Since that theorem is not validated, the proof is incomplete.
