## Referee report

### Overall verdict

I find the mathematical argument in `answer.tex` essentially correct and complete for the stated, explicitly adopted interpretation of \(R_n^{(m)}\) as the diagonal coinvariant algebra. The proof relies on two substantial external results—diagonal supersymmetry and the Rhoades–Wilson Hilbert series formula—but these are appropriate cited inputs, and the subsequent extraction of the hook coefficients is rigorous.

The LaTeX submission also satisfies the stated First Proof LaTeX contract: it uses exactly `\documentclass[12pt]{article}`, only the permitted `fullpage` layout package, no forbidden line-spacing or margin changes, no in-document font-size commands, and it compiles successfully to a 3-page PDF.

### Literature validation

The interpretation as a multivariate diagonal coinvariant setting is consistent with Bergeron’s work, whose abstract says the Hilbert series of diagonally harmonic/coinvariant spaces in multiple variable sets admits a global description independent of the number of sets. ([sciencedirect.com](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S000187081300056X))

The diagonal supersymmetry input is also supported by Lentfer’s stated theorem: for \(R_n^{(k,j)}\), the Frobenius series decomposes as a sum of super Schur functions \(s_\lambda(q/u)\) times \(\mathfrak S_n\)-Frobenius characters with coefficients independent of \(k,j\). The same source defines the super Schur convention \(s_\lambda(q/u)=\sum_{\nu\subseteq\lambda}s_\nu(q)s_{\lambda'/\nu'}(u)\), matching the positive-sign convention used in `answer.tex`. ([pages.uoregon.edu](https://pages.uoregon.edu/plhersh/CALICO/Lentfer-2026.pdf))

The Rhoades–Wilson formula used in the proof is correctly quoted: their Theorem 5.3 gives
\[
\operatorname{Hilb}(SR_n;q,z)=\sum_{k=1}^n z^{n-k}[k]!_q\,\operatorname{Stir}_q(n,k),
\]
and the recursion for \(\operatorname{Stir}_q(n,k)\) appears in the introduction in the same form used by the author. ([cambridge.org](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/forum-of-mathematics-pi/article/hilbert-series-of-the-superspace-coinvariant-ring/D7F1C91BE28068FC384AFE85B6AED8B8)) ([cambridge.org](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/forum-of-mathematics-pi/article/hilbert-series-of-the-superspace-coinvariant-ring/D7F1C91BE28068FC384AFE85B6AED8B8))

### Paragraph-by-paragraph audit

#### Title and interpretation section

The adopted interpretation of \(R_n^{(m)}\) as
\[
\mathbb C[x_i^{(r)}:1\le i\le n,\ 1\le r\le m]/
\langle \mathbb C[x_i^{(r)}]^{\mathfrak S_n}_{+}\rangle
\]
with diagonal \(\mathfrak S_n\)-action is faithful to the problem statement. The notation is slightly compressed, because \(\mathbb C[x_i^{(r)}]^{\mathfrak S_n}_{+}\) should be understood as the positive-degree invariants in the full polynomial ring, but the surrounding text makes this clear.

The statement that \(c_\lambda(n)\) are stable \(GL_m\)-multiplicities is valid under the diagonal \(GL_m\)-action mixing the \(m\) alphabets. This is a standard representation-theoretic interpretation of Schur coefficients in the multigraded Hilbert series.

#### Ordered set partition model

The definition of \(\OP_{r,k}\) is precise. The inversion statistic
\[
i<j,\qquad i=\min B_m,\qquad j\in B_\ell,\qquad \ell<m
\]
is the usual inversion statistic on ordered set partitions used with the \(q\)-Stirling recurrence. The claim that for \(k=r\) it reduces to the usual inversion number of the corresponding permutation is correct.

#### The theorem statement

The theorem gives a clean combinatorial interpretation:
\[
c_{(a,1^b)}(n)
=
\#\{\sigma\in \OP_{r,k}:\ 2\le r\le n,\ r-k=b+1,\ r\text{ not singleton},\ \inv(\sigma)=a-1\}.
\]
The indexing is consistent: after the later substitution \(r\mapsto r+1\), the exponent of \(z\) is \(r-k-1=b\), so \(r-k=b+1\). The edge case \(n=1\) is harmless: the sum is empty, and only \(c_\varnothing(1)=1\) remains.

#### First standard input: diagonal supersymmetry

This is the main nontrivial external input. The author does not prove it, but cites Lentfer and states the needed consequence correctly: the universal coefficients in the purely bosonic Schur expansion are the same universal coefficients appearing in the bosonic–fermionic super Schur expansion, after applying the dimension map to the \(\mathfrak S_n\)-Frobenius factor.

This paragraph would be improved by citing the precise theorem number, but mathematically it is not a fatal gap because the cited result directly supplies the needed stability/universality statement.

#### Super Schur specialization

The specialization
\[
s_{(a,1^b)}(q/z)=q^a z^b+q^{a-1}z^{b+1}
\]
is correct for the positive super Schur convention used by Lentfer. Non-hook partitions vanish in one even and one odd variable because they lie outside the \((1,1)\)-hook. Hence
\[
H_n(q,z)=1+(q+z)\sum_{a\ge1,b\ge0}c_{(a,1^b)}(n)q^{a-1}z^b
\]
is correct. The constant \(1\) accounts for \(\lambda=\varnothing\).

#### Second standard input: Rhoades–Wilson Hilbert series

The formula
\[
H_n(q,z)=\sum_{k=1}^{n} z^{n-k}[k]!_q\operatorname{Stir}_q(n,k)
\]
and the \(q\)-Stirling recurrence are correctly stated. The definition
\[
F_{r,k}=[k]!_q\operatorname{Stir}_q(r,k)
\]
then gives
\[
F_{r,k}=[k]_qF_{r-1,k-1}+[k]_qF_{r-1,k},
\]
which is algebraically correct.

#### Derivation of the recurrence for \(H_n\)

The passage
\[
H_n=H_{n-1}+(q+z)\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}[k]_qF_{n-1,k}z^{n-1-k}
\]
is valid. The key identity is
\[
[k+1]_q=1+q[k]_q,
\]
and the reindexing of the first summation contributes exactly \(H_{n-1}\). Iterating from \(H_1=1\) gives
\[
\frac{H_n(q,z)-1}{q+z}
=
\sum_{r=1}^{n-1}\sum_{k=1}^{r}[k]_qF_{r,k}z^{r-k}.
\]
No divisibility issue remains because the recurrence explicitly proves the quotient is a polynomial.

#### Identification of \(F_{r,k}\) with ordered set partitions

The proof that
\[
F_{r,k}=\sum_{\tau\in\OP_{r,k}}q^{\inv(\tau)}
\]
is sound. Inserting the largest element \(r\) either as a singleton into a partition of \([r-1]\) with \(k-1\) blocks or into an existing block of a partition of \([r-1]\) with \(k\) blocks gives the two terms of the recurrence. If the final position is \(i\) from the left, the new inversions are exactly contributed by the minima of the blocks to the right, giving \(k-i\) new inversions and therefore a factor \([k]_q\).

A minor presentational improvement would be to explicitly mention the base case \(F_{1,1}=1\), or the empty ordered partition convention for \(F_{0,0}=1\), but the recurrence and initial condition are clear enough.

#### Restriction to non-singleton largest element

The restricted insertion argument is bijective: inserting \(r+1\) into one of the existing \(k\) blocks of \(\tau\in\OP_{r,k}\) produces exactly those \(\sigma\in\OP_{r+1,k}\) in which \(r+1\) is not a singleton. Deleting \(r+1\) from its block recovers \(\tau\). The inversion increment is again one of \(0,\dots,k-1\), so
\[
[k]_qF_{r,k}
=
\sum_{\substack{\sigma\in\OP_{r+1,k}\\ r+1\text{ not singleton}}}
q^{\inv(\sigma)}.
\]
The final substitution \(r+1\mapsto r\) yields precisely the theorem’s generating function.

#### Coefficient extraction

Extracting the coefficient of \(q^{a-1}z^b\) gives the stated combinatorial interpretation. This final step is valid because the monomials \(q^{a-1}z^b\) are linearly independent.

### Computational cross-checks

I independently enumerated ordered set partitions for small \(r,k\) and verified:

1. \(F_{r,k}\) from the \(q\)-Stirling recurrence agrees with the inversion enumerator over \(\OP_{r,k}\).
2. \((q+z)G_n=H_n-1\), where \(G_n\) is the proposed hook-coefficient generating function.
3. The theorem’s non-singleton-largest-element enumeration agrees with \(G_n\) for the tested values \(n\le 5\).

No discrepancies appeared.
