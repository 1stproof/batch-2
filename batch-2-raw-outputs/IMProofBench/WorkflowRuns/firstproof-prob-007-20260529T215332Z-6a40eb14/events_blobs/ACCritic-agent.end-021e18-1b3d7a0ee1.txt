### Paragraph-by-paragraph mathematical audit

1. **Counterexample word.**  
   The word
   \[
   w=\bar a a\, b\bar b\, \bar a a\, c\bar c\, \bar a a
   \]
   is freely trivial by the displayed adjacent cancellations. The quasi-reduced check is correct: the only adjacent inverse pairs are the five displayed ones, and none begins a forbidden triple \(\ell\ell^{-1}\ell\).

2. **Compactification and graph \(\Gamma(M)\).**  
   Compactifying the upper half-plane to a disk and replacing crossings by vertices is legitimate. Boundary endpoints become leaves and transverse crossings become valence-four interior vertices.

3. **Lemma 1: \(\Gamma(M)\) is a forest.**  
   The innermost-cycle argument is valid. If a graph cycle existed, an innermost such cycle would bound a disk containing no graph edges; otherwise one obtains either a smaller cycle or an impossible interior leaf. The interior of that cycle would give a complementary region whose boundary component in \(M\cap \partial\overline R\) is closed and has no marked boundary endpoints, contradicting the face condition. This lemma is sound.

4. **Lemma 2: unique inverse-label occurrences force an uncrossed interval.**  
   The argument is correct. At the endpoint labelled \(x\), both local face-boundary sides must terminate at the unique endpoint labelled \(x^{-1}\). Since \(\Gamma(M)\) is a tree in that component, if the path from \(p\) to \(q\) hit a first crossing, the two local face-boundary continuations would leave along distinct components after deleting the crossing vertex, so at most one could reach \(q\). Contradiction. Thus \(p\) and \(q\) are connected by a crossing-free interval.

5. **Forcing and deleting \((3,4)\), \((7,8)\).**  
   Applying Lemma 2 to \(b,\bar b\) and \(c,\bar c\) correctly forces the uncrossed adjacent intervals \((3,4)\) and \((7,8)\). Since their endpoints are adjacent, each cuts off an empty boundary disk. Deleting them and renumbering gives a cell-preserving identification with the auxiliary six-endpoint problem for
   \[
   u=\bar a a\bar a a\bar a a.
   \]
   This reduction is valid.

6. **Six-endpoint classification.**  
   The classification by endpoint pairings is correct. Lemma 1 forbids two crossings between the same two intervals and forbids a triangle of pairwise crossings. Hence, for two intervals, crossing is determined by endpoint interleaving. The statement that there is no further isotopy datum is terse but acceptable here: a planar forest with fixed boundary leaf order and the prescribed crossing structure has a unique regular-neighborhood type.

7. **Converse admissibility for forest interleaving graph.**  
   The parity argument is valid. For any face-boundary arc, pushing it into the adjacent face gives a properly embedded arc disjoint from the matching; hence no interval can have endpoints on opposite sides. Therefore both sides contain an even number of marked endpoints, forcing the two endpoints of the face-boundary arc to have opposite parity. Since \(u\) alternates \(\bar a,a,\bar a,a,\bar a,a\), opposite parity is exactly the inverse-label condition.

8. **Enumeration of cells.**  
   The listed five vertices, six edges, and three squares are exactly the admissible pairings with 0, 1, and 2 crossings. The excluded pairing \(14|25|36\) is indeed the unique all-crossing pairing. I independently checked the enumeration.

9. **Boundary computations.**  
   The square boundaries are correct. For example, resolving the two crossings of \(13|25|46\) gives precisely the four edges
   \[
   e_{01}, e_{13}, e_{23}, e_{02}.
   \]
   The analogous checks for \(Q_{14}\) and \(Q_{24}\) also match the text.

10. **Topology of the final complex.**  
    The one-skeleton is the theta graph with poles \(v_0,v_3\). The first two squares glue along the common length-two path through \(v_1\), forming a disk whose boundary is the remaining cycle through \(v_2,v_4\). The third square glues along that full boundary. The resulting CW complex is therefore \(S^2\). Hence \(F_w\) is not contractible.

These are not fatal omissions in this finite computation.
