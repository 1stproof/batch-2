## Referee report on `answer.tex`

### Overall verdict

`answer.tex` is **not answer-ready**. The LaTeX contract appears satisfied, and the lower-bound argument is largely plausible, though it contains several repairable omissions. The fatal problem is the **upper-bound/existence lemma**, which relies on an unproved and uncited “classical triangular construction” in a form substantially stronger and more specific than what is validated by the supplied reference.

The bibliography entry for Schwartz’s Annals paper is real, and the Annals abstract confirms the smooth embedded paper Möbius band theorem and triangular-limit theorem. But that is a theorem about **smooth isometric embeddings**, not the exact **embedded polygonal, affine-on-clean-triangles, isometric triangulated** object asserted in Lemma 3 of `answer.tex`. ([annals.math.princeton.edu](https://annals.math.princeton.edu/2025/201-1/p05)) Schwartz’s preprint explicitly says the triangular Möbius band is a limit object and “does not quite fit” the smooth definition, and describes smoothing/rounding as an approximation idea. ([math.brown.edu](https://www.math.brown.edu/~res/Papers/moebius3.pdf)) A later source sketches a polygonal near-triangular construction and smoothing, but even there the smoothing details are omitted; this does not supply the precise theorem used in `answer.tex`. ([math.brown.edu](https://www.math.brown.edu/~res/Papers/optimal_effective.pdf))

## Paragraph-by-paragraph mathematical audit

### Opening quotient paragraph

The passage
\[
M_\beta=\Sigma/\langle G_\beta\rangle
\]
and the statement that a realized map descends to an embedding are essentially correct, but the proof is abbreviated. One needs to say explicitly:

1. Condition (2) implies \(f\) is constant on \(G_\beta\)-orbits, so it descends.
2. The descended map is injective by the “only if” direction of condition (2).
3. Since \(M_\beta\) is compact and \(\mathbb R^3\) is Hausdorff, the descended continuous injection is an embedding.

Likewise, the invariant locally finite triangulation descending to a **finite** triangulation uses compactness plus local finiteness. This is true, but not proved. This is a minor gap, not fatal.

### Definition of bends

The definition of ridge, apex, and bend is clear and appropriate for clean triangles.

### Lemma “all bends are lengthened”

The main quadratic-concavity argument is correct:

\[
q''(s)=2\bigl(|L(A)-L(B)|^2-|A-B|^2\bigr)<0
\]
because the ridge is strictly shortened. Since the two side edges are strictly lengthened, \(q(0),q(1)>0\), and concavity gives \(q(s)>0\).

Minor omission: the statement “every non-degenerate subarc of the boundary is shortened” is proved only for a subarc lying in one boundary edge. For an arbitrary boundary subarc crossing several boundary edges, one must subdivide at boundary vertices and sum the strict inequalities. This is easy but should be stated.

### Lemma “a polygonal \(T\)-pair”

The structure is plausible and matches the standard T-pattern/Borsuk–Ulam strategy, but several topological assertions are compressed:

1. **Bends form a circle.**  
   The centerline argument is convincing, but the proof should explicitly show that every centerline point lies in a unique bend even when it lies on a common non-boundary edge of two triangles.

2. **Continuity of \(e(u)\) and \(m(u)\).**  
   This follows from the finite polygonal structure and the previous lemma ensuring nondegenerate image segments, but it is not stated.

3. **Two-point compactification is \(S^2\).**  
   Correct, but should be made explicit: \(S^1\times(0,1)\) compactifies by collapsing the two boundary circles of \(S^1\times[0,1]\) to two points.

4. **The involution is antipodal.**  
   The claim is true, but it is asserted without proof. One can prove it by coordinates, e.g. using an angular coordinate centered at \(u+r/2\), but `answer.tex` does not provide this.

5. **Application of Borsuk–Ulam.**  
   Once the previous compactification and oddness are justified, the Borsuk–Ulam step is valid.

These are fixable omissions, but in a strict proof they are still gaps.

### Proposition lower bound: initial setup

The argument that at least one of the two image segments does not contain the intersection point \(O\) in its relative interior is correct: otherwise two distinct bend interiors would map to the same point, contradicting embeddedness.

### Lower bound: lifting \(T\) and requiring \(0\le t<\beta\)

This is an important underproved step. The text says:

> If the horizontal displacement had absolute value at least \(\beta\), the lift would meet a \(G_\beta\)-translate of itself.

But it does not explain why that is impossible. One must prove that such a meeting would create two distinct points of the same bend that are \(G_\beta\)-equivalent, hence have the same image under \(f\), contradicting the fact that the bend image is a nondegenerate affine segment. The endpoint case \(|t|=\beta\) also needs separate mention, since then the two endpoints are \(G_\beta\)-identified and their images would coincide, contradicting bend lengthening.

The phrase “reflecting the \(x\)-axis” is also imprecise; it should say reflection \(x\mapsto -x\), or otherwise specify the symmetry being used.

### Lower bound: boundary arc lengths

Given the normalized lift from \((0,0)\) to \((t,1)\) with \(0\le t<\beta\), the boundary arcs have lengths

\[
\beta-t,\qquad \beta+t.
\]

This is correct. It would be helpful to explain the boundary identification explicitly: the upper endpoint \((t,1)\) corresponds to the lower boundary point \(t+\beta\) modulo \(2\beta\).

### Inequality (2)

\[
\beta-t=\ell(H)>\ell(H^*)\ge |T^*|>\sqrt{1+t^2}
\]

is valid, assuming the earlier boundary-shortening lemma and bend-lengthening lemma are fully justified.

### Inequality (3)

The geometric idea is correct: one endpoint of \(B^*\) is at distance \(>1\) from the line of \(T^*\), so the boundary arc through that endpoint must have length exceeding the sum of two sides of a triangle with base \(>|T^*|\) and height \(>1\).

However, the proof silently uses the lemma:

\[
\text{For fixed base }L\text{ and height }h,\text{ the minimum sum of the two non-base sides is }\sqrt{L^2+4h^2}.
\]

This is elementary, but it should be stated or proved.

### Final optimization

The functions

\[
a(t)=\sqrt{1+t^2}+\sqrt{5+t^2},\qquad
b(t)=2\sqrt{5+t^2}-2t
\]

are handled correctly. A code/symbolic check confirms:

\[
a'(t)=\frac{t}{\sqrt{1+t^2}}+\frac{t}{\sqrt{5+t^2}}\ge0,
\]
\[
b'(t)=\frac{2t}{\sqrt{5+t^2}}-2<0,
\]
and
\[
a(1/\sqrt3)=b(1/\sqrt3)=2\sqrt3.
\]

So the lower-bound optimization is correct.

### Upper-bound Lemma: fatal gap

This is the main failure.

The proof begins:

> We use the classical triangular construction: for every \(\lambda>\sqrt3\) there is an embedded polygonal paper Mobius band \(P:M_\lambda\to\mathbb R^3\), affine and isometric on the triangles of a clean triangulation.

This is an essential external theorem, but it is neither proved nor cited in `answer.tex`. Moreover, the supplied `references.bib` is not used, and the Schwartz Annals theorem does not directly state this polygonal clean-triangulated existence result.

The gap is not cosmetic. The rest of the upper-bound argument depends on the exact properties:

1. \(P\) is embedded.
2. \(P\) is affine on finitely many clean triangles.
3. \(P\) is exactly isometric on each triangle.
4. The triangulation is clean in the specific sense of the problem.
5. This exists for every \(\lambda>\sqrt3\), or at least for arbitrarily many \(\lambda\downarrow\sqrt3\).

The phrase “separating (or rounding, then polyhedrally approximating) the three folds” is handwaving. Rounding gives smooth objects, not affine-on-triangles objects. Polyhedral approximation may preserve embeddedness and inequalities if done carefully, but exact isometry on clean triangles and exact compatibility with the quotient require proof. None is supplied.

Thus Lemma `\ref{lem:upper}` is not a rigorous proof.

### Conditional correctness of the scaling argument

If the asserted polygonal \(P\) existed with the required properties, the subsequent scaling argument would mostly work:

- \(h(x,y)=(sx,y)\) is well-defined because \(s\beta=\lambda\).
- Boundary edges are shortened by the factor \(rs<1\).
- Non-boundary edges with horizontal displacement \(d_e\) are lengthened if \(r\) is chosen sufficiently close to \(1/s\).
- Finiteness of the edge set is needed and should be justified.

But because the existence of \(P\) is unproved, the upper-bound lemma remains invalid.
